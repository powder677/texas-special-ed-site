CRANDALL ISD Special Education: Complete Resource Guide for Parents

Navigating special education can feel overwhelming, but CRANDALL ISD special education services are designed to support your child's unique learning needs. With a student enrollment of 6,922, CRANDALL ISD serves families throughout the CRANDALL area with comprehensive special education programs, dedicated staff, and clear pathways to ensure every student receives appropriate support.

This guide provides CRANDALL ISD parents with essential information about available services, your rights, and how to advocate effectively for your child.

Understanding CRANDALL ISD Special Education Services

CRANDALL ISD special education encompasses a full range of services designed to meet students with disabilities from Pre-K through age 22. Our district is committed to providing free appropriate public education (FAPE) in the least restrictive environment for all eligible students.

The special education program in CRANDALL ISD includes:

  • Individualized Education Plans (IEPs) tailored to each student's unique needs
  • Resource rooms and pull-out services for academic support
  • Inclusive classroom placements with co-teaching models
  • Specialized programs for autism spectrum disorder
  • Speech and language therapy services
  • Occupational and physical therapy
  • Behavioral support programs and counseling services
  • Transition services for students preparing for post-secondary life

Whether your child learns differently, has a physical disability, or needs behavioral support, CRANDALL ISD maintains services across multiple campuses to ensure accessibility and quality support.

The ARD Process: Your Child's IEP Meeting

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An CRANDALL ISD ARD meeting is where educators, parents, and specialists gather to develop or review your child's Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee decisions. This meeting results in your child's Individualized Education Plan (IEP), which is the legal document guiding all special education services.

CRANDALL ISD requires ARD meetings to occur at least once per school year. Additional meetings are held whenever your child's needs change or when parents or staff request a review.

Your Role in the ARD Meeting Process

As a CRANDALL ISD parent, you are an equal member of the ARD committee. Your input, observations, and goals for your child carry the same weight as school personnel.

Before the meeting, you should:

  1. Review previous IEPs and assessment results
  2. Gather observations about your child's strengths and challenges at home
  3. Write down specific goals you want your child to achieve
  4. List any concerns about current services or placement
  5. Prepare questions about proposed services or changes

During the CRANDALL ISD ARD meeting:

  • Ask clarifying questions about any recommendations
  • Share your perspective on what works for your child
  • Discuss placement options and service delivery models
  • Ensure accommodations and modifications are clearly documented
  • Confirm your understanding of the IEP before signing

You have the right to bring an advocate, attorney, or trusted advisor to your CRANDALL ISD ARD meeting. Many parents find this support invaluable, especially if they anticipate disagreement or significant decisions.

IEP Services and Academic Support

What Services Are Available?

CRANDALL ISD IEP services are individualized based on your child's disability category and documented needs. Services are not determined by disability category alone; instead, the ARD committee reviews assessment data, teacher input, and parent observations to determine what your child needs to access the curriculum.

Common CRANDALL ISD IEP services include:

  • Special education instruction in resource rooms or through inclusion models
  • Related services: speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or social work
  • Accommodations: extended time on tests, modified assignments, or preferential seating
  • Modifications: changed curriculum standards or grading criteria
  • Assistive technology: devices or software supporting communication or learning
  • Behavior support plans: strategies to address challenging behaviors
  • Specialized transportation: if your child has physical or behavioral needs

Service Duration and Frequency

Your CRANDALL ISD IEP specifies exactly how often and for how long your child receives each service. For example, your child might receive speech therapy twice weekly for 30 minutes each session, or resource room support four periods per week.

These specifics matter tremendously—they ensure your child receives consistent, documented support and help parents monitor whether services are actually being delivered as planned.

CRANDALL ISD Parent Rights and Procedural Safeguards

Texas Education Code and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) provide CRANDALL ISD parents with specific rights designed to ensure your child receives appropriate special education services.

Essential Parent Rights

CRANDALL ISD must provide you with written notice of:

  • Prior written notice before proposing or refusing to provide services
  • Your right to an independent evaluation if you disagree with school evaluations
  • Your right to inspect all educational records
  • Your right to request confidentiality of special education information
  • Your right to mediation or due process hearings if you disagree with CRANDALL ISD's decisions
  • Your right to participate fully in all ARD meetings
  • Your right to an interpreter if English is not your primary language

When Disagreements Arise

If you believe CRANDALL ISD is not meeting your child's needs or has violated your procedural rights, you have several options:

Informal resolution: Request a meeting with the special education coordinator or campus principal to discuss your concerns.

Mediation: Request free mediation through the Texas Education Agency (TEA) to work through disagreements with CRANDALL ISD staff.

Due Process Hearing: File a formal complaint requesting a hearing before an impartial hearing officer if you cannot resolve disputes through informal means.

Many CRANDALL area families benefit from consulting with a special education attorney near CRANDALL or hiring a special ed advocate CRANDALL families trust to help navigate these processes.

CRANDALL ISD Dyslexia Program

Dyslexia is a specific learning disability affecting reading, and Texas law requires all districts to implement comprehensive dyslexia programs. CRANDALL ISD dyslexia program provides specialized identification and intervention for students with dyslexia and related disorders.

Identification and Assessment

CRANDALL ISD uses evidence-based screening tools to identify students at risk for dyslexia beginning in kindergarten. If your child shows reading difficulties, you can request a dyslexia evaluation at any time during the school year.

The CRANDALL ISD dyslexia program assessment includes:

  • Phonological awareness screening
  • Letter-sound correspondence assessment
  • Decoding and fluency evaluation
  • Comprehensive reading assessment
  • Cognitive processing analysis

Specialized Instruction

Students identified with dyslexia in CRANDALL ISD receive instruction based on Orton-Gillingham or similar structured literacy approaches. These evidence-based methods teach phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension systematically and explicitly.

The CRANDALL ISD dyslexia program emphasizes multisensory learning, direct instruction, and regular progress monitoring. Intervention occurs in addition to—or as part of—the student's regular educational program.

Transition Services for CRANDALL ISD Students

Beginning at age 14, CRANDALL ISD special education must include transition planning to prepare students for post-secondary life. This includes employment, post-secondary education, independent living, and community involvement.

What Transition Services Include

CRANDALL ISD coordinates transition services including:

  • Career exploration and vocational training
  • Work experience and internship opportunities
  • Community-based instruction and job coaching
  • Independent living skills training
  • Post-secondary education pathways and accommodations planning
  • Connections with adult service agencies (vocational rehabilitation, disability services)

Student Involvement and Goal Setting

Beginning at age 16, your child must be invited to and meaningfully participate in all transition planning meetings. This ensures your student's voice shapes decisions about their future and develops self-advocacy skills.

Strong transition planning in CRANDALL ISD helps students move successfully to employment, college, or adult living after high school.

Getting Started: How CRANDALL ISD Parents Request an Evaluation

If you believe your child may have a disability affecting educational performance, you can request a special education evaluation at any time during the school year.

Steps to request an evaluation from CRANDALL ISD:

  1. Submit a written request to the campus principal or special education coordinator (email or hand-delivered letter works)
  2. Include specific concerns about academic performance, behavior, communication, or other areas
  3. Keep a copy for your records with date and signature
  4. CRANDALL ISD has 30 calendar days to respond with a proposal for evaluation or a refusal notice
  5. If the district refuses, you have the right to request mediation or pursue due process
  6. If approved, the evaluation typically occurs within 60 calendar days

After evaluation, CRANDALL ISD holds an ARD meeting to discuss results and determine eligibility for special education services.
