Understanding the CARTHAGE ISD IEP Process and Document

The IEP (Individualized Education Program) is the legal document that guides your child's special education. Every student in CARTHAGE special education must have a current, written IEP before receiving special education services.

Key Components of the CARTHAGE ISD IEP

Present Levels of Academic and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): This section describes your child's current abilities, disabilities, and how their disability affects learning. For CARTHAGE ISD students, this should reference recent assessment results, teacher observations, and parent input.

CARTHAGE ISD IEP Goals: These are specific, measurable, achievable objectives your child will work toward during the school year. Effective CARTHAGE ISD IEP goals follow the SMART framework: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Each goal should directly address areas where your child needs support.

Goals might include "By June 1, 2024, [Student] will read grade-level text with 90% accuracy and 80% comprehension" or "By May 15, 2024, [Student] will independently complete multi-step directions in 4 out of 5 opportunities as measured by teacher observation."

Special Education Services: This section specifies what services your child receives and how frequently. In CARTHAGE ISD, services might include resource room instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or behavior support.

Accommodations and Modifications: Accommodations allow your child to access the general curriculum (extended time, large print, readers). Modifications change the curriculum content itself. CARTHAGE ISD must specify which accommodations and modifications apply in different settings.

Least Restrictive Environment (LRE): CARTHAGE ISD must explain why your child is placed in their current setting and justify any removal from general education classes.

What to Expect During Your CARTHAGE ISD ARD Meeting

Knowing what happens during the actual meeting reduces anxiety and helps you participate more effectively. A typical CARTHAGE ISD ARD meeting follows a structured format.

Meeting Setup and Participants

You'll sit at a table with 5-8 people, typically including a special education coordinator, your child's general education teacher, special education teacher, school psychologist or diagnostician, and sometimes speech or occupational therapists. You may bring an advocate or family member for support.

CARTHAGE ISD begins by reviewing why the meeting is being held—whether it's an initial evaluation, annual review, or meeting to address new concerns. Everyone receives a copy of the current IEP (if updating) and assessment results.

Discussion and Decision-Making

The team discusses your child's performance, progress toward current goals, and any new evaluation results. This is when you share your observations from home and your priorities. For CARTHAGE ISD families, this collaborative discussion is the heart of the process.

Take notes on important points. Ask clarifying questions when you don't understand something. You're not expected to be an expert—educators should explain their recommendations in understandable language.

The team then develops new goals, reviews service levels, and discusses placement. This can take 1-2 hours. Disagreements sometimes arise; CARTHAGE ISD is required to listen to your perspective and explain their reasoning if they differ from your position.

Signatures and Next Steps

At the end, you'll see the IEP document. You have the right to sign, sign with reservations, or refuse to sign. Your signature doesn't mean you agree with everything—it means you attended the meeting. CARTHAGE ISD will explain your rights regarding dispute resolution.

Common Challenges and Solutions in CARTHAGE ISD ARD Meetings

Many parents in CARTHAGE ISD encounter similar challenges during the ARD process. Knowing how to address these situations strengthens your advocacy.

If you disagree with CARTHAGE ISD's recommendation, state your concern clearly and ask for explanation.
