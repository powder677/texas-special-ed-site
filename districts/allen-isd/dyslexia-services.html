Dyslexia Services in Allen ISD

Dyslexia Services in Allen ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available within Allen Independent School District (AISD). It is intended to help parents, educators, and students understand the identification, evaluation, and support processes for students with dyslexia in accordance with Texas law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Allen ISD adheres to the Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024 Update) published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This handbook is the primary resource for understanding dyslexia and related disorders in Texas public schools. It outlines the legal requirements, best practices, and procedures for identification, evaluation, and intervention for students with dyslexia.

Key elements of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: Dyslexia is a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Screening Requirements: Specifies mandatory screening for reading difficulties for all students in kindergarten and first grade, and as needed in subsequent grades.
  • Evaluation Procedures: Details the comprehensive evaluation process used to determine if a student has dyslexia.
  • Instructional Programs: Emphasizes the use of structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia.
  • Parent Involvement: Highlights the importance of collaboration between schools and parents in the identification and support of students with dyslexia.
  • Legal Framework: Provides information on the legal mandates and rights of students with dyslexia under federal and state laws, including Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

It is crucial for parents and educators to familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook to understand the rights and services available to students with dyslexia in Allen ISD. The handbook is available on the TEA website.

Mandatory Screening

In compliance with Texas law, Allen ISD conducts universal screening for dyslexia in kindergarten and first grade. The purpose of this screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia as early as possible. This early identification allows for timely intervention and support.

Screening Procedures:

  • Kindergarten: Students in kindergarten are screened using measures designed to assess pre-reading skills, including phonological awareness (identifying and manipulating sounds in words), letter knowledge, and rapid automatized naming (RAN).
  • First Grade: Students in first grade are screened using measures that assess phonological awareness, decoding skills (sounding out words), and reading fluency.
  • Subsequent Grades: Students in grades 2 and above may be screened if teachers or parents have concerns about their reading progress. These screenings may be triggered by classroom performance, standardized test scores, or parent/teacher observations.

Screening Results:

Parents will be notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia based on the screening results. This notification will include information about the next steps, which may involve further evaluation or targeted intervention.

Structured Literacy Programs

Allen ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and sequential approach to teaching reading that focuses on foundational skills. These programs are based on the science of reading and are designed to address the underlying language difficulties associated with dyslexia.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Instruction in the sound structure of language, including phonemic awareness (the ability to hear and manipulate individual sounds in words) and phonics (the relationship between letters and sounds).
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Explicit and systematic teaching of the relationship between letters and sounds, and how to blend sounds to read words and segment words into sounds for spelling.
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching syllable types and syllable division rules to improve decoding and spelling skills.
  • Morphology: Instruction in morphemes (meaningful parts of words, such as prefixes, suffixes, and roots) to enhance vocabulary and comprehension.
  • Syntax: Teaching sentence structure and grammar to improve reading comprehension and writing skills.
  • Semantics: Developing vocabulary and comprehension strategies to understand the meaning of text.

Programs Used in Allen ISD:

While specific programs may vary depending on the campus and the needs of the students, Allen ISD typically employs research-based structured literacy programs. Parents can contact their child's school to inquire about the specific program being used. Example programs (this list is NOT exhaustive and can change):

  • Take Flight: A Comprehensive Intervention for Students with Dyslexia: Developed by the Luke Waites Center for Dyslexia at Scottish Rite Hospital, this program is a highly structured, intensive intervention.
  • Reading by Design Another commonly used structured literacy program.

These programs are typically delivered in small group settings by trained and certified dyslexia interventionists.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support services through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the severity of their reading difficulties.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning or reading.
  • Focus: 504 plans provide accommodations to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modifications to assignments.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation for a 504 plan typically involves a review of existing data, such as classroom performance, standardized test scores, and teacher observations.

IEP (Individualized Education Program):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. Dyslexia can be the basis for an IEP under the specific learning disability category.
  • Focus: IEPs provide specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: Services may include specialized reading instruction using structured literacy programs, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation for an IEP involves a comprehensive assessment of the student's cognitive, academic, and social-emotional functioning. This often includes standardized testing and classroom observations.

Key Differences:

  • Severity of Disability: IEPs are typically for students with more significant learning disabilities who require specialized instruction. 504 plans are for students who need accommodations to access the general education curriculum.
  • Specialized Instruction: IEPs provide specialized instruction, while 504 plans primarily offer accommodations.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by IDEA, which provides more extensive procedural safeguards for parents. 504 plans are governed by Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.

The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, in the case of an IEP, or the 504 committee will determine which plan is most appropriate for the student based on their individual needs and assessment results.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are modifications to the learning environment or instructional practices that help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. The specific accommodations needed will vary depending on the individual student's needs. Here is a list of common accommodations used in Allen ISD:

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location that minimizes distractions and maximizes their ability to focus.
  • Reduced Workload: Modifying assignments to reduce the amount of reading or writing required.
  • Alternative Assessments: Providing alternative ways for students to demonstrate their learning, such as oral presentations or projects.
  • Assistive Technology: Using technology to support reading and writing, such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, or graphic organizers.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to textbooks and other reading materials in audio format.
  • Highlighted Text: Providing pre-highlighted text or allowing students to highlight important information.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids, such as graphic organizers, diagrams, and charts, to support comprehension.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing students to take frequent breaks to help them maintain focus.
  • Modified Testing Format: Providing tests in a format that is easier for students with dyslexia to access, such as large print or a simplified layout.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing students to use a word processor with spell check and grammar check.
  • Copy of Notes: Providing a copy of teacher's notes or allowing students to record lectures.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free testing environment.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the use of a calculator for math assignments and tests.

This list is not exhaustive. The ARD or 504 committee will determine the appropriate accommodations based on the student's individual needs.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you should contact their school to request an evaluation. The following steps outline the process for requesting an evaluation in Allen ISD:

  1. Contact the School: Begin by contacting your child's teacher, counselor, or principal to express your concerns. Document your concerns in writing (email is acceptable) and keep a copy for your records.
  2. Written Request: Submit a formal written request for a dyslexia evaluation to the school principal or special education director. Clearly state your concerns about your child's reading difficulties and your request for a comprehensive evaluation.
  3. Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, such as classroom performance, standardized test scores, and teacher observations, to determine if there is a need for further evaluation.
  4. Evaluation Process: If the school determines that an evaluation is warranted, they will conduct a comprehensive assessment of your child's reading skills, including phonological awareness, decoding, reading fluency, and reading comprehension. The evaluation will be conducted by qualified professionals, such as a certified dyslexia specialist or educational diagnostician.
  5. ARD or 504 Committee Meeting: Once the evaluation is complete, the ARD committee (for IEP eligibility) or the 504 committee will meet to review the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services or 504 accommodations.
  6. Parent Involvement: Parents are an integral part of the evaluation and decision-making process. You have the right to participate in all meetings and to provide input into your child's educational plan.

Important Considerations:

  • The school district is required to complete the evaluation process within a reasonable timeframe, as defined by Texas law and TEA guidelines.
  • Parents have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) if they disagree with the school's evaluation. The school district may be required to pay for the IEE under certain circumstances.
  • Parents have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency if they believe that the school district is not complying with state or federal laws regarding special education.

This guide is intended to provide general information about dyslexia services in Allen ISD. For specific information about your child's individual needs, please contact their school directly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
