Requesting an Evaluation in Allen ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Allen ISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Allen Independent School District (Allen ISD), following Texas state law and regulations. It outlines the process from submitting a written request to understanding your rights after the evaluation is complete. This information is for guidance only and should not be considered legal advice. Consult with a special education advocate or attorney for specific legal counsel.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in obtaining a special education evaluation is submitting a written request to Allen ISD. This letter initiates the legal timeline for the evaluation process. It's crucial that the request is clear, concise, and includes specific details about your concerns.

To whom should the letter be addressed? Generally, the letter should be addressed to the campus principal and the Special Education Director for Allen ISD. You can find the contact information for the Special Education Director on the Allen ISD website. Addressing the letter to both ensures it reaches the appropriate individuals promptly.

What information should the letter include?

  • Your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your child's current grade and teacher's name.
  • Your home address and contact phone number.
  • A clear statement that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation (FIE) for special education services. This should be explicitly stated.
  • A detailed explanation of your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. Be specific and provide examples. Include details about areas where your child struggles (e.g., reading comprehension, math calculations, social interaction, attention, following directions).
  • Information about any previous interventions or accommodations that have been tried, and their effectiveness. Include data if possible (e.g., benchmark scores, progress reports).
  • Any medical or psychological diagnoses or evaluations your child has received. Provide copies of relevant reports if available.
  • Your consent for the school to conduct the evaluation. This is implied by your request, but explicitly stating your consent can be helpful.
  • A request for a copy of the Procedural Safeguards. These safeguards outline your rights and the school's responsibilities under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.
  • A request to be contacted to schedule a meeting to discuss your concerns and the evaluation process.

Example Letter Excerpt:

Subject: Request for Full and Individual Initial Evaluation for [Child's Name]

Dear Principal [Principal's Name] and Special Education Director,

I am writing to request a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is currently in [Grade] at [School Name]. [Child's Name] has been struggling with [Specific Area of Concern, e.g., reading comprehension] for the past [Time Period, e.g., year]. He/She consistently [Specific Example of Struggle, e.g., scores below grade level on reading assessments, has difficulty answering comprehension questions after reading a passage]. We have tried [Interventions Tried, e.g., extra help with reading at home, participation in small group reading intervention], but these efforts have not resulted in significant improvement. I am concerned that [Child's Name]'s difficulties may indicate an underlying learning disability and request a comprehensive evaluation to determine if he/she is eligible for special education services. I also request a copy of the Procedural Safeguards. Please contact me at [Your Phone Number] or [Your Email Address] to schedule a meeting to discuss this matter further.

Submitting the letter: Keep a copy of the letter for your records. Send the letter via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery. This is important if you need to track the school's response and compliance with the timelines.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates specific timelines for the special education evaluation process. Understanding these timelines is crucial for advocating for your child's rights. The 45-school-day timeline begins when the school receives your written request for an evaluation.

What constitutes a "school day"? A school day is any day that students are scheduled to be in attendance for instructional purposes. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks (e.g., summer break, Thanksgiving break) do not count towards the 45-school-day timeline.

What happens within the 45-school-day timeline?

  • Within 15 school days of receiving your request, the school must decide whether to conduct an evaluation. They must provide you with written notice of their decision. If they agree to evaluate, they must provide you with an Assessment Plan. This plan outlines the specific assessments that will be used to evaluate your child.
  • If the school proposes to evaluate, you must provide written consent to the Assessment Plan. You have the right to review the plan and ask questions before providing consent. If you disagree with the assessments listed, you can request changes or additions.
  • Once the school receives your signed consent to the Assessment Plan, the 45-school-day clock restarts. From this point, the school has 45 school days to complete the evaluation, hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results, and determine your child's eligibility for special education services.
  • The evaluation must be comprehensive and address all areas of suspected disability. The specific assessments used will depend on your child's individual needs and the areas of concern you raised in your initial request.
  • The ARD committee meeting must include you, the parent, as an equal member. You have the right to participate fully in the discussion and decision-making process.

Exceptions to the 45-school-day timeline: There are limited exceptions to the 45-school-day timeline, such as documented and mutually agreed upon extensions. Any extension must be in writing and signed by both you and the school district.

Tracking the timeline: Keep a calendar and meticulously track the 45 school days. Note the date the school received your request, the date you received the Assessment Plan, the date you signed and returned the Assessment Plan, and the deadline for completing the evaluation and holding the ARD meeting. This will help you ensure that the school adheres to the legal timelines.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if your child has a disability and, if so, what special education and related services are needed. The FIE must cover all areas related to the suspected disability.

Key Components of the FIE:

  • Review of Existing Data: The evaluation team will review existing data, including your child's academic records, attendance records, medical records (if provided), previous evaluations, and teacher observations.
  • Individual Assessments: The evaluation team will administer a variety of assessments tailored to your child's specific needs and the areas of concern. These assessments may include:
    • Academic Assessments: To evaluate reading, writing, math, and other academic skills.
    • Cognitive Assessments: To assess intellectual ability and cognitive processing skills.
    • Language Assessments: To evaluate receptive and expressive language skills.
    • Speech-Language Assessments: To assess articulation, fluency, voice, and language skills.
    • Social-Emotional Assessments: To evaluate emotional and behavioral functioning.
    • Adaptive Behavior Assessments: To assess daily living skills, such as self-care, social skills, and communication.
    • Motor Skills Assessments: To evaluate gross and fine motor skills.
    • Sensory Assessments: To evaluate sensory processing and integration.
    • Observations: The evaluation team will observe your child in various settings, such as the classroom, cafeteria, and playground.
    • Parent and Teacher Input: The evaluation team will gather information from you and your child's teachers about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and needs.
  • Determination of Eligibility: Based on the evaluation results, the ARD committee will determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education under one of the 13 disability categories defined by IDEA and Texas law.
  • Identification of Educational Needs: If your child is found eligible for special education, the ARD committee will identify your child's specific educational needs and develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) to address those needs.

Areas of Suspected Disability: The FIE must cover all areas related to the suspected disability. For example:

  • If you suspect a learning disability, the FIE should include assessments of reading, writing, and math skills, as well as cognitive processing skills.
  • If you suspect Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), the FIE should include assessments of social interaction, communication, and repetitive behaviors.
  • If you suspect Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), the FIE should include assessments of attention, impulsivity, and hyperactivity.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school district's evaluation, you have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense under certain circumstances. The school district must either pay for the IEE or request a due process hearing to show that its evaluation was appropriate. If the school district requests a due process hearing and the hearing officer determines that the school district's evaluation was appropriate, you still have the right to an IEE, but you will have to pay for it yourself. The results of any IEE must be considered by the ARD committee.

Child Find Obligations

Allen ISD has a legal obligation under IDEA and Texas law to actively seek out and identify children with disabilities who may be in need of special education services. This is known as "Child Find."

What does Child Find mean for you?

  • Allen ISD is responsible for identifying and evaluating children with disabilities, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or are homeschooled.
  • If you suspect that your child has a disability, you have the right to request an evaluation from Allen ISD, even if your child is not currently enrolled in public school.
  • Allen ISD cannot deny your request for an evaluation simply because your child is doing well academically. A child can have a disability even if they are achieving passing grades.

How Child Find Works: Allen ISD implements various activities to fulfill its Child Find obligations, including:

  • Screening programs: Conducting screenings for developmental delays or learning difficulties.
  • Public awareness campaigns: Disseminating information about special education services and how to request an evaluation.
  • Collaboration with community agencies: Working with healthcare providers, social service agencies, and other organizations to identify children with disabilities.

Your Role in Child Find: As a parent, you play a vital role in the Child Find process. If you have concerns about your child's development or learning, it is important to contact Allen ISD and request an evaluation. Do not hesitate to voice your concerns, even if others tell you that your child is "fine."

What If They Refuse

Allen ISD cannot simply refuse to evaluate your child without providing you with written notice and a valid reason. If the school district refuses your request for an evaluation, they must provide you with Prior Written Notice (PWN) explaining the reason for the refusal and outlining your rights to appeal that decision.

Reasons for Refusal: The school district might refuse an evaluation if they believe that there is not enough evidence to suggest that your child has a disability. However, they must base this decision on data and observations, not simply on a subjective opinion.

Your Options if They Refuse:

  • Review the Prior Written Notice: Carefully review the PWN to understand the reason for the refusal.
  • Request Additional Information: Ask the school district to provide you with any data or observations that they used to make their decision.
  • Provide Additional Information: Submit any additional information that supports your request for an evaluation, such as medical records, private evaluations, or examples of your child's work.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe that Allen ISD has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Texas law.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence to a hearing officer, who will then make a decision about whether your child is entitled to an evaluation. This is a more formal and adversarial process than mediation.

Remember to Document Everything: Keep detailed records of all communication with the school district, including dates, times, and the names of individuals you spoke with. This documentation will be essential if you need to file a complaint or request a due process hearing.

After the Evaluation

After the evaluation is complete, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to discuss the results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. You are a crucial member of the ARD committee.

The ARD Committee Meeting:

  • Who Attends: The ARD committee includes you (the parent), a representative of the school district (usually an administrator or special education staff member), your child's teacher(s), and other professionals who participated in the evaluation (e.g., diagnostician, speech-language pathologist, psychologist). Your child may also attend the meeting, depending on their age and maturity. You can also invite other individuals to attend, such as a special education advocate or attorney.
  • What Happens at the Meeting: The evaluation team will present the results of the FIE and explain their findings. The committee will discuss whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education under one of the 13 disability categories. If your child is found eligible, the committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

The Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • What is it? The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs and the special education and related services that will be provided to meet those needs.
  • What Does it Include? The IEP must include:
    • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance: A description of your child's current academic and functional skills.
    • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child's academic and functional progress.
    • Special Education and Related Services: A list of the special education and related services that will be provided to your child, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and assistive technology.
    • Accommodations and Modifications: A description of the accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom, such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, and modified assignments.
    • Placement: A description of where your child will receive special education services (e.g., general education classroom, resource room, self-contained classroom).
    • Dates and Duration of Services: The dates when services will begin and end, and the frequency and duration of services.
    • Progress Monitoring: A description of how your child's progress towards the annual goals will be measured and reported to you.
  • Your Role in the IEP: You are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in the development of the IEP. Do not hesitate to ask questions, voice your concerns, and advocate for your child's needs.

If You Disagree with the IEP: If you disagree with the IEP, you have several options:

  • Request Another ARD Meeting: You can request another ARD meeting to discuss your concerns and try to reach an agreement.
  • Mediation: As mentioned earlier, mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe that Allen ISD has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Texas law.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence to a hearing officer, who will then make a decision about the IEP.

Remember, you are your child's best advocate. Understanding your rights and responsibilities under IDEA and Texas law is crucial for ensuring that your child receives the appropriate special education services to meet their individual needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
