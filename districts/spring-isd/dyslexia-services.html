Dyslexia Services in Spring ISD

Dyslexia Services in Spring ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Spring Independent School District (ISD) in Texas. It outlines key components of the district's approach to identifying and supporting students with dyslexia, based on the Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024) and relevant state laws.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Spring ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This handbook serves as the primary resource for educators, parents, and administrators regarding dyslexia identification, instruction, and support. Key updates and features of the 2024 Handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a clear and comprehensive definition of dyslexia, emphasizing its neurological origin and impact on reading and spelling skills. Dyslexia is defined as "a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede the growth of vocabulary and background knowledge."
  • Multi-Tiered Systems of Support (MTSS): The handbook promotes the use of MTSS to provide increasingly intensive support to struggling readers. This framework ensures that all students receive high-quality instruction and that those with dyslexia receive targeted interventions. Spring ISD utilizes a robust MTSS process to identify and support struggling learners.
  • Universal Screening: The handbook emphasizes the importance of universal screening for reading difficulties in the early grades. This allows for early identification of students at risk for dyslexia and timely intervention.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook advocates for the use of structured literacy approaches in dyslexia instruction. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to teaching reading and spelling.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook stresses the importance of parent involvement in the identification and support of students with dyslexia. Open communication and collaboration between school staff and parents are crucial for student success.
  • Assistive Technology: The handbook highlights the potential benefits of assistive technology for students with dyslexia. Assistive technology can help students access and engage with the curriculum.
  • Legal Requirements: The handbook outlines the legal requirements related to dyslexia services in Texas, including the requirements for screening, evaluation, and instruction.

You can access the full Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 on the Texas Education Agency website.

Mandatory Screening

In accordance with Texas law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, Spring ISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten through first grade. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. The screening typically involves assessments of:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to hear and manipulate the sounds of language (e.g., rhyming, blending, segmenting).
  • Letter Knowledge: The ability to recognize and name letters.
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly and accurately name a series of familiar items (e.g., letters, numbers, colors).
  • Phonological Memory: The ability to hold and manipulate sounds in short-term memory.

Students who demonstrate characteristics of dyslexia during the screening process will be further evaluated to determine if they meet the criteria for dyslexia services. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as being at risk and informed about the next steps in the evaluation process. Screenings are generally conducted in the fall and winter of each school year. Please contact your child's school for specific dates.

Structured Literacy Programs

Spring ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an evidence-based approach to reading instruction that is explicit, systematic, cumulative, and diagnostic. These programs are designed to address the underlying phonological deficits that are characteristic of dyslexia. Key features of structured literacy programs include:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions made about prior knowledge.
  • Systematic Instruction: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Cumulative Instruction: New concepts are integrated with previously learned concepts, ensuring retention and generalization.
  • Diagnostic Instruction: Instruction is tailored to meet the individual needs of the student, based on ongoing assessment.
  • Phonological Awareness Training: Activities to improve the student's ability to hear and manipulate the sounds of language.
  • Phonics Instruction: Explicit teaching of the relationships between letters and sounds.
  • Spelling Instruction: Explicit teaching of spelling rules and patterns.
  • Morphology Instruction: Teaching the meaning of prefixes, suffixes, and root words.
  • Syntax Instruction: Teaching the rules of sentence structure.
  • Semantics Instruction: Teaching the meaning of words and phrases.

Specific structured literacy programs used in Spring ISD may vary depending on the campus and the needs of the student. Common programs include (but are not limited to) research-based programs aligned with the Science of Reading. Contact your child's school or the district's Special Education Department for more information about the specific program used at your child's campus.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs.

  • 504 Plan: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to ensure that a student with a disability has equal access to the general education curriculum. To qualify for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. A 504 plan focuses on providing accommodations to address the impact of the disability on the student's learning. Examples of accommodations are listed below.
  • IEP: An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specialized instruction and related services that a student with a disability needs to make progress in school. To qualify for an IEP, a student must have one of the 13 disabilities listed in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), including a Specific Learning Disability (SLD) like dyslexia, and must require specialized instruction to access the general education curriculum. An IEP includes measurable goals, specific interventions, and progress monitoring procedures.

The key difference between a 504 plan and an IEP is that an IEP provides specialized instruction tailored to the student's individual needs, while a 504 plan provides accommodations to level the playing field. Students with dyslexia who require significant individualized instruction to make progress may be eligible for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability (SLD). The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee determines eligibility for special education services and develops the IEP.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way a student learns that do not change the content being taught. Accommodations are designed to help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Some common accommodations for students with dyslexia include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Providing a seat in the classroom that minimizes distractions and maximizes access to instruction.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Providing visual aids to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Use of Multi-Sensory Strategies: Incorporating visual, auditory, and kinesthetic learning activities.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Access to Notes: Providing copies of notes or allowing the student to record lectures.
  • Reduced Visual Clutter: Presenting information in a clear and uncluttered format.
  • Highlighters and Color-Coding: Using highlighters or color-coding to emphasize important information.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Dictation Software: Allowing the student to dictate responses to assignments and tests.
  • Word Prediction Software: Using software that predicts words as the student types.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the use of a calculator for math assignments and tests.

The specific accommodations provided to a student will be determined based on their individual needs and documented in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request an Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from Spring ISD. The process for requesting an evaluation is as follows:

  1. Contact Your Child's Teacher: Begin by discussing your concerns with your child's teacher. The teacher can provide valuable insights into your child's academic performance and may be able to suggest strategies to support your child's learning.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or Principal: If you continue to have concerns after speaking with the teacher, contact the school counselor or principal. They can provide information about the district's dyslexia services and the evaluation process.
  3. Submit a Written Request: To formally request an evaluation, submit a written request to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a detailed explanation of your concerns. Be sure to include specific examples of your child's difficulties with reading and spelling.
  4. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, the evaluation process will begin. This process may involve a review of your child's academic records, observations of your child in the classroom, and standardized testing. The evaluation team will include qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians and speech-language pathologists.
  5. Evaluation Report: After the evaluation is complete, you will receive a written report outlining the findings. The report will indicate whether or not your child meets the criteria for dyslexia services.
  6. ARD Meeting (If Eligible for Special Education): If your child is found eligible for special education services, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting will be scheduled to develop an IEP. The IEP will outline the specific goals, interventions, and accommodations that will be provided to your child.
  7. 504 Meeting (If Eligible for 504 Services): If your child is found eligible for 504 services, a 504 meeting will be scheduled to develop a 504 plan. The 504 plan will outline the specific accommodations that will be provided to your child.

It is important to remember that parents have the right to request an evaluation for their child at any time. Spring ISD is committed to providing timely and appropriate support to students with dyslexia.

Contact Information:

For more information about dyslexia services in Spring ISD, please contact the Special Education Department at your child's school or the district's central administration office.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
