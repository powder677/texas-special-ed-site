Dispute & Discipline Rights in Spring ISD

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Discipline Rights & Grievances in Spring ISD: An Urgent Guide for Parents

Discipline Rights & Grievances in Spring ISD: An Urgent Guide for Parents

This guide is for parents facing school discipline issues in Spring ISD. Time is often of the essence. This information is NOT legal advice, but aims to equip you with knowledge to understand your rights and act quickly. If your child faces suspension, MDR, or a Change of Placement, seek legal counsel immediately.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Act Fast!

Texas law allows schools to suspend students. But there's a critical threshold: 10 cumulative school days. Once a student with disabilities (IEP or 504 plan) is removed for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single school year for violating the student code of conduct, it triggers significant procedural protections under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). This is a HUGE deal, impacting your child's right to education and potentially leading to a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).

What to do IMMEDIATELY if your child is suspended:

  • Document Everything: Dates, times, reasons for suspension, who informed you, and any communication with school officials. Keep a log. Digital copies are essential.
  • Calculate Cumulative Days: Keep a running tally of all suspension days this school year. Don't rely on the school's count; verify it yourself.
  • Review the Student Code of Conduct: Understand the specific rule your child allegedly violated. Request a copy if you don't have one. Examine the evidence.
  • Contact the School Immediately: Demand to know the total number of suspension days this year. Request all documentation related to the suspensions.
  • If your child has an IEP or 504 Plan: Explicitly remind the school that your child has a disability and is covered under IDEA or Section 504. Demand to know the process for an MDR if the 10-day threshold is approaching or has been crossed.
  • Monitor for Escalation: Even if the suspension is for less than 10 days, be alert for escalating disciplinary actions. Frequent short suspensions can be a sign of a bigger problem and can still negatively impact your child's education.

Warning Signs: Be particularly concerned if your child's behaviors leading to suspension are related to their disability. Schools cannot punish students for behaviors that are a manifestation of their disability.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a meeting required under IDEA when a student with an IEP or 504 plan faces disciplinary action that could result in a Change of Placement (see below). The purpose is to determine if the student's misconduct was:

  • Caused by the student's disability; OR
  • A direct result of the school's failure to implement the student's IEP/504 plan.

If the answer to either of these questions is YES, the conduct is considered a "manifestation" of the disability. The school cannot discipline the student in the same way they would a student without a disability. The school must return the student to their previous placement (unless you agree otherwise) and take steps to address the underlying cause of the behavior. This could involve revising the IEP/504 plan, providing additional supports, or implementing a behavior intervention plan.

Preparing for the MDR:

  • Review the IEP/504 Plan: Is it being followed? Are the goals and accommodations appropriate? Document any instances where the plan was not implemented.
  • Gather Evidence: Collect any documentation that supports your claim that the behavior is related to the disability or that the IEP/504 plan was not followed. This includes emails, teacher observations, medical records, and independent evaluations.
  • Bring an Advocate or Attorney: You have the right to bring someone to the MDR to support you and advocate for your child. An experienced advocate or attorney can be invaluable in navigating the complex legal issues involved.
  • Be Prepared to Explain: Clearly and concisely explain how the disability affects the student's behavior and why the IEP/504 plan was not effective (if applicable).
  • Demand Documentation: Get a written copy of the MDR decision and the reasons for the decision.

What if the MDR finds that the conduct WAS NOT a manifestation of the disability? You have the right to challenge this decision through due process (see below).

Change of Placement: A Critical Threshold

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student with an IEP or 504 plan is:

  • Removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days; OR
  • Subjected to a series of removals that constitute a pattern, considering factors such as the length of each removal, the total time the student is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another. Even short suspensions can add up to a Change of Placement if they are frequent and disruptive.

A Change of Placement triggers the MDR process. Spring ISD *cannot* unilaterally change your child's placement to a more restrictive setting (like a disciplinary alternative education program - DAEP) without following proper procedures, including the MDR and due process rights.

What to Do if Facing a Change of Placement:

  • Immediately demand an MDR meeting. Do this in writing and keep a copy.
  • Review your child's IEP/504 and the school's documentation leading to the proposed change.
  • Consider an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE). This is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. You may be able to get the district to pay for this if you disagree with their evaluation.
  • Get legal advice. A lawyer specializing in special education law can help you understand your rights and advocate for your child.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in Spring ISD

If you believe Spring ISD violated your child's rights or the IEP/504 plan, you have the right to file a grievance. Spring ISD likely has a specific grievance policy outlined on their website or in the student handbook. Level 1 is typically the initial step.

Steps for Filing a Level 1 Grievance:

  • Obtain the Grievance Policy: Locate the official Spring ISD grievance policy. It usually outlines the specific procedures, timelines, and required forms. Find it on the Spring ISD website or request a copy from the school.
  • Identify the Grievance: Clearly and specifically state the issue you are grieving. Include dates, times, names of individuals involved, and the specific actions or omissions that violate your child's rights. Be as detailed as possible.
  • Gather Evidence: Collect all relevant documentation to support your grievance. This includes emails, letters, reports, IEP/504 plans, disciplinary records, and witness statements.
  • Complete the Grievance Form (if required): Some districts provide a specific form for filing grievances. If so, complete the form accurately and thoroughly. If there is no specific form, prepare a written statement that includes all the necessary information.
  • Submit the Grievance: Submit the grievance to the designated individual or office specified in the grievance policy. Typically, this is the principal or a designated administrator. Keep a copy of the submitted grievance for your records. Submit it with proof of delivery (e.g., certified mail with return receipt).
  • Attend the Grievance Meeting: The school district will likely schedule a meeting to discuss the grievance. Be prepared to present your case and provide supporting documentation. Bring an advocate or attorney if you feel it is necessary.
  • Follow Up: After the meeting, follow up with the school district to ensure that your grievance is being addressed. Document all communication with the school district.
  • Deadlines are CRITICAL: The Spring ISD grievance policy will have strict deadlines for filing. Missed deadlines can invalidate your grievance. Know the deadlines and meet them.

What to Include in Your Grievance:

  • Your child's name and student ID number
  • The date of the incident(s) giving rise to the grievance
  • A clear and concise description of the problem
  • The specific policy or law that was violated (e.g., IDEA, Section 504, Spring ISD policy)
  • The relief you are seeking (e.g., reinstatement, revision of the IEP, apology, training for staff)

If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the Level 1 grievance, you can usually appeal to the next level in the grievance process (Level 2, Level 3, etc.). Each level has its own procedures and deadlines, so carefully review the grievance policy.

TEA (Texas Education Agency) State Complaint

A TEA State Complaint is a formal complaint filed with the Texas Education Agency alleging that a school district has violated the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or other special education laws. This is a separate process from a grievance and can be used to address systemic issues or violations that affect multiple students.

When to File a TEA State Complaint:

  • You believe Spring ISD is violating IDEA or other special education laws.
  • You have tried to resolve the issue with the school district through other means (e.g., IEP meetings, grievances) and have been unsuccessful.
  • The violation affects your child's right to a free appropriate public education (FAPE).

How to File a TEA State Complaint:

  • Obtain the TEA Complaint Form: Download the official TEA Complaint Form from the TEA website (tea.texas.gov) or request a copy from TEA.
  • Complete the Complaint Form: Fill out the form accurately and thoroughly. Include all required information, such as your child's name, the school district's name, the specific violations of IDEA, and the dates of the alleged violations.
  • Provide Supporting Documentation: Attach any relevant documentation to the complaint form, such as IEPs, evaluations, reports, emails, and letters.
  • Submit the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA by mail or email, as specified on the TEA website.

TEA Investigation: Once TEA receives the complaint, they will investigate the allegations. TEA may request additional information from you and the school district. TEA will issue a written decision within 60 days of receiving the complaint.

Important Considerations:

  • Timeline: You must file the complaint within one year of the date of the alleged violation.
  • Specificity: Be as specific as possible in your complaint. Include dates, times, names of individuals involved, and the specific actions or omissions that violate IDEA.
  • Focus: Focus on violations of IDEA or other special education laws. Do not include personal grievances or complaints that are not related to special education.

Due Process: The Ultimate Protection

Due process is a formal legal process that allows parents to challenge decisions made by the school district regarding their child's special education. It is a more formal and adversarial process than a grievance or a TEA State Complaint. This is the most powerful tool you have and almost certainly requires legal representation.

When to Request Due Process:

  • You disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child.
  • You disagree with the IEP developed for your child.
  • You believe the school district is not providing your child with a FAPE.
  • You disagree with the MDR decision.
  • You disagree with a proposed Change of Placement.

How to Request Due Process:

  • File a Due Process Complaint: File a written due process complaint with the Texas Education Agency. The complaint must include specific information, such as your child's name, the school district's name, the nature of the problem, and the proposed resolution.
  • Mediation: TEA will offer mediation to attempt to resolve the dispute. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps the parties reach an agreement.
  • Due Process Hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful, a due process hearing will be held before an impartial hearing officer. Both you and the school district will have the opportunity to present evidence and arguments.
  • Hearing Officer Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision. The decision is binding on both you and the school district, unless it is appealed.

Key Considerations:

  • Legal Representation: Due process is a complex legal process. It is highly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law.
  • Timeline: There are strict timelines for filing a due process complaint and for each stage of the due process process. Missed deadlines can invalidate your claim.
  • Evidence: You must present evidence to support your claims. Gather all relevant documentation, such as IEPs, evaluations, reports, emails, and letters.
  • Child's Right to Stay Put: During the due process proceedings, your child generally has the right to remain in their current educational placement (this is known as the "stay put" provision). There are exceptions to this rule, so consult with an attorney.

Final Thoughts:

Navigating the special education system and disciplinary actions can be overwhelming. Remember that you are your child's best advocate. Document everything, know your rights, and don't hesitate to seek help from advocates, attorneys, and other parents. Act quickly, stay informed, and fight for your child's right to a free and appropriate public education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
