Temple ISD Special Education Resources

⚡ Quick Answer

What can I do right now if Temple ISD is ignoring my child's needs?

Submit a written evaluation request today — verbal requests do not start the legal clock. Temple ISD has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the evaluation. If your child is being disciplined or suspended, that timeline is even more urgent. Use the guides below to take action now.

Special Education in Temple ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Navigating the special education system can feel overwhelming, but you are not alone. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is a federal law that ensures children with disabilities receive a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs. Texas law mirrors and reinforces IDEA, establishing procedures and protections for students with disabilities in our state. This means that your child is entitled to receive specialized instruction and related services if they meet eligibility criteria under one of the 13 disability categories outlined in IDEA.

Temple ISD, like all public school districts in Texas, is ultimately overseen by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). However, districts also receive direct support from Education Service Centers, and Temple ISD is fortunate to be served by TEA Region 12. Region 12 provides professional development, technical assistance, and resources to Temple ISD staff to help ensure they are equipped to meet the needs of all students, including those with disabilities. It's important to remember that while TEA and Region 12 provide oversight and support, the responsibility for providing FAPE lies directly with Temple ISD.

Throughout the special education process, from initial evaluation to the development and implementation of an Individualized Education Program (IEP), you, as a parent, have enforceable legal rights. These rights are designed to ensure your active participation in all decisions affecting your child's education. Understanding and exercising these rights is crucial to advocating effectively for your child within Temple ISD. Remember, the school district must follow specific procedures, and you have recourse if they fail to do so. Your voice matters, and your involvement is essential. Take the first step today by learning more about your rights and resources available to you.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Temple ISD

The first step in accessing special education services for your child is to request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). This evaluation is designed to determine if your child has a disability and, if so, what their educational needs are. It is absolutely critical that your request for an evaluation is submitted in writing. Verbal requests, while perhaps well-intentioned, do not trigger the legal timelines that protect your child's rights. Only a written request starts the clock.

Once Temple ISD receives your written consent to evaluate your child, they have 45 school days to complete the FIE. This timeline is crucial, so it's important to document the date you provided consent. Remember also that under Child Find laws, Temple ISD has a responsibility to proactively identify students who may need special education services, even if you haven't specifically requested an evaluation. If you suspect your child has a disability that is impacting their learning, do not wait. While the school may initiate the process, you should not rely on them to do so. The onus is on you as a parent to be proactive. Take action now to protect your child's rights and ensure they receive the support they need.

Take action now: Draft a letter to the Special Education Director at Temple ISD requesting a Full Individual Evaluation for your child. Clearly state your concerns and why you believe your child may have a disability. Keep a copy of the letter for your records, and send it via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that Temple ISD received your request and starts the 45-school-day timeline.

ARD Meetings in Temple ISD: Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. The ARD committee is responsible for determining your child's eligibility for special education services, developing their Individualized Education Program (IEP), and making placement decisions. You are a critical member of this committee, and your input is invaluable. Temple ISD is required to provide you with at least five calendar days written notice before any ARD meeting. This notice should include the purpose of the meeting, the names of the attendees, and information about your rights.

During the ARD meeting, you have the right to actively participate in all discussions and decisions. Do not hesitate to ask questions, express your concerns, and advocate for your child's needs. If you feel overwhelmed or need more time to consider the proposed IEP, you have the right to request a 10-day recess. This means the meeting will be paused for 10 calendar days, giving you time to review the IEP, seek outside advice, or simply gather your thoughts. Remember, you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. Take your time to carefully review the document and ensure it adequately addresses your child's needs.

Your active participation and knowledge of your rights are crucial to ensuring your child receives the best possible education within Temple ISD. Do not be afraid to assert your rights and advocate for your child's needs. Remember, the ARD process is a collaborative one, and your voice matters. Knowledge is power; arm yourself with it to be an effective advocate for your child.

Dyslexia Services in Temple ISD

Texas law mandates that Temple ISD screen all students for dyslexia. This screening process is designed to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties. If the screening indicates your child may have dyslexia, Temple ISD is required to provide further evaluation and, if diagnosed, provide appropriate interventions. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook sets the standards for identification and intervention of students with dyslexia, and Temple ISD must adhere to these guidelines.

Students identified with dyslexia are entitled to structured literacy intervention, which is a research-based approach to reading instruction that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. Depending on the severity of your child's dyslexia and their individual needs, they may qualify for a 504 Plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) to receive additional support and accommodations within Temple ISD. The key is early identification and evidence-based intervention. Take action to protect your child's academic future.

If your child has not already been screened for dyslexia in Temple ISD, and you have concerns about their reading skills, request a dyslexia screening in writing from the school principal immediately. This simple step can be the key to unlocking your child's reading potential.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Temple ISD

Discipline can be a complex issue for students with disabilities. If your special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year in Temple ISD, this triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). The MDR is an ARD committee meeting to determine if the student's behavior was a manifestation of their disability. If the ARD committee determines that the behavior was a manifestation of the disability, the school cannot suspend the student in the same manner as a general education student.

Additionally, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative days constitutes a Change of Placement under IDEA. This requires an immediate ARD meeting to discuss the situation and determine the appropriate course of action. This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face as a parent of a special education student. You must act within days to protect your child's rights. Understand this: if a behavior is a manifestation of their disability, suspending your child as punishment is likely illegal.

If your child has been suspended from Temple ISD, download and immediately use the Discipline Defense Kit. This kit provides critical information and resources to help you navigate the discipline process and protect your child's rights.

Filing a Complaint Against Temple ISD

If you believe that Temple ISD is violating your child's rights under IDEA or state law, you have several avenues for resolving the issue. It's important to remember that you have these rights regardless of what any Temple ISD staff member tells you. These are guaranteed by law, and you have options for recourse.

The first is filing a TEA State Complaint. This is a free process, and TEA is required to resolve the complaint within 60 days. You can also pursue Mediation through TEA, which is also free and involves a neutral third party facilitating a discussion between you and Temple ISD to reach a resolution. If mediation is unsuccessful, or you prefer a more formal process, you can request a Due Process Hearing, which is a legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer makes a binding decision. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe Temple ISD is discriminating against your child based on their disability. Choose the path that feels right for your individual situation, but realize the power you have as a parent.

Frequently Asked Questions About Temple ISD Special Education

The questions every Temple ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Temple ISD? +

Submit a written request to the Temple ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Temple ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Temple ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Temple ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Temple ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Temple ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Temple ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
