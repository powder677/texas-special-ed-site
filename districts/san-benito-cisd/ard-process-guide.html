What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting

Welcome! If you are reading this, it likely means your child has been identified as potentially needing special education services in San Benito Consolidated Independent School District (CISD). This can be a confusing time, but this guide is designed to help you understand one of the most important parts of the special education process: the ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is the cornerstone of developing your child's Individualized Education Program, often referred to as an IEP.

The purpose of an ARD meeting is to bring together a team of people who know your child and can contribute to planning their education. This team works together to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an IEP that meets your child's unique needs, to review your child's progress, and, if appropriate, to determine if special education services are no longer needed. The ARD committee makes decisions about all aspects of your child's special education program.

Who attends an ARD meeting? The law requires specific individuals to be part of the ARD committee. These include: you, the parent or legal guardian; a representative of the school district who is knowledgeable about the general curriculum and the resources available (this is often an administrator); at least one of your child's general education teachers (if your child is participating in general education); at least one of your child's special education teachers or providers; an educational diagnostician or other qualified professional who can interpret evaluation results; and, whenever appropriate, your child themselves. Other individuals may be invited to the meeting, such as therapists, counselors, or advocates, depending on your child's specific needs and with your agreement. You have the right to invite anyone you feel can contribute to the team and advocate for your child. It is important to remember that everyone at the ARD meeting, including you, is an equal member of the team. Decisions are made collaboratively.

The ARD process is required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law that ensures children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE). In Texas, the ARD process is further defined by the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which outlines the state's requirements for special education services. Understanding your rights and responsibilities as a parent in the ARD process is crucial to ensuring your child receives the support they need to succeed.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

The law recognizes that parents need time to prepare for ARD meetings. Therefore, San Benito CISD is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This five-day notice is designed to give you ample time to review any relevant information, gather your thoughts, and prepare any questions or concerns you may have. Weekends and holidays are included in the five-day count, so it's important to keep that in mind.

This written notice must include specific information about the meeting. It must state the purpose of the meeting, for example, is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility, an annual review to update the IEP, or a meeting to address specific concerns? The notice must also include the date, time, and location of the meeting. Critically, the notice must list the individuals who will be attending the meeting, including their roles and titles. This allows you to anticipate who will be present and whether you need to invite anyone else to support you and your child.

The notice should also inform you of your rights as a parent under IDEA, including your right to participate in the ARD process, to review your child's educational records, and to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. The notice may also include copies of relevant documents, such as evaluation reports or draft IEP goals. It is essential to carefully review all documents provided before the meeting. If you have any questions or concerns, contact the school district representative listed on the notice.

It is possible to waive your right to the five-day notice. This might be appropriate if an urgent situation arises or if you are available to meet sooner. However, you should only waive your right to the five-day notice if you are fully comfortable and prepared to proceed with the meeting. To waive your right, you must do so in writing. The school district will likely provide you with a waiver form to sign. Remember, you are not obligated to waive your right, and you should never feel pressured to do so.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is essential to ensure that the team has a complete picture of your child's strengths, needs, and overall educational journey. Gathering and organizing relevant documentation beforehand will help you actively participate in the discussion and advocate effectively for your child.

First, bring any prior evaluations, assessments, or reports related to your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. This includes evaluations conducted by the school district, as well as any independent evaluations you have obtained from outside professionals. These documents provide valuable data and insights into your child's learning style, challenges, and areas of strength. Make sure these are up-to-date and relevant to the decisions that the ARD team will be making.

Secondly, bring letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other healthcare professionals who have worked with your child. These documents can provide medical or clinical perspectives on your child's condition and its impact on their education. They can also offer recommendations for specific accommodations or interventions that may be beneficial.

Third, gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, attendance records, disciplinary reports, and samples of your child's work. These documents provide a snapshot of your child's performance in the classroom and can help identify patterns or trends that may be affecting their learning. If you have concerns about a particular subject or skill, bring examples of your child's work in that area.

Fourth, prepare a written list of your concerns and priorities for your child's education. This list will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. Be specific and provide examples to illustrate your points. Consider what you want your child to achieve academically, socially, and emotionally.

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person to the meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you take notes, and ensure that your voice is heard. Having another person present can also help you remember important details and ensure that the meeting is conducted fairly.

Finally, remember that Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. If you plan to record the ARD meeting, you must inform the other participants that you are doing so. This can be a valuable tool for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that accurate records are kept. Be respectful and transparent about your intention to record the meeting. While it is perfectly legal to record if you state it at the beginning, it might be a good idea to inform the school ahead of time, as well.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The ARD process is designed to be collaborative, but sometimes, even with the best intentions, the team may not have all the information it needs to make informed decisions. Texas law includes a provision known as the "10-day recess rule" that allows any member of the ARD committee, including you, the parent, to request a temporary pause in the meeting to gather additional information or consult with an advocate. This can be an invaluable tool if you feel overwhelmed, unsure, or simply need more time to consider the proposals being made.

The recess can be for up to 10 school days, giving you and the other team members time to obtain further evaluations, seek advice from outside experts, or simply process the information that has been presented. The purpose of the recess is to allow for more informed decision-making, ensuring that the IEP is truly tailored to your child's needs. The 10 days are *school* days, so weekends and school holidays do not count towards the recess period.

How do you invoke the 10-day recess rule? It's simple. During the ARD meeting, if you feel that you need more time or information, simply state that you would like to request a recess. There is no specific form to fill out or complex procedure to follow. Clearly express that you are invoking your right to a recess under the 10-day rule. It is helpful to be specific about why you are requesting the recess. For example, you might say, "I would like to request a recess to consult with an advocate regarding the proposed goals" or "I would like to request a recess to obtain an independent evaluation of my child's reading skills."

The other members of the ARD committee are required to honor your request for a recess. They cannot pressure you to continue the meeting or make decisions before you are ready. Once the recess period is over, the ARD meeting will reconvene, and the team will continue working collaboratively to develop your child's IEP. Remember, you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. Don't hesitate to use the 10-day recess rule if you need it.

Understanding the IEP Document

The ARD meeting culminates in the creation of an Individualized Education Program (IEP) document. This document serves as a roadmap for your child's special education services and outlines the specific supports and interventions they will receive to make progress in their education. It's crucial to understand the different sections of the IEP to ensure that it accurately reflects your child's needs and goals.

One of the first sections of the IEP is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" (often called Present Levels). This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in all relevant areas, such as reading, writing, math, behavior, and social skills. It's based on information from evaluations, assessments, classroom observations, and your input as a parent. The Present Levels section forms the foundation for the rest of the IEP, as it identifies the areas where your child needs support.

Based on the Present Levels, the ARD committee will develop "Annual Goals" for your child. These are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. Each goal should address a specific need identified in the Present Levels section. The IEP should also include short-term objectives or benchmarks that break down the annual goals into smaller, more manageable steps.

The IEP also outlines the specific "Services" that your child will receive to help them achieve their goals. This includes special education instruction, related services (such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, or transportation), and supplementary aids and services (such as assistive technology or accommodations). The IEP should specify the frequency, duration, and location of each service.

Another critical component of the IEP is "Placement." This refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. Placement options range from the general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or self-contained classroom. The ARD committee must consider the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

Finally, the IEP includes a section on "Accommodations and Modifications." Accommodations are changes to how your child learns or is assessed, while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and access to assistive technology. Examples of modifications include reduced assignments or alternative assessments. It's important to review the accommodations and modifications listed in the IEP to ensure that they are appropriate for your child's needs.

Remember, you do not have to sign the IEP on the day of the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home to review it carefully and consult with others before making a decision. If you have any questions or concerns about the IEP, don't hesitate to contact the school district representative and request a follow-up meeting.

When You Disagree With the Team

While the ARD process is designed to be collaborative, disagreements can sometimes arise between parents and the school district. It's important to know your rights and options when you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions.

One of the most fundamental rights you have is the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's decision that your child needs special education services, you can refuse to sign the IEP and your child will not be placed in special education. However, it is worth noting that if you refuse initial services, the school district can pursue Due Process to try to override your refusal. If they do win in Due Process, they can move forward with initial placement even without your consent.

Even if you initially consent to special education services, you always have the right to disagree with specific aspects of the IEP. You can refuse to sign the IEP document itself, indicating that you do not agree with the proposed goals, services, or placement. You can also write "Parent Disagrees" on the IEP document next to any specific section with which you disagree. This serves as a formal record of your disagreement and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Another important right you have is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. You are entitled to an IEE if you believe that the district's evaluation was flawed or incomplete. The district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that its evaluation was appropriate. If the district pays for the IEE, its results must be considered by the ARD committee.

Escalation Options

If you continue to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions and are unable to resolve the issue through informal discussions, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are designed to protect your child's rights and ensure that they receive a free and appropriate public education.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process that allows you to formally raise your concerns with the state education agency. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. To file a complaint, you must submit a written statement outlining your concerns and providing supporting documentation. The TEA complaint process is governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) 2004 and 19 Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89.

The second option is mediation, which is also offered free through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process that brings you and the school district together with a neutral third-party mediator to try to resolve the dispute. The mediator facilitates communication and helps the parties reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is a confidential process, and any agreement reached is legally binding. To request mediation, you must submit a written request to TEA.

The third option is a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process that involves presenting evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer, who will make a binding decision. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. Due process hearings can be time-consuming and expensive, but they are sometimes necessary to protect your child's rights. The decision of the hearing officer can be appealed to state or federal court.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action.

Remember, you are not alone in this process. There are many resources available to help you understand your rights and advocate for your child. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process, based on IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89, provides a comprehensive overview of special education laws and regulations in Texas. Don't hesitate to seek assistance from advocacy organizations, legal aid services, or other parents who have navigated the special education system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
