Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

This guide is designed to help parents navigate the often-complex discipline procedures in Pflugerville ISD. It's written with the understanding that you're likely in crisis mode, worried about your child's education and well-being. The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Pflugerville ISD, like all Texas public schools, operates under specific rules regarding suspensions. A key concept to understand is the "10-day suspension rule." This rule has significant implications, particularly for students with disabilities.

What it means: When a student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single school year, it triggers certain rights and procedural safeguards, ESPECIALLY if the student has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or a Section 504 plan.

Why it's important: Multiple short suspensions can add up quickly. Keep a meticulous record of every suspension your child receives, no matter how short. Pay close attention to the total number of suspension days accumulated throughout the school year.

Actionable steps:

  • Track Suspension Days: Maintain a detailed record of each suspension. Include the date, length of the suspension, reason for the suspension, and who issued the suspension.
  • Request Suspension Documentation: Immediately request a copy of all documentation related to each suspension from the school. This includes the referral form, incident report, and any disciplinary action taken.
  • Be Aware of Patterns: Are the suspensions for similar behaviors? Is there an underlying cause that needs to be addressed (e.g., bullying, unmet academic needs, mental health concerns)?

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a crucial process designed to protect students with disabilities from being unfairly disciplined for behaviors that are a direct result of their disability. This is ESPECIALLY important for students with IEPs under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

When is an MDR required? An MDR *must* be conducted if the school proposes a suspension of more than 10 consecutive school days OR a series of suspensions that constitute a "change of placement" (more on that below).

What happens during an MDR? The IEP team (including you, the parent) will meet to determine if the student's behavior was:

  1. Caused by or directly and substantially related to the student's disability. For example, a student with ADHD may have difficulty staying seated, leading to a disruption.
  2. The direct result of the school's failure to implement the student's IEP. For example, if the IEP specifies that a student requires a specific type of classroom support, and that support was not provided, leading to behavioral issues.

What are the outcomes of an MDR?

  • If the behavior *was* a manifestation of the disability OR resulted from a failure to implement the IEP: The student cannot be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team *must* conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA), implement or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP), and return the student to their previous placement (unless the parents and school agree otherwise).
  • If the behavior *was not* a manifestation of the disability OR did not result from a failure to implement the IEP: The student can be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. However, the school *must* still provide services to the student, even during a suspension, so that they can continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals.

Actionable steps:

  • Demand an MDR: If your child with an IEP faces a suspension of more than 10 consecutive days or a "change of placement," *immediately* request an MDR in writing. Do not delay!
  • Prepare for the MDR: Gather all relevant documentation, including the IEP, behavior data, suspension notices, and any other information that supports your position that the behavior is related to your child's disability or the school's failure to implement the IEP.
  • Advocate at the MDR Meeting: Be prepared to clearly explain how your child's disability contributes to the behavior in question. Don't be afraid to ask questions and challenge the school's assumptions. Bring an advocate if possible.
  • Document EVERYTHING: Keep detailed notes of the MDR meeting, including who attended, what was discussed, and the outcome. Request a written copy of the MDR determination.

Change of Placement

A "change of placement" is a critical concept under IDEA. It's not always as simple as just moving a student to a different classroom. It's about the *cumulative* effect of disciplinary actions and whether they effectively remove the student from their educational setting.

What constitutes a change of placement? There is no hard and fast rule, but it generally occurs when a series of suspensions or other disciplinary actions results in the student being removed from their current educational placement for more than 10 school days in a school year AND the disciplinary actions create a pattern of removal.

Why is "change of placement" important? A change of placement triggers the MDR process, ensuring that a student with a disability is not unfairly disciplined for behaviors related to their disability or the school's failure to implement the IEP.

Examples of situations that *could* constitute a change of placement:

  • Repeated short-term suspensions that, in total, exceed 10 school days in a school year AND demonstrate a pattern of removing the student from their educational environment.
  • A series of in-school suspensions (ISS) that significantly alter the student's access to the general education curriculum.
  • Transferring a student to a different school or program as a disciplinary measure.

Actionable steps:

  • Monitor Disciplinary Actions: Carefully track all disciplinary actions taken against your child, including suspensions, ISS, and other consequences.
  • Argue for a Change of Placement: If you believe your child's disciplinary actions constitute a change of placement (based on the criteria above), *demand* an MDR in writing. The school may try to avoid this, but you have the right to request it.
  • Focus on Pattern: When arguing for a change of placement, emphasize the *pattern* of removals and how they are impacting your child's education.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe Pflugerville ISD has violated your child's rights, you have the right to file a grievance. The grievance process is a formal way to raise concerns and seek resolution.

Level 1: Campus Level This is the first step in the process and involves filing a written complaint with the principal or the appropriate campus administrator. This must be done promptly; check Pflugerville ISD's policy (usually available on their website) for specific deadlines, which are often VERY short (e.g., 10 business days from when you knew about the issue).

What to include in your Level 1 grievance:

  • Your child's name and student ID number
  • The date(s) of the incident(s) in question
  • A clear and concise description of the issue or violation
  • The specific policy or law that you believe has been violated
  • The remedy or resolution you are seeking (e.g., removal of the suspension, revision of the IEP, apology, etc.)
  • Any supporting documentation (e.g., suspension notices, IEP documents, emails, witness statements)

Actionable steps:

  • Act Quickly: Do not delay in filing a grievance. Missing the deadline can prevent you from pursuing further action.
  • Be Specific: Avoid vague complaints. Provide as much detail as possible about the incident(s) and the alleged violation.
  • Keep a Copy: Make a copy of your grievance and all supporting documentation for your records.
  • Document Communication: Keep a record of all communication with the school regarding the grievance, including dates, times, and who you spoke with.
  • Follow Up: If you do not receive a response within the timeframe specified in the school's policy, follow up with the principal or administrator.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Pflugerville ISD is violating IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act), you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a local grievance.

When to file a TEA complaint: You can file a TEA complaint if you believe the district is not following federal special education law (IDEA). Examples include failure to conduct an MDR when required, failure to implement the IEP, or denying a student with a disability appropriate services.

Requirements for a TEA complaint:

  • The complaint must be in writing and signed.
  • It must allege a violation of IDEA that occurred no more than one year prior to the date the complaint is filed.
  • It must include specific facts about the alleged violation, including the date(s) of the incident(s), the name(s) of the individual(s) involved, and the specific IDEA requirement that was violated.
  • It must include a proposed resolution to the problem.

Where to find more information and the complaint form: Visit the TEA website and search for "Special Education Complaints." You'll find detailed information and the complaint form there.

Actionable steps:

  • Gather Evidence: Collect all relevant documentation to support your complaint, including IEPs, evaluation reports, communication with the school, and any other evidence of the alleged violation.
  • Draft a Clear and Concise Complaint: Clearly and concisely describe the alleged violation of IDEA, providing specific facts and supporting documentation.
  • Submit the Complaint: Submit the complaint to TEA according to the instructions on their website.
  • Cooperate with TEA's Investigation: Cooperate fully with TEA's investigation, providing any additional information or documentation they request.

Due Process

Due process is the most formal legal avenue available to parents of students with disabilities. It is a hearing conducted by an impartial hearing officer, where you can present evidence and arguments to support your position that the school district has violated IDEA.

When to request due process: You can request due process if you disagree with the school district's identification, evaluation, or educational placement of your child, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE). This is often pursued after exhausting other remedies, such as grievances and TEA complaints.

Important considerations regarding due process:

  • It's a Legal Process: Due process is a legal proceeding with formal rules of evidence and procedure. It is highly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law.
  • Strict Timelines: There are strict timelines for requesting due process and participating in the hearing. Missing these deadlines can jeopardize your case.
  • Costly: Due process can be expensive, as it involves attorney fees, expert witness fees, and other costs.

Actionable steps:

  • Consult with an Attorney: If you are considering due process, *immediately* consult with an attorney specializing in special education law. They can advise you on the merits of your case, the procedures involved, and the costs associated with due process.
  • Gather Documentation: Assemble all relevant documentation, including IEPs, evaluation reports, communication with the school, and any other evidence to support your case.
  • File the Request for Due Process: Your attorney will help you prepare and file the request for due process with TEA.
  • Prepare for the Hearing: Work closely with your attorney to prepare for the due process hearing, including gathering evidence, preparing witnesses, and developing your legal strategy.

Disclaimer: This guide provides general information and is not intended as legal advice. If you have specific legal questions or concerns, please consult with an attorney.

Remember: You are your child's best advocate. Don't be afraid to ask questions, demand answers, and fight for their rights. Time is of the essence, so act quickly and decisively.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
