What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a very important meeting for parents of children receiving special education services in Pflugerville ISD and across Texas. It's required by both the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Chapter 29 of the Texas Education Code. The main purpose of an ARD meeting is to create, review, and update your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. Think of the IEP as a roadmap designed specifically to help your child succeed in school. The IEP outlines your child's unique needs, sets measurable goals, and details the specific services and supports the school will provide to help your child reach those goals. This might include things like specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, or accommodations in the classroom.

Who attends an ARD meeting? The ARD committee, which is essentially the team making decisions about your child's education, is made up of several individuals. First and foremost, you, the parent, are a crucial member of the team. Your input and perspective are essential because you know your child best. Other members usually include a school administrator who is knowledgeable about special education services, your child's general education teacher (if appropriate), a special education teacher, an educational diagnostician who can interpret evaluation results, and other related service providers, such as a speech therapist or occupational therapist, if those services are relevant to your child's needs. Sometimes, if it's appropriate and your child is old enough, the student themselves may attend the ARD meeting. Anyone who has specific knowledge or expertise about your child can be invited to the meeting, including advocates or outside therapists you choose to bring.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Pflugerville ISD, like all school districts in Texas, is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is your right, and it's designed to give you ample time to prepare and gather your thoughts and any information you want to share with the ARD committee. The notice must include several important pieces of information. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting. For example, is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility for special education services, an annual review to update your child's IEP, or a meeting to address a specific concern or change in services? The notice must also list the date, time, and location of the meeting. Most importantly, the notice must include a list of who will be attending the meeting, including their roles. This allows you to know in advance who will be involved in the decision-making process for your child.

It's important to remember that you have the right to waive the five-day notice requirement. This means that if you and the school district agree, you can hold the ARD meeting sooner than five days from the date of the notice. This might be helpful if there's an urgent situation or if you prefer to meet sooner for any reason. However, waiving the notice is entirely your decision. You should only do so if you feel fully prepared to participate in the meeting and make informed decisions about your child's education. The waiver must be in writing, indicating that you understand your right to the five-day notice and are choosing to forgo it.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your ARD meeting is essential to ensuring your child's needs are fully addressed. There are several things you can bring to help the ARD committee understand your child's strengths, challenges, and overall progress. First, bring any prior evaluations or assessments that have been conducted, whether they were done by the school district or by private professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, or any other relevant reports. These reports provide valuable data about your child's academic and developmental levels.

Also, bring any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other professionals who work with your child outside of school. These documents can provide important insights into your child's medical history, therapeutic interventions, and any specific recommendations for their educational program. Another helpful item to bring is your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, and any samples of your child's work that you feel are representative of their abilities. Perhaps the most important thing to bring is a written list of your concerns and goals for your child. This list can serve as a guide for the ARD meeting and ensure that all of your questions and concerns are addressed. Writing everything down beforehand can help you stay focused and organized during the meeting.

Finally, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, a friend, or a professional advocate who has experience with special education. Having someone there to take notes, ask questions, and provide emotional support can be extremely helpful. In Texas, it is legal to audio record the ARD meeting as long as you provide the school district with reasonable notice that you intend to do so. Recording the meeting can be beneficial for your own records and can help you remember the discussions and decisions that were made. Remember to inform the ARD committee that you will be recording the meeting before it begins.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a valuable safeguard for parents and ARD committee members called the 10-day recess rule. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a recess of up to 10 school days before decisions are finalized in the IEP. The purpose of this recess is to provide time to gather more information, consult with outside experts or advocates, or simply to take a break and process the information that has been discussed. You might invoke the recess rule if, for example, the ARD committee is proposing a placement or service that you are unsure about, or if you feel that you need more information to make an informed decision.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, you simply need to verbally state your request during the ARD meeting. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation, although it can be helpful to briefly explain your reason for requesting the recess. For example, you might say, "I would like to request a 10-day recess to consult with an advocate about the proposed goals." Once a recess is requested, the ARD committee must grant it. The meeting will then be reconvened after the recess period has ended. It's crucial to use this time wisely to gather any additional information or support you need to feel confident in the decisions being made about your child's education. The recess rule is there to empower you and ensure that the ARD process is collaborative and well-informed.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program, or IEP, is the central document created during the ARD meeting. It's important to understand the different sections of the IEP and what they mean for your child's education. One of the first sections is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance," often shortened to "Present Levels." This section describes your child's current academic and functional abilities, including their strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they need support. It should be based on a variety of data sources, including evaluations, observations, and parent input. This section lays the foundation for the rest of the IEP.

Based on the present levels, the ARD committee will develop annual goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They should address the areas where your child needs the most support and should be designed to help them make progress in their education. The IEP will also outline the specific services and supports that the school will provide to help your child reach their goals. This might include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or other related services. The IEP will also specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

The IEP also includes information about your child's placement, which refers to the educational setting where they will receive their services. This could be a general education classroom with supports, a special education classroom for part of the day, or a more specialized setting. The IEP will also list any accommodations that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered or assessments are administered that do not change the content of what is being taught. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. It's vital to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and ask any questions before signing. Your signature indicates your agreement with the IEP, so it's important to be comfortable with all of its components.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee on certain aspects of their child's IEP. It's important to know your rights in these situations. One of the most fundamental rights is the right to refuse consent for initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's decision to place your child in special education services for the first time, you have the right to refuse consent. This will prevent the school from providing those services until your consent is obtained. Similarly, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with any of its components. Signing the IEP indicates your agreement with the plan, so if you have concerns or disagreements, you should not sign it until those issues are resolved.

Another important right is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation, or IEE, at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. You can request an IEE if you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child. The school district is required to either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate. Even if you sign the IEP, you can still write "Parent Disagrees" on the document next to your signature. This indicates that you do not fully agree with the IEP, even though you are signing it. This can be a useful way to document your concerns while still allowing the IEP to be implemented.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, there are several formal options available to you. These rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Chapter 89 of Title 19 of the Texas Administrative Code (19 TAC Chapter 89), which you can access through the Legal Framework website. The first option is to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. This is generally best for violations of process, like failing to provide notice.

The next option is mediation, which is also a free service offered through TEA. Mediation involves a neutral third party who helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is a voluntary process, and both parties must agree to participate. If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer to skip mediation, you can request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a decision. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigations focus on whether the school district has violated federal civil rights laws.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
