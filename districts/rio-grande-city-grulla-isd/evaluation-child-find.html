How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Rio Grande City Grulla ISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child within the Rio Grande City Grulla Independent School District (ISD) in Texas. Understanding your rights and the processes involved is crucial for advocating for your child's educational needs. Texas law requires that school districts identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside in the district, regardless of whether they attend public or private school. This is known as Child Find.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is submitting a written request to the school district. This request is a crucial legal document that triggers specific timelines and obligations for the district. The request should be addressed to the Special Education Director, Principal, or Section 504 Coordinator at your child's school. Clearly state your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. Be specific and provide concrete examples. Include information about any interventions or supports your child has already received, and the results of those interventions. For example, if your child struggles with reading, describe specific difficulties like sounding out words or comprehending text. Date the letter and keep a copy for your records. It is recommended to send the letter via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Upon receiving your written request, Rio Grande City Grulla ISD is required by Texas law to respond within a specific timeframe. The district has 15 school days to provide you with a written notice of their decision regarding the evaluation. If the district chooses to evaluate, they must also obtain your written consent for the evaluation. Once you provide consent, the district then has 45 school days to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). This timeline is strict and important. The 45 school days begin when the district receives your signed consent for the evaluation. School holidays and summer breaks are not counted as school days. Document everything, including the date you submitted the request, the date you provided consent, and any communications with the school district regarding the evaluation.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine whether your child has a disability and, if so, the nature and extent of their special education and related services needs. The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals. It should include a variety of assessments tailored to your child's individual needs and areas of concern. At a minimum, the FIE should address all areas related to the suspected disability. This may include academic achievement, cognitive abilities, communication skills, adaptive behavior, social-emotional functioning, and motor skills. The FIE must be nondiscriminatory and culturally responsive, taking into account your child's background and language proficiency. It is important to actively participate in the evaluation process by providing information about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and learning style. Remember that you have the right to an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation. However, the district can choose to defend their evaluation in a due process hearing.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law mandates that all school districts, including Rio Grande City Grulla ISD, have a "Child Find" obligation. This means that the district has a responsibility to actively seek out and identify children with disabilities within its boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. If you suspect your child has a disability, even if they are homeschooled or attend private school, you have the right to request an evaluation. The district cannot deny a request for evaluation simply because your child is not currently attending public school. If you have concerns about your child's development, even if they are very young, contact the district to inquire about their Child Find procedures. The district should have a system in place for identifying and evaluating young children with disabilities, even those who are not yet eligible for kindergarten.

What If They Refuse

If Rio Grande City Grulla ISD refuses to evaluate your child, they must provide you with written notice explaining their reasons. This notice must include a description of the data considered in making the decision. You have several options if the district refuses. First, you can request a copy of all records the district used in making its decision. You can also request a meeting with the district to discuss their reasons for refusing the evaluation. If you disagree with the district's decision, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the district work with a neutral third party to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. A due process hearing is a more formal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision. It is highly recommended that you seek legal counsel or advocacy assistance if you choose to pursue mediation or a due process hearing.

After the Evaluation

After the FIE is completed, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to discuss the results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services. The ARD committee includes you, the parent, as well as school personnel such as teachers, administrators, and specialists. If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible, they will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's specific needs. The IEP will outline the special education services and related services your child will receive, as well as measurable goals and objectives. You have the right to participate fully in the ARD process and to disagree with any aspect of the IEP. Remember that the IEP is a living document that should be reviewed and revised at least annually to ensure that it continues to meet your child's needs. You are an essential member of the ARD committee, and your input is vital in ensuring that your child receives the appropriate support and services to succeed in school.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
