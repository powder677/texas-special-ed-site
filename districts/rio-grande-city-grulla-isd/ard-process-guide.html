What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a very important meeting in your child's educational journey if your child has been identified as needing special education services. This meeting is required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code 29, which governs special education in Texas. The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's unique needs, and to review and update that IEP at least annually. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's present levels of academic and functional performance, sets measurable annual goals, specifies the services and supports your child will receive, and determines the most appropriate educational placement for your child.

The ARD committee is comprised of several key individuals, and you, as the parent, are a crucial and indispensable member of the team. Other required members typically include a school administrator or a designated representative of the school district who is knowledgeable about general education curriculum and the availability of resources, your child's general education teacher (if applicable), your child's special education teacher, an evaluation specialist (like an educational diagnostician or psychologist) who can interpret evaluation results, and, when appropriate, your child. The team may also include related service providers such as a speech therapist, occupational therapist, physical therapist, or counselor, depending on your child's specific needs. The ARD committee works collaboratively to make decisions about your child's education. Your voice is vital in this process, as you know your child best and can provide valuable insights into their strengths, weaknesses, needs, and preferences. You have the right to participate fully in all ARD committee meetings and to have your concerns and perspectives seriously considered.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Rio Grande City Grulla ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD committee meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is not just a formality; it's designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting, gather any necessary information, and arrange for support if needed. The written notice must include specific information, such as the purpose of the meeting (e.g., initial eligibility determination, annual review, revision of the IEP), the date, time, and location of the meeting, a list of the individuals who will be attending the meeting, and a clear statement of your parental rights under IDEA and Texas Education Code. The notice should also describe the issues that will be discussed at the meeting.

It is crucial to carefully review this notice upon receiving it. If the proposed date or time is inconvenient for you, you have the right to request a reschedule. You also have the right to request clarification about any aspect of the notice that is unclear to you. If you are comfortable with the proposed meeting date and wish to meet sooner than five days, you can waive your right to the five-day notice in writing. However, it's generally advisable to take the full five days to prepare for the meeting and gather your thoughts. Remember, this meeting is about your child's education, and having adequate preparation time can make a significant difference in the outcome.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting can significantly contribute to a productive and successful discussion. One of the most valuable things you can bring is any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy assessments, and any other reports that provide insight into your child's strengths and areas of need. If you have letters or reports from your child's doctors or other healthcare providers, bring those as well. These documents can provide medical context for your child's educational challenges.

Gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, attendance records, and disciplinary records. These documents can help paint a comprehensive picture of your child's academic performance and behavior at school. Another incredibly helpful tool is a written list of your specific concerns and goals for your child. Prioritize your concerns and articulate them clearly. This will help ensure that your concerns are addressed during the meeting. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or a professional advocate who is familiar with special education law and procedures. Having someone there to take notes, ask questions, and provide emotional support can be invaluable. Finally, Texas is a one-party consent state, which means you are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting as long as you notify the ARD committee that you are doing so. A recording can be a valuable tool for reviewing the meeting later and ensuring that decisions are accurately documented.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a valuable tool that you, as a parent, can utilize during an ARD meeting: the 10-day recess rule. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you, to request a recess of up to ten school days before any final decisions are made about your child's IEP. The purpose of this recess is to provide time to gather additional information, consult with outside experts, or simply to process the information discussed during the meeting. This recess can be particularly helpful if you feel overwhelmed by the information presented, if you disagree with a proposed service or placement, or if you believe that additional information is needed to make informed decisions.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, you simply need to state your request during the ARD meeting. There is no specific form to fill out or lengthy procedure to follow. You can simply say, "I would like to request a recess of ten school days to allow me to [explain your reason, e.g., consult with a specialist, gather additional information, review the proposed IEP more carefully]." The ARD committee is legally obligated to grant your request for a recess. The ten school days begin the day after the request is made. The meeting will reconvene after the recess period to continue the discussion and make final decisions.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the central document that is developed during the ARD meeting. It is a comprehensive plan that outlines your child's specific educational needs, the goals that your child will work towards, and the services and supports that your child will receive to achieve those goals. The IEP includes several key components. "Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance" describes your child's current skills and abilities in different areas, based on assessments, observations, and input from teachers and parents. This section serves as a baseline for measuring progress.

The IEP also sets measurable annual goals for your child. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). They should address your child's identified areas of need and be challenging but attainable. The IEP specifies the special education and related services that your child will receive. This may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and other supports. The amount, frequency, and duration of each service will be clearly outlined. The IEP also outlines your child's educational placement. This refers to the setting where your child will receive their education. Placement options range from the general education classroom with supports to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or self-contained classroom. Your child should be placed in the least restrictive environment (LRE) that meets their needs. The IEP also details any accommodations and modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material (e.g., extended time on tests, preferential seating), while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn (e.g., reduced assignments, alternative assessments).

It is extremely important to remember that you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before making a decision. Take the time you need to ensure that you fully understand and agree with all aspects of the IEP before signing it.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD committee at times. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your perspective is valuable. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that the school district cannot begin providing special education services to your child if you do not agree. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with any of its provisions. Refusing to sign the IEP does not necessarily mean that the school district cannot implement the plan; however, it does indicate that you do not agree with the content of the document. In this situation, the district would then have to provide you prior written notice of their intent to implement the IEP, and you would then have the right to pursue other options.

If you disagree with the evaluation of your child conducted by the school district, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the district agrees to pay for the IEE, they must ensure that the evaluator meets certain qualifications. Even if the district does not agree to pay for the IEE, you still have the right to obtain one at your own expense and have the results considered by the ARD committee. Another way to voice your disagreement is to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it. This clearly indicates that you do not fully agree with the contents of the IEP, even if you are signing it to acknowledge your participation in the meeting. By writing "parent disagrees," you preserve your right to pursue further action if necessary.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, there are several formal options available to you. These options are governed by IDEA 2004 and 19 Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89, also known as the Legal Framework. The first option is to file a formal State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. A State Complaint can address violations of IDEA or Texas special education law.

The second option is Mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a trained, impartial mediator to try to resolve your disagreements. Mediation is also free through TEA. The mediator facilitates communication and helps you and the district find common ground. The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer, who will then issue a binding decision. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is generally advisable to seek legal representation if you pursue this option. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that your child has been discriminated against based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination in educational settings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
