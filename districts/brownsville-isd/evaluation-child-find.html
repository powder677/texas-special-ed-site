Requesting an Evaluation in Brownsville ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Brownsville ISD

This guide provides information for parents and guardians seeking a special education evaluation for their child within the Brownsville Independent School District (BISD) in Texas. It outlines the process, timelines, legal rights, and available options when navigating the special education evaluation process. This information is based on Texas state laws and regulations, specifically the Texas Education Code (TEC) and Texas Administrative Code (TAC), which govern special education services in Texas.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is submitting a formal, written request to BISD. This request should be clear, concise, and unambiguous. It's crucial to document everything, and a written request provides a verifiable record of your intent. Your written request should be addressed to the appropriate school official. Ideally, this would be the campus principal, the special education director at the school, or the designated special education contact person for your child's campus. Check the BISD website or contact the school administration to confirm the correct recipient. The letter should include the following information: * **Your child's full name and date of birth:** This ensures the school accurately identifies the student. * **Your child's current grade level and teacher's name:** This provides context for the school to understand your child's academic placement. * **Your child's school name:** Clearly state the school your child attends within BISD. * **Your name and contact information (phone number and email address):** The school needs to be able to easily contact you. Provide multiple ways to reach you. * **A clear statement requesting a full and individual initial evaluation (FIIE) for special education services:** Be explicit in your request. Avoid vague language. State directly that you are requesting an evaluation to determine if your child is eligible for special education services. * **A brief explanation of your concerns:** Describe the specific difficulties your child is experiencing that are causing you to believe a special education evaluation is necessary. This could include academic struggles, behavioral issues, social-emotional difficulties, or suspected developmental delays. Be specific and provide examples. For instance, instead of saying "my child is struggling in reading," say "My child is struggling with reading comprehension, as evidenced by failing grades on reading quizzes and difficulty summarizing texts in class." If you have documented evidence, such as report cards, teacher communications, or medical reports, mention these and indicate you are willing to provide copies. * **Date and signature:** Date the letter and sign it. This confirms the request's authenticity and establishes the date from which timelines will be calculated. It is strongly recommended to send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school received your request. Keep a copy of the letter and the return receipt for your records. Example language for the request: "To Whom It May Concern, I am writing to request a full and individual initial evaluation (FIIE) for my child, [Child's Full Name], date of birth [Child's Date of Birth], who is currently in the [Grade Level] grade at [School Name] in Mrs./Mr. [Teacher's Name]'s class. I am concerned that my child is experiencing difficulties in [Specific Area of Concern, e.g., reading, math, behavior]. For example, [Provide Specific Examples]. Therefore, I am requesting a full and individual initial evaluation to determine if my child is eligible for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Texas Education Code. I can be reached at [Your Phone Number] or [Your Email Address]. Sincerely, [Your Name] [Date]"

The 45 School Day Timeline

Upon receiving your written request for an FIIE, BISD is obligated to adhere to a specific timeline outlined in the Texas Administrative Code. The key element is the 45-school-day timeline. This timeline refers to instructional days, not calendar days. Holidays, weekends, and summer breaks are not counted. Within 15 school days of receiving your written request, the school must take the following actions: * **Provide you with a copy of the Notice of Procedural Safeguards:** This document explains your rights and responsibilities under special education law. Make sure to read this document carefully. * **Obtain your written consent to conduct the evaluation:** The school cannot proceed with the evaluation without your informed written consent. They will likely provide you with a consent form to sign. Before signing, ensure you understand the scope of the evaluation and the types of assessments that will be used. You have the right to ask questions and request clarification. * **Convene an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to determine whether to evaluate your child:** The ARD committee, which includes you as a key member, will review your request, consider existing data about your child (e.g., grades, attendance records, teacher observations), and decide whether an evaluation is warranted. If the ARD committee decides to evaluate your child, the evaluation must be completed and an ARD committee meeting held to discuss the results within the remaining 30 school days of the 45-school-day timeline. The entire process, from the date the school receives your written request to the date the ARD committee meets to discuss the evaluation results, must be completed within 45 school days. It is important to note that this timeline can be extended by mutual written agreement between you and the school. Any extension should be documented in writing and clearly state the reason for the extension and the new deadline. Keep detailed records of all communication and meetings with the school, including dates, times, and attendees. This documentation can be crucial if there are any disagreements or delays in the process.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability and, if so, the nature and extent of their educational needs. The FIIE must be individualized to the child's specific needs and suspected disabilities. It is not a one-size-fits-all process. The FIIE must assess the child in all areas related to the suspected disability. This may include, but is not limited to: * **Academic Achievement:** This assesses the child's performance in reading, writing, math, and other academic subjects. Standardized tests, classroom work samples, and teacher observations are commonly used. * **Intellectual Functioning:** This evaluates the child's cognitive abilities, such as reasoning, problem-solving, and memory. An individually administered IQ test is typically used. * **Adaptive Behavior:** This assesses the child's ability to function independently in daily life, including self-care, social skills, and communication. Adaptive behavior scales are often used, completed by parents and teachers. * **Communication Skills:** This evaluates the child's ability to understand and express language, including receptive and expressive language skills, articulation, and fluency. A speech-language pathologist typically conducts this assessment. * **Motor Skills:** This assesses the child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, drawing). An occupational therapist or physical therapist may conduct this assessment. * **Social-Emotional Functioning:** This evaluates the child's emotional well-being, social skills, and behavior. Observations, interviews, and rating scales may be used. * **Health:** A review of the child's medical history and any relevant medical information is necessary. * **Vision and Hearing:** Screenings for vision and hearing impairments are essential to rule out these factors as contributing to the child's difficulties. The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in administering and interpreting the assessments used. The evaluators must use a variety of assessment tools and strategies to gather comprehensive information about the child. You have the right to request that specific assessments be included in the FIIE if you believe they are necessary to fully evaluate your child's needs. Be prepared to justify your request with specific reasons. The evaluation report must clearly describe the assessment procedures used, the results obtained, and the evaluator's interpretation of the results. It must also include recommendations for educational interventions and supports.

Child Find Obligations

BISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This is known as "Child Find." This obligation extends to children who are suspected of having a disability but have not yet been formally identified or diagnosed. It also applies to children who are home-schooled or attend private schools within the BISD boundaries. If you suspect that your child has a disability and may be eligible for special education services, you have the right to request an evaluation, even if your child is not currently enrolled in a BISD school. BISD must have procedures in place to ensure that all children with disabilities are identified, located, and evaluated in a timely manner. This includes public awareness campaigns, screenings, and referral processes. If you believe that BISD is not fulfilling its Child Find obligations, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What If They Refuse

BISD may refuse to conduct a special education evaluation if they determine that there is no reasonable suspicion that your child has a disability. This is a critical point of contention for many parents. If the school refuses your request for an evaluation, they must provide you with written notice of their decision, including: * **The reasons for the refusal:** The school must clearly explain why they believe an evaluation is not warranted. The explanation should be based on specific data and observations. * **A description of other options considered:** The school should explain any alternative interventions or supports they considered before denying the evaluation request. * **Information about your right to appeal the decision:** You have the right to challenge the school's refusal to evaluate. You have several options if BISD refuses to evaluate your child: * **Request a meeting with the ARD committee to discuss your concerns further:** This allows you to present additional information and advocate for your child's needs. Document everything discussed during the meeting. * **Request a formal independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense:** If you disagree with the school's decision, you have the right to request an IEE conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by BISD. The school must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. * **File a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA):** You can file a formal complaint with TEA alleging that BISD violated special education law by refusing to evaluate your child. * **Request mediation:** Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school reach a mutually agreeable resolution. * **Request a due process hearing:** A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the dispute. This is a more adversarial process and may require legal representation. It is crucial to consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law if you are facing resistance from the school district. They can advise you on your legal rights and help you navigate the appeals process.

After the Evaluation

Once the FIIE is completed, the ARD committee must meet to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services. If the ARD committee determines that your child has a disability and requires special education services, they will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports that will be provided to help them achieve those goals. You are a critical member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate in all decisions regarding your child's IEP. The IEP must include: * **Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance:** This describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses. * **Measurable annual goals:** These are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. * **Special education and related services:** This includes the specific services and supports that will be provided to your child, such as specialized instruction, therapy, counseling, and assistive technology. * **Modifications and accommodations:** These are changes to the curriculum or classroom environment that will help your child access and participate in learning. * **Placement:** This refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their education. You have the right to approve or reject the IEP. If you disagree with any aspect of the IEP, you have the right to request changes or file a complaint. The ARD committee must meet at least once per year to review and revise the IEP. Remember that you are your child's most important advocate. Stay informed, document everything, and don't hesitate to seek legal assistance if needed. Navigating the special education system can be challenging, but understanding your rights and responsibilities is essential to ensuring that your child receives the services and supports they need to succeed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
