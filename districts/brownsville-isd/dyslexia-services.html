Dyslexia Services in Brownsville ISD

Dyslexia Services in Brownsville ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students within Brownsville Independent School District (BISD), adhering to Texas state guidelines and best practices. It outlines the district's procedures for identification, evaluation, and support for students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024 Update), published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the foundation for BISD's dyslexia program. It provides comprehensive information and guidelines for identifying, assessing, and providing effective instruction for students with dyslexia. Key aspects covered in the handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: Dyslexia is defined as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook outlines the procedures for identifying students who may be at risk for dyslexia, including universal screening and comprehensive evaluations.
  • Instructional Practices: It emphasizes the importance of providing evidence-based, structured literacy instruction to students with dyslexia.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: The handbook details appropriate accommodations and modifications that can be implemented to support students with dyslexia in the classroom.
  • Parent Involvement: It highlights the importance of parent involvement in the identification and support of students with dyslexia.

BISD adheres strictly to the guidelines and recommendations presented in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Parents are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the document, which can be accessed online at the TEA website.

Mandatory Screening

BISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. This universal screening helps to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia early on. The screenings typically assess areas such as:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds in spoken language.
  • Phonological Memory: The ability to hold and manipulate sounds in working memory.
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly and accurately name a series of familiar objects or symbols.
  • Letter-Sound Correspondence: The ability to associate letters with their corresponding sounds.
  • Decoding Skills: The ability to sound out words.

If a student demonstrates potential risk factors for dyslexia based on the screening results, BISD will conduct further assessments to determine eligibility for dyslexia services. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as potentially at-risk and will be informed about the next steps in the evaluation process. It's important to note that screening is not a diagnosis, but rather an indicator for further investigation.

Structured Literacy Programs

BISD utilizes evidence-based structured literacy programs to provide targeted instruction to students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading and spelling. It emphasizes the following principles:

  • Systematic and Cumulative: Instruction is presented in a logical sequence, building from basic concepts to more complex skills. Each skill is taught explicitly and systematically before moving on to the next.
  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions made about what students already know.
  • Diagnostic Teaching: Instruction is tailored to meet the individual needs of each student, based on ongoing assessment of their progress.
  • Multisensory Instruction: Instruction involves the use of multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile) to enhance learning.

Specific structured literacy programs used by BISD may include (but are not limited to):

  • Take Flight: Developed by the Luke Waites Center for Dyslexia at Scottish Rite Hospital, this comprehensive program focuses on phonology, reading, spelling, handwriting, and reading comprehension.
  • Neuhaus Education Center Programs: Neuhaus offers a variety of structured literacy programs, addressing different aspects of reading and spelling.
  • Other research-based programs: BISD stays informed of current research and adopts other research-based programs that align with structured literacy principles as appropriate. Contact your child's school for specifics about which program is being used.

These programs are delivered by trained educators who have received specialized training in structured literacy methods. The goal is to equip students with the necessary skills to become proficient readers and spellers.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or through an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is most appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the level of support required.

Section 504 Plan

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: A 504 plan focuses on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services under a 504 plan may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, and access to assistive technology. It may also include dyslexia intervention, but doesn't require it.
  • Evaluation: Requires an evaluation to determine if the student has a disability that substantially limits a major life activity.

Individualized Education Program (IEP)

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that falls under one of the categories listed in IDEA (including Specific Learning Disability, which dyslexia falls under) and requires specialized instruction to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Focus: An IEP focuses on providing specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: Services under an IEP may include specially designed instruction in reading and spelling, as well as related services such as speech-language therapy. The IEP will include specific goals and objectives.
  • Evaluation: Requires a comprehensive evaluation to determine the student's strengths and weaknesses and to develop an individualized education program.

The key difference is that an IEP involves specially designed instruction, while a 504 plan focuses on accommodations. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, including parents, makes the determination as to whether a student requires an IEP.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns material. They do not change what a student is taught. BISD provides a range of accommodations to support students with dyslexia in the classroom. Examples of common accommodations include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing students additional time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing students in a location in the classroom that minimizes distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Visual Aids: Providing visual supports such as graphic organizers and charts.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Incorporating visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile elements into instruction.
  • Oral Administration: Reading test questions and instructions aloud to students.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing students to use a calculator for math problems.
  • Frequent Breaks: Providing opportunities for students to take short breaks to refocus.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Access to Notes: Providing students with copies of notes or outlines.
  • Use of Highlighters: Allowing students to use highlighters to mark important information in text.
  • Reading Aloud: Allowing students to read aloud to themselves or a teacher.
  • Spelling Support: Providing access to spelling aids such as spell check or a word processor.
  • Alternative Assessment: Providing alternative ways for students to demonstrate their understanding of the material. Examples include oral reports, projects, or presentations.

The specific accommodations provided to a student will be determined based on their individual needs, as documented in their 504 plan or IEP. It is crucial to remember that accommodations are designed to level the playing field, allowing students with dyslexia to demonstrate their knowledge and skills without being hindered by their reading difficulties.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia should contact their child's teacher or the school's 504 coordinator/special education administrator. The process for requesting an evaluation typically involves the following steps:

  1. Initial Contact: Contact the teacher or school administration to express your concerns and request an evaluation. This is typically done in writing (email or letter).
  2. Parent Input: Provide information about your child's learning history, strengths, and weaknesses. Share any relevant medical or educational documentation.
  3. Response to Intervention (RTI): The school may implement Response to Intervention (RTI) strategies to provide targeted support to the student and monitor their progress. This involves implementing research-based interventions and tracking the student's response to these interventions.
  4. Referral for Evaluation: If RTI strategies are not successful, or if there is strong evidence of dyslexia, the school will refer the student for a comprehensive evaluation.
  5. Evaluation Process: The evaluation will be conducted by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians or licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs). The evaluation will assess areas such as reading, spelling, phonological awareness, and language skills.
  6. Evaluation Report: A written report will be provided summarizing the evaluation findings and recommendations.
  7. Eligibility Determination: Based on the evaluation results, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will determine whether the student is eligible for special education services under IDEA or accommodations under Section 504.
  8. Development of IEP or 504 Plan: If the student is eligible for services, an IEP or 504 plan will be developed to address their individual needs.

BISD is committed to providing timely and appropriate evaluations for students who may have dyslexia. Parents are active participants in the evaluation process and have the right to access all relevant information. If you have any questions or concerns about the evaluation process, please contact your child's school or the BISD Special Education Department.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
