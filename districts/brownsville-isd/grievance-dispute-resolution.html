Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Brownsville ISD

This guide is for parents and guardians of students in Brownsville ISD who are facing disciplinary actions. It is designed to help you understand your rights and navigate the grievance process. Time is of the essence! Do not delay in taking action. This guide focuses on key areas: The 10-Day Suspension Rule, Mandatory Disciplinary Reviews (MDRs), Change of Placement, Filing a Level 1 Grievance, TEA State Complaint, and Due Process. This is not a substitute for legal advice, but it provides critical information to protect your child.

Disclaimer: This guide provides general information and is not legal advice. You should consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law and student discipline matters for personalized guidance specific to your child's situation.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Brownsville ISD, like all Texas school districts, is governed by state law regarding suspensions. Understanding the 10-Day Suspension Rule is crucial. Here's what you need to know:

  • Short-Term Suspension: A school administrator can suspend your child for up to three school days for any violation of the Student Code of Conduct.
  • Longer Suspensions (Up to 10 Days): For more serious offenses, or repeated misbehavior, a student can be suspended for a longer period, up to ten school days. A principal, assistant principal, or other designated administrator can impose this.
  • Parent Notification: The school *must* notify you immediately, preferably by phone or email, and in writing, about the suspension. The written notification *must* include the reason for the suspension, the length of the suspension, and information about your child's rights and the grievance process. Demand written notification; verbal communication isn't enough.
  • Student with Disabilities: If your child has an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or 504 plan, the 10-day rule takes on extra significance. Repeated suspensions, even if each is shorter than 10 days, can trigger a Change of Placement, which requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). We'll cover MDRs in detail below.
  • Counting the Days: The "10-day rule" refers to *cumulative* suspensions. Keep a detailed record of every suspension your child receives. A seemingly minor suspension of 2 days here and 3 days there adds up quickly.
  • "Removal": A "removal" from the classroom, even if it's not officially labeled a "suspension," can count toward the 10-day threshold if your child is not receiving educational services equivalent to what they would receive in their normal placement. This is especially critical for students with IEPs.

What to do:

  1. Demand Information: Immediately upon notification of a suspension, demand the specific rule your child allegedly violated, all evidence supporting the accusation, and a clear explanation of the school's reasoning.
  2. Document Everything: Keep a detailed record of all communications with the school, including dates, times, who you spoke with, and what was discussed. Save all written notices, emails, and letters.
  3. Review the Student Code of Conduct: Familiarize yourself with Brownsville ISD's Student Code of Conduct. Ensure the rule your child allegedly violated is clearly defined and consistently applied.
  4. Consider Legal Counsel: If you believe your child's rights have been violated, or if you're unsure about your next steps, consult with an attorney or advocate experienced in special education and student discipline.

What Is an MDR? (Manifestation Determination Review)

A Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is a crucial process for students with disabilities (IEP or 504 plan) who are facing disciplinary action that could lead to a Change of Placement. It's a safeguard designed to protect students from being punished for behavior that is a direct result of their disability.

When is an MDR Required?

  • Change of Placement: An MDR is required when a disciplinary change of placement is being considered. This includes suspensions exceeding 10 consecutive school days, or a series of suspensions that create a pattern amounting to a Change of Placement. This is where that cumulative suspension count becomes vital.
  • Expulsion: An MDR *must* be conducted before a student with an IEP or 504 plan can be expelled.

What Happens in an MDR?

The MDR team, which *must* include you (the parent/guardian), relevant members of the IEP team (or 504 team), and district representatives, will review all relevant information, including:

  • The student's IEP or 504 plan.
  • The student's educational records.
  • Teacher observations.
  • Information provided by you, the parent. This is YOUR opportunity to explain how your child's disability contributed to the behavior.
  • Any relevant medical or psychological evaluations.

The MDR team will determine two critical things:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school's failure to implement the student's IEP or 504 plan?

Outcomes of an MDR:

  • If the answer to *either* question is YES: The conduct is considered a "manifestation" of the disability. The student cannot be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team must then conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) (if one hasn't already been done) and implement or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The student must return to their original placement, unless you and the school agree to a change.
  • If the answer to *both* questions is NO: The conduct is *not* considered a manifestation of the disability. The student can be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. However, the school *must* still provide FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education) to the student during the period of disciplinary removal.

What to do:

  1. Demand an MDR: If your child with an IEP or 504 plan is facing a suspension exceeding 10 days, or a series of suspensions that create a pattern, immediately demand a Manifestation Determination Review. Put your request in writing.
  2. Prepare for the MDR: Gather all relevant information about your child's disability and how it relates to the behavior in question. Talk to your child's doctor or therapist for their professional opinion. Prepare a written statement explaining your perspective.
  3. Attend the MDR Meeting: Participate actively in the MDR meeting. Advocate for your child. Be prepared to explain how their disability affects their behavior. Do not be intimidated.
  4. Document Everything: Keep detailed notes of the MDR meeting and all related communications.
  5. Appeal: If you disagree with the outcome of the MDR, you have the right to appeal. Consult with an attorney or advocate immediately to understand your appeal options.

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for disciplinary reasons for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when a series of removals creates a pattern. This pattern can be determined by factors such as the length of each removal, the total amount of time the student is removed, and the proximity of the removals to each other. Even if individual suspensions are short, repeated suspensions that disrupt your child's education can be considered a Change of Placement.

Changes of placement can involve:

  • Suspension to home
  • Placement in a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP)
  • Expulsion

For students with IEPs and 504 plans, a Change of Placement triggers the MDR process (described above). This is why understanding cumulative suspension days and the definition of "removal" is so critical. The school might try to avoid triggering the MDR process by strategically issuing short suspensions. Be vigilant!

What to do:

  1. Track Suspension Days: Keep a precise record of all suspensions, including the dates, duration, and reasons for each suspension.
  2. Monitor Educational Services: Ensure your child is receiving appropriate educational services during any period of suspension or removal. If they are not, document this and raise it with the school.
  3. Demand Justification: If the school proposes a Change of Placement, demand a clear and detailed justification for the change, including specific evidence to support their claims.
  4. Advocate for Alternatives: Explore alternatives to out-of-school suspension or DAEP placement, such as in-school suspension, restorative justice practices, or modifications to the IEP or 504 plan.
  5. Legal Consultation: If you disagree with the proposed Change of Placement, consult with an attorney or advocate to understand your rights and options.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

Brownsville ISD has a formal grievance process for resolving complaints. Filing a Level 1 grievance is often the first step in challenging a disciplinary action.

Key Points:

  • Time Limit: You typically have a very limited time (often 10-15 school days) to file a Level 1 grievance after the event that triggered the complaint. Check the Brownsville ISD policy manual for the exact timeframe. Do not delay!
  • Written Grievance: The grievance must be in writing and should clearly state:
    • Your child's name and student ID.
    • The date of the incident.
    • A detailed description of the incident and the disciplinary action taken.
    • The specific policy or procedure that you believe was violated.
    • The specific relief you are seeking (e.g., removal of the suspension from your child's record, reinstatement to their original placement).
  • Submission: The Level 1 grievance is typically filed with the school principal. Obtain proof of delivery (e.g., a signed receipt or certified mail).
  • Response: The principal is required to investigate the grievance and provide a written response within a specified timeframe (again, check the Brownsville ISD policy manual).

What to do:

  1. Act Quickly: Determine the exact deadline for filing a Level 1 grievance and ensure you meet it.
  2. Be Clear and Concise: Write a clear, concise, and factual account of the events. Avoid emotional language or personal attacks.
  3. Cite Specific Policies: Refer to specific Brownsville ISD policies or procedures that you believe were violated. The Student Code of Conduct and special education policies are good starting points.
  4. Keep Copies: Keep copies of your grievance and all related documentation.
  5. Escalate if Necessary: If you are not satisfied with the principal's response, you can typically appeal to the next level of the grievance process (e.g., to the superintendent or a designated district administrator). Follow the timelines and procedures outlined in the Brownsville ISD policy manual.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Brownsville ISD has violated state or federal laws related to special education, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is separate from the district's grievance process.

Key Points:

  • Violation of IDEA: TEA investigates complaints alleging violations of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). This includes failures to provide FAPE, improper IEP development, denial of due process rights, etc.
  • Timeline: You must generally file a complaint with TEA within one year of the alleged violation.
  • Requirements: Your complaint must be in writing and must include:
    • A statement that Brownsville ISD has violated a requirement of IDEA.
    • The facts on which the statement is based.
    • Your signature and contact information.
    • A proposed resolution of the problem.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision.

What to do:

  1. Research TEA Complaint Procedures: Visit the TEA website for detailed information on filing a complaint.
  2. Gather Evidence: Collect all relevant documentation to support your complaint, including IEPs, evaluations, school records, and correspondence.
  3. Draft a Clear Complaint: Clearly and concisely explain the specific violations of IDEA that you believe occurred.
  4. Submit the Complaint: Submit your complaint to TEA according to their specified procedures.
  5. Cooperate with TEA: Cooperate fully with TEA's investigation.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal procedure that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding their child's special education. It's a more serious step than filing a grievance or a TEA complaint.

Key Points:

  • Disputes Regarding IEP: Due process is typically used to resolve disputes about the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability, or the provision of FAPE.
  • Mediation: Before filing for due process, you are generally required to participate in mediation with the school district. This is an attempt to resolve the dispute informally.
  • Filing for Due Process: To file for due process, you must submit a written due process complaint to the school district and TEA. The complaint must meet specific requirements outlined in IDEA.
  • Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is similar to a court trial. You and the school district will present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer.
  • Hearing Officer Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision resolving the dispute.

What to do:

  1. Consult with an Attorney: Due process is a complex legal process. You should consult with an attorney specializing in special education law before filing for due process.
  2. Understand Your Rights: Familiarize yourself with your rights under IDEA.
  3. Gather Evidence: Collect all relevant documentation to support your case.
  4. Prepare for the Hearing: Work closely with your attorney to prepare for the due process hearing.

Conclusion:

Navigating the disciplinary process in Brownsville ISD can be challenging, especially when your child has a disability. Remember to act quickly, document everything, advocate for your child's rights, and seek legal advice when needed. This guide is a starting point. Do not hesitate to reach out to legal professionals or advocacy groups for further assistance. Your child's education and well-being depend on your proactive involvement.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
