Dispute & Discipline Rights in Fort Bend ISD

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Fort Bend ISD, like all Texas school districts, must adhere to specific rules regarding suspensions, especially when a student has disabilities covered under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The cornerstone of this is the 10-day suspension rule:

  • Cumulative Suspensions: If your child is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single academic year, a significant process is triggered, especially if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or 504 plan. This means every single suspension day gets added together. Even if they are for seemingly minor incidents, once you hit 10 days combined, alarm bells should be ringing.
  • The Importance of "Days": Pay close attention to how "days" are counted. Is it school days? Calendar days? This can drastically affect timelines and deadlines. Confirm with the school district how they are calculating the suspension days.
  • Consequences of Exceeding 10 Days: Once that 10-day threshold is met (or close to being met), the district must determine if the suspension constitutes a "Change of Placement." This is a critical distinction.
URGENT ACTION: Keep meticulous records of all disciplinary actions, dates, and reasons for each suspension. This is your evidence! Do not rely solely on the school's records.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a critical meeting convened when a student with an IEP or 504 plan faces disciplinary action that could result in a change of placement (e.g., expulsion, long-term suspension, or placement in a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP)).

  • Purpose of the MDR: The primary goal of the MDR is to determine if the student's misconduct was a manifestation of their disability. In simpler terms, did the disability cause the behavior? Or, did the school's failure to properly implement the IEP or 504 plan contribute to the behavior?
  • Who Attends the MDR? The IEP team (including parents/guardians), relevant school personnel (administrators, teachers), and potentially a behavior specialist. You have the right to bring advocates or legal counsel. Do not go alone!
  • Key Questions Asked During the MDR:
    • Was the conduct in question caused by or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to the child's disability?
    • Was the conduct in question the direct result of the district's failure to implement the IEP?
  • Possible Outcomes of the MDR:
    • Manifestation: If the team determines the conduct was a manifestation of the disability, the student must be returned to their previous placement (unless the parents and school agree otherwise). The IEP team must also conduct a Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) if one hasn't been done, and develop (or revise) a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior.
    • No Manifestation: If the team determines the conduct was not a manifestation of the disability, the student can be disciplined in the same manner as a student without a disability. However, the school must continue to provide educational services to ensure the student can continue to participate in the general education curriculum, although in another setting.
URGENT ACTION: If your child is facing disciplinary action and has an IEP or 504 plan, immediately request an MDR. Do this in writing and keep a copy. Demand to see all documentation related to your child's disciplinary record and IEP/504 implementation.

Change of Placement

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a disciplinary action significantly alters a student's educational setting or services. For students with IEPs or 504 plans, this triggers specific protections under IDEA.

  • What Constitutes a Change of Placement? Generally, a suspension of more than 10 consecutive school days, or a series of suspensions that create a pattern of removal totaling more than 10 school days in a school year, and which constitute a significant change in placement, can be considered a Change of Placement. Placement in a DAEP or expulsion are almost always considered a Change of Placement.
  • Your Rights During a Change of Placement: You have the right to:
    • Receive prior written notice of the proposed change of placement.
    • Participate in the MDR.
    • Request a due process hearing if you disagree with the MDR decision or the proposed placement.
URGENT ACTION: If you believe your child's disciplinary action constitutes a Change of Placement, demand that the school district conduct an MDR and provide you with all required notices and information.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe the school district has violated your child's rights or has acted unfairly, you can file a grievance. Fort Bend ISD likely has a specific grievance policy outlined on their website (find it!). Here's a general guide, but always refer to the official FBISD policy.

  • Level 1 Grievance (Typically): This is usually filed with the principal or immediate supervisor involved in the issue.
  • How to File:
    • In Writing: Always submit your grievance in writing. This creates a formal record.
    • Be Specific: Clearly state the facts, the specific policy or law you believe was violated, and the remedy you are seeking. "My child was treated unfairly" is not enough. Provide dates, names, specific actions, and the relevant policies or laws.
    • Timelines: Be aware of the deadlines for filing a grievance. Missed deadlines can mean your grievance is dismissed. The FBISD policy will specify this.
    • Keep Copies: Keep a copy of your grievance and any supporting documentation.
  • Typical Grievance Process: The principal or supervisor will investigate the grievance and provide a written response within a specified timeframe (again, check FBISD policy).
  • Appeal to Level 2: If you are not satisfied with the response at Level 1, you can usually appeal to the next level (e.g., the Superintendent or a designated district administrator). Follow the deadlines and procedures outlined in the FBISD policy.
URGENT ACTION: Familiarize yourself with the Fort Bend ISD grievance policy. If you believe your child's rights have been violated, begin drafting your Level 1 grievance immediately.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Fort Bend ISD has violated federal special education law (IDEA), you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

  • What IDEA Violations Can Be Reported? Examples include failure to provide a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE), failure to properly implement an IEP, denial of procedural safeguards, etc.
  • How to File a TEA Complaint: TEA has a specific process for filing complaints, which is outlined on their website. Generally, you will need to:
    • Submit in Writing: Follow TEA's specific form or format.
    • Include Specific Information: Provide detailed information about the alleged violation, including dates, names, and the specific provisions of IDEA that were violated.
    • Provide Supporting Documentation: Attach copies of IEPs, disciplinary records, correspondence, and any other relevant documents.
    • Deadlines: TEA complaints must typically be filed within one year of the alleged violation.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision. If they find a violation, they may order the school district to take corrective action.
URGENT ACTION: If you believe Fort Bend ISD has violated IDEA, review TEA's complaint procedures and consider filing a complaint.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal process under IDEA that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding their child's special education. It is the most serious and formal step you can take.

  • When to Consider Due Process: When you disagree with the district about identification, evaluation, educational placement, or the provision of FAPE, and you have exhausted other avenues of resolution (such as grievances), due process may be necessary.
  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: You must file a written due process complaint with the TEA and the school district. The complaint must meet specific requirements under IDEA.
  • Mediation: Before proceeding to a due process hearing, the district and parents must participate in mediation (unless both parties agree to waive it).
  • Due Process Hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful, a due process hearing will be held before an impartial hearing officer. Both sides will present evidence and witnesses.
  • Hearing Officer's Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision that is binding on both parties (unless appealed).
  • Legal Representation: It is strongly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law if you are considering due process. This is a complex legal process.
URGENT ACTION: Due process is a complex and potentially expensive process. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law to determine if it is the right course of action for your situation.

REMEMBER: Document everything. Keep copies of all correspondence, disciplinary records, IEPs, and other relevant documents. Act quickly, know your rights, and seek legal counsel if needed. Protecting your child is paramount.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
