How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Fort Bend ISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Fort Bend ISD (FBISD), according to Texas state law. It outlines the process, timelines, required assessments, your rights, and what to do if the district fails to meet its obligations. It is crucial to document all communication with the school district in writing, keeping copies for your records.

How to Submit a Written Request

The most important step in initiating the special education evaluation process is submitting a written request to the school district. This request formally puts FBISD on notice that you believe your child may have a disability impacting their education and requires an evaluation. A verbal request is not sufficient; it *must* be in writing.

Here's what your written request should include:

  • Date: Include the date you are writing the letter.
  • Your Name and Contact Information: Provide your full name, address, phone number, and email address.
  • Your Child's Name and Information: Include your child's full name, date of birth, grade level, and the name of their current school.
  • Statement of Concern: Clearly state that you are requesting a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) for special education services. For example, "I am writing to formally request a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) to determine if my child, [Child's Name], is eligible for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law."
  • Reasons for Your Concern: Provide specific examples of why you believe your child may have a disability affecting their academic performance or access to the general curriculum. Be as detailed as possible. Include information about:
    • Academic struggles (reading, writing, math, etc.)
    • Behavioral concerns (attention difficulties, hyperactivity, defiance, social-emotional challenges)
    • Speech and language difficulties
    • Physical or sensory impairments
    • Any interventions or accommodations that have already been tried, and their results.
    • Any relevant medical diagnoses or evaluations from outside professionals.
  • Specific Areas for Evaluation: If you have specific areas you want the evaluation to address, mention them. For example, "I am particularly concerned about [Child's Name]'s reading comprehension and writing skills. I would like the evaluation to specifically assess these areas."
  • Consent for Evaluation: Clearly state that you consent to the evaluation. For example, "I consent to the school district conducting a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation of my child."
  • Your Signature: Sign and date the letter.

Submitting the Request:

  • Send the written request to the school principal and the school's special education director. Consider sending it via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure you have proof of delivery. Keep a copy of the letter for your records.

Example Statement of Concern: "My child, [Child's Name], is in the 3rd grade at [School Name]. I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s reading progress. Despite receiving small group intervention in reading, [he/she] continues to struggle with decoding and comprehension. [He/She] is significantly behind [his/her] peers in reading level and often expresses frustration and anxiety about reading assignments. I am also concerned about [his/her] difficulty focusing in class. [He/She] often fidgets, has trouble staying on task, and requires frequent redirection from the teacher."

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law (specifically, the Texas Education Code and the Texas Administrative Code) mandates a specific timeline for the special education evaluation process once a written request is received. It is essential to understand this timeline to ensure the school district adheres to its legal obligations.

The 45 School Day Timeline:

  • The school district has 15 school days from the date of your written request to decide whether or not to conduct an evaluation. During this time, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, including you as the parent, will meet to review existing data, determine if an evaluation is warranted, and if so, identify the areas of suspected disability that will be assessed.
  • If the ARD committee determines that an evaluation is warranted, the district must complete the Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) and hold an ARD meeting to discuss the evaluation results within 45 school days of receiving your written consent for the evaluation. This timeline *includes* the time it takes to conduct the evaluation, write the report, and schedule the ARD meeting.

Important Considerations Regarding the Timeline:

  • School Days vs. Calendar Days: The timeline refers to *school days*, not calendar days. This means weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted.
  • Counting Begins With Consent: The 45-school-day timeline starts after you provide written consent for the evaluation, *not* when you initially submit the request. It is critical to provide your consent promptly once the ARD committee has decided to evaluate.
  • Delays: If the district anticipates a delay in completing the evaluation within the 45-day timeline, they must notify you in writing and explain the reason for the delay. They must also propose a revised timeline. You have the right to disagree with the proposed delay.
  • Documentation: Keep careful records of all dates and communication with the school district related to the evaluation request and timeline. This documentation will be crucial if you need to file a complaint or pursue legal action.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability and, if so, the nature and extent of their educational needs. Texas law requires that the FIIE be individualized and address all areas of suspected disability.

Required Components of the FIIE:

  • Comprehensive Assessment: The FIIE must assess all areas of suspected disability, including academic, cognitive, communication, adaptive behavior, social-emotional, and motor skills.
  • Variety of Assessment Tools: The evaluation should use a variety of assessment tools and strategies, including standardized tests, observations, interviews, and review of existing records. Relying solely on one test is generally insufficient.
  • Qualified Professionals: The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in assessing children with disabilities. This may include educational diagnosticians, school psychologists, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, and other specialists.
  • Consideration of Existing Data: The evaluation team must consider existing data about the child, including:
    • Classroom observations and teacher reports
    • Medical records and evaluations from outside professionals
    • Previous academic assessments
    • Parent input and observations
  • Linguistic Needs: If the child's primary language is not English, the evaluation must be conducted in the child's native language or another mode of communication that the child understands.
  • Specific Areas to Consider: The specific areas assessed in the FIIE will depend on the child's individual needs and the areas of suspected disability. However, some common areas that may be assessed include:
    • Cognitive Abilities: Intelligence testing (e.g., WISC-V)
    • Academic Achievement: Reading, writing, and math assessments
    • Speech and Language: Articulation, fluency, language comprehension, and expression
    • Motor Skills: Fine motor and gross motor skills
    • Adaptive Behavior: Daily living skills, social skills, and communication skills
    • Social-Emotional Functioning: Emotional regulation, behavior problems, and social interactions
  • Educational Needs: The evaluation must identify the child's specific educational needs and how the disability impacts their access to the general education curriculum.

Parent Rights During the Evaluation:

  • Active Participation: You have the right to actively participate in the evaluation process and provide input to the evaluation team.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school district's evaluation, you have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense (under certain conditions) or at your own expense. The school district must consider the results of an IEE.

Child Find Obligations

The "Child Find" mandate is a critical component of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law. It requires all school districts, including FBISD, to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public schools.

What Child Find Means for FBISD:

  • Proactive Identification: FBISD has a legal obligation to actively seek out children who may have disabilities, even if those children are not currently enrolled in public school (e.g., homeschooled children or children attending private schools).
  • Public Awareness: FBISD must conduct public awareness campaigns to inform parents and the community about the availability of special education services.
  • Screening and Referral: FBISD must have procedures in place for screening and referring children who are suspected of having a disability. This includes responding to referrals from parents, teachers, and other professionals.
  • Evaluation at No Cost: If FBISD suspects that a child may have a disability, they must evaluate the child at no cost to the parents.

Your Role in Child Find:

  • Referral: As a parent, you have the right to refer your child for a special education evaluation if you suspect they may have a disability. This is your right, regardless of any prior interventions or assessments.
  • Advocacy: Be an advocate for your child and ensure that FBISD is fulfilling its Child Find obligations. If you believe that FBISD has failed to identify or evaluate your child in a timely manner, you have the right to file a complaint.

What If They Refuse

If Fort Bend ISD refuses to conduct a special education evaluation after you have submitted a written request, you have several options under Texas law.

Reasons for Refusal:

FBISD may refuse to conduct an evaluation if they determine, based on existing data, that your child does not have a suspected disability impacting their education. However, they must provide you with a written explanation of their decision, including the reasons for the refusal and the data they relied upon.

Your Options When They Refuse:

  • Request Prior Written Notice: If FBISD refuses to evaluate your child, they are required to provide you with "Prior Written Notice" (PWN). This notice must explain the school's decision, the reasons for the decision, and the information they used to make the decision. It must also inform you of your rights to disagree with the decision.
  • Request a Meeting: Request a meeting with the ARD committee, including the principal and special education staff, to discuss your concerns and the reasons for their refusal. Prepare to present your evidence and advocate for your child's evaluation.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third party to try to resolve the dispute. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) offers mediation services at no cost to parents.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You have the right to file a formal complaint with TEA if you believe FBISD has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Texas special education law. The complaint must be filed within one year of the alleged violation. TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a decision.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and argue your case before an impartial hearing officer. This is a more adversarial process than mediation and may require the assistance of an attorney. You must request a due process hearing within two years of the date you knew or should have known about the alleged action that forms the basis of the hearing request.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): Even if the district refuses to evaluate, you still have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at your own expense. If you disagree with the school district's evaluation (or lack thereof), you can present the IEE results to the ARD committee. You may also be able to obtain an IEE at public expense if you can demonstrate that the school district's evaluation was inappropriate.

Document Everything:

It is crucial to document all communication with the school district, including dates, times, names of individuals you spoke with, and the content of the conversations. Keep copies of all letters, emails, and other documents related to your child's special education evaluation.

After the Evaluation

After the Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is completed, the next step is to hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services.

The ARD Meeting:

  • ARD Committee Members: The ARD committee must include:
    • You (the parent)
    • A representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of special education (e.g., the special education director or a designated administrator)
    • Your child's teacher (or if the child is new to the school, a teacher qualified to teach a child of that age)
    • An educational diagnostician or other qualified professional who conducted the evaluation
    • Other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, including related service providers (e.g., speech-language pathologist, occupational therapist)
    • Your child (when appropriate)
  • Reviewing the Evaluation Report: The ARD committee will review the FIIE report and discuss the findings. The educational diagnostician or other professionals who conducted the evaluation will explain the results and answer your questions.
  • Determining Eligibility: Based on the evaluation results, the ARD committee will determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law. To be eligible, your child must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specially designed instruction.
  • Developing the Individualized Education Program (IEP): If your child is determined to be eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and services.

Key Components of the IEP:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): A description of your child's current academic and functional skills.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child's academic and functional progress.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the special education and related services that your child will receive, including the frequency, duration, and location of services. Related services may include speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, transportation, and other supports.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom and during assessments. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, while modifications are changes to what a student learns.
  • Participation in Statewide Assessments: A description of how your child will participate in statewide assessments, including any accommodations that will be provided.
  • Transition Plan: For students age 16 and older (or younger, if appropriate), the IEP must include a transition plan that outlines goals for post-secondary education, employment, and independent living.

Parent Rights After the Evaluation:

  • Active Participation in the ARD Process: You have the right to actively participate in the ARD process and provide input on your child's IEP.
  • Right to Disagree: You have the right to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. If you disagree with the IEP, you can request mediation or a due process hearing.
  • Periodic Review of the IEP: The IEP must be reviewed at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to request an ARD meeting at any time to review and revise your child's IEP.

Remember to document all communication and keep copies of all documents related to your child's special education evaluation and IEP. This information will be crucial if you need to advocate for your child's rights or resolve any disputes with the school district. Consult with legal counsel if you have any questions or concerns regarding your rights.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
