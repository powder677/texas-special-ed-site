Dyslexia Services in Fort Bend ISD

Dyslexia Services in Fort Bend ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available within Fort Bend ISD (FBISD) in accordance with Texas state law and guidelines. It is designed to assist parents, educators, and students in understanding the process for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (TDH) is the primary resource for understanding dyslexia and related services in Texas public schools. The 2024 update provides the most current guidelines and procedures for identification, evaluation, and instruction for students with dyslexia. It's crucial for parents and educators to familiarize themselves with this document. You can access the handbook on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Key updates in the 2024 handbook may include clarifications on:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The state's definition of dyslexia remains consistent, emphasizing the neurobiological origin and difficulties with accurate and fluent word recognition and spelling abilities.
  • Screening Procedures: Updated guidelines regarding universal screening measures and timelines for implementation.
  • Instructional Programs: Specific guidance on the components of Structured Literacy programs and evidence-based interventions.
  • Documentation and Data Collection: Requirements for documenting student progress and making data-driven decisions.
  • Parent Involvement: Emphasis on the importance of parent communication and collaboration in the dyslexia intervention process.

It is imperative to consult the official Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 for the most accurate and up-to-date information. This guide is intended as an overview and should not be substituted for professional legal or educational advice. Fort Bend ISD follows the guidelines outlined in the TDH.

Mandatory Screening

In Texas, all students in kindergarten and first grade are required to be screened for dyslexia using TEA-approved screening instruments. FBISD adheres to this mandate. The purpose of universal screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia early in their academic careers.

Screening Process in FBISD:

  • Kindergarten: Students are screened during the school year using a TEA-approved screener. This usually focuses on phonological awareness and rapid automatized naming (RAN).
  • First Grade: Students are screened again, typically focusing on phonological awareness, phonological memory, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and decoding skills.
  • Students New to FBISD: Students entering FBISD in grades 2-12 who have a history of reading difficulties or who are suspected of having dyslexia may be screened or referred for a full evaluation.

What Happens After Screening?

  • Positive Screening Result: If a student demonstrates characteristics indicative of dyslexia based on the screening results, parents will be notified. The school will then provide appropriate interventions and progress monitoring. A full evaluation may be recommended if the interventions are not sufficiently effective.
  • Negative Screening Result: A negative screening result does not guarantee that a student does not have dyslexia. It simply means that the student did not exhibit the specific characteristics measured by the screener at that time. If concerns persist, parents should communicate with the school.

Structured Literacy Programs

Structured Literacy is the instructional approach recommended by the TEA for students with dyslexia. It is an evidence-based, explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to teaching reading, spelling, and writing. It emphasizes the foundational skills necessary for literacy development.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Understanding the sound structure of language (phonemes).
  • Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics): Connecting sounds (phonemes) to letters (graphemes).
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching syllable types and syllable division rules.
  • Morphology: Understanding the meaning of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, roots).
  • Syntax: Understanding sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: Understanding the meaning of words and phrases.

FBISD Dyslexia Programs:

FBISD utilizes structured literacy programs aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook guidelines. While specific program names may vary depending on the campus and student needs, all programs will incorporate the core components of Structured Literacy. Parents should contact their child's school to inquire about the specific dyslexia intervention program used at their campus. Examples of commonly used programs or approaches include (but are not limited to):

  • Take Flight: A Comprehensive Intervention for Students with Dyslexia: This is a program developed by the Luke Waites Center for Dyslexia at Texas Scottish Rite Hospital for Children.
  • Other evidence-based reading interventions: FBISD may implement other scientifically based reading interventions that align with the principles of Structured Literacy.

Progress Monitoring: Students receiving dyslexia intervention will be regularly monitored to assess their progress. This data is used to inform instructional decisions and adjust interventions as needed.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determining factor is the severity of the student's needs and whether the dyslexia substantially limits a major life activity, such as learning.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. Dyslexia can qualify as such an impairment.
  • Focus: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to ensure the student has equal access to the learning environment.
  • Services: Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Evaluation: A 504 evaluation involves a review of existing data, including screening results, grades, teacher observations, and parent input. No comprehensive psychoeducational evaluation is necessarily required.

Individualized Education Program (IEP) - Special Education:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for special education services under an IEP if they have a disability (such as a Specific Learning Disability, which can include dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: An IEP provides specialized instruction and related services designed to meet the student's unique needs.
  • Services: IEPs include accommodations, modifications, and specialized instruction delivered by certified special education teachers. This may include direct, intensive, and systematic instruction in reading, writing, and spelling using a structured literacy approach.
  • Evaluation: An IEP requires a comprehensive psychoeducational evaluation to determine the student's strengths and weaknesses, learning style, and specific needs. The evaluation team must include qualified professionals and the parent(s).

Key Differences:

  • Severity of Need: IEPs are typically for students with more significant learning challenges who require specialized instruction.
  • Specialized Instruction: IEPs always involve specialized instruction; 504 plans focus on accommodations.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs have more stringent legal requirements and procedural safeguards under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

The school's ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee or 504 committee will determine the most appropriate plan for the student based on their individual needs and evaluation results. Parents are integral members of these committees.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the way a student learns or is assessed that do not change the content of what is being taught. They provide students with disabilities equal access to the curriculum. The specific accommodations provided will be determined by the student's 504 or IEP committee based on their individual needs.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Reading:
    • Extended time on reading tasks: Allows more time to process written information.
    • Text-to-speech software: Converts written text into spoken words.
    • Audiobooks: Provides access to books in an audio format.
    • Highlighted text: Highlights key information in reading passages.
    • Use of a reading guide or ruler: Helps maintain focus and track reading.
    • Reduced reading load: Provides shorter reading assignments.
  • Writing:
    • Extended time on writing tasks: Allows more time to generate ideas and write.
    • Speech-to-text software: Converts spoken words into written text.
    • Use of a word processor with spelling and grammar check: Provides support with spelling and grammar.
    • Use of graphic organizers: Helps organize thoughts and ideas before writing.
    • Pre-written notes or outlines: Provides a starting point for writing.
    • Scribe: A person who writes down the student's dictation.
  • Spelling:
    • Use of a dictionary or spell checker: Provides support with spelling.
    • Reduced spelling expectations: Focuses on content rather than perfect spelling on certain assignments.
    • Use of multi-sensory techniques for spelling instruction: Helps reinforce spelling patterns through visual, auditory, and kinesthetic methods.
  • Testing:
    • Extended time on tests: Allows more time to complete tests.
    • Quiet testing environment: Reduces distractions during testing.
    • Oral administration of tests: Allows the student to hear the test questions read aloud.
    • Breaks during testing: Provides opportunities to rest and refocus.
  • Classroom Management:
    • Preferential seating: Allows the student to sit in a location that minimizes distractions.
    • Clear and concise instructions: Ensures the student understands the task.
    • Frequent checks for understanding: Helps monitor the student's comprehension.

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations provided will be tailored to the individual student's needs.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia have the right to request an evaluation. The process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Communicate Concerns to the School: The first step is to contact your child's teacher, counselor, or principal to express your concerns about your child's reading, writing, or spelling difficulties. Document your concerns in writing (e.g., email).
  2. Formal Written Request: Submit a formal written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. Clearly state that you are requesting an evaluation for dyslexia. Keep a copy of your request for your records.
  3. Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, including screening results, grades, teacher observations, and any previous evaluations.
  4. Evaluation (if warranted): If the school determines that an evaluation is warranted, they will obtain parental consent and conduct a comprehensive evaluation to determine if the student meets the criteria for dyslexia and requires special education services or a 504 plan. The evaluation must be completed within a reasonable timeframe as defined by state regulations.
  5. ARD or 504 Committee Meeting: Following the evaluation, an ARD (IEP) or 504 committee meeting will be held to discuss the evaluation results and determine the appropriate services and supports for the student. Parents are active participants in these meetings.

Important Considerations:

  • Parent Rights: Parents have the right to request an evaluation at any time.
  • Timeline: Schools are required to adhere to specific timelines for completing evaluations. Inquire about these timelines from the school.
  • Documentation: Keep copies of all correspondence, evaluations, and IEPs or 504 plans.
  • Advocacy: Parents have the right to advocate for their child's needs and seek assistance from advocacy organizations if needed.

Contact Information:

For specific questions about dyslexia services in Fort Bend ISD, please contact your child's school or the FBISD Special Education Department. You can find contact information on the FBISD website.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
