Dyslexia Services in Mesquite ISD

Dyslexia Services in Mesquite ISD

This guide provides an overview of dyslexia services available to students within the Mesquite Independent School District (MISD) in Texas. It outlines key information based on the Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024), mandatory screening procedures, structured literacy programs used, the difference between 504 plans and IEPs for dyslexia, common accommodations, and how to request an evaluation.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024) is the guiding document for all public schools in Texas regarding the identification and instruction of students with dyslexia. It is crucial that parents and educators are familiar with its contents. The Handbook provides comprehensive guidelines on:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The Handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Identification Procedures: The Handbook details the multi-tiered system of supports (MTSS) approach for identifying students at risk for dyslexia. This includes universal screening, progress monitoring, and more intensive evaluations when concerns arise.
  • Instructional Components: It specifies the characteristics of effective dyslexia instruction, including the core components of structured literacy.
  • Parent Involvement: The Handbook emphasizes the importance of collaboration between parents and schools in supporting students with dyslexia.
  • Legal Requirements: It outlines the legal mandates related to dyslexia services in Texas, including requirements under the Texas Education Code and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Use of Technology: The Handbook provides guidance on the use of assistive technology to support students with dyslexia.

Key Updates in the 2024 Handbook: Be sure to review the most recent version of the Handbook as updates are made periodically. Common updates often involve clarifications on screening procedures, instructional methods, and eligibility criteria.

Mandatory Screening

Mesquite ISD, in accordance with Texas law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, implements a mandatory dyslexia screening process for all students. The purpose of this screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and to provide appropriate interventions as early as possible.

  • Grade 1 Screening: All first-grade students are screened for dyslexia.
  • Kindergarten Screening (Optional): While not mandatory, MISD may implement additional screening procedures in kindergarten to identify students who may exhibit early indicators of dyslexia.
  • Screening Components: The screening typically includes measures of phonological awareness (e.g., rhyming, blending, segmenting), phonological memory, rapid automatized naming (RAN), letter knowledge, and decoding skills.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia. The notification will include information about the screening results, recommended interventions, and resources for parents.
  • Follow-Up: Students identified as being at risk through the initial screening receive targeted interventions. Their progress is carefully monitored to determine if further evaluation is needed.
  • Transfer Students: Students transferring into MISD are screened for dyslexia if they have not previously been screened or if there are concerns about their reading abilities. Parents should inform the school of any prior dyslexia diagnoses or interventions.

Structured Literacy Programs

Mesquite ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to teaching reading that focuses on the underlying structure of language. It addresses the core deficits associated with dyslexia by providing intensive instruction in phonological awareness, phonics, spelling, morphology, syntax, and semantics.

While specific program names may vary depending on campus and student needs, the following core components are typically included in the structured literacy programs used by MISD:

  • Phonological Awareness: Activities that develop awareness of the sounds of language, including rhyming, blending, segmenting, and manipulating phonemes.
  • Phonics: Explicit instruction in the relationships between letters and sounds, enabling students to decode words accurately and fluently.
  • Spelling: Instruction in spelling rules and patterns, helping students to encode words correctly.
  • Morphology: Teaching the meaning of morphemes (prefixes, suffixes, and roots) to enhance vocabulary and reading comprehension.
  • Syntax: Instruction in sentence structure and grammar to improve reading comprehension and writing skills.
  • Semantics: Focus on the meaning of words and phrases to develop vocabulary and comprehension skills.

Program Implementation: Structured literacy programs are typically delivered in small groups or individually, providing targeted instruction tailored to the student's specific needs. Progress is monitored regularly to ensure that the intervention is effective.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). Understanding the differences between these two plans is crucial.

  • 504 Plan: A 504 plan is provided under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. It ensures that students with disabilities have equal access to education by providing accommodations and modifications to the learning environment. To qualify for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning, reading, or concentrating. A 504 plan focuses on providing access to the general education curriculum.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program): An IEP is provided under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). It is a legally binding document that outlines the specific educational needs of a student with a disability and the services required to meet those needs. To qualify for an IEP, a student must have one of the specific disabilities listed in IDEA (which includes specific learning disability, such as dyslexia) and require specialized instruction to make progress in their education. An IEP includes measurable annual goals, specific accommodations, modifications, and related services, such as speech therapy or occupational therapy, if needed.

Key Differences:

  • Eligibility: 504 plans have broader eligibility criteria than IEPs. An IEP requires a more significant educational need and a specific disability category under IDEA.
  • Scope of Services: IEPs provide a more comprehensive range of services, including specialized instruction, related services, and measurable goals. 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations.
  • Legal Protections: IEPs offer stronger legal protections under IDEA, including due process rights for parents.
  • Instruction: Students on a 504 plan typically remain in the general education classroom, receiving accommodations. Students with IEPs may receive specialized instruction in a resource room or other setting.

Which is Right for My Child?: The decision of whether a 504 plan or an IEP is appropriate for a student with dyslexia depends on the individual student's needs and the severity of their reading difficulties. If the student requires significant specialized instruction to make progress, an IEP may be necessary. If the student primarily needs accommodations to access the general education curriculum, a 504 plan may be sufficient.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, but they do not change what the student is expected to learn. They level the playing field and allow students with dyslexia to demonstrate their knowledge and skills without being hindered by their reading difficulties. Here are some common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia in Mesquite ISD, whether through a 504 plan or an IEP:

  • Extended Time: Providing extra time on tests and assignments to compensate for slower reading and processing speeds.
  • Read Aloud: Allowing the student to have test questions, reading passages, or other materials read aloud to them.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that converts written text into spoken words.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to audiobooks for reading assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location in the classroom that minimizes distractions.
  • Reduced Workload: Modifying assignments to reduce the amount of reading or writing required.
  • Alternative Assessments: Using alternative assessment methods, such as oral presentations or projects, instead of written tests.
  • Visual Aids: Providing visual aids, such as graphic organizers, charts, and diagrams, to support comprehension.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Incorporating multi-sensory techniques into instruction, such as using manipulatives or movement activities.
  • Scribe: Allowing the student to dictate their answers to a scribe who writes them down.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check and Grammar Check: Allowing the use of technology to assist with writing.
  • Breaks: Providing frequent breaks to reduce fatigue and maintain focus.
  • Highlighting Tools: Allowing the student to highlight important information in texts.
  • Access to Notes: Providing copies of notes or allowing the student to record lectures.

Note: The specific accommodations provided will be individualized based on the student's needs and determined by the 504 committee or IEP team.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading abilities and suspect they may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from Mesquite ISD. Here's how to initiate the process:

  • Contact the School: The first step is to contact your child's teacher, principal, or the school's special education department. Express your concerns and request an evaluation for dyslexia.
  • Written Request: It's best to submit your request in writing. This creates a formal record of your request and ensures that the school is aware of your concerns. Your written request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth.
    • Your contact information.
    • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading abilities.
    • Any relevant information, such as family history of dyslexia or previous interventions that have been tried.
  • Timeline: Once the school receives your written request, they have a specific timeline (outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and IDEA regulations) to respond to your request and determine whether an evaluation is warranted.
  • Evaluation Process: If the school decides to conduct an evaluation, they will administer a battery of tests to assess your child's reading skills, phonological awareness, phonics skills, and other relevant areas.
  • ARD Meeting/504 Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, the school will hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting (for IEP eligibility) or a 504 committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services or a 504 plan. You have the right to participate in these meetings and provide input.
  • Parent Rights: You have specific rights as a parent under IDEA and Section 504. These rights include the right to participate in all meetings, the right to review your child's records, and the right to challenge the school's decisions if you disagree with them. Familiarize yourself with the Texas Parent's Guide to the Admission, Review, and Dismissal Process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
