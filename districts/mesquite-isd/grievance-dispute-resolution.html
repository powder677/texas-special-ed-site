Discipline Rights & Grievances in Mesquite ISD: An Urgent Guide for Parents

This guide is designed to provide parents with essential information regarding student discipline rights and grievance procedures within Mesquite ISD. This is NOT legal advice, but aims to empower you to advocate effectively for your child. Time is often of the essence in these situations; don't delay in seeking assistance if your child is facing disciplinary action.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Understanding Your Child's Rights

Texas law allows schools, including Mesquite ISD, to suspend students for up to three school days for certain violations of the Student Code of Conduct. However, a series of short suspensions can quickly add up and significantly impact your child's education. If your child is facing a suspension that will exceed 10 cumulative school days within a single school year (for any one incident OR cumulatively across separate incidents), it triggers significant procedural protections, particularly for students with disabilities under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Why is the 10-day mark so important? Once a student is suspended for more than 10 days (cumulative), it is considered a "Change of Placement" under IDEA. This means the school district must conduct a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR), discussed in detail below.

What should you do if your child is approaching the 10-day suspension threshold?

  • Track Suspension Days: Keep meticulous records of all suspensions, including the date, reason for the suspension, and duration. Do not rely solely on the school to provide this information.
  • Request Documentation: Immediately request a written explanation of the reason for each suspension. This documentation is crucial for understanding the school's rationale and for building your case if you need to file a grievance or complaint.
  • Contact the School: Schedule a meeting with the principal and, if applicable, the special education administrator, to discuss your concerns. Document everything discussed during the meeting.
  • Consult with an Advocate or Attorney: If your child has a disability, contact a special education advocate or attorney immediately. They can help you understand your rights and navigate the complex legal landscape.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a crucial process mandated by IDEA for students with disabilities who face disciplinary action resulting in a Change of Placement (more than 10 cumulative suspension days). The purpose of the MDR is to determine whether the student's misconduct was caused by or directly and substantially related to their disability, or whether the misconduct was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the student's Individualized Education Program (IEP) or 504 plan.

Who Participates in the MDR? The MDR team typically includes the parent(s), relevant members of the IEP team (e.g., special education teacher, general education teacher), a representative of the school district (e.g., administrator), and any other individuals who have relevant knowledge of the student.

What Happens During the MDR? The MDR team reviews all relevant information, including the student's IEP or 504 plan, teacher observations, and any relevant medical or psychological evaluations. The team asks two key questions:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the LEA's (Local Education Agency, i.e., Mesquite ISD) failure to implement the IEP?

What Are the Outcomes of the MDR?

  • If the misconduct was a manifestation of the disability or resulted from a failure to implement the IEP: The student cannot be disciplined in the same way as a non-disabled student. The IEP team must conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA), implement or revise a behavior intervention plan (BIP), and return the student to their placement (unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement). The district is responsible for addressing the failures in IEP implementation.
  • If the misconduct was not a manifestation of the disability and was not the result of a failure to implement the IEP: The student can be disciplined in the same way as a non-disabled student. However, the school must still provide educational services to the student during the period of suspension or expulsion.

What if you disagree with the MDR decision? You have the right to challenge the MDR decision through due process procedures (discussed below). Do not delay in seeking legal counsel if you disagree with the outcome.

Change of Placement: Understanding the Implications

As mentioned above, a Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 cumulative school days due to disciplinary action. This includes suspensions exceeding 10 days, expulsions, or placements in alternative educational settings (e.g., DAEP - Disciplinary Alternative Education Program). It's vital to understand that even *short* suspensions can accumulate to trigger this right.

What are the implications of a Change of Placement?

  • MDR Requirement: As discussed above, a Change of Placement triggers the requirement for an MDR for students with disabilities.
  • Continued Educational Services: Regardless of whether the misconduct is determined to be a manifestation of the disability, the school district must continue to provide educational services to the student during the period of the removal. This may include alternative instruction, tutoring, or other supports to ensure the student can continue to make progress in their education.
  • Right to Challenge: You have the right to challenge the Change of Placement decision through due process procedures.

Be wary of: Schools pressuring you into *agreeing* to a change of placement to avoid an MDR. Understand your rights and don't be intimidated. Insist on the MDR if your child qualifies.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in Mesquite ISD

If you believe that Mesquite ISD has violated your child's rights or has acted unfairly in a disciplinary matter (regardless of whether your child has a disability), you have the right to file a grievance. Mesquite ISD has a formal grievance policy, typically outlined in the student handbook or on the district's website. The Level 1 grievance is the initial step in this process. Time limits are strict, so act quickly!

Steps to Filing a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Policy: Immediately obtain a copy of Mesquite ISD's grievance policy. This document outlines the specific procedures and timelines for filing a grievance. Look on the district website or contact the school administration.
  2. Prepare Your Grievance in Writing: Your grievance must be in writing and should include the following information:
    • Your name and contact information
    • Your child's name and student ID number
    • A clear and concise description of the issue you are grieving
    • The specific policy or regulation that you believe was violated
    • The date of the incident(s) giving rise to the grievance
    • The remedy you are seeking (e.g., reinstatement, removal of suspension, revision of the IEP)
    • Any supporting documentation (e.g., suspension notices, emails, witness statements)
  3. File Your Grievance: Submit your written grievance to the designated individual specified in the district's grievance policy. This is typically the principal of your child's school. Obtain proof of submission, such as a date-stamped copy of the grievance.
  4. Attend the Meeting: The principal (or designee) is required to investigate the grievance and respond to you within a specified timeframe (usually 10 school days). You may be asked to attend a meeting to discuss your grievance. Prepare thoroughly for this meeting and bring any additional information that supports your case. Consider bringing an advocate or attorney with you.
  5. Keep Detailed Records: Maintain detailed records of all communications, meetings, and documents related to the grievance.

What if you are not satisfied with the Level 1 response? You have the right to appeal the decision to the next level in the grievance process (typically the superintendent or a designated administrator). Again, strict deadlines apply!

TEA State Complaint: An Alternative Avenue

In some situations, filing a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) may be an appropriate course of action, particularly if you believe that Mesquite ISD has violated state or federal laws or regulations, especially related to special education. A TEA complaint can be filed concurrently with or independently of the district's grievance process.

When to Consider Filing a TEA Complaint:

  • Violation of IDEA or 504: If you believe Mesquite ISD has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.
  • Failure to Implement IEP: If you believe the district has failed to implement your child's IEP or 504 plan.
  • Discrimination: If you believe your child has been discriminated against based on their disability.

How to File a TEA Complaint:

TEA provides detailed instructions and a complaint form on their website. The complaint must be in writing, signed, and must include specific information, such as:

  • Your name and contact information
  • Your child's name and student ID number
  • The name and address of the school district
  • A clear and concise description of the problem
  • The facts on which the complaint is based
  • The specific laws or regulations that you believe have been violated
  • Any supporting documentation

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision. If TEA finds that the district has violated state or federal laws, it will order the district to take corrective action. Remember, TEA has specific timelines for receiving and investigating complaints. Be sure to adhere to them.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Recourse

Due process is a formal legal procedure available to parents of students with disabilities under IDEA. It provides a mechanism for resolving disputes with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE). It is a powerful tool but should be considered after exhausting other avenues or if timelines are critical. Due process is adversarial and time-consuming.

When to Consider Due Process:

  • Disagreement with the IEP: If you disagree with your child's IEP and are unable to resolve the issue through informal means.
  • Denial of Services: If you believe your child is being denied necessary special education services.
  • Inappropriate Placement: If you believe your child is placed in an inappropriate educational setting.
  • Disciplinary Actions: If you disagree with a disciplinary action (e.g., suspension, expulsion) and believe it violates your child's rights under IDEA. Especially if the MDR outcome is unfavorable.

Filing a Due Process Complaint:

To initiate due process, you must file a written complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) and provide a copy to Mesquite ISD. The complaint must include specific information, such as:

  • Your name and contact information
  • Your child's name and student ID number
  • A description of the problem
  • The facts on which the complaint is based
  • A proposed resolution to the problem

Resolution Meeting and Hearing:

After a due process complaint is filed, the school district must convene a resolution meeting within 30 days. The purpose of the resolution meeting is to attempt to resolve the dispute. If the dispute is not resolved at the resolution meeting, you have the right to proceed to a due process hearing. The hearing is conducted by an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision based on the evidence presented. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at the due process hearing. The hearing officer's decision is legally binding, but can be appealed to a state or federal court.

Due process is a complex legal process. It is highly recommended that you seek legal counsel from an attorney specializing in special education law if you are considering filing a due process complaint. Time is of the essence!

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
