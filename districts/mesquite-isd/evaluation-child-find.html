Requesting an Evaluation in Mesquite ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Mesquite ISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Mesquite ISD. Understanding the process and your rights is crucial in advocating for your child's needs. This information is based on Texas law and may be subject to change. It is always recommended to consult with legal counsel or disability rights organizations for the most up-to-date advice.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in obtaining a special education evaluation is submitting a written request to Mesquite ISD. A verbal request is not sufficient under Texas law. The request should be clear, concise, and include specific information about your child and your concerns. Here's what you should include:

  • Your Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: This ensures the request is properly linked to your child's educational record.
  • Your Contact Information: Include your phone number, email address, and mailing address so the district can easily reach you.
  • School Name and Grade Level: Specify the school your child attends and their current grade.
  • Specific Concerns: Detail your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or social-emotional development. Provide specific examples of difficulties your child is experiencing. For example, instead of saying "My child is struggling in reading," say "My child has difficulty sounding out words, frequently guesses at words instead of decoding them, and often becomes frustrated during reading activities. They are reading below grade level."
  • Supporting Information: Include any relevant documentation, such as:
    • Report cards showing failing grades or significant drops in performance.
    • Teacher communications detailing concerns about your child's progress.
    • Medical diagnoses or reports from doctors, therapists, or other professionals.
    • Work samples demonstrating academic difficulties.
    • Observations you have made at home that support your concerns.
  • Request for a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE): Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual evaluation to determine if your child has a disability and requires special education services.
  • Your Signature and Date: This validates the request.

Send the request to the appropriate school official. Typically, this is the school principal, the special education director, or the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) chairperson. You can also send a copy to the district's special education department. Keep a copy of the request for your records. Certified mail with a return receipt requested is recommended to ensure the district receives your request and provides proof of delivery. This helps protect your rights in case of delays or disputes.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law establishes a strict timeline for the special education evaluation process. Once the district receives your written request for an evaluation, they have 45 school days to complete several key steps:

  • Review the Request and Existing Data: The district must review your request and any existing data about your child, including academic records, teacher observations, and any information you provided.
  • Decision to Evaluate: The district must decide whether to conduct a full individual evaluation (FIE). They must notify you of their decision in writing within a reasonable time.
  • Consent for Evaluation: If the district decides to evaluate, they must obtain your written consent before proceeding. The consent form will outline the areas of evaluation. You have the right to refuse consent.
  • Evaluation Completion: Once consent is obtained, the district has the remaining time within the 45 school days to conduct the evaluation.
  • ARD Meeting: Within the 45-school-day timeline, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting must be held to review the evaluation results and determine eligibility for special education services. You, as the parent, are a critical member of this ARD committee.

It's essential to understand that the 45 days are school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not included in the count. The timeline begins the day the district receives your written request. Maintain careful records of all communication with the district, including dates of submission, receipt confirmations, and any correspondence. If the district fails to meet the 45-day timeline, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and weaknesses. It must be tailored to your child's individual needs and address all areas of concern that led to the evaluation request. Texas law requires the FIE to be conducted by qualified professionals and to utilize a variety of assessment tools and techniques. The specific areas covered in the FIE may vary depending on your child's needs, but generally include:

  • Cognitive Abilities: This assesses your child's intellectual functioning, including problem-solving skills, reasoning abilities, and memory.
  • Academic Achievement: This evaluates your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. Standardized tests and classroom-based assessments are commonly used.
  • Speech and Language Skills: This assesses your child's communication abilities, including articulation, language comprehension, expressive language, and social communication.
  • Motor Skills: This evaluates your child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Adaptive Behavior: This assesses your child's ability to function independently in daily life, including self-care skills, social skills, and communication skills.
  • Social and Emotional Development: This evaluates your child's emotional well-being, social interactions, and behavior. This may include observations, interviews, and standardized questionnaires.
  • Sensory Processing: This may be included if sensory processing issues are suspected. It assesses how your child processes sensory information from their environment.

The FIE must be comprehensive enough to provide a clear picture of your child's strengths and areas of need. The evaluation team should use multiple sources of information, including parent input, teacher observations, and standardized test results. You have the right to request that specific assessments be included in the FIE if you believe they are necessary to fully understand your child's needs. Ensure that the evaluators are qualified and experienced in assessing children with similar needs to your child. Review the FIE report carefully and ask questions if anything is unclear.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law mandates that school districts have a "Child Find" obligation. This means that Mesquite ISD has a legal responsibility to identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This includes children who are suspected of having a disability, even if they are performing well academically. Child Find aims to ensure that all children with disabilities receive the special education and related services they need to succeed. Several avenues exist to fulfill this obligation, including:

  • Screening Activities: Districts may conduct routine screenings of students to identify potential learning or developmental delays.
  • Teacher Referrals: Teachers are often the first to notice signs of a potential disability and are responsible for referring students for further evaluation.
  • Parent Referrals: As a parent, you have the right to request an evaluation if you suspect your child has a disability. This triggers the district's obligation to respond to your request.
  • Community Outreach: Districts may conduct outreach activities to inform the community about special education services and how to access them.

If you believe your child has a disability but the school has not initiated an evaluation, you can and should submit a written request for an evaluation. The Child Find obligation reinforces your right to have your child evaluated if you suspect a disability, even if the school has not identified any concerns. If the school district fails to fulfill its Child Find obligations, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What If They Refuse

Mesquite ISD may refuse to conduct a full individual evaluation (FIE) if they determine, after reviewing existing data and your request, that there is no reasonable suspicion that your child has a disability. However, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for refusing the evaluation. This written notice is very important, as it provides the basis for your next steps.

If you disagree with the district's decision to refuse the evaluation, you have several options under Texas law:

  • Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to request an IEE at public expense if you disagree with the district's refusal to evaluate. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their refusal to evaluate was appropriate. If the district chooses to initiate a due process hearing, they bear the burden of proof.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the school district meet with a neutral third party to try to resolve the dispute. A mediator can help you and the district communicate effectively and find common ground.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about the dispute. This is a more adversarial process than mediation and may require legal representation. You have the right to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine witnesses presented by the school district.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a complaint with the TEA if you believe the district has violated state or federal special education laws. The TEA will investigate the complaint and take corrective action if necessary.

It is highly recommended that you seek legal advice from an attorney specializing in special education law if the district refuses to evaluate your child. An attorney can help you understand your rights and navigate the legal process. Disability Rights Texas is another valuable resource that can provide information and support.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is completed, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to review the results. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the ARD committee. The ARD committee will determine whether your child meets the criteria for a disability under Texas law and requires special education services. If your child is determined eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports they will receive.

The IEP must include the following:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance: A description of your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic and functional areas.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound goals for your child's progress in each area of need.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the specific services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to help your child access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, while modifications are changes to what a student learns.
  • Placement: A description of the educational setting where your child will receive services. The law requires that children with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.
  • Dates, Frequency, and Location of Services: Specifies when, how often, and where the services will be delivered.

You have the right to participate fully in the ARD committee meeting and to contribute to the development of the IEP. You can disagree with any part of the IEP and request changes. If you and the school district cannot agree on the IEP, you have the right to pursue mediation or a due process hearing. Regularly review your child's IEP and monitor their progress towards their goals. The IEP should be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. Remember that advocating for your child's educational needs is an ongoing process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
