What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is a very important meeting required by a federal law called the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and by Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. If your child is suspected of having a disability that impacts their ability to learn in school, or if they are already receiving special education services, then an ARD committee must convene to determine if your child is eligible for special education and related services, and if so, to develop an Individualized Education Program, often called an IEP. The IEP outlines the specific supports and services that the school district will provide to help your child access the general education curriculum and make progress in school.

The purpose of the ARD meeting is to bring together a team of individuals who have knowledge about your child, including you as the parent, to make decisions about your child's education. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of this team, and your input and perspectives are essential. The ARD committee typically includes you, at least one of your child's general education teachers (if your child is in general education, even part of the time), a special education teacher, a school administrator or other district representative who is knowledgeable about special education services and the general curriculum, an evaluation specialist (someone who can interpret evaluation results), and other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, such as a therapist or counselor. You also have the right to invite anyone you choose to the ARD meeting, such as an advocate or family member. The ARD committee will discuss your child's strengths and areas where they need support, review any relevant evaluations or assessments, and develop an IEP that is tailored to your child's individual needs. This IEP will include specific goals, accommodations, and services that the school district will provide to help your child succeed. The decisions made in the ARD meeting are very important for your child's educational future, so it's essential to be prepared and participate actively in the process.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Under Texas law, Longview ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD committee meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This five-day notice is very important because it gives you time to prepare for the meeting, gather any relevant information, and consider what you want to discuss with the ARD committee. The written notice must include specific information, such as the date, time, and location of the meeting; the purpose of the meeting; a list of the individuals who are expected to attend; and a statement of your parental rights. The notice should also inform you that you have the right to invite other individuals to the meeting, such as an advocate or a family member, who can support you and provide additional input. It's very important to carefully review the notice when you receive it and to contact the school if you have any questions or concerns. If you feel that you need more time to prepare for the meeting, you can request a postponement. You can also waive your right to the five-day notice in writing if you choose to meet sooner. Sometimes, it's more convenient or necessary to have the meeting as soon as possible, and waiving the notice can allow you to do that. However, it's important to make sure that you are comfortable and prepared before waiving your right to the five-day notice.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Being prepared for your child's ARD meeting is key to ensuring their needs are understood and met. Gather any prior evaluations or assessments that have been conducted, whether by the school district or by outside professionals. These documents provide valuable information about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they need support. If your child has seen any doctors or specialists, bring letters or reports from them that describe your child's medical or developmental history, diagnoses, and recommendations. School records such as report cards, work samples, and attendance records can also be helpful in providing a comprehensive picture of your child's academic performance and behavior. Consider creating a written list of your specific concerns and observations about your child's learning, behavior, or social-emotional development. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. It can be overwhelming to remember everything you want to discuss, so writing it down beforehand can be very beneficial. Bring a trusted advocate or support person to the meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you understand the information presented, and ensure that your voice is heard. An advocate can also help you take notes and ask questions. Finally, in Texas, it is legal to record the ARD meeting with your own recording device, but you must inform the other members of the ARD committee that you will be recording the meeting before it begins. This can be helpful for you to refer back to the discussion later and ensure that you have an accurate record of what was said and agreed upon.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides an important safeguard that empowers you, as a parent, to ensure your child's IEP is truly tailored to their needs. This is called the 10-day recess rule. Any member of the ARD committee, including you, has the right to request a recess of the ARD meeting for a period of up to 10 school days. This recess allows time to gather more information, consult with experts, or simply reflect on the information that has been presented before making final decisions. For example, if the ARD committee is considering a specific service or placement that you're unsure about, you can request a recess to research the service further, speak with other parents who have experience with it, or consult with an advocate who can provide guidance. The recess can also be used to obtain additional evaluations or assessments if you believe that the current information is insufficient. To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state that you would like to request a recess of the ARD meeting to gather more information or consult with an advocate. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation or justification for your request. The ARD committee is legally obligated to grant the recess. The recess period begins the next school day and continues for up to 10 school days. During the recess, the ARD committee will not make any final decisions about your child's IEP. The meeting will reconvene after the recess period, and the discussion will continue based on any new information or insights that have been gained. It's important to note that the 10-day recess is a powerful tool that you should not hesitate to use if you feel that you need more time or information to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the written record of the decisions made during the ARD meeting, and it is a legally binding document that outlines the specific supports and services that Longview ISD will provide to your child. It is important to understand each section of the IEP so you can be sure it addresses your child's unique needs. One of the first sections of the IEP is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance," often called "Present Levels." This section describes your child's current academic and functional skills, including their strengths, areas where they need support, and how their disability impacts their ability to participate in the general education curriculum. This section should be based on objective data, such as test scores, classroom observations, and parent input. The IEP will also include "Annual Goals," which are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within a year. These goals should be aligned with your child's present levels and should address their specific needs in areas such as reading, writing, math, behavior, or social-emotional development. The IEP will then list the "Services" that the school district will provide to help your child achieve their annual goals. These services may include special education instruction, related services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling, and supplementary aids and services such as assistive technology or accommodations. "Placement" refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The ARD committee will determine the least restrictive environment (LRE) that is appropriate for your child, meaning that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP will also include a list of "Accommodations" that will be provided to your child in the classroom to help them access the general education curriculum. These accommodations may include things like extended time on tests, preferential seating, or modified assignments. Finally, it's crucial to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and consult with others before making a decision. You should only sign the IEP if you agree with all of the components and believe that it will meet your child's needs.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that as a parent, you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice and perspective are valuable. If you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions, you have several rights. First, you have the right to refuse consent for the initial provision of special education and related services to your child. This means that if you do not believe that your child needs special education, or if you disagree with the proposed IEP, you can refuse to allow the school district to provide those services. Second, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with any of its components. Signing the IEP indicates that you agree with the plan, so if you have concerns or reservations, you should not sign it. Even if you attend the meeting, provide input, and participate in the discussion, you are not obligated to sign if you do not agree. You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the district believes their evaluation was appropriate, they may initiate a due process hearing to defend it; in such a case, the administrative law judge makes the final decision on whether the IEE will be at district expense. Finally, even if you do sign the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document next to any sections with which you have concerns. This indicates that you have reservations about certain aspects of the IEP and that you may want to revisit those issues in the future.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all attempts to resolve your disagreements with Longview ISD through the ARD process, and you still believe that the school district is not meeting your child's needs, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege violations of IDEA or state special education law. The second option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution to your dispute. TEA provides mediation services free of charge. The third option is to request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your dispute. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at the due process hearing, and the school district will also be represented by an attorney. This can be a complex and time-consuming process, so it is important to consider whether it is the right option for you. The fourth option is to file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. Your complaint must allege that the school district has discriminated against your child because of their disability. Each of these options has different timelines and procedures, so it is important to carefully consider your options and seek legal advice if necessary. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process is a valuable resource for understanding your rights and responsibilities in the special education process, and can be found on the TEA website.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
