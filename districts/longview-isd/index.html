Special Education in Longview ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Navigating the world of special education can feel overwhelming, but understanding your rights and the processes involved is crucial for ensuring your child receives the appropriate support. In Texas, special education is governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and state laws, ensuring that students with disabilities receive a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs. This means that Longview ISD, like all school districts in Texas, is obligated to identify, evaluate, and provide services for eligible students with disabilities.

Longview ISD operates under the oversight of the Texas Education Agency (TEA) and receives guidance and support from TEA Region 7 Education Service Center. This regional center provides resources and training to Longview ISD staff, helping them implement best practices in special education. It's important to remember that you, as a parent, are a vital member of your child's educational team. You have the right to participate in all decisions regarding your child's education, from initial evaluation to the development and implementation of their Individualized Education Program (IEP).

Throughout the entire special education process in Longview ISD, you have enforceable legal rights. These rights are designed to protect your child's access to a quality education and ensure that your voice is heard. It is essential to understand these rights so that you can effectively advocate for your child. Remember, you are not alone in this journey. Knowledge is power, and understanding the process is the first step towards ensuring your child receives the support they need to thrive within Longview ISD.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Longview ISD

If you suspect your child may have a disability that is affecting their educational performance, you have the right to request a special education evaluation. This request must be submitted in writing to Longview ISD. A verbal request is not sufficient to start the timeline for evaluation. Once Longview ISD receives your written request and obtains your written consent, the district has 45 school days to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). This evaluation will assess your child's strengths and weaknesses and determine if they meet the criteria for special education services.

It's also important to understand "Child Find," a legal requirement that obligates Longview ISD to proactively identify, locate, and evaluate students who may need special education services. This means that even if you haven't made a formal request, Longview ISD has a responsibility to identify students who may be struggling. However, it is always best to be proactive. To formally begin the process of requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Longview ISD, write a letter to the school principal and the special education director. Clearly state your concerns and request a Full Individual Evaluation. Keep a copy of the letter for your records, and send the original via certified mail with a return receipt requested. This provides proof that Longview ISD received your request and starts the 45-day timeline.

ARD Meetings in Longview ISD: Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. The ARD committee is responsible for making decisions about your child's special education program, including eligibility, goals, and services. Longview ISD is required to provide you with at least five calendar days' written notice before any ARD meeting. This notice should include the purpose of the meeting, who will be attending, and your rights as a parent. It is crucial to attend these meetings and actively participate in the discussion. Your input is invaluable, as you know your child best.

During the ARD meeting, you have the right to ask questions, express your concerns, and propose modifications to your child's IEP. You also have the right to request a ten-day recess, allowing you time to consider the information presented and seek outside consultation if needed. This recess allows you to pause the meeting and gather further information or support before making any decisions. It's important to remember that you are not obligated to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully and consult with others before providing your consent.

The ARD process is designed to be collaborative. Remember, you are a vital member of the team. Your insights and concerns are crucial to developing an IEP that meets your child's unique needs. Before your next ARD meeting in Longview ISD, download our free ARD preparation checklist to ensure you are fully prepared to advocate for your child.

Dyslexia Services in Longview ISD

Texas law mandates that Longview ISD screen all students for dyslexia. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties and ensure they receive appropriate support. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook provides the guidelines and standards that Longview ISD must follow in identifying and serving students with dyslexia. This handbook outlines the procedures for screening, evaluation, and intervention for students with dyslexia.

If your child is identified as having dyslexia in Longview ISD, they are entitled to structured literacy intervention. This intervention is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. Depending on the severity of your child's dyslexia and their individual needs, they may also qualify for a 504 Plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). If your child has not been screened for dyslexia, or if you have concerns about their reading development, request a dyslexia screening in writing from the school principal and special education director at Longview ISD. This will initiate the process of evaluation and, if necessary, intervention.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Longview ISD

Discipline policies and procedures can be particularly complex for students with disabilities. If your child is a special education student in Longview ISD and is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year, this triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This review is a critical process that determines whether your child's misconduct was a manifestation of their disability or a result of Longview ISD's failure to properly implement their IEP. A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative days also constitutes a Change of Placement under IDEA, meaning the ARD committee must convene to determine if a change in your child's educational setting is necessary.

When a suspension triggers an MDR, an ARD meeting must be held promptly. This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face in special education. You must act quickly to protect your child's rights. If the ARD committee determines that your child's behavior was a manifestation of their disability, Longview ISD must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) or revise the existing BIP. It's essential to be prepared for this meeting and understand your rights. If your child has been suspended from Longview ISD, immediately use our Discipline Defense Kit. This kit provides essential information and resources to help you navigate the MDR process and protect your child's rights.

Filing a Complaint Against Longview ISD

If you believe that Longview ISD is not meeting its obligations to your child, you have several options for filing a complaint. It's important to remember that you have these rights regardless of what any staff member at Longview ISD might tell you.

The first option is to file a TEA State Complaint. This is a free process that involves submitting a formal written complaint to the Texas Education Agency. TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Another option is to request Mediation through TEA. This is also a free service that involves a neutral third party facilitating a discussion between you and Longview ISD in an attempt to resolve the issue. If mediation is unsuccessful, you can pursue a Due Process Hearing, which is a more formal legal proceeding where an administrative law judge will hear evidence and make a decision. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe Longview ISD has discriminated against your child based on their disability. Each of these escalation paths has its own procedures and timelines. It's important to choose the option that best suits your specific situation and needs. To learn more about each of these options and access the necessary forms, visit the TEA website and the OCR website.

Frequently Asked Questions About Longview ISD Special Education

The questions every Longview ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Longview ISD? +

Submit a written request to the Longview ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Longview ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Longview ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Longview ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Longview ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Longview ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Longview ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
