Dyslexia Services in Goose Creek CISD

Dyslexia Services in Goose Creek CISD

Goose Creek Consolidated Independent School District (GCCISD) is committed to providing comprehensive support for students with dyslexia. This guide outlines the services available to students who may be experiencing difficulties with reading and spelling. We adhere to the guidelines and procedures outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and provide evidence-based interventions to ensure student success.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024, serves as the guiding document for identification, assessment, and instruction of students with dyslexia in Texas public schools. GCCISD strictly adheres to the principles and procedures detailed in this handbook. Key updates and information from the 2024 Handbook that GCCISD integrates include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Early Identification: The importance of early identification is emphasized throughout the handbook. GCCISD implements universal screening measures to identify students at risk for dyslexia in the early grades (Kindergarten through 1st grade, with ongoing screening for older students as needed).
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The handbook promotes the use of MTSS to provide tiered interventions to students based on their individual needs. GCCISD provides support at various tiers, starting with high-quality classroom instruction and moving to more intensive, individualized interventions for students who require them.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook mandates the use of structured literacy approaches in dyslexia intervention programs. GCCISD uses evidence-based structured literacy programs, described further below.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook highlights the crucial role of parents in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. GCCISD encourages open communication and collaboration with parents, providing them with information, resources, and opportunities to participate in their child's education.
  • Assessment Practices: The handbook outlines specific assessment measures that should be used to evaluate students suspected of having dyslexia. GCCISD employs comprehensive assessment procedures to determine whether a student meets the criteria for dyslexia.
  • Technology Integration: The handbook recognizes the importance of assistive technology in supporting students with dyslexia. GCCISD provides access to a variety of assistive technology tools to help students overcome reading and writing challenges.

Parents are encouraged to review the full Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024, available on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. You can find the most up-to-date version by searching for "Texas Dyslexia Handbook" on the TEA website. This handbook provides comprehensive information about dyslexia, related disorders, and the rights of students with dyslexia in Texas.

Mandatory Screening

GCCISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in Kindergarten and 1st grade. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. The screening process includes assessments of:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds of language. This includes skills like rhyming, segmenting, and blending sounds.
  • Phonological Memory: The ability to hold phonological information in working memory.
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly name a series of familiar objects, colors, letters, or numbers.
  • Letter Identification: The ability to accurately identify letters of the alphabet.
  • Sound-Symbol Correspondence: The ability to match sounds to letters and letters to sounds.

Students who demonstrate difficulties in these areas during the initial screening will be provided with targeted interventions within the general education classroom. Progress monitoring will be conducted regularly to determine the effectiveness of these interventions. If a student continues to struggle despite these interventions, further evaluation for dyslexia may be recommended.

Screening may also occur at other grade levels if teachers or parents have concerns about a student's reading development. It is important to communicate any concerns about your child's reading skills to their teacher or the campus principal.

Structured Literacy Programs

GCCISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide evidence-based instruction to students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to teaching reading and spelling. It focuses on the underlying structure of language, including phonology, orthography, morphology, syntax, and semantics. Our programs emphasize the following principles:

  • Explicit Instruction: Skills are taught directly and systematically, with clear explanations and modeling.
  • Systematic Instruction: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building from simpler to more complex concepts.
  • Cumulative Instruction: Previously learned skills are reviewed and integrated with new skills.
  • Multisensory Instruction: Instruction engages multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance learning and memory.

Examples of structured literacy programs used in GCCISD may include (but are not limited to):

  • Wilson Reading System: A comprehensive, research-based program that addresses the needs of students with significant reading difficulties.
  • Orton-Gillingham Approach: A multisensory, structured approach to teaching reading and spelling. Many variations and programs are based on Orton-Gillingham principles.
  • SPIRE (Specialized Program Individualizing Reading Excellence): A comprehensive, research-based reading intervention program.
  • Fundations: A preventative program used in early grades to establish foundational literacy skills.

The specific program used will depend on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee or the 504 committee.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's specific needs and the impact of dyslexia on their access to the general education curriculum.

Section 504 Plan

A Section 504 plan is designed to provide accommodations and modifications to students with disabilities to ensure they have equal access to the general education environment. A student qualifies for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. For students with dyslexia, a 504 plan may include accommodations such as:

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Preferential seating
  • Use of assistive technology
  • Modified assignments
  • Access to notes or outlines

A 504 plan focuses on leveling the playing field and providing access to the general education curriculum. It does not typically involve specialized instruction.

Individualized Education Program (IEP)

An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the individualized educational needs of a student with a disability who requires specialized instruction and related services. To qualify for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability (SLD), which can include dyslexia, the student must meet specific criteria outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). This typically includes a significant discrepancy between the student's intellectual ability and their academic achievement in reading. The IEP will include:

  • Specific goals and objectives
  • Specially designed instruction
  • Related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy) if needed
  • Accommodations and modifications
  • Progress monitoring procedures

An IEP provides specialized instruction tailored to the student's individual needs and is more intensive than a 504 plan. The ARD committee, which includes parents, teachers, administrators, and other relevant professionals, develops the IEP.

The key difference between a 504 plan and an IEP is the level of support provided. A 504 plan provides accommodations to ensure access to the general education curriculum, while an IEP provides specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.

Accommodations List

The following is a list of common accommodations that may be included in a 504 plan or IEP for students with dyslexia in GCCISD. The specific accommodations provided will be determined based on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the 504 committee or ARD committee:

  • Extended Time: Providing additional time to complete tests, assignments, and reading tasks.
  • Preferential Seating: Seating the student in a location that minimizes distractions and maximizes their ability to focus.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and graphic organizers.
  • Audiobooks: Allowing the student to listen to audiobooks instead of reading printed text.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length, complexity, or format of assignments to meet the student's individual needs.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the student to use a calculator for math problems.
  • Highlighting or Underlining: Allowing the student to highlight or underline key information in texts.
  • Access to Notes or Outlines: Providing the student with copies of notes or outlines.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take frequent breaks to reduce fatigue and maintain focus.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing the student to use a word processor with spell check for writing assignments.
  • Graphic Organizers: Providing the student with graphic organizers to help them organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Dictation: Allowing the student to dictate their responses to a scribe.
  • Reduced Workload: Reducing the amount of work assigned, focusing on key concepts.
  • Visual Aids: Providing visual aids to support learning.

This list is not exhaustive, and other accommodations may be appropriate based on the student's individual needs. The 504 committee or ARD committee will work with the student, parents, and teachers to determine the most effective accommodations.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading development and suspect they may have dyslexia, you should contact your child's teacher or the campus principal. You can also contact the Special Education department at the GCCISD Administration Building.

To request an evaluation, you should submit a written request to the school principal. The request should include the following information:

  • Your child's name and date of birth
  • Your child's school and grade level
  • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading skills
  • Any relevant information about your child's educational history
  • Your contact information

Upon receiving your request, the school will initiate a process to gather information and determine whether an evaluation is warranted. This may involve:

  • Reviewing your child's academic records
  • Observing your child in the classroom
  • Administering informal assessments
  • Consulting with teachers and other professionals

If the school determines that an evaluation is necessary, you will be asked to provide consent. The evaluation will be conducted by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians or licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs). The evaluation will assess your child's reading skills, including phonological awareness, decoding, fluency, and comprehension. The results of the evaluation will be used to determine whether your child meets the criteria for dyslexia.

If your child is identified as having dyslexia, the school will develop a plan to provide them with appropriate support and interventions. This plan may include a 504 plan or an IEP, depending on the student's individual needs. The school will work with you to develop a plan that meets your child's specific needs and helps them achieve their full potential.

We are committed to providing a supportive and inclusive learning environment for all students in GCCISD. If you have any questions or concerns about dyslexia services, please do not hesitate to contact us.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
