How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Goose Creek CISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Goose Creek Consolidated Independent School District (CISD) in Texas. It outlines the process, legal timelines, requirements, and what to do if the district refuses to evaluate. This information is based on Texas state law, specifically the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) implementation in Texas, as detailed in the Texas Education Code (TEC) and Texas Administrative Code (TAC).

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in seeking special education services for your child is to submit a written request for a full and individual evaluation (FIE) to the school. This request should be clear, concise, and addressed to the appropriate school official. Ideally, the request should be sent to the school principal, the special education director, or the campus 504 coordinator. It is crucial to maintain a copy of the request for your records as proof of submission. The written request should include the following information: * **Your child's full name and date of birth.** * **Your child's current grade level and teacher's name.** * **The school your child attends.** * **A statement clearly stating that you are requesting a full and individual evaluation (FIE) for special education services.** * **A description of your child's specific areas of concern.** This is crucial. Be as specific as possible. Describe the academic, behavioral, social, or emotional challenges your child is experiencing. Provide examples. For instance, "My child struggles with reading comprehension and has difficulty following multi-step instructions. He is frequently disruptive in class and has difficulty staying on task. He scores significantly below grade level on reading assessments." or "My child exhibits significant anxiety in social situations and has difficulty making and maintaining friendships. She often refuses to participate in group activities and avoids eye contact. Her academic performance is declining due to her anxiety." * **Any supporting documentation you may have.** This could include report cards, previous evaluations (even if they are private evaluations), medical records, doctor's notes, teacher observations, or any other information that supports your concerns. * **Your contact information, including your phone number and email address.** * **The date of the request.** It is highly recommended to send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides documented proof that the school received your request. Alternatively, you can hand-deliver the request and obtain a signed and dated receipt from the school acknowledging its receipt. Keep all documentation in a safe place. Example Request: [Date] [Principal's Name] [School Name] [School Address] Subject: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Full Name] Dear [Principal's Name], I am writing to request a full and individual evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Full Name], who is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. [Child's Full Name]'s date of birth is [Date of Birth]. I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s [Specific Areas of Concern]. For example, [Provide specific examples]. [Provide specific examples]. I have attached [List any attached documents, e.g., report cards, doctor's notes]. Please contact me at [Your Phone Number] or [Your Email Address] to discuss this further. Thank you for your time and consideration. Sincerely, [Your Full Name]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Under Texas law, once the school district receives your written request for an FIE, they have a specific timeline to follow. The timeline is crucial and understanding it is essential for advocating for your child. The entire process from receipt of your request to the completion of the evaluation and the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting to determine eligibility cannot exceed 45 school days. Weekends, holidays, and school closures (like summer break) are not counted as school days. The 45-school-day timeline includes the following key steps: 1. **Review of Existing Data:** The school district must review existing data regarding your child, including academic records, medical records, teacher observations, and parent input. 2. **Decision to Evaluate:** Within a reasonable time after receiving the request, the school district must decide whether or not to conduct an FIE. This decision is based on the review of existing data and whether there is reason to suspect that the child has a disability and needs special education services. 3. **Notice and Consent:** If the school district decides to conduct an evaluation, they must provide you with a written notice of the proposed evaluation and obtain your informed consent before proceeding. This notice will describe the specific assessments and procedures that will be used in the evaluation. You must sign the consent form before the evaluation can begin. It is vital to carefully review the proposed evaluation plan to ensure it addresses all areas of concern. 4. **Conducting the Evaluation:** Once you provide consent, the school district must conduct the FIE within the remaining time frame of the 45 school days. 5. **ARD Committee Meeting:** After the evaluation is completed, the school district must convene an ARD committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services. The ARD meeting must be held within the 45-day timeline. It is important to note that the 45-school-day timeline is a legal requirement, and the school district must adhere to it. If the district fails to meet the deadline, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Keep meticulous records of all communication and dates to track the timeline effectively.

What the FIE Must Cover

A full and individual evaluation (FIE) must assess your child in all areas related to the suspected disability. The purpose of the FIE is to determine whether your child has a disability and, if so, to identify the specific educational needs of your child. The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals and must use a variety of assessment tools and strategies. The FIE typically includes assessments in the following areas, depending on the suspected disability: * **Academic Achievement:** This includes assessments of reading, writing, math, and other academic skills. * **Cognitive Abilities:** This includes assessments of intellectual functioning, such as IQ tests. * **Language and Communication Skills:** This includes assessments of receptive and expressive language, articulation, and fluency. * **Motor Skills:** This includes assessments of gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, drawing). * **Adaptive Behavior:** This includes assessments of daily living skills, such as self-care, social skills, and communication. * **Social and Emotional Functioning:** This includes assessments of emotional regulation, social interactions, and mental health. * **Speech-Language Pathology:** An evaluation in this area, completed by a licensed Speech-Language Pathologist, will assess for articulation, phonology, voice, fluency, and expressive and receptive language skills. * **Occupational Therapy:** An evaluation in this area, completed by a licensed Occupational Therapist, will assess for fine motor skills, visual motor integration, sensory processing, and self-help skills. The FIE must be comprehensive and individualized to meet your child's specific needs. It should not rely solely on one assessment or test. The evaluation team must consider all relevant information, including parent input, teacher observations, and medical records. The evaluation report must clearly document the assessment procedures used, the results of the assessments, and the team's conclusions and recommendations. The report must also outline how the suspected disability affects the child's ability to access the general education curriculum. You have the right to request that specific assessments be included in the FIE if you believe they are necessary to fully evaluate your child's needs. You also have the right to obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school district's evaluation.

Child Find Obligations

Under IDEA, school districts have a legal obligation known as "Child Find." This means they must actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This includes children who are homeschooled, attending private schools, or are suspected of having a disability but are not yet identified. Goose Creek CISD is responsible for implementing Child Find activities to ensure that all eligible children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE). Parents can play a critical role in Child Find by referring children who they suspect may have a disability to the school district for evaluation. If you suspect that your child has a disability, you do not need to wait for the school district to initiate an evaluation. You have the right to request an FIE at any time. The school district cannot deny your request simply because they have not yet identified your child as potentially having a disability. The Child Find obligation reinforces the parent's right to request an evaluation.

What If They Refuse

If the school district refuses to conduct an FIE, they must provide you with written notice of their refusal. This notice must explain the reasons for their refusal and must inform you of your procedural safeguards under IDEA. The school district cannot simply deny your request without providing a valid reason. Common reasons for refusing to conduct an FIE include: * **Lack of Sufficient Evidence:** The school district may argue that there is not enough evidence to suggest that your child has a disability. * **Academic Difficulties Due to Other Factors:** The school district may argue that your child's academic difficulties are due to factors other than a disability, such as lack of instruction or attendance issues. * **Adequate Progress in General Education:** The school district may argue that your child is making adequate progress in the general education classroom and does not need special education services. If you disagree with the school district's refusal to conduct an FIE, you have several options: 1. **Request a Meeting:** Request a meeting with the school district to discuss your concerns and provide additional information that supports your request for an evaluation. 2. **File a Complaint with TEA:** File a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). TEA will investigate your complaint and determine whether the school district violated IDEA. 3. **Request Mediation:** Request mediation with the school district. Mediation is a process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable solution. 4. **Request a Due Process Hearing:** Request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer will hear evidence and make a decision about whether the school district is required to conduct an FIE. Due process hearings can be complex and require legal representation. 5. **Obtain an IEE:** Pay for a private Independent Educational Evaluation and then submit it to the school district. If the evaluator indicates a disability, this would be further evidence for the need for an FIE. It is important to note that you have the right to legal representation at any stage of this process. If you are considering filing a complaint or requesting a due process hearing, it is highly recommended that you consult with an attorney who specializes in special education law.

After the Evaluation

After the FIE is completed, the school district must convene an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting. The ARD committee includes you as the parent, school administrators, teachers, and evaluation personnel. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to review the evaluation results, determine whether your child is eligible for special education services, and, if so, to develop an individualized education program (IEP). During the ARD meeting, the committee will discuss the following: * **Evaluation Results:** The evaluation team will present the results of the FIE and explain how the results relate to your child's educational needs. * **Eligibility Determination:** The committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under IDEA. * **IEP Development:** If your child is eligible for special education services, the committee will develop an IEP. The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and services. * **Placement Decision:** The committee will determine the appropriate placement for your child, considering the least restrictive environment (LRE). You are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in all discussions and decisions. You have the right to ask questions, provide input, and disagree with the committee's recommendations. It is crucial to actively participate in the ARD meeting and advocate for your child's needs. The IEP must include the following information: * **Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance:** A description of your child's current academic and functional performance. * **Measurable Annual Goals:** Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child's academic and functional progress. * **Special Education and Related Services:** A description of the special education and related services that will be provided to your child, including the frequency, duration, and location of the services. * **Accommodations and Modifications:** A list of the accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom and during assessments. * **Placement:** A description of your child's placement, including the type of educational setting and the amount of time your child will spend in the general education classroom. You have the right to review the IEP and provide your consent before it is implemented. If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to request revisions or to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing. Following these steps and understanding your rights will assist you in navigating the special education evaluation process in Goose Creek CISD. Remember to document all communications and maintain accurate records. Advocate strongly for your child's needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
