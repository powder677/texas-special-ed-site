What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

The ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a cornerstone of special education services for your child in Wichita Falls ISD. It's a meeting specifically designed to create, review, and, if necessary, change your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. This meeting is mandated by both the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, ensuring your child's educational rights are protected. The main purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop a plan tailored to your child's unique learning needs, enabling them to make meaningful progress in school.

So, who attends this important meeting? The ARD committee is a team, and you, as the parent, are a crucial member of that team. Other required members typically include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of special education (often an administrator), your child's special education teacher, and a general education teacher (if your child is, or may be, participating in the general education environment). Depending on your child's specific needs, other individuals may also be invited, such as a diagnostician who conducted assessments, related service providers like speech therapists or occupational therapists, and even your child themselves, particularly as they get older and their input becomes increasingly valuable. You, as the parent, also have the right to invite anyone you wish to the ARD meeting who has knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. Remember, the ARD meeting is designed to be a collaborative process, where everyone works together to create the best possible educational plan for your child. The team will discuss evaluation data, progress (or lack thereof), and determine the appropriate supports, services, and placement to help your child succeed.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Wichita Falls ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This is a crucial right designed to give you ample time to prepare, gather your thoughts and documentation, and arrange for any support you may want during the meeting. This notice isn't just a formality; it must include specific information. It will tell you the purpose of the meeting, such as an annual review, eligibility determination, or consideration of a change in placement. The notice will also state the date, time, and location of the meeting, and list the individuals who will be in attendance. This allows you to know who will be there and to anticipate the areas that will be discussed. The notice should also clearly state that you, as the parent, are invited and encouraged to participate. It's important to read this notice carefully.

However, there are times when a sooner meeting might be beneficial or necessary. You have the option to waive the five-day notice requirement in writing. This means you agree to hold the meeting sooner than the five-day timeframe allows. This is entirely your choice. Perhaps a situation has arisen that requires immediate attention, or the team is readily available to meet earlier. If you feel comfortable and prepared to proceed without the full five days, you can sign a waiver agreeing to do so. But remember, you are never obligated to waive your right to the five-day notice. If you feel rushed or unprepared, it is perfectly acceptable to insist on the full five-day notice period.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting is essential to ensuring your child's needs are fully considered. Here's a practical list of things you should consider bringing with you: First, any prior evaluations or assessments that you have for your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by outside professionals. These could include psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy reports, or any other relevant documentation. These reports provide valuable information about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and specific learning needs. Second, bring any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other medical professionals who work with your child. These documents can provide insights into your child's medical history, diagnoses, and any medical interventions that may impact their education. Third, gather any relevant school records you have, such as report cards, progress reports, work samples, or any notes from teachers or administrators. These documents can help illustrate your child's academic performance and behavior in the school setting.

Fourth, and perhaps most importantly, bring a written list of your concerns and questions. Before the meeting, take some time to reflect on what you want to discuss and what your priorities are for your child's education. Writing down your concerns ensures that you don't forget anything important during the meeting. Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns. Having someone else present can be incredibly helpful in navigating the ARD process. Finally, remember that Texas law allows you to record the ARD meeting, provided that you give notice to the other attendees that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for documenting the meeting and ensuring that you have an accurate record of what was discussed and agreed upon. This can be especially helpful if you need to refer back to the meeting later. Be sure to announce at the beginning of the meeting that you are recording. While bringing all these items may seem daunting, it's about equipping yourself with the information you need to be an active and informed participant in your child's education.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During an ARD meeting, there may be times when you or another member of the ARD committee feels that more information is needed before making a decision. Texas law provides for a "recess" of the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days to allow for the gathering of additional information or for you to consult with an advocate or outside expert. This is a powerful tool that you, as a parent, can utilize to ensure that you have all the information you need to make informed decisions about your child's education. Any member of the ARD committee, including you, the parent, can request this recess.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state during the ARD meeting that you would like to recess the meeting for up to 10 school days to gather more information, consult with an advocate, or for any other valid reason. It's helpful to state specifically what information you intend to gather or who you plan to consult with during the recess. The ARD committee should document your request in the ARD minutes. The meeting will then be paused, and reconvened within those 10 school days. It's important to note that these are school days, not calendar days. This allows time for assessments, consultations, or record gathering. Don't hesitate to use this option if you feel unsure about a decision, if you need more information, or if you simply want to ensure that you're making the best possible choice for your child.

Understanding the IEP Document

The ARD meeting culminates in the creation or revision of your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. This is a legally binding document that outlines your child's unique educational needs and the specific supports and services that the school district will provide to meet those needs. Several key areas are addressed within the IEP. First, the IEP includes a section on present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in various academic and functional areas, based on evaluations, observations, and input from teachers and parents. Second, the IEP outlines annual goals for your child. These goals are measurable, ambitious, and designed to help your child make progress in their areas of need. Third, the IEP specifies the special education and related services that your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. The frequency, duration, and location of these services are also detailed.

Fourth, the IEP addresses your child's placement, which refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a specialized classroom setting. The IEP will also list any accommodations and modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum and participate in school activities. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material, while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn. It is crucial to understand that you, as the parent, do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and consider whether you agree with the plan. You can then sign the IEP at a later date or request another ARD meeting to discuss any concerns you may have. Never feel pressured to sign something you don't fully understand or agree with.

When You Disagree With the Team

While the ARD process is designed to be collaborative, there may be times when you disagree with the decisions made by the ARD committee. It's important to know your rights and how to address these disagreements. First, you have the right to refuse consent for the initial provision of special education services to your child. This means that if you disagree with the team's determination that your child needs special education, you can refuse to allow the school district to provide those services. Second, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with the plan outlined in the document. Refusing to sign the IEP does not necessarily mean that your child will not receive any services, but it does indicate that you have concerns that need to be addressed.

Third, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation, or IEE, at the school district's expense if you disagree with the evaluation conducted by the school district. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the district believes their evaluation was appropriate, they can either fund the IEE or request a due process hearing to defend their evaluation. Fourth, even if you do sign the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document. This indicates that you do not fully agree with the plan, even though you are allowing it to be implemented. This can be important for documenting your concerns and preserving your rights to pursue further action. Remember, your voice matters, and you have the right to advocate for your child's needs, even if it means disagreeing with the ARD team. The district must consider your input.

Escalation Options

If you have tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD team through discussion and collaboration but are still unable to reach a resolution, you have several formal options for escalating your concerns. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Texas Administrative Code (TAC), specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, also known as the Legal Framework. The first option is to file a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law.

The second option is Mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA provides mediation services free of charge. A mediator can help facilitate communication and find common ground. The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a decision on the issues in dispute. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation if you pursue this option. The final option is to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). This is appropriate if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action. It is important to remember that you have the right to advocate for your child's needs, and these escalation options are available to you if you are unable to resolve your concerns through the ARD process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
