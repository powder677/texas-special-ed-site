What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Bastrop ISD! If your child has been identified as potentially needing special education services, you'll be invited to what's called an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This might sound intimidating, but it's simply a meeting where a team of people comes together to discuss your child's educational needs and create a plan to help them succeed. This process is required by federal law under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and is also outlined in the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. Think of it as a collaborative effort to ensure your child receives the support they need to thrive in school.

The purpose of the ARD meeting is multi-faceted. First, it's to determine if your child has a disability that qualifies them for special education services. If so, the team then develops an Individualized Education Program, or IEP. This IEP is like a roadmap, outlining specific goals for your child, the services they will receive, and how their progress will be measured. The meeting also serves as a regular review of your child's progress. At least once a year, the ARD committee meets to discuss whether the IEP is still appropriate, if goals need to be adjusted, or if any changes need to be made to the services being provided. Finally, the ARD committee is responsible for determining when a student no longer needs special education services and is ready for dismissal from the program.

So, who attends these meetings? The law requires certain individuals to be present. This includes you, the parent, as you are a crucial member of the team and your input is incredibly valuable. A representative of the school district, often an administrator or special education director, also attends to ensure the IEP aligns with district policies and resources. A special education teacher, who will be directly involved in your child's education, is also a required member. Your child's general education teacher must attend (or provide written input) if the child is participating in the regular education environment. An evaluation specialist, often called an Educational Diagnostician, also attends to explain the results of any assessments that have been conducted. The school district may also invite other individuals who have knowledge or expertise related to your child, such as a therapist or counselor. You also have the right to invite anyone you believe can contribute to the discussion, such as a trusted family member, friend, or educational advocate. Don't hesitate to bring someone who can help you feel more comfortable and supported during the meeting.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Texas law mandates that Bastrop ISD provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before it is scheduled to take place. This five-day period is designed to give you adequate time to prepare for the meeting, gather any relevant information, and arrange for childcare or other support if needed. This notice is not just a formality; it's a crucial safeguard to ensure you are fully informed and have the opportunity to actively participate in the ARD process.

The written notice must include specific information about the meeting. First and foremost, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting. Is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility for special education, a review of an existing IEP, or a meeting to discuss a specific concern? The notice must also include the date, time, and location of the meeting. It should list the names and titles of the individuals who are expected to attend. The notice must also inform you of your right to invite other individuals to the meeting who have knowledge or expertise regarding your child. Finally, the notice must provide a clear explanation of your parental rights under IDEA and Texas Education Code 29, including your right to participate in all decisions regarding your child's education.

It's important to understand that the five-day notice requirement can be waived, but only with your informed, written consent. If you agree that it's in your child's best interest to hold the meeting sooner, you can sign a waiver indicating that you are giving up your right to the full five-day notice. This might be the case if there is an urgent issue that needs to be addressed or if you simply prefer to resolve matters quickly. However, never feel pressured to waive your rights. If you need the full five days to prepare, don't hesitate to assert your right to the notice period.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your ARD meeting can make a significant difference in ensuring your child's needs are fully addressed. Think of yourself as your child's advocate, and the information you bring will help the team understand your child's strengths, challenges, and what you believe is necessary for their success.

One of the most valuable things you can bring is any prior evaluations or assessments related to your child. This includes evaluations conducted by the school district, as well as any independent evaluations you may have obtained from doctors, therapists, or other specialists. These reports provide valuable data about your child's academic, cognitive, and social-emotional functioning. Also, bring letters from your child's pediatrician or any other medical professionals who have relevant information about your child's disability or health concerns. These letters can help the team understand the medical aspects of your child's needs and how they impact their education.

Gather any school records you have, such as report cards, progress reports, and samples of your child's work. These documents can provide valuable insights into your child's academic performance and areas where they may be struggling. It's also a good idea to prepare a written list of your specific concerns about your child's education. This could include concerns about their academic progress, behavior, social skills, or any other issues that you believe are impacting their ability to learn. Having a written list will help you stay organized and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed during the meeting. You are the expert on your child, so your perspective is important!

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you understand the information being presented, and ensure that your voice is heard. Finally, remember that in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided you inform the other participants that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be helpful for reviewing the meeting later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of what was discussed and agreed upon.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The Texas Education Code provides an important safeguard for parents and other ARD committee members: the 10-day recess rule. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a recess of up to 10 school days before any decisions are finalized regarding your child's IEP. This recess provides an opportunity to gather additional information, consult with experts, or simply take time to process the information that has been presented during the meeting.

The purpose of the 10-day recess is to ensure that all decisions are made thoughtfully and with a complete understanding of the child's needs. You might request a recess if you feel that you need more time to review evaluation reports, consult with a therapist or advocate, or research different service options. Perhaps the team has proposed a placement that you are unsure about, and you want time to visit the proposed classroom or speak with other parents whose children have attended that program. Or perhaps you just feel overwhelmed by the amount of information presented and need time to process it all before making a decision. Whatever the reason, you have the right to request a recess.

Invoking the 10-day recess is simple. During the ARD meeting, simply state that you would like to request a recess of up to 10 school days. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation of your reasons for requesting the recess. The ARD committee is obligated to grant your request. The meeting will then be adjourned and rescheduled to reconvene within 10 school days. During the recess, use the time wisely to gather any additional information you need and to prepare for the next meeting.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program, or IEP, is the cornerstone of your child's special education. It's a legally binding document that outlines your child's present levels of academic and functional performance, establishes measurable annual goals, specifies the services they will receive, determines their educational placement, and lists any necessary accommodations or modifications.

The "present levels" section describes your child's current abilities and challenges in different areas, such as reading, math, writing, communication, and social skills. This section is based on data from evaluations, observations, and input from teachers and parents. The "annual goals" section outlines what your child is expected to achieve within a year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). For example, instead of a vague goal like "improve reading comprehension," a SMART goal might be "increase reading comprehension to the third-grade level by reading and answering comprehension questions with 80% accuracy by the end of the school year."

The "services" section details the specific supports and interventions that your child will receive to help them achieve their goals. This could include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, assistive technology, or other related services. The IEP also specifies the frequency, duration, and location of these services. "Placement" refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their instruction. This could be a general education classroom, a resource room, a self-contained classroom, or a combination of settings. The IEP team will consider your child's individual needs and strengths when determining the most appropriate placement. Finally, the "accommodations" section lists any modifications or supports that will be provided to help your child access the general education curriculum and participate in classroom activities. This could include preferential seating, extended time on tests, or the use of assistive technology.

It is crucial to remember that you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek advice from trusted sources before making a decision. Never feel pressured to sign something you don't fully understand or agree with.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's perfectly normal to disagree with the ARD committee at times. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your perspective is valuable. The law recognizes your right to disagree and provides mechanisms for resolving disagreements.

One of the most fundamental rights you have is the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you do not agree with the team's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can withhold your consent, and the district cannot proceed with the placement. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with any of its provisions. Signing the IEP indicates that you agree with the plan, so if you have concerns, it's important to refrain from signing until those concerns are addressed.

Another important right is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation, or IEE, at the school district's expense. If you disagree with the evaluations conducted by the school district, you can request an IEE from a qualified professional who is not employed by the district. The district is required to pay for the IEE unless they can demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. Even if the district refuses to pay for the IEE, you always have the right to obtain an independent evaluation at your own expense.

Even if you ultimately agree to sign the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document next to any specific section or provision with which you disagree. This statement serves as a formal record of your disagreement and preserves your right to pursue further action if necessary.

Escalation Options

When you've tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through discussion and collaboration, and you still feel that your child's rights are not being protected, there are formal escalation options available to you. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, commonly referred to as the Legal Framework: IDEA 2004.

The first option is to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege that the school district has violated a requirement of IDEA or state special education law. This is a good option when the disagreement centers on systemic issues or failures to follow legal procedures.

The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third-party mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA offers mediation services free of charge to parents of children with disabilities. The mediator facilitates communication and helps the parties explore different options for resolving their dispute. Mediation can be a helpful way to preserve relationships and find creative solutions.

The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal and adversarial process than mediation. In a Due Process Hearing, you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer, who then makes a binding decision based on the law. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a Due Process Hearing. Due Process Hearings can be complex and time-consuming, so it's important to carefully consider whether this is the right option for you.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates allegations of discrimination in federally funded programs and activities. If OCR finds that discrimination has occurred, they can order the school district to take corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
