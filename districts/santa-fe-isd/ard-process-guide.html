What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal meeting, is a very important event for parents of students in Santa Fe ISD who receive special education services. It's a meeting required by both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. Think of it as the central place where decisions about your child's special education program are made. The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, often called an IEP. This document outlines everything your child needs to succeed in school, including goals, services, and accommodations.

Several people will attend your child's ARD meeting. Of course, you, as the parent, are a critical member of the ARD committee. Other attendees typically include a school administrator (or their designee) who is knowledgeable about special education, your child's special education teacher, a general education teacher (if your child participates in general education), an educational diagnostician who can interpret evaluation results, and sometimes related service providers like speech therapists, occupational therapists, or counselors, depending on your child's needs. Your child might also attend, especially as they get older, to participate in the discussion about their own goals and preferences. The ARD committee works together to create the best possible educational plan for your child. It's important to remember that all members of the committee, including you, have equal say in the decisions that are made. The meeting ensures that your child receives a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Santa Fe ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This notification period is designed to give you enough time to prepare for the meeting, gather your thoughts, and collect any relevant documents or information that you want to share with the ARD committee. This written notice isn't just a courtesy; it's a requirement under IDEA and Texas law. The notice must include several important pieces of information. First, it must state the purpose of the meeting. For example, it might say that the purpose is to develop a new IEP, review an existing IEP, or consider evaluation results. It must also specify the date, time, and location of the meeting. Critically, the notice will also list the anticipated attendees. This allows you to see who will be at the table making decisions about your child's education. Finally, the notice should inform you of your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, including related service personnel as appropriate. This is very important. Don't hesitate to invite someone who can support you at the meeting.

If you would like to meet sooner than the five-day notice allows, you can waive your right to the full notice period. This must be done in writing. Perhaps the school has availability for a meeting sooner, and you are available as well. Waiving the notice period is entirely your choice, and you should only do so if you feel comfortable and prepared to participate in the ARD meeting on short notice. If the district attempts to pressure you into waiving your rights to the 5-day notice, be firm and explain that you need that time to prepare.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is essential for advocating effectively for their needs. Consider bringing several key items to help facilitate a productive discussion. First, gather any prior evaluations, assessments, or testing reports related to your child's academic performance, cognitive abilities, or any specific areas of concern. This includes not only school-based evaluations but also reports from outside professionals, such as psychologists, therapists, or medical doctors. These reports provide valuable data and insights that can inform the development of your child's IEP. If your child has been seen by any medical professionals or specialists, bring any letters or reports they have provided. These letters can provide important medical context for your child's educational needs.

Gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, or work samples. These documents can illustrate your child's strengths and areas where they may be struggling. You should also prepare a written list of your specific concerns about your child's education. Be as clear and specific as possible when articulating these concerns. For example, instead of saying "My child is struggling in math," try saying "My child is struggling with multi-step word problems in math and needs additional support in this area." Having a written list will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, a friend, a special education advocate, or an attorney. Having someone else present can provide emotional support, help you take notes, and offer a different perspective during the discussion. In Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, as long as you provide reasonable notice to the school district that you plan to do so. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for accurately documenting the discussions and decisions made during the meeting. Make sure that you inform the ARD committee at the beginning of the meeting that you will be recording.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a very important safeguard called the "10-Day Recess Rule". This allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a temporary pause, or "recess," in the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days. The purpose of this recess is to allow time to gather additional information, seek clarification on specific issues, or consult with an advocate or other expert before making final decisions about your child's IEP. The 10-day recess provides a crucial opportunity to avoid feeling rushed or pressured into making decisions without having all the necessary information.

If at any point during the ARD meeting you feel uncomfortable, uncertain, or like you need more time to consider the information being presented, you can invoke the 10-Day Recess Rule. Simply state clearly that you are requesting a recess in the meeting for up to 10 school days. You don't need to provide a lengthy explanation, although you may choose to briefly explain why you are requesting the recess. For example, you could say, "I am requesting a 10-day recess to review the evaluation data more closely and consult with an advocate." Once you invoke the recess, the ARD committee must pause the meeting and reschedule it to resume within 10 school days. This recess is your right, and the school district cannot deny your request. Use this time wisely to gather information, seek support, and prepare to advocate effectively for your child's needs when the ARD meeting reconvenes.

Understanding the IEP Document

The ARD meeting culminates in the creation or revision of your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. This document is the cornerstone of your child's special education services and outlines their educational needs, goals, and the specific supports and services that will be provided to help them succeed. There are several key components to understand within the IEP document. "Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance" describe your child's current skills, strengths, and areas where they need improvement. This section is based on evaluation data, teacher observations, and parent input.

The IEP will also include annual goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The IEP will also list the specific services that your child will receive, such as special education instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or assistive technology. The amount of time and frequency of these services will also be specified. The "placement" section describes where your child will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom with support, in a resource room, or in a self-contained special education classroom. Accommodations are changes to the way your child learns or demonstrates their knowledge, without changing the content of what they are being taught. Examples include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology.

Importantly, you do not have to sign the IEP document at the ARD meeting itself. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and consider whether you agree with the goals, services, and placement outlined in the document. You can sign it at a later date once you are comfortable with the contents.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly normal and acceptable to disagree with the ARD committee about aspects of your child's IEP. Remember that you are an equal member of the team, and your input is valuable and essential. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you disagree with the school's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can decline to give your consent, and your child will not be placed in special education. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with the goals, services, or placement outlined in the document. Simply stating that you are not signing the IEP indicates that you do not agree with the contents.

If you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child's needs, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide a different perspective on your child's needs and can be used to inform the development of the IEP. If you choose to sign the IEP document but still disagree with certain aspects of it, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" next to the specific sections with which you disagree. This indicates that you are not in agreement with those particular components of the IEP, even though you are signing the document overall. This documents your disagreement for the record. You do not need to explain why you disagree in the document, but you should keep a separate record of the specific issues.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions and the 10-Day Recess Rule, and you still feel that your child's needs are not being adequately addressed, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These rights are governed by the Legal Framework for the Child Centered Special Education Process based on IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89.

The first option is to file a formal State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or Texas special education law. Another option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also free and can be a helpful way to resolve disagreements without resorting to more formal legal proceedings.

If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer to pursue a more formal legal route, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is an administrative hearing conducted by an impartial hearing officer who is not employed by the school district. The hearing officer will hear evidence from both sides and issue a written decision. Due Process Hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is generally recommended to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that your child has been discriminated against on the basis of their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
