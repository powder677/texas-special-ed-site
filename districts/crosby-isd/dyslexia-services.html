Dyslexia Services in Crosby ISD

Dyslexia Services in Crosby ISD

Crosby ISD is committed to providing comprehensive support for students with dyslexia. This guide outlines the services available within the district, in accordance with Texas regulations and best practices.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Crosby ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This handbook serves as the foundation for dyslexia identification, intervention, and support in Texas public schools. The 2024 version includes updates and clarifications to ensure consistent and effective implementation of dyslexia services. Parents are encouraged to review the handbook, which is available on the TEA website (search "Texas Dyslexia Handbook"). Key areas covered in the handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia
  • Identification Procedures
  • Structured Literacy Interventions
  • Progress Monitoring
  • Parent Communication and Involvement
  • Legal Requirements

Crosby ISD staff receive ongoing professional development on the Texas Dyslexia Handbook to ensure they are up-to-date on the latest research and best practices.

Mandatory Screening

Texas Education Code mandates universal dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. Crosby ISD conducts these screenings as required, using TEA-approved screening instruments. The screening process assesses foundational reading skills, including phonological awareness, letter knowledge, and rapid naming. Parents will be notified if their child's screening results indicate a potential risk for dyslexia. Further evaluation may be recommended based on the screening results. Screening is a brief, initial assessment, and a positive screening result does not automatically mean a child has dyslexia, but rather signals the need for further investigation.

Structured Literacy Programs

Crosby ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. These programs are explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multisensory, addressing the core deficits associated with dyslexia. These programs typically include:

  • Phonological Awareness: Instruction in recognizing and manipulating sounds in spoken language.
  • Phonics: Explicit teaching of letter-sound relationships.
  • Fluency: Practice reading connected text to improve reading speed and accuracy.
  • Vocabulary: Direct instruction in word meanings.
  • Reading Comprehension: Strategies for understanding and retaining information from text.

Interventionists are trained in delivering these programs with fidelity. Progress is monitored regularly to ensure students are making adequate progress. Specific program names used in Crosby ISD may vary by campus; please contact your child's school for more information on the specific program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) through an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

  • Section 504: Provides accommodations and modifications to ensure students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum. Eligibility is based on a disability that substantially limits a major life activity, such as learning.
  • IEP (IDEA): Provides specialized instruction and related services to meet the unique needs of students with disabilities who qualify under one of the 13 disability categories outlined in IDEA, including Specific Learning Disability (SLD), which may encompass dyslexia. An IEP includes goals, objectives, and specific services tailored to the student's individual needs.

The determination of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and evaluation results. A student who requires significant, intensive intervention to address their dyslexia may be eligible for an IEP under the SLD category. A 504 plan may be appropriate for students who need accommodations to access the general curriculum but do not require specially designed instruction.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional methods that help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning. Common accommodations include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time for tests and assignments.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Providing access to auditory versions of text.
  • Audiobooks: Using audiobooks as a supplement to reading.
  • Graphic Organizers: Providing visual aids to help organize thoughts and information.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Oral Testing: Allowing students to answer questions orally.
  • Use of a Calculator: Providing a calculator for math assignments.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a distraction-free testing location.

This is not an exhaustive list, and accommodations are determined based on individual student needs. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee or 504 committee will determine appropriate accommodations for each student.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, you should contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to discuss your concerns. A written request for evaluation is recommended. The request should include your specific concerns and any supporting documentation you may have, such as previous assessments or observations. Crosby ISD will then follow established procedures to determine if further evaluation is warranted. The evaluation process will involve a comprehensive assessment of reading, writing, and spelling skills, as well as cognitive abilities. Parental consent is required before any evaluation is conducted. Following the evaluation, the school will convene a meeting to discuss the results and determine if the student is eligible for dyslexia services. Parents are active participants in this process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
