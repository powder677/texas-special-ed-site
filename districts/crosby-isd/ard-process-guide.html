What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is a meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, Section 29, to determine whether your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) if your child is eligible, and to review and revise that IEP at least annually. Think of it as your child's educational planning session. It's a collaborative process where a team, including you as the parent, works together to create a plan to help your child succeed in school. The purpose is to ensure your child receives a Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) that is tailored to their individual needs.

The ARD committee is composed of several key people. You, as the parent, are a vital member of this team and your input is invaluable. Other members typically include a school administrator (or their designee), a special education teacher, a general education teacher, an evaluation specialist (someone who can interpret evaluation results), and, when appropriate, your child. Related service providers, such as speech therapists, occupational therapists, or counselors, may also attend if their expertise is needed to discuss your child's needs. The school district's representative must be qualified to provide or supervise the provision of specially designed instruction to meet the unique needs of children with disabilities, be knowledgeable about the general curriculum, and be knowledgeable about the availability of resources of the district. These are the people who will make decisions about your child's education, so it's important to understand their roles and how they contribute to the process.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Crosby ISD is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This gives you time to prepare and gather any information you think is important to share with the team. The notice must include several key pieces of information. First, it will tell you the purpose of the meeting - for example, to determine eligibility for special education, to develop an IEP, or to review an existing IEP. Second, it will list the date, time, and location of the meeting. Third, it will identify who will be attending the meeting, including the names and titles of school personnel. Fourth, it will inform you of your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. Finally, the notice will explain your rights as a parent in the special education process.

It's important to carefully review the notice and make sure you understand all the information. If you have any questions, don't hesitate to contact the school to ask for clarification. If you feel you need more time to prepare, you can request to reschedule the meeting. However, if you are comfortable meeting sooner, you can waive the 5-day notice requirement in writing. This might be helpful if you and the school agree on a quick resolution to an issue or if you want to address a concern as soon as possible.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to the ARD meeting is crucial for advocating for your child. Think of yourself as the expert on your child, and bring any information that will help the team understand their strengths, needs, and challenges. Start by gathering any prior evaluations or assessments, whether they were conducted by the school, a private therapist, or a medical professional. These documents can provide valuable insights into your child's learning style, academic progress, and any specific disabilities they may have.

If your child has been diagnosed with any medical conditions or receives any medical treatment, bring letters from their doctors outlining their condition, treatment plan, and any recommendations for accommodations or modifications in the school setting. Also, collect any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and attendance records. These records can give the team a picture of your child's academic performance and behavior over time. Before the meeting, take some time to write down a list of your specific concerns about your child's education. What are you worried about? What do you hope the ARD team will address? Having a written list will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that you don't forget to raise important issues.

It's perfectly acceptable, and often helpful, to bring a trusted advocate or support person to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, a friend, or a professional advocate who is knowledgeable about special education laws and procedures. Your advocate can provide emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns and goals for your child. Finally, you are legally allowed to audio record the ARD meeting in Texas, as long as you provide the school with reasonable notice that you will be doing so. This can be helpful for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a valuable tool for parents during the ARD process: the 10-day recess rule. This rule states that any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, can request a recess of the meeting for up to 10 school days. This recess allows time to gather additional information, consult with experts or advocates, or simply to process the information discussed during the meeting before making final decisions. You might invoke this rule if new information is presented at the meeting that you need time to consider, if you feel pressured to make a decision without fully understanding the implications, or if you want to seek outside advice before agreeing to the IEP.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state that you are requesting a recess of the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation of why you are requesting the recess, although it's often helpful to give the team a general idea of what you plan to do during the recess. The ARD committee is legally obligated to grant your request. Once the recess is over, the ARD meeting will reconvene to continue the discussion and make final decisions about your child's IEP.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the written record of the decisions made at the ARD meeting. It's a comprehensive plan that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the services they will receive to meet those goals. Understanding the different sections of the IEP is essential for ensuring that your child receives the appropriate support. One of the most important sections is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" (Present Levels). This section describes your child's current academic and functional abilities, including their strengths and areas where they need support. It should be based on a variety of sources of information, such as evaluations, teacher observations, and your input as the parent.

The IEP will also include annual goals, which are measurable statements of what your child is expected to achieve in one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). The IEP will also specify the services your child will receive, such as special education instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling. It will also outline the frequency, duration, and location of these services. Placement refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom with support, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered or the way a student is assessed. They are designed to help students with disabilities access the general education curriculum without changing the content of what is being taught. Examples include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and the use of assistive technology.

It is crucial to remember that you, as the parent, do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and consult with others before making a decision. If you have any questions or concerns about the IEP, don't hesitate to contact the school to ask for clarification or to request a follow-up meeting.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD team on certain aspects of their child's IEP. You have several rights and options available to you if you find yourself in this situation. First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you disagree with the team's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can refuse to allow them to be placed in a special education program. Second, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document. This does not mean that your child will not receive any services. It simply means that you do not agree with all of the decisions made in the IEP. The school district is still obligated to provide your child with a Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE), but they may need to seek alternative methods to implement the IEP.

Another important right is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. You can request an IEE if you disagree with the results of the school's evaluation of your child. The school district must either grant your request or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. Finally, even if you ultimately sign the IEP document, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document next to any areas where you have concerns. This ensures that your disagreement is documented and preserved for future reference.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD team through informal discussions, you have several formal options available to you. These options are designed to help you advocate for your child's rights and ensure that they receive a Free and Appropriate Public Education. These rights are governed by the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process: IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a resolution within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third-party mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also free through TEA and can be a helpful way to resolve disputes without resorting to more formal legal proceedings. The third option is to request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about your child's special education needs. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option.

Finally, you have the right to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability, race, color, national origin, sex, age, or religion. It is important to remember that these are your rights as a parent, and you have the power to advocate for your child's educational needs. Don't hesitate to seek assistance from advocacy organizations, legal professionals, or TEA if you need help navigating the special education process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
