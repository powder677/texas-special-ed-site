Dyslexia Services in Laredo ISD: A Comprehensive Guide

This guide provides an overview of dyslexia services available within the Laredo Independent School District (LISD), in accordance with Texas state law and guidelines. It is intended to inform parents, educators, and students about the identification, evaluation, and support systems in place for students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024: Key Updates and Information

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024 by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the primary resource for understanding and addressing dyslexia in Texas schools. Laredo ISD adheres to the principles and practices outlined in this handbook. Key areas covered in the handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede the growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook provides guidance on screening, evaluation, and diagnosis of dyslexia. It emphasizes a multi-tiered system of supports (MTSS) approach, with increasingly intensive interventions provided to students who are struggling.
  • Instructional Practices: The handbook advocates for the use of structured literacy approaches, which are explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multi-sensory. These approaches focus on phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and reading comprehension.
  • Legal Requirements: The handbook details the legal obligations of school districts in Texas regarding dyslexia, including screening, evaluation, instruction, and accommodations. It references the Texas Education Code (TEC) §38.003 and the Texas Administrative Code (TAC) §74.28.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the importance of collaboration between schools and parents in supporting students with dyslexia. It includes information on parents' rights and responsibilities.
  • Updates in the 2024 Handbook: The 2024 update includes refinements to screening procedures, clarification on the use of specific assessments, updated information on effective instructional practices, and strengthened guidance on supporting students with dyslexia in secondary settings. Specific attention is paid to the importance of reading comprehension instruction alongside decoding.

Important Note: It is crucial for parents and educators to familiarize themselves with the most recent version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, available on the TEA website. Changes and updates are made periodically to reflect current research and best practices.

Mandatory Screening for Dyslexia

Laredo ISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening for students in kindergarten through second grade, as required by Texas law. The purpose of screening is to identify students who are at risk for dyslexia early on, allowing for timely intervention and support. The screening process typically involves:

  • Universal Screening: All students in kindergarten, first, and second grade participate in universal screening.
  • Screening Measures: Screening tools assess phonological awareness (e.g., rhyming, blending, segmenting), letter-sound knowledge, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and other foundational reading skills. LISD utilizes TEA-approved screening instruments.
  • Tiered Approach: Students who demonstrate difficulties on the initial screening may receive further screening or targeted intervention to determine if a more comprehensive evaluation is warranted.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia based on screening results. The notification will include information about intervention options and further evaluation procedures.

Beyond second grade, screening may be conducted at any grade level if a teacher, parent, or other school personnel suspects a student may be experiencing difficulties consistent with dyslexia. Such referrals are carefully considered and investigated.

Structured Literacy Programs in Laredo ISD

Laredo ISD is committed to providing evidence-based, structured literacy instruction to students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multi-sensory approach to teaching reading. Key characteristics of structured literacy programs include:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions about prior knowledge.
  • Systematic Instruction: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building from simpler to more complex concepts.
  • Cumulative Instruction: Previously learned skills are reviewed and reinforced to ensure mastery.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Learning engages multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance understanding and retention.
  • Focus on Key Components: Instruction covers phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and reading comprehension.

LISD utilizes specific structured literacy programs that align with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and the Science of Reading. These programs are implemented by trained teachers and dyslexia specialists. Specific program names may vary depending on the campus and the individual student's needs. Parents can inquire about the specific program being used with their child by contacting the campus principal or special education department.

504 Plan vs. IEP for Dyslexia: Understanding the Differences

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under either a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). Understanding the differences between these two plans is crucial:

  • Section 504 Plan:
    • Legal Basis: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.
    • Eligibility: Students with a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading).
    • Focus: Provides accommodations to ensure equal access to education.
    • Services: Accommodations such as extended time, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
    • Evaluation: Evaluation is conducted to determine if the student has a disability that substantially limits a major life activity.
  • Individualized Education Program (IEP):
    • Legal Basis: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
    • Eligibility: Students with a specific learning disability (such as dyslexia) who require specially designed instruction to access the general education curriculum.
    • Focus: Provides specially designed instruction and related services to address the student's unique needs.
    • Services: Includes specialized instruction, accommodations, related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy), and modifications to the curriculum.
    • Evaluation: Requires a comprehensive evaluation by a multidisciplinary team to determine eligibility and identify specific educational needs.

Key Differences Summarized: A 504 plan focuses on accommodations to provide equal access, while an IEP focuses on providing specially designed instruction to address a student's specific learning needs. A student with dyslexia may qualify for either a 504 plan or an IEP, depending on the severity of their learning difficulties and the level of support required.

Accommodations List for Students with Dyslexia

Accommodations are changes to the way a student learns, without changing the content of what they learn. They are designed to provide students with dyslexia with equal access to the curriculum and to help them demonstrate their knowledge. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia in Laredo ISD include:

  • Reading Accommodations:
    • Extended time on reading assignments and tests.
    • Text-to-speech software.
    • Audiobooks.
    • Preferential seating near the teacher.
    • Visual aids and graphic organizers.
    • Chunking of text.
    • Use of a reading guide or ruler.
  • Writing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on writing assignments and tests.
    • Use of assistive technology such as speech-to-text software.
    • Alternative methods of assessment (e.g., oral reports, presentations).
    • Pre-writing organizers and outlines.
    • Use of a word processor with spell check and grammar check.
    • Reduced writing workload.
  • Spelling Accommodations:
    • Use of a spell checker.
    • Acceptance of phonetic spelling on first drafts.
    • Focus on content rather than perfect spelling.
    • Use of a personal spelling list.
  • Testing Accommodations:
    • Extended time.
    • Small group testing.
    • Read aloud (when permissible).
    • Use of assistive technology.
    • Breaks during testing.
  • Classroom Management Accommodations:
    • Clear and concise instructions.
    • Visual schedules.
    • Frequent checks for understanding.
    • Positive reinforcement and encouragement.
    • Opportunities for movement and breaks.

This is not an exhaustive list, and specific accommodations will be determined based on the individual student's needs and documented in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request an Evaluation for Dyslexia in Laredo ISD

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia can request an evaluation from Laredo ISD. The process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Contact the School: Reach out to your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to express your concerns.
  2. Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation. This request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a detailed description of your concerns about their reading and spelling skills. Be specific about the difficulties you have observed.
  3. Response from the School: The school will review your request and determine if an evaluation is warranted. They will notify you of their decision in writing.
  4. Evaluation Process: If an evaluation is approved, the school will conduct a comprehensive evaluation to assess your child's reading, spelling, and language skills. This evaluation may include standardized tests, observations, and interviews.
  5. Evaluation Report: Following the evaluation, you will receive a written report summarizing the findings. The report will indicate whether or not your child meets the criteria for dyslexia.
  6. ARD/504 Meeting: If your child is identified as having dyslexia, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee (for IEP) or a 504 committee will be convened to develop an individualized plan of support.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
