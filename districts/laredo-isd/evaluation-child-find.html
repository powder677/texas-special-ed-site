Requesting an Evaluation in Laredo ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Laredo ISD: A Guide for Parents

This guide provides information for parents in Laredo Independent School District (LISD) who believe their child may need special education services. It outlines the process of requesting a special education evaluation, understanding your rights under Texas law, and navigating the procedures involved. It's crucial to remember that you are your child's strongest advocate, and understanding these processes is the first step in ensuring they receive the support they need.

How to Submit a Written Request

The most important first step is submitting a written request for a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE). This puts the school district on notice that you suspect your child may have a disability requiring special education. The request should be clear, concise, and dated. While there is no specific form required, the letter should contain the following information:

  • Your child's full name, date of birth, and school.
  • Your name, address, phone number, and email address.
  • A clear statement that you are requesting a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) for special education services. For example, "I am writing to formally request a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) to determine if my child, [Child's Name], is eligible for special education services."
  • A brief explanation of your concerns regarding your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental difficulties. Be specific and provide examples. For example, "I am concerned that [Child's Name] is significantly behind his peers in reading. He struggles with decoding words and has difficulty comprehending written text. He is also exhibiting signs of frustration and anxiety when faced with reading tasks." Or, "My child has been displaying disruptive behaviors in the classroom, including difficulty staying seated, frequent outbursts, and problems following directions. This is impacting his ability to learn and is also affecting other students in the classroom."
  • Include any supporting documentation that you believe is relevant, such as report cards, teacher communication, doctor's reports, or previous evaluations. You can state in your letter that these documents are attached.
  • Consider including specific areas you want the evaluation to cover based on your concerns. For instance, "I am specifically requesting that the evaluation assess [Child's Name]'s reading skills, writing skills, and math skills, as well as his cognitive abilities and any potential learning disabilities."

Keep a copy of the request letter for your records. You can hand-deliver the request to the school principal or the school's special education director. When hand-delivering, ask for a signed and dated receipt acknowledging that they received the letter. Alternatively, you can send the letter via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school district received your request.

After submitting the written request, it's a good practice to follow up with the school within a week to confirm they received it and to inquire about the next steps in the evaluation process.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Under Texas law (specifically the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, IDEA, as implemented in Texas through state statutes and regulations), Laredo ISD has a specific timeline to follow after receiving your written request for an evaluation. This timeline is 45 school days, not calendar days. This means that weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not included in the 45-day count.

The 45-school-day timeline begins on the date the school district receives your written request. Within this timeframe, the following must occur:

  • Review of Existing Data (REED): The school district must conduct a Review of Existing Evaluation Data (REED). This involves gathering and reviewing all existing information about your child, including academic records, teacher observations, medical records (if provided), and previous evaluations.
  • Decision Regarding Evaluation: Based on the REED, the school district must decide whether to conduct an evaluation. They must provide you with written notice of their decision. If they decide not to evaluate, they must explain their reasons in writing and inform you of your right to dispute their decision (see the section on "What If They Refuse").
  • ARD Committee Meeting: If the school district decides to proceed with an evaluation, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting must be held to determine the scope of the evaluation. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the ARD committee. During this meeting, the committee will discuss your concerns, review the existing data, and decide which specific areas of your child's development and academic performance will be assessed.
  • Evaluation: The school district must conduct the agreed-upon evaluation. This involves administering various tests and assessments to your child by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians, psychologists, speech-language pathologists, and occupational therapists, depending on the areas being assessed.
  • Evaluation Report (FIE): The evaluators must prepare a comprehensive Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) report. This report will summarize the results of the evaluation, provide an interpretation of the findings, and make recommendations regarding your child's eligibility for special education services and the types of supports and services that may be appropriate.
  • ARD Committee Meeting to Discuss Evaluation Results: Another ARD committee meeting must be held to discuss the results of the FIE. During this meeting, the evaluation team will present their findings, and the committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law. If your child is found eligible, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) to address your child's unique needs.

It's important to keep track of the 45-school-day timeline. If the school district fails to meet the deadline, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine whether a child has a disability that requires special education and related services. The specific areas covered in the FIE will depend on the individual child's needs and the concerns raised in the initial request and during the ARD committee meeting to determine the scope of the evaluation. However, a comprehensive FIE typically includes assessments in the following areas:

  • Cognitive Abilities: These assessments evaluate a child's intellectual functioning, including their reasoning abilities, problem-solving skills, and information processing speed. Commonly used tests include the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC) and the Stanford-Binet Intelligence Scales.
  • Academic Achievement: These assessments measure a child's academic skills in areas such as reading, writing, and mathematics. They can identify specific areas of academic weakness and determine whether a child is performing at grade level. Examples of achievement tests include the Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Achievement and the Wechsler Individual Achievement Test (WIAT).
  • Adaptive Behavior: These assessments evaluate a child's ability to function independently in daily life. They measure skills such as self-care, communication, social skills, and community living skills. The Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales is a commonly used assessment in this area.
  • Communication Skills: If there are concerns about a child's communication skills, the FIE will include assessments of their receptive language (understanding language) and expressive language (using language). This may involve tests of articulation, fluency, vocabulary, grammar, and pragmatics (social use of language).
  • Motor Skills: If there are concerns about a child's motor skills, the FIE may include assessments of their gross motor skills (large muscle movements, such as running and jumping) and fine motor skills (small muscle movements, such as writing and using scissors).
  • Social-Emotional Functioning: These assessments evaluate a child's emotional and behavioral functioning. They may include observations, interviews with parents and teachers, and standardized questionnaires to assess for anxiety, depression, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), and other emotional or behavioral disorders.
  • Sensory Processing: If there are concerns about sensory processing difficulties, the evaluation may include assessments of how a child processes sensory information, such as touch, sound, sight, and movement.
  • Assistive Technology Needs: The evaluation should consider whether the child requires assistive technology to access the curriculum and participate in school activities.

The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in administering and interpreting the specific assessments used. The evaluators must use a variety of assessment methods, including standardized tests, observations, interviews, and review of records, to obtain a comprehensive understanding of the child's strengths and weaknesses. The FIE report must be written in clear and understandable language and must provide specific recommendations for addressing the child's needs.

Child Find Obligations

Laredo ISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under Child Find to identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within the district's boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This includes children who are homeschooled or attend private schools. This responsibility extends to children suspected of having a disability, even if the school district has not received a specific request for an evaluation.

If you suspect that your child has a disability that may require special education services, you can contact Laredo ISD's special education department or your child's school to express your concerns. The school district is obligated to investigate your concerns and take appropriate steps to determine whether an evaluation is warranted. They cannot simply dismiss your concerns without conducting a thorough review.

Child Find also requires the school district to conduct public awareness activities to inform parents and community members about the availability of special education services. This may include distributing information through local media, hosting workshops, and collaborating with community organizations.

If you believe that Laredo ISD is not fulfilling its Child Find obligations, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What If They Refuse

Laredo ISD may refuse to conduct a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) if they believe that there is not sufficient evidence to suggest that your child has a disability that requires special education services. However, they must provide you with written notice of their refusal and explain their reasons for the denial. This notice must also inform you of your right to challenge their decision. This written notice is known as Prior Written Notice.

If you disagree with the school district's decision to refuse an evaluation, you have several options:

  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the school district meet with a neutral third party (a mediator) to attempt to resolve your dispute. The mediator helps to facilitate communication and explore possible solutions.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence and arguments to a hearing officer, who will make a decision about whether the school district is required to conduct an evaluation. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a complaint with the TEA if you believe that the school district has violated your child's rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The TEA will investigate your complaint and take appropriate action if they find that the school district has violated the law.

It's important to remember that you have the right to advocate for your child's needs. If you believe that your child needs a special education evaluation, don't be afraid to challenge the school district's decision if they refuse to conduct one. Document everything - every conversation, meeting, and piece of communication. This documentation will be crucial if you need to pursue mediation, a due process hearing, or a complaint with the TEA.

After the Evaluation

After the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is completed, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to discuss the results. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of this committee. The evaluation team will present their findings, and the committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law.

If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education services, they will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's educational goals, the specific services and supports they will receive, and how their progress will be measured. The IEP must be tailored to meet your child's unique needs and must be reviewed and revised at least annually. The IEP is a legally binding document, and the school district is obligated to implement it as written.

Even if the ARD committee determines that your child is not eligible for special education services, they may still be eligible for accommodations under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. Section 504 prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in programs and activities that receive federal funding. If your child has a disability that substantially limits one or more major life activities, they may be entitled to accommodations under Section 504, even if they do not qualify for special education services.

Remember, the special education process is a collaborative one. It's important to work closely with the school district to ensure that your child receives the appropriate supports and services to reach their full potential. Don't hesitate to ask questions, express your concerns, and advocate for your child's needs every step of the way. If you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions, you have the right to pursue mediation, a due process hearing, or a complaint with the TEA. Your involvement is key to your child's success.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
