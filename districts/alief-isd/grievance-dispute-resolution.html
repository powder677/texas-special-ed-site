Dispute & Discipline Rights in Alief ISD

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: When Suspension Becomes More Than Just a Few Days

In Alief ISD, as in all Texas school districts, students can be suspended for disciplinary infractions. A single suspension is generally less concerning than a pattern of suspensions or one lasting longer than a few days. But the key trigger is 10 cumulative school days of suspension in a school year. This is where things get complicated, especially if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Why is 10 days important? Because once a student with an IEP is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, a Change of Placement may be considered. This is NOT just a simple suspension; it can drastically alter your child's educational environment and potentially lead to a more restrictive setting. Alief ISD cannot just accumulate suspensions without considering if the behavior is a manifestation of your child's disability. This is where your active involvement becomes paramount.

What you need to know:

  • Track those days: Keep meticulous records of every suspension, noting the dates and reasons. Don't rely solely on the school to do this. Verify their records.
  • Demand an IEP Meeting: If your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 days of suspension, IMMEDIATELY demand an IEP meeting to discuss the situation. Make this request in writing (email is acceptable but keep a copy) and keep proof of delivery (read receipts, etc.).
  • Focus on Manifestation: During the IEP meeting, the critical issue is whether the behavior leading to the suspension is a manifestation of your child's disability. This means: did the disability cause or contribute to the behavior?
  • Documentation is Key: Bring any documentation to the IEP meeting that supports your claim that the behavior is disability-related. This could include: medical records, psychologist reports, therapist notes, past IEP goals addressing the behavior, and examples of how the school failed to implement the IEP.
  • If the behavior is NOT a manifestation: Your child can be disciplined in the same way as students without disabilities. However, the school is still required to provide educational services during the suspension to ensure your child continues to participate in the general curriculum, although in another setting.

What Is an MDR (Mandatory Disciplinary Removal)?

An MDR, or Mandatory Disciplinary Removal, is a more serious consequence than a typical suspension. It typically involves removing a student from their regular classroom and placing them in a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP) or even expulsion. MDRs are usually reserved for severe offenses, such as:

  • Bringing a weapon to school
  • Selling or distributing drugs
  • Assaulting a school employee
  • Repeated serious violations of the student code of conduct.

MDR and Students with IEPs: The same 10-day rule and manifestation determination applies to MDRs. If the MDR is considered a Change of Placement (see below), a manifestation determination review MUST occur. This is your chance to argue that your child's actions were a direct result of their disability, preventing a potentially damaging consequence like DAEP placement or expulsion.

Important Considerations During an MDR:

  • Review the Evidence: Demand to see ALL evidence the school is using to justify the MDR. This includes witness statements, incident reports, and any other relevant documentation.
  • Independent Evaluation: If you believe the school is misinterpreting your child's behavior, consider obtaining an independent educational evaluation (IEE) from a qualified professional. This evaluation can provide valuable evidence to support your claim that the behavior is disability-related. (Note: You may have to pay for this out of pocket, but it can be a worthy investment.)
  • Consider Legal Representation: Facing an MDR can be overwhelming. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law. They can advocate for your child's rights and navigate the legal complexities.

Change of Placement: A Game Changer You Must Understand

A Change of Placement occurs when a disciplinary action significantly alters your child's educational environment. This could involve:

  • Moving your child to a more restrictive classroom
  • Placing them in a DAEP
  • Expulsion

When is it a Change of Placement? Generally, a Change of Placement occurs if a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or when a pattern of removals constitutes a pattern of exclusion.

The Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is Crucial: As mentioned above, whenever a Change of Placement is considered for a student with an IEP due to disciplinary reasons, a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) MUST be conducted by the IEP team. This is not optional; it is a legal requirement under IDEA.

During the MDR, the IEP team must determine:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to the child's disability? OR
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the Alief ISD's failure to implement the IEP?

If the answer to either of these questions is YES (meaning the behavior WAS a manifestation):

  • The IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) if one hasn't already been conducted, and implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP).
  • If a BIP is already in place, the IEP team must review and modify it, as necessary, to address the behavior.
  • The student MUST return to the placement from which they were removed, unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the BIP.
  • The MDR cannot be used to sidestep implementing an existing BIP or creating a new one.

What happens if the IEP team decides the behavior was NOT a manifestation? You have the right to challenge that decision through due process (see below).

Filing a Level 1 Grievance: Your First Step in Challenging District Decisions

If you disagree with a disciplinary decision made by the school or district, the first step is often to file a Level 1 grievance. This is a formal written complaint submitted to the school principal. It's crucial to follow Alief ISD's specific grievance policy, which should be available on their website or upon request. Typically, the process involves:

  1. Obtaining the Grievance Form: Get the official Alief ISD grievance form. Don't use a generic letter.
  2. Writing a Clear and Concise Complaint: Clearly state the disciplinary action you are challenging, why you believe it was unfair or violated your child's rights, and the specific remedy you are seeking. Be specific. Cite dates, times, names, and relevant details. Don't be emotional; focus on facts and applicable laws (e.g., IDEA violations).
  3. Gathering Supporting Documentation: Include any relevant documents, such as suspension notices, incident reports, IEP documents, or emails.
  4. Submitting the Grievance: Submit the grievance form to the school principal within the timeframe specified in Alief ISD's policy (usually a short window - often within 10 school days of the incident). Keep a copy for your records, and confirm receipt by the principal.
  5. Receiving a Response: The principal is required to investigate and respond to your grievance within a specified timeframe (again, consult Alief ISD's policy).

What if the Level 1 grievance is unsuccessful? You can appeal to the next level, typically the superintendent's office or a designated grievance committee. Each level has its own deadlines and procedures, so be sure to follow them meticulously.

TEA State Complaint: Taking Your Concerns to the State Level

If you believe Alief ISD has violated IDEA or other special education laws, you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a grievance and can trigger a state investigation.

Key requirements for filing a TEA State Complaint:

  • Timeline: The complaint must typically be filed within one year of the alleged violation.
  • Specificity: The complaint must clearly describe the alleged violation and the specific facts that support your claim. You must cite the specific legal requirements that you believe have been violated.
  • Supporting Documentation: Include any relevant documents, such as IEPs, evaluations, correspondence with the school, and grievance responses.
  • Contact Information: Provide your contact information and the contact information for the school and district personnel involved.

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision. If TEA finds that Alief ISD violated the law, they can order the district to take corrective action, such as providing compensatory education, revising policies, or providing staff training.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Legal Recourse

Due Process is the most formal and legally binding avenue for resolving disputes with Alief ISD regarding your child's special education. It's essentially a legal hearing before an impartial hearing officer. Due process should be considered after other methods of dispute resolution (like grievances and TEA complaints) have been exhausted, or if you believe the situation is critical and requires immediate legal intervention.

Key aspects of Due Process:

  • Legal Representation: Due process is a complex legal process. You should absolutely seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law.
  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: You must file a formal due process complaint with TEA, outlining the specific issues you are disputing and the relief you are seeking.
  • Resolution Meeting: Before a due process hearing, TEA typically requires a resolution meeting between you and the district to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  • Due Process Hearing: If the resolution meeting is unsuccessful, a due process hearing will be held. You and the district will present evidence and witnesses to the hearing officer, who will issue a final decision.
  • Legal Binding Decision: The hearing officer's decision is legally binding on both you and the school district. If you disagree with the decision, you can appeal it to state or federal court.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
