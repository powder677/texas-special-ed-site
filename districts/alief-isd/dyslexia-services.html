Dyslexia Services in Alief ISD

Dyslexia Services in Alief ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Alief Independent School District (ISD) in Texas. It covers key aspects of identification, support, and legal frameworks related to dyslexia in Texas public schools.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is the cornerstone document guiding dyslexia identification and intervention in Texas public schools, including Alief ISD. The 2024 update is the most current version and outlines required procedures and best practices. It's crucial for parents, educators, and administrators to be familiar with its content.

Key Updates and Considerations from the 2024 Handbook:

  • Emphasis on Early Identification: The Handbook stresses the importance of identifying students at risk for dyslexia as early as possible, ideally in Kindergarten and first grade. Early intervention is significantly more effective.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): Dyslexia services are delivered within an MTSS framework. This means students receive increasingly intensive support based on their needs. Tier 1 includes high-quality classroom instruction for all students. Tier 2 provides targeted interventions for students who need additional support. Tier 3 offers intensive, individualized support for students with diagnosed dyslexia.
  • Structured Literacy Programs: The Handbook mandates the use of structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. These programs are evidence-based and specifically designed to address the core deficits associated with dyslexia. More details on structured literacy are provided in a later section.
  • Assessment Procedures: The Handbook outlines specific procedures for screening and diagnosing dyslexia. These procedures involve a comprehensive evaluation of a student's reading, spelling, and writing skills, as well as phonological awareness, phonemic awareness, rapid naming, and other relevant cognitive skills.
  • Parent Involvement: The Handbook emphasizes the importance of parent involvement in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. Parents have the right to be informed about their child's progress and to participate in decision-making.
  • Training Requirements: The Handbook specifies training requirements for teachers and other staff members involved in providing dyslexia services. This training ensures that educators have the knowledge and skills necessary to effectively support students with dyslexia.
  • Technology Integration: The Handbook acknowledges the role of assistive technology in supporting students with dyslexia. Assistive technology can help students access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge in alternative ways.

Accessing the Texas Dyslexia Handbook:

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 is available for free download on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. It is essential to consult this official document for the most accurate and up-to-date information.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law requires all public schools, including those in Alief ISD, to screen students for dyslexia. These screenings are conducted to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and who may benefit from early intervention.

Screening Procedures in Alief ISD:

  • Kindergarten and First Grade: All students in Kindergarten and first grade are screened for dyslexia risk factors. This screening typically occurs during the fall of each school year.
  • Ongoing Screening: Students in other grades may be screened if they are exhibiting signs of reading difficulties. Teachers and parents can request a screening if they have concerns.
  • Screening Tools: Alief ISD uses TEA-approved screening tools to assess students' skills in areas such as phonological awareness, phonemic awareness, letter-sound knowledge, and rapid naming.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia. The notification includes information about the screening results and recommendations for further evaluation or intervention.

What to Expect During Screening:

The dyslexia screening is a brief assessment that is typically administered individually or in small groups. The screening is not intended to diagnose dyslexia, but rather to identify students who may need further evaluation. Parents should be informed about the screening process and have the opportunity to ask questions.

Structured Literacy Programs

Structured Literacy is a research-based approach to reading instruction that is specifically designed to address the needs of students with dyslexia. The Texas Dyslexia Handbook mandates the use of Structured Literacy programs in Texas public schools.

Key Principles of Structured Literacy:

  • Systematic and Explicit Instruction: Instruction is systematic and explicit, meaning that skills are taught in a logical sequence and are clearly explained.
  • Phonological Awareness: Phonological awareness is a key focus. Students learn to identify and manipulate the sounds in spoken words.
  • Phonics: Students learn the relationships between letters and sounds. They learn to decode words by sounding them out.
  • Fluency: Students develop reading fluency through practice and repetition.
  • Vocabulary: Students learn the meaning of words.
  • Comprehension: Students learn strategies for understanding what they read.
  • Multisensory Approach: Instruction often incorporates multisensory techniques, engaging visual, auditory, and kinesthetic modalities.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) through an Individualized Education Program (IEP). Understanding the differences between these two frameworks is crucial.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Focus: Ensures that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (such as learning).
  • Services: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to help students succeed in the general education classroom. These accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or access to assistive technology.
  • IEP Not Required: A 504 plan does not require an IEP.
  • Dyslexia and 504: Many students with dyslexia who do not require specialized instruction beyond accommodations can be appropriately supported through a 504 plan.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Focus: Provides specially designed instruction to meet the unique needs of students with disabilities.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have one of the 13 disabilities listed in IDEA (including Specific Learning Disability, which can encompass dyslexia) and require specially designed instruction to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Services: An IEP includes specific goals, objectives, and services to address the student's individual needs. These services may include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, or occupational therapy.
  • Comprehensive: An IEP is a more comprehensive plan than a 504 plan.
  • Dyslexia and IEP: Students with dyslexia who require intensive, specialized instruction (often within a Structured Literacy program) to make adequate progress are more likely to require an IEP.

Key Differences Summarized:

Feature Section 504 Plan Individualized Education Program (IEP)
Focus Equal access to general education Specially designed instruction
Eligibility Impairment substantially limits a major life activity Meets IDEA criteria for a disability and requires specially designed instruction
Services Accommodations and modifications Specific goals, objectives, and services, including specialized instruction
Legal Framework Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)

Determining the Appropriate Plan:

The decision of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and specialists. Parents are also an important part of this team. The team will consider the student's individual needs and determine the level of support that is necessary for the student to succeed.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way a student learns, but they do not change the content of what the student is learning. Accommodations are designed to help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia in Alief ISD:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Seating the student in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that reads text aloud to the student.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to audiobooks.
  • Graphic Organizers: Using graphic organizers to help the student organize their thoughts.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing the student with notes or allowing them to record lectures.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Alternative Assessments: Allowing the student to demonstrate their knowledge in alternative ways (e.g., oral presentations, projects).
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the use of a calculator for math assignments.
  • Spelling Exceptions: Grading spelling based on effort rather than accuracy. (This should be carefully considered and documented.)
  • Use of a Keyboard or Computer: Allowing the student to type assignments instead of handwriting them.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet, distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Chunking Information: Breaking down large assignments into smaller, more manageable chunks.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids, such as pictures, diagrams, and charts, to support learning.
  • Access to Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology, such as screen readers, voice recognition software, and word prediction software.

Important Note: The specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will depend on their individual needs. The accommodations should be clearly documented in the student's 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, it is important to request an evaluation. Here are the steps to take in Alief ISD:

  1. Contact Your Child's Teacher: The first step is to discuss your concerns with your child's teacher. The teacher can provide valuable insights into your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or 504 Coordinator: If you continue to have concerns after speaking with the teacher, contact the school counselor or the 504 coordinator. These individuals can provide information about the district's dyslexia evaluation procedures and can help you initiate the evaluation process.
  3. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for a dyslexia evaluation to the school principal or the Special Education Department. The request should include your child's name, grade level, school, and a detailed explanation of your concerns. Be specific about the difficulties your child is experiencing with reading, spelling, and writing. Also, mention any family history of dyslexia or learning disabilities.
  4. Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, such as report cards, standardized test scores, and classroom work samples, to determine if an evaluation is warranted.
  5. Evaluation Process: If the school determines that an evaluation is necessary, a team of professionals will conduct a comprehensive evaluation of your child's skills. This evaluation may include assessments of reading, spelling, writing, phonological awareness, phonemic awareness, rapid naming, and other relevant cognitive skills.
  6. Evaluation Report: You will receive a written report summarizing the results of the evaluation. The report will indicate whether or not your child meets the criteria for dyslexia.
  7. Eligibility Determination: If your child is diagnosed with dyslexia, the school will convene a meeting to determine eligibility for services under Section 504 or IDEA. You will be invited to participate in this meeting.
  8. Development of a 504 Plan or IEP: If your child is eligible for services, a 504 plan or IEP will be developed to address their individual needs. You will be actively involved in the development of this plan.

Key Considerations:

  • Document Everything: Keep copies of all correspondence and documents related to your child's evaluation and services.
  • Advocate for Your Child: You are your child's best advocate. Don't be afraid to ask questions and express your concerns.
  • Understand Your Rights: Familiarize yourself with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and your rights under Section 504 and IDEA.

Disclaimer: This guide provides general information about dyslexia services in Alief ISD. It is not intended to be a substitute for professional advice. For specific questions or concerns, please contact your child's school or the Alief ISD Special Education Department.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
