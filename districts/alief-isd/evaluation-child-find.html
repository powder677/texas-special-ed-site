How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Alief ISD: A Comprehensive Guide

This guide outlines the process for requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Alief ISD, focusing on Texas-specific laws and regulations. Understanding your rights and responsibilities is crucial to ensuring your child receives the support they need to succeed academically. This guide covers submitting a written request, the mandated timeline, the scope of the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE), the district's Child Find obligations, what to do if your request is denied, and what happens after the evaluation is completed.

How to Submit a Written Request

The process begins with a formal, written request for a special education evaluation. This letter serves as official documentation of your concerns and initiates the legal timeline for the evaluation process. It's crucial to be thorough and clear in your communication. The written request should include the following information: * **Your Child's Full Name and Date of Birth:** Accurate identification is essential. * **Your Child's Current School and Grade Level:** This helps the district locate your child's records. * **Your Contact Information:** Include your phone number, email address, and mailing address. Provide multiple methods of contact. * **Specific Concerns Regarding Your Child's Academic, Behavioral, or Emotional Development:** This is the most crucial part of the letter. Be detailed and specific about the difficulties your child is experiencing. Instead of saying "my child is struggling in reading," provide specific examples like "my child has difficulty sounding out words, struggles to comprehend grade-level text, and consistently scores below average on reading assessments." Describe behavioral concerns with specifics such as "my child frequently disrupts the classroom, has difficulty following instructions, and has been sent to the office multiple times for behavioral issues." Documenting these issues provides evidence for the need for an evaluation. * **Any Interventions or Supports Your Child Has Already Received:** If your child has received tutoring, counseling, or other interventions, document them. Include dates, duration, and the effectiveness of these interventions. For example, "My child received tutoring in math for three months. While there was some initial improvement, her performance has plateaued, and she is still significantly behind her peers." * **Copies of Relevant Documents:** Attach copies of report cards, standardized test scores, teacher notes, medical reports, or any other documentation that supports your concerns. Do not send originals; keep copies for your records. * **A Clear Statement Requesting a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE):** State explicitly that you are requesting a Full and Individual Evaluation to determine if your child is eligible for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). * **Date and Signature:** Ensure the letter is dated and signed by you. It is highly recommended that you send the letter via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school district received your request. Keep a copy of the letter and the return receipt for your records. You should address the letter to the school principal and the special education director for Alief ISD, if possible. Sending it to both ensures it reaches the appropriate personnel.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once the school district receives your written request for an evaluation, a specific timeline is triggered under Texas law and IDEA. Alief ISD has a maximum of 45 school days to complete the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. It is crucial to understand this timeline and hold the district accountable for adhering to it. Here's a breakdown of the timeline: * **Receipt of the Written Request (Day 0):** The 45-school-day clock starts on the day the district receives your written request. * **Notice and Consent for Evaluation (Within a Reasonable Time):** The district must provide you with a written notice of their intent to evaluate your child. This notice should describe the evaluations they plan to conduct and explain your parental rights, including the right to obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense under certain circumstances. You must provide your written consent for the evaluation to proceed. The district cannot begin the evaluation without your consent. It is important to read this notice carefully and ask questions if anything is unclear. * **Evaluation Completion (Within 45 School Days):** The district must complete the evaluation within 45 school days of receiving your consent. This includes conducting all necessary assessments, gathering information from teachers and other relevant professionals, and preparing the written evaluation report. * **ARD Meeting (Within the 45 School Days):** The Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting must be held within the 45 school days to review the evaluation results, determine your child's eligibility for special education services, and, if eligible, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). It is important to note that the 45-school-day timeline refers to instructional days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not included in the calculation. If the district anticipates needing more time to complete the evaluation, they must inform you in writing and provide a valid justification for the delay. You have the right to disagree with the extension and insist that the evaluation be completed within the original 45-school-day timeline. If the district fails to meet the deadline without a valid reason and your consent, you have grounds to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability and, if so, to identify the specific educational needs resulting from that disability. Texas law and IDEA require the FIE to be thorough and address all areas of suspected disability. The FIE must include: * **Assessment in All Areas of Suspected Disability:** The evaluation must assess all areas where your child is experiencing difficulties. If you suspect a learning disability in reading and writing, the FIE must include comprehensive assessments of reading fluency, reading comprehension, spelling, and written expression. If you suspect an emotional disturbance, the FIE must include assessments of your child's emotional and behavioral functioning, including observations in multiple settings. * **Use of Multiple Assessment Tools and Strategies:** The evaluation should not rely solely on a single test or assessment. The evaluators must use a variety of methods to gather information, including standardized tests, informal assessments, observations, interviews with the child, parents, and teachers, and review of existing records. * **Evaluation by Qualified Professionals:** The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in assessing children with disabilities. The specific professionals involved will depend on the areas of suspected disability. For example, a licensed specialist in school psychology (LSSP) may conduct psychological evaluations, while a speech-language pathologist may evaluate communication skills. * **Consideration of the Child's Educational, Developmental, and Medical History:** The evaluation must consider your child's past experiences, including their academic history, developmental milestones, and any medical conditions that may be affecting their learning or behavior. You should provide the evaluation team with all relevant information and documentation. * **Documentation of Strengths and Needs:** The FIE must not only identify your child's weaknesses but also document their strengths and areas of proficiency. This information is crucial for developing an effective IEP. * **Recommendations for Educational Programming:** The FIE must include specific recommendations for educational programming, including accommodations, modifications, and specialized instruction that will address your child's unique needs. The FIE report should be written in clear and understandable language. You have the right to receive a copy of the FIE report at least five school days before the ARD meeting. Review the report carefully and ask questions if anything is unclear or if you disagree with the findings.

Child Find Obligations

The "Child Find" mandate is a crucial component of IDEA. It requires all school districts, including Alief ISD, to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within their boundaries who may be in need of special education and related services, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or are homeschooled. This applies to children from birth through age 21. Alief ISD has a legal obligation to: * **Raise Public Awareness:** The district must conduct outreach activities to inform parents, community members, and professionals about the availability of special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation. * **Establish Referral Procedures:** The district must have clear and accessible procedures for referring children who are suspected of having a disability for evaluation. This includes accepting referrals from parents, teachers, doctors, and other professionals. * **Provide Evaluation Services:** The district must provide timely and comprehensive evaluations to children who are referred for evaluation. * **Protect Confidentiality:** The district must protect the confidentiality of student records and information related to the evaluation process. If you believe that Alief ISD is not fulfilling its Child Find obligations, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Examples of failures to meet Child Find obligations could include a pattern of delaying or denying evaluations, failing to adequately inform the public about special education services, or failing to follow established referral procedures.

What If They Refuse

If Alief ISD refuses to conduct a special education evaluation after you have submitted a written request, you have several options: * **Request a Written Explanation:** Request a written explanation from the district detailing the reasons for their refusal. This explanation must be provided within a reasonable timeframe and should be based on objective data and observations. * **Request Mediation:** You can request mediation with the district to attempt to resolve the dispute informally. Mediation is a voluntary process that involves a neutral third party who helps the parties reach a mutually agreeable solution. * **File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA):** You can file a formal complaint with the TEA. TEA will investigate the complaint and determine whether the district has violated IDEA or state special education law. * **Request a Due Process Hearing:** You have the right to request a due process hearing, which is a formal legal proceeding before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will hear evidence from both sides and issue a decision. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at the due process hearing. * **Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at Public Expense:** If you disagree with the district's decision not to evaluate, or if you disagree with the results of a district-conducted evaluation, you may have the right to obtain an IEE at public expense. The district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. It is important to document all communications with the district and to keep copies of all relevant documents. Seeking legal advice from an attorney specializing in special education law is highly recommended if the district refuses to conduct an evaluation or if you are unsure of your rights.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is complete, the next step is the Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting. This meeting is crucial for determining your child's eligibility for special education services and, if eligible, developing an Individualized Education Program (IEP). At the ARD meeting: * **The Evaluation Results Will Be Reviewed:** The evaluation team will present the findings of the FIE and explain the implications of the results. * **Eligibility Will Be Determined:** The ARD committee will determine whether your child meets the criteria for one of the 13 disability categories under IDEA and whether the disability adversely affects their educational performance. * **If Eligible, an IEP Will Be Developed:** If your child is found eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's educational goals, the specific services and supports they will receive, and how their progress will be measured. * **Parental Input is Crucial:** Parents are equal members of the ARD committee and have the right to actively participate in all decisions. Your input is essential for developing an IEP that meets your child's unique needs. The IEP should include the following components: * **Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance:** A description of your child's current academic and functional performance, including their strengths and weaknesses. * **Measurable Annual Goals:** Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child's academic and functional progress. * **Special Education and Related Services:** A description of the special education and related services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. * **Accommodations and Modifications:** A list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to help your child access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material (e.g., extended time on tests, preferential seating), while modifications are changes to what a student is expected to learn (e.g., reduced assignments, simplified materials). * **Placement:** A description of the educational setting where your child will receive special education services. The goal is to educate children with disabilities in the least restrictive environment (LRE). * **Progress Monitoring:** A description of how your child's progress toward their goals will be measured and reported to you. You have the right to disagree with any part of the IEP. If you disagree, you can request another ARD meeting, request mediation, or file a complaint with the TEA. It is important to remember that the IEP is a living document that can be reviewed and revised as needed to meet your child's evolving needs. Regularly communicate with your child's teachers and therapists and attend all ARD meetings to ensure that the IEP is effectively addressing your child's needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
