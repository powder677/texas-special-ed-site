Special Education in Georgetown ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Navigating special education can feel overwhelming, but you are not alone. Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas law, children with disabilities are entitled to a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs. This means your child has the right to receive specialized instruction and related services to help them succeed in school. In Georgetown ISD, this commitment extends to all eligible students from early childhood through graduation.

Georgetown ISD, like all school districts in Texas, is overseen by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). TEA provides guidance and support to districts to ensure compliance with federal and state special education laws. Additionally, Georgetown ISD benefits from the resources and expertise of TEA Region 13 Education Service Center, which offers professional development, technical assistance, and other services to support students with disabilities. Remember, the special education process is designed to be collaborative, involving parents, educators, and other professionals working together to create the best possible educational plan for your child.

Crucially, throughout this entire process, you, as a parent, have enforceable legal rights. These rights are designed to ensure that you are an active and informed participant in your child's education. You have the right to access your child's records, to participate in all meetings concerning your child's special education program, and to disagree with decisions made by the school district. Georgetown ISD is legally obligated to respect and uphold these rights. Remember, you are your child's strongest advocate.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Georgetown ISD

The first step in determining if your child is eligible for special education services is to request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). It's important to understand that this request must be in writing. A verbal request to a teacher or administrator, while helpful in raising awareness, does not officially start the evaluation timeline. The written request should clearly state that you are requesting a special education evaluation for your child. You do not need to provide a specific diagnosis, simply state your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress.

Once Georgetown ISD receives your written request and obtains your written consent, the district has 45 school days to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). This evaluation is a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and weaknesses, designed to determine if they meet the criteria for a specific disability under IDEA. Keep in mind that Georgetown ISD also has a legal responsibility under Child Find laws to proactively identify students who may need special education services, even if a parent has not specifically requested an evaluation. This means that if school staff observe concerns about your child, they should initiate the evaluation process. To ensure your child receives the evaluation they may need, it's wise to document your concerns and submit a written request. Your next step should be to write that letter! Make sure to keep a copy for your records, and send it via certified mail, return receipt requested, so you have proof that Georgetown ISD received your request.

ARD Meetings in Georgetown ISD: Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is the cornerstone of the special education process. It's the meeting where your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP) is developed, reviewed, and revised. The ARD committee is a team that includes you, your child (when appropriate), teachers, administrators, and other professionals who have knowledge or expertise related to your child's needs. This committee works together to determine your child's eligibility for special education services, to develop appropriate goals and objectives, and to decide on the services and supports your child needs to succeed.

Georgetown ISD is required to provide you with at least five calendar days written notice before any ARD meeting. This notice should include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location, and a list of the individuals who will be attending. During the meeting, you have the right to share your concerns, ask questions, and provide input into the development of your child's IEP. Remember, you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. You also have the right to request a 10-day recess at any time during the ARD meeting. This allows you time to review the information presented, consult with experts, or simply gather your thoughts before making a decision. Finally, you are not obligated to sign the IEP the same day as the meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and sign it later if you are in agreement with the plan.

Dyslexia Services in Georgetown ISD

Texas law mandates that Georgetown ISD screen every student for dyslexia. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties due to dyslexia. If a student is identified as potentially dyslexic, Georgetown ISD is required to provide appropriate interventions and support. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook sets the standards for identification and intervention that Georgetown ISD must follow. This handbook outlines specific evidence-based practices and instructional strategies that should be used to support students with dyslexia.

Students identified with dyslexia are entitled to structured literacy intervention, which is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. Depending on the severity of their needs, students with dyslexia may also qualify for a 504 Plan or an IEP. A 504 Plan provides accommodations and modifications to the general education curriculum, while an IEP provides specialized instruction and related services tailored to the student's individual needs. If your child has not been screened for dyslexia, or if you have concerns about your child's reading development, request a dyslexia screening in writing from Georgetown ISD as soon as possible.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Georgetown ISD

Discipline can be a complex issue for students with disabilities. It's crucial to understand that suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative days in Georgetown ISD triggers specific protections under IDEA. Specifically, such a suspension constitutes a Change of Placement under IDEA, meaning the district must conduct a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) before the suspension can continue. An MDR is an ARD meeting to determine if the student's behavior was caused by or directly related to their disability, or if it was the direct result of the district's failure to implement the IEP. If either of these conditions is met, the behavior is considered a manifestation of the disability, and the district must take steps to address the underlying causes of the behavior.

This situation is one of the most time-sensitive you will face as a parent of a special education student. Acting within days is paramount to protect your child's rights and education. The school district must follow specific procedures and timelines when disciplining a student with disabilities, and failure to do so can result in legal challenges. Filing a Complaint Against Georgetown ISD

If you believe that Georgetown ISD has violated your child's rights under IDEA or other special education laws, you have several options for filing a complaint. It's important to remember that these rights exist regardless of what school district staff may tell you. The first option is to file a TEA State Complaint. This is a free process, and the TEA is required to investigate the complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The second option is to request Mediation through TEA. This is also a free process, where a trained mediator will help you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer will hear evidence and make a decision. This option may require you to hire an attorney. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe Georgetown ISD has discriminated against your child based on their disability.

Frequently Asked Questions About Georgetown ISD Special Education

The questions every Georgetown ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Georgetown ISD? +

Submit a written request to the Georgetown ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Georgetown ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Georgetown ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Georgetown ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Georgetown ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Georgetown ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Georgetown ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
