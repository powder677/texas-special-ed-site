What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Copperas Cove ISD! As a parent of a child with a disability, you are a vital member of your child's educational team. One of the most important ways you participate is through the Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting. Don't be intimidated by the name! The ARD meeting is simply a meeting where important decisions are made about your child's special education program. The name ARD comes from the three main things the committee does: determine if a child is eligible for special education (Admission), review the child's progress and Individualized Education Program (IEP) at least once a year, and determine if a child no longer needs special education services (Dismissal).

This meeting is required by both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. These laws ensure that students with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's IEP. The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services the school will provide to help them succeed.

Who attends an ARD meeting? The team is composed of several key members: You, the parent, are a crucial part of the team and your input is invaluable. A representative of the school district, often an administrator or special education director, is present to ensure the district is following procedures. Your child's general education teacher (if your child is participating in general education classes) and special education teacher will both attend to provide insights on your child's performance in the classroom. An assessment personnel, often a diagnostician or educational specialist, is present to explain any evaluations or assessments that have been conducted. Finally, your child may also attend the ARD meeting, particularly as they get older, to share their own perspectives and goals. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited, such as therapists, counselors, or outside specialists. You have the right to invite anyone you believe can contribute to the discussion and support your child. This could include family members, advocates, or educational consultants.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Copperas Cove ISD, like all districts, is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is your right, and it's designed to give you ample time to prepare. This notice isn't just a simple reminder; it must include specific information. The notice must include the purpose of the meeting, for example, is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility, an annual review of the IEP, or a meeting to discuss a specific concern? It also needs to state the date, time, and location of the meeting, as well as a list of the individuals who will be attending. This list might not be exhaustive, but it should give you a good idea of who will be present and what their roles are. Finally, the notice must inform you of your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child.

It's important to carefully review this notice when you receive it. If the proposed date or time doesn't work for you, contact the school immediately to reschedule. The district is required to make reasonable efforts to accommodate your schedule. Also, consider who is listed as attending. If you feel someone important is missing, such as a specific therapist or counselor, contact the school and request that they be invited.

While you have the right to a five-day notice, you can choose to waive this right in writing if you prefer to meet sooner. This might be beneficial if there is an urgent issue that needs to be addressed quickly, or if you simply want to get the process moving forward. However, before waiving your right, make sure you feel adequately prepared for the meeting. Don't feel pressured to waive your notice if you need more time to gather information or consult with others. The decision is entirely yours.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Being prepared for your child's ARD meeting is crucial for ensuring their needs are met. Treat this meeting like a very important job interview, because it truly is. Gather as much information as possible to support your input and advocate effectively for your child.

First, bring any prior evaluations or assessments related to your child's disability. This includes evaluations conducted by the school district, as well as any independent evaluations you may have obtained. These documents provide valuable data about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and specific learning needs. Be sure to review these documents carefully before the meeting so you are familiar with the findings and can discuss them intelligently.

If your child has seen any doctors, therapists, or other specialists, bring any relevant letters or reports from them. These professionals can offer valuable insights into your child's medical or therapeutic needs, which can inform the development of the IEP. For example, if your child has a diagnosis of ADHD, a letter from their pediatrician outlining their medication and any behavioral recommendations can be very helpful.

Gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and work samples. These documents provide evidence of your child's academic performance and can help illustrate any areas where they are struggling. Pay attention to patterns in their grades and any comments from teachers that might highlight specific challenges.

Before the meeting, take the time to write down a list of your concerns. What are you most worried about regarding your child's education? What are their biggest challenges? What are your hopes and dreams for their future? Having a written list will help you stay focused and ensure you address all of your concerns during the meeting. It can be easy to get overwhelmed or sidetracked, so a list will help you stay on track.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you understand the information being presented, and ensure your voice is heard. It's always helpful to have another set of ears and eyes in the room, especially if you're feeling nervous or overwhelmed.

Finally, remember that Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. This means that you can legally record the ARD meeting, as long as you inform the team that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be an invaluable tool for reviewing the meeting later, ensuring you accurately remember the discussions and decisions that were made. Simply state at the beginning of the meeting that you will be recording it for your personal records. This provides an accurate record of the meeting and can be invaluable if disagreements arise later.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

One of the most empowering things to know as a parent in an ARD meeting is the "10-Day Recess Rule." This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you, the parent, to request a recess of up to ten school days before any final decisions are made about your child's IEP. This is incredibly important because it gives you time to pause, reflect, gather more information, or seek outside advice if you're feeling unsure or overwhelmed by the discussions.

The 10-day recess can be used for various reasons. Perhaps you need time to review complex evaluation data, consult with an advocate or specialist, or simply process the information presented at the meeting. Maybe you want to observe your child in the classroom setting to better understand their needs. Or maybe you just need a break to clear your head and make sure you're making the best decisions for your child. Whatever the reason, you have the right to request this recess. It's important to note that these are 10 school days, not calendar days. This means weekends and holidays are not counted toward the 10-day period.

To invoke the 10-Day Recess Rule, simply state clearly during the ARD meeting that you would like to recess the meeting for up to ten school days. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation for your request. It's enough to simply say something like, "I would like to request a recess of this ARD meeting for up to ten school days to allow me time to [briefly state your reason, such as consult with an advocate]." The team is legally obligated to grant your request.

The ARD committee will then schedule a date and time to reconvene after the recess period. During the recess, take the time you need to gather the information or support you require. When the meeting resumes, you'll be in a better position to make informed decisions about your child's IEP. Remember, this rule is designed to protect your rights and ensure that you are an active and informed participant in your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the heart of your child's special education program. It outlines all the key decisions made during the ARD meeting and serves as a roadmap for their education. It's essential to understand the different components of the IEP so you can ensure it accurately reflects your child's needs and goals.

One of the first sections of the IEP is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" (Present Levels). This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in all areas of development, including academic, social, emotional, and behavioral. It should include information from evaluations, assessments, classroom observations, and your own input as a parent. The Present Levels section is the foundation for the rest of the IEP, as it identifies the areas where your child needs support.

Based on the Present Levels, the ARD committee will develop "Annual Goals" for your child. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. Goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). For example, a goal might be: "By the end of the school year, Sarah will increase her reading comprehension skills from a first-grade level to a second-grade level, as measured by a standardized reading assessment."

The IEP will also outline the "Services" your child will receive to help them achieve their goals. This includes special education instruction, related services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and any necessary accommodations or modifications. The IEP will specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services. For instance, it might state that your child will receive 30 minutes of speech therapy twice a week in a small group setting.

Another crucial component of the IEP is "Placement." This refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. Placement options can range from the general education classroom with support to a self-contained special education classroom. The IEP team must consider the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

Finally, the IEP will include a list of "Accommodations." These are changes to the way instruction is delivered or assessed that help your child access the curriculum. Accommodations do not change what your child is learning, but rather how they are learning it. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and the use of assistive technology.

It's critical to understand that you do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and seek advice from others before making a decision. Don't feel pressured to sign something you're not comfortable with. Your signature indicates your agreement with the IEP, so it's important to be fully informed before signing.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that you, as a parent, are an equal and essential member of the ARD committee. While the school district has expertise in education, you have unique knowledge of your child's strengths, weaknesses, and needs. Disagreements can arise during the ARD process, and it's crucial to know your rights and options when this happens.

One fundamental right is the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you disagree with the school's recommendation for special education services, you can withhold your consent, which means your child will not be placed in special education. This decision requires careful consideration, and it's important to discuss your concerns with the school and explore alternative options. You also have the right to refuse consent for the implementation of the IEP. You are not required to sign it.

Even if you initially agree with the IEP, you have the right to disagree with specific aspects of it. If you disagree with a particular goal, service, or placement, you can refuse to sign the IEP. In this case, the school district may choose to implement the parts of the IEP you agree with, while working with you to resolve the areas of disagreement.

Another important right is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide a second opinion and help inform the ARD committee's decisions. To request an IEE, you must notify the school district in writing and explain why you disagree with their evaluation. The district must either grant your request or initiate a due process hearing to defend their evaluation. If the due process hearing sides with the school district, you still have the right to obtain an IEE, but at your own expense.

Regardless of whether you sign the IEP or not, you have the right to write "Parent Disagrees" on the document. This notation clearly indicates that you do not agree with some or all of the IEP, and it preserves your right to pursue further action. It's a simple but powerful way to ensure your voice is heard and your concerns are documented.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve disagreements with the ARD committee and are still not satisfied, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) 2004 and Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89, also known as the Legal Framework. It's important to understand these options so you can advocate effectively for your child.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a relatively simple and free process. You can submit a written complaint to TEA outlining the specific violations of IDEA or state law you believe have occurred. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This option is useful for addressing procedural violations or systemic issues within the school district. Information and forms for filing a TEA complaint can be found on the TEA website.

The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district work with a trained, impartial mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable solution. TEA offers free mediation services to parents of students with disabilities. The mediator facilitates communication and helps both parties explore possible resolutions. Mediation is a less adversarial approach than other options and can be a good way to preserve a positive relationship with the school district while addressing your concerns. Mediation is confidential, and any agreement reached through mediation is legally binding.

If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer a more formal process, you can request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will then issue a written decision. Due process hearings are more complex and can be time-consuming and expensive. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. Due process hearings are governed by strict timelines and procedures outlined in IDEA and state law.

Finally, you can also file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR enforces federal laws that prohibit discrimination based on disability. If you believe the school district has discriminated against your child because of their disability, you can file a complaint with OCR. OCR will investigate your complaint and determine whether discrimination has occurred. If OCR finds that discrimination has occurred, they can order the school district to take corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
