Dyslexia Services in Melissa ISD

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Melissa ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This handbook serves as the primary resource for identifying, assessing, and providing services for students with dyslexia. Parents and educators are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the handbook, available on the TEA website. The handbook provides detailed information on the characteristics of dyslexia, screening procedures, instructional methods, and legal requirements under Texas law, specifically Senate Bill 48. Any reference to services or procedures within Melissa ISD are aligned with the principles and practices described within this document. Important updates in the 2024 handbook may include refined guidance on early intervention strategies, technological accommodations, and specific requirements for monitoring student progress.

Mandatory Screening

Melissa ISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade, as required by Texas law. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. The screening process involves assessing key literacy skills, including phonological awareness, letter knowledge, rapid automatized naming, and reading fluency. Students who demonstrate indicators of dyslexia on the initial screening may receive further evaluation to determine if they meet the criteria for a diagnosis. Parents will be notified of screening results and any recommendations for further evaluation or intervention. The screening process is continuous throughout the school year, allowing teachers to identify students who may develop characteristics of dyslexia at a later stage.

Structured Literacy Programs

Melissa ISD utilizes structured literacy approaches to address the needs of students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is a research-based instructional approach that emphasizes explicit, systematic, and sequential instruction in phonology, sound-symbol association, syllable instruction, morphology, syntax, and semantics. Our programs focus on building foundational reading skills and providing targeted interventions to support students' reading development. These programs may include components such as phonics-based reading instruction, multisensory learning activities, and individualized instruction based on students' specific needs. Specific program names will be communicated to parents as part of the ARD or 504 process if their child qualifies for services.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the severity of the student's dyslexia and the extent of the support they require. A 504 plan provides accommodations to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum. An IEP, on the other hand, is a more comprehensive plan that provides specialized instruction and related services to meet the unique needs of students with disabilities who require more intensive support. Generally, students whose dyslexia significantly impacts their ability to access the general education curriculum and require specialized instruction in reading and/or writing may be eligible for an IEP. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee determines if a student qualifies for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Accommodations List

Melissa ISD provides a variety of accommodations to support students with dyslexia. These accommodations may include:

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Use of assistive technology (e.g., text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software)
  • Preferential seating
  • Modified assignments
  • Oral presentations instead of written reports
  • Use of graphic organizers
  • Multi-sensory instruction
  • Frequent breaks
  • Copies of notes
  • Audio recordings of lectures

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations provided will be determined based on the individual needs of the student, as outlined in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia should contact their child's teacher or the school principal. They can formally request a dyslexia evaluation from the school. The request should be made in writing and should include specific concerns about the child's reading skills. The school will then initiate the evaluation process, which may involve assessments of reading, spelling, and writing skills. Parents will be actively involved in the evaluation process and will receive a copy of the evaluation report. If the evaluation determines that the student has dyslexia, the school will work with the parents to develop a 504 plan or IEP to address the student's needs. Please note that referrals can also be made by teachers and other school personnel.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
