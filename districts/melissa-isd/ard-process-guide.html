What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child is eligible for special education services in Melissa ISD, you will be invited to attend an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. The ARD committee is a group of individuals, including you as the parent, who work together to make decisions about your child's special education program. This meeting is extremely important because it's where your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP, is developed, reviewed, and revised. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific supports and services your child will receive to help them make progress in school.

The ARD committee typically includes you, the parent; a representative from the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services (often an administrator or special education director); your child's special education teacher; a general education teacher (if your child is participating in general education classes); an evaluation specialist who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results; and, when appropriate, your child. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited, such as related service providers (e.g., speech therapist, occupational therapist, counselor) or outside experts you choose to bring. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an IEP tailored to your child's unique needs, to review your child's progress, and to decide if your child is ready to be dismissed from special education services. This entire process is required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code §29. The ARD process ensures that your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) designed to meet their individual needs.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

The school district is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is crucial because it gives you time to prepare for the meeting, gather information, and consider what you want to discuss. This notice must include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of the individuals who will be attending. It should also inform you of your right to participate in the meeting and bring other people with you who have knowledge or special expertise about your child. Review this notice carefully to ensure you understand the meeting's agenda and who will be present.

While the law requires a five-day notice, you, as the parent, can waive this requirement if you choose to meet sooner. This might be beneficial if you and the school team agree that addressing a particular issue quickly is in your child's best interest. If you decide to waive the five-day notice, you will need to provide written consent, indicating that you understand your right to the notice but are voluntarily choosing to proceed with the meeting earlier. Take your time to consider whether waiving the notice is right for you and your child. Don't feel pressured to waive the notice if you need more time to prepare.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting is essential for advocating for your child's needs. Gather any documents that can help the ARD committee understand your child's strengths, weaknesses, and educational needs. This may include previous evaluations, both from the school and from outside sources like private psychologists or therapists. Any medical documentation, such as letters from doctors or specialists, outlining your child's diagnoses or medical needs, can also be valuable.

Bring any school records you have, like report cards, progress reports, or samples of your child's work. These items offer concrete examples of your child's performance in the classroom. It's also extremely helpful to create a written list of your specific concerns and goals for your child. This list will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all your important points are addressed. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person, such as a family member, friend, or professional advocate. They can offer emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns.

Finally, remember that it is legal to audio record ARD meetings in Texas, provided you give the school district prior notice that you intend to do so. A voice recorder can be a useful tool for documenting the meeting and ensuring you have an accurate record of the discussions and decisions made. Be sure to inform the ARD committee that you will be recording the meeting. Having a recording can be beneficial when reviewing the IEP later or if any disagreements arise.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

One of the most important rights you have as a parent during the ARD process is the ability to request a recess. Any member of the ARD committee, including you, has the right to call for a recess of up to ten school days. This recess allows time to gather more information, consult with outside experts or advocates, or simply to reflect on the information presented during the meeting before making any final decisions about your child's IEP.

You might request a recess if you feel you need more time to review evaluation data, if you want to seek a second opinion on a particular service or placement recommendation, or if you feel overwhelmed by the information presented during the meeting. To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state that you are requesting a recess. It is helpful to state the reason for your request. The team should document this in the ARD minutes. The school cannot deny a request for a recess. Using this recess can ensure that you are making informed decisions in your child's best interest and that you have the time needed to fully understand the IEP before it is finalized.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the cornerstone of your child's special education program. It is a written plan that outlines the specific educational and related services your child will receive. Several key decisions are made during the ARD meeting that directly impact the content of the IEP.

The ARD committee will first determine your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, social skills, and behavior. Next, the committee will develop annual goals for your child. These goals are measurable objectives that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The IEP will also specify the special education and related services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. The document will describe the frequency, duration, and location of these services. Placement decisions, outlining where your child will receive their education (e.g., general education classroom, resource room, self-contained classroom), are also made. The IEP will also include any necessary accommodations or modifications to the curriculum, instruction, or testing procedures that will help your child succeed.

Remember, as a parent, you do not have to sign the IEP the same day it is developed. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before providing your consent. Never feel pressured to sign an IEP if you are not comfortable with its contents.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with the school district regarding their child's special education program. You have specific rights when disagreements arise. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the school's decision that your child needs special education services, you can refuse to allow them to be placed in a special education program. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with its contents. By refusing to sign, you are not preventing your child from receiving services; you are simply indicating that you do not agree with the proposed plan.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the school district believes their evaluation was appropriate, they can initiate a due process hearing to defend their evaluation. If the hearing officer rules in your favor, the district must pay for the IEE. Even if you agree with most of the IEP but disagree with a specific aspect, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document next to the area of disagreement. This documents your concerns and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve disagreements with the school district through informal means, you have several formal options available to you. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege violations of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or state special education laws. The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA offers free mediation services to parents and school districts involved in special education disputes. Mediation is non-adversarial and focuses on finding solutions that meet your child's needs.

If mediation is unsuccessful, you can request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the dispute. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. The decision of the hearing officer is binding on both you and the school district, subject to appeal. Finally, you also have the right to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. These rights are governed by the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process, which interprets IDEA 2004 and 19 Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89. Familiarizing yourself with these resources can further empower you in advocating for your child.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
