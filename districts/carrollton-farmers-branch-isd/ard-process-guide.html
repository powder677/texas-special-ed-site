What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is a meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code Section 29, where decisions are made about your child's special education program. Think of it as a collaborative planning session where parents, educators, and other relevant professionals come together to create an Individualized Education Program, or IEP, tailored to your child's unique needs. The primary purpose is to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an IEP that outlines specific goals, services, and accommodations, and to decide on the most appropriate educational placement for your child. This meeting is about ensuring your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) designed to meet their individual needs.

The ARD meeting isn't just a formality; it's a critical process where your voice as a parent is essential. You are a key member of the ARD committee, bringing invaluable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, needs, and aspirations. Other attendees typically include a school administrator who is knowledgeable about special education services, your child's general education teacher (if applicable), a special education teacher, an educational diagnostician (who has conducted assessments), and related service providers such as speech therapists, occupational therapists, counselors, or behavior specialists, depending on your child's specific needs. The school district may invite other professionals who can contribute to the discussion, and you also have the right to invite anyone you believe can offer valuable input, such as a therapist, family member, or advocate. Remember, the ARD committee is a team working together to support your child's success, and your active participation is crucial to ensuring the IEP is effective.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This timeframe allows you adequate time to prepare, gather information, and arrange for support, if needed. The notice must include specific information, such as the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location, and a list of individuals who will be attending. It should also inform you of your right to invite other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. This notice is not just a courtesy; it's a legal requirement designed to ensure you have ample opportunity to participate meaningfully in the ARD process.

While you are entitled to a five-day notice, you can choose to waive this right in writing if you prefer to meet sooner. For instance, if an urgent situation arises or if you and the school agree that an expedited meeting would be beneficial, you can sign a written waiver to proceed with the ARD meeting before the five-day period has elapsed. However, it's important to carefully consider the implications of waiving your notice rights, as it might limit your ability to fully prepare for the meeting. Make sure you understand why the district is requesting a waiver and that you feel comfortable proceeding before agreeing to waive your right to the five-day notice.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is essential to ensuring their needs are fully understood and addressed. A well-prepared parent can significantly contribute to the development of an effective IEP. Here's a detailed list of items you should consider bringing:

First, gather any prior evaluations or assessments conducted by independent professionals. These could include psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy evaluations, or any other reports that shed light on your child's strengths and areas of need. These evaluations provide valuable data points for the ARD committee to consider.

Next, collect any relevant letters or reports from your child's doctors or other medical professionals. These documents can provide important medical context that may influence your child's educational needs. For example, if your child has a medical condition that affects their learning, documentation from their doctor can help the ARD committee understand the necessary accommodations.

Bring any relevant school records that you think might be helpful. This could include report cards, progress reports, work samples, or any other documentation that showcases your child's academic performance or behavior in the classroom. These records offer a snapshot of your child's current educational standing.

Prepare a written list of your specific concerns and goals for your child. Articulating your concerns in writing ensures that you don't forget anything important during the meeting. Think about what you want your child to achieve academically, socially, and emotionally. Having clear goals will help guide the ARD committee's discussion.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or a professional advocate who is familiar with special education law and procedures. Having someone by your side can provide emotional support and help you navigate the complexities of the ARD process. An advocate can also help you articulate your concerns and ensure that your voice is heard.

Finally, be aware that Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. This means you can legally record the ARD meeting, provided you inform the ARD team that you are doing so. Recording the meeting can be a valuable tool for ensuring accurate record-keeping and can help you recall important details later on. It's advisable to inform the ARD committee at the beginning of the meeting that you will be recording.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a powerful tool available to any ARD committee member, including you as the parent. This rule allows any member of the ARD team to request a temporary pause, or recess, of up to 10 school days in the ARD meeting process before decisions are finalized. This recess provides time to gather additional information, consult with outside experts or advocates, or simply to reflect on the information presented and consider the best course of action for your child.

You can invoke the 10-day recess rule if you feel that you need more time to consider the IEP, if you have questions that haven't been adequately answered, or if you believe additional information is needed to make informed decisions. For example, if the team is proposing a new placement that you're unsure about, you can request a recess to visit the proposed classroom or to speak with other parents whose children attend that program. Or, if you want to get a second opinion from an outside specialist, the recess gives you time to arrange that consultation.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request clearly during the ARD meeting. There is no specific form or procedure required. It is helpful to state the reason you are requesting the recess, such as "I would like to request a 10-day recess to consult with an advocate before finalizing the IEP." The ARD committee must honor your request. It's important to remember that this is *school* days, so weekends and school holidays are not counted toward the 10 days. Use this time wisely to gather the information you need to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the written record of the decisions made during the ARD meeting. It's a comprehensive plan that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, services, and accommodations. Understanding the different sections of the IEP is crucial for ensuring that your child receives the appropriate support to succeed.

The IEP typically begins with a section on Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses, as well as the impact of their disability on their ability to participate in the general education curriculum. It's based on information gathered from assessments, observations, and input from parents and teachers. This section serves as the foundation for developing appropriate goals.

Next, the IEP will include Annual Goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within a one-year period. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They should address the areas where your child needs the most support, as identified in the Present Levels section.

The IEP also outlines the specific special education and related services that your child will receive. This could include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or any other services necessary to help your child meet their goals. The IEP specifies the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

The IEP also determines the appropriate educational placement for your child. Placement refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. The law requires that children with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning that they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Placement options can range from full inclusion in a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting such as a resource room or self-contained classroom.

Finally, the IEP includes a list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material, while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn. Accommodations might include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. It's important to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP at the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home to review it thoroughly before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that the ARD process is a collaborative one, and your input as a parent is invaluable. However, disagreements can sometimes arise. It's crucial to know your rights and options when you disagree with the ARD team's decisions.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you disagree with the ARD team's decision to place your child in special education, you can decline to give your consent, and your child will not be placed in special education at that time. However, the district may pursue mediation or due process to override your refusal, if they believe that special education services are essential for your child's success.

You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document itself. Signing the IEP indicates that you attended the ARD meeting and received a copy of the document. It does not necessarily mean that you agree with everything in the IEP. If you disagree with certain aspects of the IEP, you can still sign the document while indicating that you disagree with specific sections. You can write "Parent Disagrees" next to those sections.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The IEE can provide a second opinion on your child's needs and can be valuable in resolving disagreements. The district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve disagreements with the ARD team and are still unable to reach an agreement, there are several formal escalation options available to you, governed by IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89 (The Legal Framework):

Your first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process and involves submitting a written complaint to TEA outlining your concerns. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. This is often the quickest and least adversarial route. Information on how to file a complaint can be found on the TEA website.

Mediation is another option. This is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement. TEA offers free mediation services. Mediation can be a helpful way to resolve disputes in a collaborative and constructive manner.

If mediation is unsuccessful or if you prefer not to pursue it, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's IEP. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, so it's advisable to seek legal counsel if you choose this option.

Finally, you have the option of filing a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. If you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child, you can file a complaint with OCR.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
