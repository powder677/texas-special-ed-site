The 10-Day Suspension Rule

CFBISD, like all Texas school districts, has rules regarding suspensions. It is critical to remember the "10-day suspension rule," especially if your child has an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or a 504 plan. This rule dictates that if a student with disabilities is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single school year, it may constitute a Change of Placement, triggering specific rights and procedures under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

What you NEED to know:

  • Track all suspensions: Keep a detailed record of every suspension, including the date, length, and reason. The district is supposed to do this, but you need your own record as well.
  • Cumulative Days Count: The 10 days do not have to be consecutive. Any combination of suspensions that adds up to more than 10 days in a school year can trigger IDEA protections.
  • Consider the pattern: Even if the total suspension days are under 10, a pattern of short suspensions for similar behaviors may still trigger a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).
  • "In-School" Suspension Alert: If the in-school suspension results in the student not receiving their IEP supports or is functionally equivalent to an out-of-school suspension (e.g., isolated and not receiving instruction), it could count towards the 10-day limit.

What to do IMMEDIATELY if your child is facing suspension:

  • Request all documentation: Demand copies of all incident reports, witness statements, and any other documents related to the disciplinary action. Do this in writing and keep a copy of your request.
  • Consult your child's IEP/504 plan: Carefully review the plan. Are the supports and accommodations being implemented correctly? Is the behavior a result of the disability?
  • Contact the ARD committee (if applicable): Request an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting immediately to discuss the situation and determine if the behavior is related to your child's disability.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a critical process required under IDEA for students with IEPs who face disciplinary action that could result in a Change of Placement (suspension exceeding 10 days or a pattern of shorter suspensions). The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's behavior was caused by or directly and substantially related to their disability, OR if the behavior was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

Key aspects of an MDR:

  • Timeline is critical: The MDR must occur within 10 school days of the decision to change the student's placement. Do not delay in pushing for this.
  • ARD Committee Responsibility: The MDR is conducted by the ARD committee, which includes you, the parent.
  • Two-Part Test: The ARD committee must answer TWO crucial questions:
    1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
    2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the LEA's (Local Education Agency - CFBISD) failure to implement the IEP?
  • IEP Implementation is Key: If the ARD committee finds that the school failed to implement the IEP, the behavior MUST be determined to be a manifestation of the disability. The school MUST take immediate steps to correct the deficiencies.
  • "Causation" is Crucial: Even if the IEP was fully implemented, if the behavior is found to be caused by or directly and substantially related to the disability, it is a manifestation. Examples: impulsivity due to ADHD, aggression due to autism, etc. Gather any medical or psychological evidence to support this connection.
  • Parent Input Matters: Your voice is critical in the MDR. Prepare to clearly articulate how your child's disability impacts their behavior. Bring documentation and examples.

What to do to prepare for an MDR:

  • Review the IEP thoroughly: Make sure you understand all goals, objectives, accommodations, and modifications.
  • Gather evidence: Collect any relevant information, such as doctor's reports, psychological evaluations, previous incident reports, and your own observations of your child's behavior.
  • Prepare a statement: Write a clear and concise statement explaining how your child's disability impacts their behavior and why you believe the behavior is a manifestation of the disability.
  • Know your rights: Familiarize yourself with IDEA regulations regarding discipline and MDRs.
  • Bring an advocate: Consider bringing a special education advocate or attorney to the MDR meeting for support.

If the MDR determines the behavior IS a manifestation of the disability:

  • The student must return to their original placement unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the IEP.
  • The ARD committee MUST conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) if one has not already been conducted, and implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. If a BIP is already in place, the ARD committee MUST review and modify it as necessary.

If the MDR determines the behavior IS NOT a manifestation of the disability:

  • The student can be disciplined in the same manner as students without disabilities, EXCEPT the school must still provide FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education). This means they must continue to receive services that enable them to participate in the general education curriculum, although the location may change.
  • You have the right to challenge the MDR decision through due process.

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when a series of removals creates a pattern that totals more than 10 school days in a school year and constitutes a significant change in the student's educational experience. This is a BIG DEAL, especially for students with disabilities.

What constitutes a Change of Placement?

  • Suspension exceeding 10 days: As mentioned previously, a suspension for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year can trigger a Change of Placement.
  • Pattern of Removals: Even shorter suspensions can constitute a Change of Placement if they create a pattern of removals, taking into account the length of each removal, the total time the child is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another.
  • Expulsion: This is a permanent removal from the school.
  • Alternative Educational Setting: If the student is placed in an alternative educational setting (e.g., DAEP - Disciplinary Alternative Education Program) for more than 45 school days, regardless of whether the behavior is determined to be a manifestation of the disability, it is considered a Change of Placement.

Your Rights During a Change of Placement:

  • Prior Written Notice: CFBISD must provide you with prior written notice before any Change of Placement. This notice must explain the reasons for the action, the procedural safeguards available to you, and how you can obtain more information.
  • Manifestation Determination Review: As explained above, if the Change of Placement is due to disciplinary reasons, an MDR must be conducted.
  • Continued FAPE: Even if the student is removed from their regular educational setting, CFBISD is still responsible for providing them with a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). This includes access to the general education curriculum and the services outlined in their IEP.
  • Due Process Rights: You have the right to challenge a Change of Placement decision through due process.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe CFBISD has violated your child's rights or has failed to follow its own policies, you can file a grievance. The first step is to file a Level 1 grievance. This process is typically outlined in the CFBISD student handbook or on their website. Locate this information and familiarize yourself with the specific procedures.

Key Considerations for Filing a Level 1 Grievance:

  • Act Quickly: There are strict deadlines for filing a grievance. Do not delay!
  • Formal Written Grievance: The grievance must be in writing and must clearly state the specific issue, the policy or law that was violated, the facts of the situation, and the remedy you are seeking.
  • Gather Documentation: Include copies of all relevant documents, such as incident reports, IEPs, emails, and witness statements.
  • Submit to the Appropriate Person: The grievance is typically filed with the principal or other designated administrator. Check the CFBISD policy to determine the correct person to contact.
  • Keep Records: Keep copies of everything you submit, including the date and method of submission.
  • Follow Up: If you do not receive a response within the specified timeframe (usually outlined in the policy), follow up with the administrator in writing.

If the Level 1 grievance is not resolved to your satisfaction, you may have the option to escalate to a Level 2 grievance, and potentially higher levels. Be aware of the deadlines for each level and continue to document everything.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe CFBISD has violated federal or state special education laws, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a grievance and can result in TEA investigating the district. However, it is only useful for violations of special education law, and not every grievance qualifies.

When to consider filing a TEA complaint:

  • Significant violations of IDEA: Examples include failure to provide FAPE, failure to implement the IEP, denial of procedural safeguards, or discrimination based on disability.
  • Exhaustion of local remedies: It's often advisable to exhaust the local grievance process before filing a TEA complaint.

How to file a TEA complaint:

  • Obtain the complaint form: Download the complaint form from the TEA website.
  • Provide detailed information: The complaint form requires you to provide detailed information about the alleged violation, including the specific laws or regulations that were violated, the facts of the situation, and the relief you are seeking.
  • Include supporting documentation: Attach copies of all relevant documents, such as IEPs, incident reports, emails, and evaluations.
  • Submit the complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA according to their instructions.

TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a decision. If TEA finds that CFBISD has violated special education laws, they may order the district to take corrective action.

Due Process

Due process is the most formal and adversarial method of resolving disputes with CFBISD regarding special education matters. It is a legal proceeding that allows you to present your case before an impartial hearing officer. This is a serious step and should not be taken lightly. Consult with a special education attorney before initiating due process.

When to consider due process:

  • Significant disagreements regarding your child's IEP: Examples include disagreements about eligibility, placement, services, or goals.
  • Denial of FAPE: If you believe CFBISD has denied your child a Free Appropriate Public Education.
  • Manifestation Determination Review Disputes: If you disagree with the findings of the MDR.

Key aspects of due process:

  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: You must file a formal due process complaint with TEA and CFBISD. The complaint must meet specific requirements, including a description of the problem, the facts of the situation, and the relief you are seeking.
  • Resolution Meeting: Within 15 days of receiving the due process complaint, CFBISD must convene a resolution meeting with you to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  • Due Process Hearing: If the dispute is not resolved at the resolution meeting, a due process hearing will be scheduled. The hearing will be conducted by an impartial hearing officer who will hear evidence and issue a decision.
  • Legal Representation: It is highly recommended to have legal representation during due process.

Due process is a complex and time-consuming process. It is important to understand your rights and to seek legal advice from an experienced special education attorney.

Remember, you are your child's best advocate. Don't be afraid to ask questions, demand answers, and fight for their rights. Document everything, keep detailed records, and seek professional help when needed.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
