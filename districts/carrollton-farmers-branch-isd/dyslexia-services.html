Dyslexia Services in Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in the Carrollton-Farmers Branch Independent School District (CFBISD) in accordance with Texas state law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the primary guide for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia in Texas public schools. The most recent version is the 2024 edition. It is crucial for parents, educators, and administrators in CFBISD to be familiar with this document. You can find the most up-to-date version on the TEA website (search for "Texas Dyslexia Handbook" on the TEA website to locate the official PDF). The handbook outlines the following key components regarding dyslexia services:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook clearly defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Legal Framework: The handbook describes the legal requirements related to dyslexia screening, evaluation, and instruction as mandated by Texas Education Code §38.003.
  • Screening Procedures: It details the screening measures to be implemented to identify students at risk for dyslexia, including universal screening in kindergarten and first grade, and ongoing monitoring in subsequent grades. The handbook also specifies the use of appropriate screening instruments and the data collection process.
  • Evaluation Procedures: The handbook outlines the procedures for comprehensive evaluations for students suspected of having dyslexia, including the components of a comprehensive evaluation, the qualifications of evaluators, and the process for determining eligibility for dyslexia services.
  • Instructional Practices (Structured Literacy): It emphasizes the importance of providing evidence-based, systematic, and explicit instruction in reading and spelling, known as Structured Literacy. The handbook details the essential components of Structured Literacy, which will be further elaborated in a later section of this guide.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: The handbook provides guidance on implementing accommodations and modifications to support students with dyslexia in the classroom.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook underscores the importance of parent involvement in the identification, evaluation, and instruction of students with dyslexia. It encourages collaboration between schools and parents to ensure the success of students with dyslexia.
  • Professional Development: The handbook emphasizes the need for ongoing professional development for teachers and administrators on dyslexia identification, evaluation, and instruction.

Parents in CFBISD should familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook to understand their rights and responsibilities regarding their child's education. Understanding the handbook will help parents effectively advocate for their child's needs.

Mandatory Screening

CFBISD adheres to the Texas Education Code §38.003, which mandates universal screening for dyslexia for all students in kindergarten and first grade. This screening is designed to identify students who are at risk for developing dyslexia. The specific screening instruments used by CFBISD may vary, but they are typically designed to assess foundational reading skills such as:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to hear and manipulate the individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words. This includes tasks like rhyming, segmenting, and blending.
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly name a series of familiar objects, colors, letters, or numbers.
  • Letter Knowledge: The ability to recognize and name letters of the alphabet.
  • Phoneme-Grapheme Correspondence: Understanding the relationship between sounds (phonemes) and the letters or letter combinations that represent them (graphemes).

If a student demonstrates indicators of dyslexia risk during the universal screening process, CFBISD will:

  • Notify the parents in writing.
  • Provide targeted interventions and progress monitoring.
  • If the student does not make adequate progress with targeted interventions, a comprehensive evaluation for dyslexia may be recommended.

It's important to note that the universal screening is not a diagnostic assessment. It identifies students who may benefit from additional support and further evaluation. Screening occurs within the first semester of Kindergarten and First Grade. Schools will communicate screening results to parents.

Structured Literacy Programs

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook mandates the use of Structured Literacy approaches for students identified with dyslexia. Structured Literacy is an evidence-based instructional approach that is systematic, explicit, and cumulative. It emphasizes the foundational skills necessary for reading and spelling success. CFBISD implements structured literacy programs designed to meet the specific needs of students with dyslexia. These programs focus on the following essential components:

  • Phonology: Teaching the sound structure of language, including phonemes, syllables, and morphemes.
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Explicitly teaching the relationship between sounds and letters/letter combinations (graphemes).
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching the different types of syllables and how they are combined to form words.
  • Morphology: Teaching the meaning-bearing units of language (e.g., prefixes, suffixes, root words).
  • Syntax: Teaching the rules of sentence structure.
  • Semantics: Teaching the meaning of words and sentences.

CFBISD uses various Structured Literacy programs or approaches for dyslexia intervention. While specific program names may not be publicly disclosed due to proprietary information, parents can inquire with their child's school about the specific program or approach being used and its alignment with the principles of Structured Literacy as outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. The program implemented should be designed to address the individual needs of the student. Regular progress monitoring is essential to ensure the effectiveness of the intervention.

Key characteristics of effective Structured Literacy instruction in CFBISD include:

  • Systematic and Sequential: Skills are taught in a logical order, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Explicit: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions made about prior knowledge.
  • Cumulative: New skills are built upon previously learned skills, and previously learned skills are continuously reviewed.
  • Multisensory: Instruction engages multiple senses (e.g., visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance learning and memory.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for accommodations and support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under special education. Understanding the difference between these two options is crucial.

504 Plan:

  • A 504 plan is based on Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, a federal civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities.
  • To be eligible for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading, writing).
  • A 504 plan provides accommodations to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum. It focuses on leveling the playing field.
  • Students with dyslexia who do not require specialized instruction may be eligible for a 504 plan.

IEP (Individualized Education Program):

  • An IEP is based on the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law that provides special education and related services to children with disabilities.
  • To be eligible for an IEP, a student must have a disability that falls under one of the 13 categories defined by IDEA (including Specific Learning Disability, which includes dyslexia) and requires specialized instruction to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • An IEP includes specific goals and objectives, specialized instruction, related services (e.g., speech therapy), and accommodations.
  • Students with dyslexia who require intensive, individualized instruction beyond what can be provided through general education interventions may be eligible for an IEP. This indicates the need for specially designed instruction.

Key Differences:

Feature 504 Plan IEP
Legal Basis Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
Eligibility Disability that substantially limits one or more major life activities Disability that falls under one of the 13 IDEA categories AND requires specialized instruction
Focus Accommodations to ensure equal access to general education Specialized instruction and related services to meet individual needs
Team Typically a smaller team, including teachers, counselors, and administrators. A larger team, including teachers, parents, administrators, specialists, and related service providers.

The determination of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the evaluation team.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way a student learns that do not change the content of what they are learning. These can be provided under both 504 plans and IEPs. CFBISD provides a variety of accommodations to support students with dyslexia. Here is a sample list of common accommodations:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Providing a seat near the teacher or away from distractions.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that reads text aloud.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to audio versions of textbooks and other materials.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing a copy of notes or allowing the student to record lectures.
  • Graphic Organizers: Using visual aids to help organize thoughts and ideas.
  • Use of Technology: Allowing the use of computers, tablets, or other assistive technology.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing short breaks during tasks.
  • Reading Aloud: Allowing the student to read aloud during instruction or testing.
  • Spelling Support: Allowing the use of a spell checker or providing a list of commonly misspelled words.
  • Alternatives to Written Work: Allowing oral presentations, projects, or other alternative assessments.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Dictation: Allowing the student to dictate responses to tests or assignments.
  • Visual Supports: Using visual cues, charts, and diagrams to support learning.

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations provided will be determined based on the individual needs of the student. Parents should work closely with the school to develop a plan that meets their child's specific needs.

How to Request an Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from CFBISD. Here are the steps to follow:

  1. Contact the School: Begin by contacting your child's teacher, counselor, or principal to express your concerns. Document the date of your communication and who you spoke with.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for a dyslexia evaluation to the school principal or special education director. Be sure to include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a detailed description of your concerns. You can also include any supporting documentation, such as report cards, work samples, or previous testing results.
  3. Parent Information: If you believe that your child may be struggling with dyslexia, it may be helpful to gather information about your child's developmental history, academic performance, and any family history of reading difficulties. This information can be valuable during the evaluation process.
  4. Timeline: CFBISD has a specific timeline for responding to your request and completing the evaluation process, as dictated by the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and IDEA (if an IEP is suspected). The district must provide you with prior written notice of their decision to evaluate or not evaluate your child. This timeline is typically within 15 school days.
  5. Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to conduct an evaluation, it will be conducted by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians, licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs), or speech-language pathologists. The evaluation will assess various areas, including reading, spelling, writing, and phonological awareness.
  6. Evaluation Report: Following the evaluation, you will receive a written report outlining the findings and recommendations.
  7. ARD/504 Meeting: If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia and requires specialized instruction or accommodations, a meeting will be scheduled to develop an IEP (Admission, Review, and Dismissal committee) or a 504 plan. You have the right to actively participate in this meeting and contribute to the development of the plan.
  8. Parent Rights: Parents have specific rights under IDEA and Section 504, including the right to review their child's records, participate in meetings, and appeal decisions made by the school. CFBISD provides information about parent rights to parents of students with disabilities.

Remember, you are your child's strongest advocate. By understanding the process and working collaboratively with CFBISD, you can ensure that your child receives the support they need to succeed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
