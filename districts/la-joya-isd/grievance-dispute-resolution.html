The 10-Day Suspension Rule

La Joya ISD, like all Texas school districts, is subject to Texas Education Code laws regarding student discipline. One crucial aspect of these laws concerns the accumulation of suspensions. If your child receives suspensions totaling more than 10 school days in a school year, specific procedures kick in, particularly if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or a Section 504 plan.

Why 10 Days Matters:

  • For Students with IEPs or 504 Plans: Exceeding 10 cumulative days of suspension can trigger a Change of Placement review. This means the school is legally obligated to consider whether the behavior leading to the suspensions is a manifestation of your child's disability. We will discuss Manifestation Determination Reviews (MDRs) in detail below.
  • Even Without an IEP/504: Repeated suspensions can indicate a deeper problem. It might point to bullying, unmet needs, or a mismatch between the school environment and your child's learning style. Documentation of suspensions is critical if you believe your child might be eligible for special education services.

What to Do:

  • Keep meticulous records: Track every suspension, no matter how short. Note the date, length of suspension, reason for suspension, and any communication you had with the school.
  • Communicate with the school: If your child is nearing 10 days of suspension, proactively contact the school principal, counselor, and special education staff (if applicable). Express your concerns and request a meeting to discuss strategies to address your child's behavior.
  • Document Everything: Keep copies of all emails, letters, and meeting notes. These documents are essential if you need to file a grievance or appeal.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

A Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is a critical process under IDEA for students with IEPs or 504 plans facing disciplinary action that could result in a Change of Placement. A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days OR when there's a pattern of removals that constitute a change of placement (e.g., repeated short-term suspensions).

Purpose of the MDR:

The MDR team (which must include you, the parent, and relevant members of the IEP team) determines if the child's behavior that led to the disciplinary action was:

  1. Caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability; or
  2. The direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP.

What to Expect at an MDR Meeting:

  • Review of Information: The team will review all relevant information, including the child's IEP, evaluations, teacher observations, disciplinary records, and parent input.
  • Your Voice Matters: You are a crucial member of the team. Advocate strongly for your child. Explain how their disability affects their behavior. Point out any failures in the IEP implementation.
  • Outcomes:
    • If the Behavior Is a Manifestation: The school cannot discipline the child in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA), if one hasn't already been done, and implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The child must be returned to their original placement unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the IEP.
    • If the Behavior Is Not a Manifestation: The school can apply the same disciplinary procedures to the child as it would to a student without a disability. However, the school still must provide Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). This might require alternative educational settings.

Critical Considerations for MDRs:

  • Parent Rights: You have the right to access all relevant records, participate fully in the MDR meeting, and disagree with the team's decision. If you disagree, you have the right to pursue mediation, file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA), or request a due process hearing.
  • Independent Evaluation: If you believe the school's evaluations are inadequate, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense (under certain circumstances).
  • Don't Be Intimidated: School personnel may try to pressure you into agreeing with their conclusions. Stand your ground and advocate for your child's rights. If you feel overwhelmed, bring a knowledgeable advocate or attorney to the meeting.

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement is a significant alteration in a student's educational environment. It can involve suspension for more than 10 consecutive days, expulsion, placement in an alternative educational setting (like a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program - DAEP), or any other action that removes the student from their regular classroom. As mentioned above, a pattern of removals can also constitute a change of placement.

Why Changes of Placement Are Concerning:

  • Disruption to Learning: Changes of placement can disrupt a student's academic progress, social development, and emotional well-being.
  • Potential for Stigmatization: Placement in a DAEP or other alternative setting can stigmatize a student and create barriers to future opportunities.
  • Impact on IEP Goals: Changes of placement can make it difficult to implement a student's IEP effectively.

Your Rights Regarding Changes of Placement:

  • Notice: The school must provide you with written notice of any proposed change of placement, including the reasons for the change and your procedural safeguards under IDEA.
  • Consent (Sometimes): While the school doesn't necessarily need your consent for a short-term suspension, they generally need your agreement, or a due process order, for a long-term placement change, especially for students with IEPs.
  • Right to Challenge: You have the right to challenge a proposed change of placement through various means, including filing a grievance, requesting mediation, or initiating a due process hearing.

Things to Consider:

  • Is the proposed placement appropriate? Does it meet your child's academic, social, and emotional needs?
  • Is the school following proper procedures? Are they providing adequate notice and opportunities for you to participate in the decision-making process?
  • What are the alternatives? Are there less restrictive options that could address your child's behavior without removing them from their regular classroom?

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

La Joya ISD, like most school districts, has a grievance policy that outlines the steps you can take to formally complain about a school-related issue. This is often your first step in challenging a disciplinary action or other problem.

Steps in Filing a Level 1 Grievance (Typically):

  1. Obtain the Grievance Policy: Find the current La Joya ISD grievance policy. It's usually available on the district's website or from the school principal's office. Read it carefully! Pay attention to timelines and required procedures.
  2. Prepare Your Grievance: Your grievance should be written, clear, and concise. Include the following:
    • Your child's name and student ID number.
    • The date of the incident or action you are grieving.
    • A detailed description of the issue, including who was involved, what happened, and why you believe it was wrong.
    • The specific policy or law that you believe was violated.
    • The remedy you are seeking (e.g., removal of the suspension from your child's record, reinstatement to their original placement, changes to the IEP, etc.).
    • Any supporting documentation (e.g., emails, letters, witness statements).
  3. File Your Grievance: The policy will specify where to file the Level 1 grievance. It's usually with the principal or another designated school official.
  4. Meet with the School Official: The school official is typically required to investigate the grievance and meet with you to discuss it. Be prepared to present your case and answer questions.
  5. Receive a Decision: The school official will issue a written decision regarding your grievance.

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines: Pay close attention to the deadlines for filing a grievance and appealing the decision. Missing a deadline can jeopardize your ability to challenge the school's actions.
  • Documentation: Keep copies of everything you submit and receive.
  • Level 2 and Beyond: If you are not satisfied with the Level 1 decision, the grievance policy will outline the process for appealing to higher levels within the school district (e.g., the superintendent, the school board).

TEA State Complaint

If you believe La Joya ISD has violated state or federal laws related to special education, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a school-level grievance.

When to File a TEA Complaint:

  • The school has failed to implement your child's IEP.
  • The school has discriminated against your child based on their disability.
  • The school has violated your procedural safeguards under IDEA.

How to File a TEA Complaint:

  1. Obtain the Complaint Form: Download the official TEA complaint form from the TEA website.
  2. Complete the Form: The form requires detailed information about the alleged violation, including:
    • Your child's name and school.
    • A description of the problem.
    • The specific laws or regulations that you believe were violated.
    • Supporting documentation.
  3. Submit the Complaint: Follow the instructions on the form for submitting the complaint to TEA.

TEA Investigation:

TEA will investigate your complaint and determine whether the school district violated any laws or regulations. TEA may require the school district to take corrective action, such as providing compensatory education or revising its policies.

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines: TEA complaints generally must be filed within one year of the date of the alleged violation.
  • Exhaust Local Remedies: TEA may require you to exhaust local remedies (e.g., file a grievance) before they will investigate your complaint.
  • Focus on Legal Violations: TEA is primarily concerned with whether the school district violated laws or regulations. They may not address issues that are simply matters of disagreement between you and the school.

Due Process

Due process is the most formal and adversarial process for resolving disputes with a school district regarding special education. It involves a hearing before an impartial hearing officer who will make a binding decision about the issue.

When to Request a Due Process Hearing:

  • You disagree with the school district's identification, evaluation, or placement of your child.
  • You believe the school district has failed to provide your child with a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE).

Due Process Procedures:

  1. File a Due Process Complaint: You must file a written due process complaint with the school district and TEA. The complaint must meet specific requirements under IDEA.
  2. Resolution Session: The school district must convene a resolution session with you to try to resolve the dispute.
  3. Due Process Hearing: If the dispute is not resolved during the resolution session, a due process hearing will be scheduled. You and the school district will present evidence and arguments to the hearing officer.
  4. Hearing Officer's Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision within a specified timeframe.

Important Considerations:

  • Legal Representation: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. It is highly recommended that you hire an attorney who specializes in special education law.
  • Cost: Due process hearings can be expensive. You may be responsible for your attorney's fees and other costs.
  • Timeline: Due process hearings are subject to strict timelines.
  • Burden of Proof: In many due process cases, the burden of proof is on the parent to show that the school district violated the law.

This guide is a starting point. Do not hesitate to seek legal counsel to fully understand your rights and options. Protect your child's future.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
