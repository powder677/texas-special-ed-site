What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, for students who have or are suspected of having a disability that affects their ability to learn. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine whether your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) that meets your child's unique needs, and to decide where your child will receive those services. Think of it as a collaborative planning session where everyone around the table shares their expertise and insights to create a roadmap for your child's educational success.

The ARD committee is composed of several key individuals, all of whom play a vital role in the decision-making process. You, as the parent, are the most important member of the team. You know your child best and your input is invaluable. Other members of the ARD committee typically include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education (often the principal or assistant principal), your child's general education teacher, a special education teacher, an evaluation specialist who can interpret evaluation results, and your child, if appropriate. The school district may also invite other professionals, such as therapists or counselors, who have specific knowledge or expertise related to your child's needs. You also have the right to invite anyone you feel would be beneficial to the ARD process, such as a family member, friend, or advocate. It's important to remember that all members of the ARD committee have equal standing and contribute to the collaborative development of your child's IEP.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

La Joya ISD, like all school districts in Texas, is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting takes place. This allows you time to prepare for the meeting, gather any necessary information, and arrange for childcare or other obligations. The written notice must include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of the individuals who will be attending. It should also inform you of your right to participate in the meeting and to bring anyone you choose with you. The notice must be easily understandable and provided in your native language, if possible. If you don't receive this notice within the required timeframe, immediately contact the school to reschedule the meeting.

While you have the right to this five-day notice, you also have the option to waive this right in writing if you prefer to meet sooner. This might be the case if you feel it's important to address an urgent issue quickly, or if a mutually convenient time is difficult to find within the five-day window. However, you should only waive your right to the five-day notice if you are comfortable and feel adequately prepared for the meeting. Don't feel pressured to waive your rights if you need the time to gather information or seek support.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is crucial for ensuring that your voice is heard and your child's needs are met. Consider bringing the following items:

First, gather any prior evaluations or assessments that have been conducted on your child, whether they were done by the school district or by outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other relevant assessments. These documents provide valuable information about your child's strengths and weaknesses, and can help the ARD committee develop an appropriate IEP.

Second, bring any letters or reports from your child's doctors or other medical professionals. These documents can provide important medical information that may be relevant to your child's educational needs. For example, if your child has a diagnosed medical condition, such as ADHD or epilepsy, the doctor's letter can explain the condition and its potential impact on your child's learning. Medical documentation can also support requests for specific accommodations or services.

Third, collect any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and attendance records. These documents can provide insights into your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom. If your child has struggled in certain subjects or has had attendance issues, these records can help the ARD committee understand the challenges your child is facing and develop strategies to address them.

Fourth, and perhaps most importantly, prepare a written list of your concerns and priorities for your child's education. Think about what you want your child to achieve academically, socially, and emotionally. What are your biggest worries about your child's learning? What specific skills or support do you believe your child needs to succeed? Writing down your concerns ahead of time will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your questions are answered.

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who understands special education laws and procedures. Having someone with you can provide emotional support, help you take notes, and ensure that your voice is heard. An advocate can also help you understand the IEP document and navigate the special education process.

Finally, in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided that you inform the other members of the committee that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for documenting the discussions and decisions made during the meeting. It allows you to review the meeting later and ensure that the IEP accurately reflects the agreements that were reached. Be sure to notify the ARD committee at the beginning of the meeting that you will be recording. Openly and honestly stating your intent can help build trust and transparency throughout the process.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides an important safeguard called the "10-day recess rule." This means that any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess, or temporary pause, of up to 10 school days before decisions are finalized on the IEP. This recess allows time to gather more information, consult with experts or advocates, or simply to reflect on the discussion and consider the proposed IEP more carefully. The 10-day recess is there to protect your rights and your child's best interests.

You can invoke the 10-day recess rule by simply stating your desire to recess the meeting. It's helpful to be clear about why you are requesting the recess. For example, you might say, "I'd like to recess the meeting for 10 school days to have time to review the proposed goals with an advocate" or "I need time to gather more information about the recommended placement." The ARD committee must grant your request for a recess of up to 10 school days. They cannot pressure you to continue the meeting or to make a decision before you are ready. If the school denies your request to recess the ARD meeting, contact an advocate or attorney immediately. The district is obligated to follow the law.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the written plan that outlines your child's special education program. It is a legally binding document that specifies the services and supports your child will receive to address their individual needs. Several key elements are determined during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP.

The IEP includes a section called "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" (often shortened to Present Levels). This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic and functional areas. It provides a baseline for measuring your child's progress and for setting appropriate goals. The Present Levels should be based on objective data, such as test scores, classroom observations, and parent input.

The IEP also includes "Annual Goals," which are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be aligned with your child's Present Levels and should address their specific needs. The goals should be ambitious but realistic, and they should be written in a way that allows progress to be tracked and measured.

The "Services" section of the IEP describes the specific services and supports that your child will receive to help them achieve their annual goals. This might include special education instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or other related services. The IEP should specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services. For example, it might state that your child will receive 30 minutes of speech therapy twice a week in the speech therapist's office.

"Placement" refers to the setting in which your child will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom, a resource room, a self-contained classroom, or a combination of settings. The IEP must explain why the chosen placement is appropriate for your child and how it will meet their needs. IDEA requires that students with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning that they should be placed in a general education setting to the maximum extent appropriate, with necessary supports and accommodations.

Finally, the IEP includes a section on "Accommodations," which are changes to the way your child learns or is assessed that help them overcome their disability. Accommodations do not change the content of the curriculum, but they allow your child to access the curriculum more effectively. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and the use of assistive technology.

Remember that you do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and consult with others before making a decision. The school district cannot pressure you to sign the IEP immediately. Take your time and make sure you are comfortable with all of the provisions before giving your consent.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee on certain issues. If you disagree with the team's recommendations, it's important to know your rights and how to proceed. You have the right to refuse to consent to the initial placement of your child in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the school district's assessment of your child's needs or the proposed placement, you can refuse to allow your child to receive special education services at that time. However, keep in mind that this may prevent your child from receiving the specialized support they need.

You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with its contents. By signing the IEP, you are indicating that you agree with the plan and that you consent to the services and supports outlined in the document. If you disagree with any aspect of the IEP, you can refuse to sign it. This does not necessarily mean that the IEP cannot be implemented, but it does give you the right to pursue further action to resolve the disagreement.

One option for resolving disagreements is to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The purpose of an IEE is to provide a second opinion on your child's needs and to ensure that the evaluation process is fair and unbiased. If you request an IEE, the school district must either pay for the evaluation or file a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. You are entitled to use the IEE results at the ARD meeting.

Regardless of whether you sign the IEP or not, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document. This indicates that you do not agree with certain aspects of the IEP, even if you are allowing the plan to be implemented. Writing "parent disagrees" on the IEP preserves your right to pursue further action to resolve the disagreement.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal means, you have several formal options for escalating the issue. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Texas Administrative Code, Title 19, Chapter 89.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. TEA will investigate and make a determination if the district violated any special education regulations. Information and the complaint form can be found on the TEA website.

The second option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. The mediator does not make a decision, but rather facilitates communication and helps you explore potential solutions. Mediation is a free service offered by TEA and can be a valuable tool for resolving disputes without resorting to more formal legal proceedings.

The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal process in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a decision on the issue. Due Process Hearings are similar to court trials, and it is generally advisable to seek legal representation if you pursue this option. The hearing officer's decision is binding on both you and the school district. Due process is a complex process, so seeking legal counsel from a special education attorney is advised. The ARD document should provide specific guidelines for filing due process.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. If you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability, you can file a complaint with OCR. This can be done simultaneously with the other options listed above, but is best used in situations that involve clear discrimination, and not necessarily educational decisions. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process, located on the TEA website, contains detailed information about these rights and procedures.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
