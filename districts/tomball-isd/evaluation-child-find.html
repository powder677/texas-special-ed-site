Requesting an Evaluation in Tomball ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Tomball ISD

This guide provides information for parents in Tomball ISD who are concerned their child may have a disability and require special education services. It outlines the process of requesting a special education evaluation, the legal timelines involved, the required components of the evaluation, the district's Child Find obligations, what to do if the district refuses to evaluate, and the steps to take after the evaluation is completed. This information is based on Texas state law and regulations governing special education.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first and most crucial step in initiating the special education evaluation process is submitting a formal, written request to Tomball ISD. This written request serves as official notification to the district that you suspect your child may have a disability and require special education services. While verbal requests are sometimes considered, a written request provides a clear record of the date and content of your concern, which is critical for triggering the legal timelines and ensuring your rights are protected under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code Chapter 29.

Who to Address the Request To: The request should be addressed to the appropriate school official. This is generally the school principal, the school's special education director or campus diagnostician, or the school counselor. If you are unsure, contacting the principal's office is a good starting point. They can direct your request to the correct person.

Content of the Written Request: The written request should be clear and concise, stating your concerns about your child's academic performance, behavior, or developmental progress. While you don't need to provide a formal diagnosis, you should clearly articulate the specific areas where your child is struggling. Consider including the following information:

  • Your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your child's school and grade level.
  • A clear statement requesting a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services. For example: "I am writing to formally request a full and individual initial evaluation to determine my child's eligibility for special education services."
  • A description of your concerns about your child's academic performance. Be specific. For instance, "My child is significantly behind in reading comprehension and struggles to decode basic words." Or, "My child is unable to complete math problems involving fractions and has difficulty grasping abstract concepts."
  • A description of your concerns about your child's behavior, if applicable. Provide specific examples. For instance, "My child has frequent outbursts in class and difficulty following directions." Or, "My child exhibits significant anxiety and avoidance behaviors related to schoolwork."
  • Any relevant medical or developmental history, if applicable. This could include previous diagnoses, therapies, or significant medical events.
  • Any interventions or supports that have already been tried, and their effectiveness. For example, "The teacher has provided small group instruction in reading, but my child continues to struggle." Or, "We have implemented a behavior chart at home, but it has not significantly reduced my child's disruptive behavior."
  • Your contact information, including your phone number and email address.
  • The date of the request.

Delivery of the Request: Keep a copy of the written request for your records. You should deliver the request in a way that provides proof of delivery. Options include:

  • Certified mail with return receipt requested: This provides confirmation that the school received your request.
  • Hand delivery with a signed and dated receipt: Ask the school official receiving the request to sign and date a copy of your request, acknowledging receipt.
  • Email with read receipt requested: While less formal than certified mail, an email with a read receipt provides some documentation that the request was opened.

Important Considerations: Be polite and professional in your written request. Avoid accusatory language. Focus on your concerns about your child's needs and your desire to collaborate with the school to determine the best course of action. Clearly stating that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation is crucial. Using this specific language leaves no room for ambiguity about your intentions.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a strict timeline once the school district receives your written request for a special education evaluation. This timeline is crucial, and understanding it will help you advocate for your child's rights. The district has 45 school days from the date they receive your written request to complete the following:

  1. Obtain Parental Consent for Evaluation: Within a reasonable time after receiving the written request, the school must provide you with a Permission to Evaluate form (Consent to Evaluate). You must sign and return this form to authorize the evaluation process to begin. The 45-school-day timeline starts once the school receives your signed consent.
  2. Complete the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE): The district must complete the evaluation, which will assess your child's strengths and weaknesses in various areas of concern.
  3. Hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) Committee Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, the ARD committee, which includes you, school administrators, teachers, and evaluation personnel, must meet to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services.

Important Considerations Regarding the 45-School-Day Timeline:

  • School Days vs. Calendar Days: The timeline refers to school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks (e.g., summer break, Thanksgiving break) are not counted. This can significantly extend the actual calendar time it takes to complete the evaluation.
  • Prompt Return of Consent: To ensure the evaluation process begins as quickly as possible, return the Permission to Evaluate form promptly after receiving it. Delaying the return of the consent form will delay the start of the 45-school-day timeline.
  • Documentation is Key: Keep meticulous records of all communication with the school, including the date you submitted the written request, the date you received and returned the Permission to Evaluate form, and the dates of any meetings or phone calls. This documentation is crucial if you need to file a complaint or pursue legal action.
  • Requesting the Evaluation Report in Advance: Under Texas law, you are entitled to receive a copy of the evaluation report at least five school days before the ARD committee meeting. Requesting this report in advance will allow you to thoroughly review the findings and prepare any questions or concerns you may have.
  • What if the Timeline is Not Met?: If the district fails to complete the evaluation and hold the ARD committee meeting within the 45-school-day timeline, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Document the delay and consult with a special education advocate or attorney to understand your options.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if your child has a disability and, if so, the nature and extent of their educational needs. The FIE must be tailored to your child's specific needs and concerns. The evaluation team must use a variety of assessment tools and strategies to gather relevant functional, developmental, and academic information about your child. Texas regulations require the FIE to be conducted by qualified professionals, such as licensed educational diagnosticians, school psychologists, speech-language pathologists, and other specialists, depending on the suspected disability.

Required Components of the FIE: The FIE must cover all areas related to the suspected disability, including, but not limited to:

  • Academic Achievement: This assesses your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. Standardized tests, curriculum-based assessments, and teacher observations may be used.
  • Intellectual Functioning (Cognitive Abilities): This evaluates your child's general cognitive abilities, such as reasoning, problem-solving, and memory. This is typically assessed using standardized intelligence tests (IQ tests).
  • Adaptive Behavior: This assesses your child's ability to function independently in everyday life, including skills such as self-care, communication, and social skills. The Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales is a commonly used assessment tool.
  • Speech and Language: If speech or language delays are suspected, a speech-language pathologist will evaluate your child's articulation, fluency, language comprehension, and expressive language skills.
  • Motor Skills: If motor skill deficits are suspected, an occupational or physical therapist will evaluate your child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Social and Emotional Functioning: This assesses your child's social skills, emotional regulation, and behavior. This may involve observations, interviews, and behavior rating scales.
  • Health and Medical History: The evaluation team will review your child's health and medical history to identify any factors that may be contributing to their difficulties.
  • Observation in the Classroom Setting: The evaluation team will observe your child in their classroom environment to assess their behavior, interactions, and academic performance in a natural setting.

Parental Input: Your input as a parent is crucial to the FIE process. The evaluation team must consider your concerns, observations, and information about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and developmental history. Share any relevant information you have with the evaluation team.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school district's evaluation, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. You can only request an IEE if you disagree with the district's evaluation.

Child Find Obligations

Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law, Tomball ISD has a legal obligation to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This is known as "Child Find." This obligation extends to children who are suspected of having a disability, even if they are not yet receiving special education services.

Key Aspects of Child Find:

  • Public Awareness: Tomball ISD must conduct public awareness activities to inform parents and the community about the availability of special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation. This may include information on the district's website, in school newsletters, and at community events.
  • Screening and Referral: Schools often conduct screenings of students to identify those who may be at risk for learning or developmental delays. Parents can also refer their child for an evaluation if they suspect a disability.
  • Responsibility to Evaluate: If the district has reason to suspect that a child has a disability and needs special education services, they have a responsibility to conduct an evaluation, even if the parent has not specifically requested one. This is particularly important if a child is exhibiting significant academic or behavioral difficulties in school.

What Parents Should Know: If you suspect your child has a disability, do not hesitate to contact the school and express your concerns. Even if you are unsure whether your child qualifies for special education services, the school has a responsibility to investigate your concerns and determine if an evaluation is warranted. The district cannot refuse to evaluate a child simply because they are doing well academically in some areas. If a child is suspected of having a disability that impacts their ability to access the general education curriculum, they are entitled to an evaluation.

What If They Refuse

While Tomball ISD is obligated to evaluate children suspected of having disabilities, there are instances where the district may refuse to conduct an evaluation. It's essential to know your rights and what steps to take if this occurs.

Reasons for Refusal: The district may refuse to conduct an evaluation if they believe there is no reasonable suspicion that the child has a disability that requires special education services. This decision must be based on data, observations, and professional judgment.

Your Rights When Refused: If the district refuses to conduct an evaluation, they must provide you with prior written notice of their decision. This notice must include:

  • A description of the action the district proposes to take or refuses to take.
  • An explanation of why the district proposes or refuses to take the action.
  • A description of any other options the district considered and the reasons why those options were rejected.
  • A description of each evaluation procedure, test, record, or report the district used as a basis for the proposed or refused action.
  • A description of any other factors that are relevant to the district's proposal or refusal.
  • Information about your right to appeal the district's decision.

What to Do Next: If you disagree with the district's decision to refuse an evaluation, you have several options:

  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the district reach an agreement. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) offers mediation services free of charge.
  • File a Complaint with the TEA: You can file a formal complaint with the TEA alleging that the district violated the IDEA or Texas special education laws. The TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will issue a decision that is binding on both you and the district. Due process hearings are complex and often require the assistance of an attorney.
  • Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): As mentioned previously, if you disagree with the district's assessment of your child (or lack thereof), you have the right to request an IEE at public expense. If the district refuses to pay for the IEE, they must initiate a due process hearing to defend their evaluation or lack thereof.

Important Considerations: Document everything. Keep copies of all correspondence with the school district, including your written request for an evaluation, the district's refusal to evaluate, and any other relevant documents. Seek legal advice. Consult with a special education attorney or advocate to understand your rights and options. They can provide guidance on navigating the special education process and representing your child's interests.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is completed, the next step is the Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting. This meeting is crucial for determining your child's eligibility for special education services and developing an Individualized Education Program (IEP), if applicable.

The ARD Committee Meeting: The ARD committee is a team that includes you (the parent), school administrators, teachers, evaluation personnel, and other relevant professionals. The purpose of the ARD committee meeting is to:

  • Review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under one of the 13 disability categories defined by the IDEA.
  • If your child is eligible for special education services, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the supports and services they will receive.
  • Determine the appropriate placement for your child, which refers to the setting where they will receive their special education services. This could include the general education classroom with supports, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom.

Key Components of the IEP: If your child is found eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's educational program. It must include:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance: This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in academic and functional areas.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: These are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year.
  • Special Education and Related Services: This section lists the specific services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: These are changes to the curriculum or instruction that will help your child access the general education curriculum and achieve their goals. Accommodations do not change the content of the material, while modifications do.
  • Placement: This specifies the setting where your child will receive their special education services.
  • Dates and Duration: This specifies when the services will begin, how often they will be provided, and for how long.
  • Progress Monitoring: This describes how your child's progress towards their goals will be measured and reported to you.

Your Role in the ARD Committee: As a parent, you are an essential member of the ARD committee. You have the right to participate fully in the ARD committee meeting and to express your opinions and concerns. The ARD committee must consider your input when making decisions about your child's IEP. You have the right to disagree with the ARD committee's recommendations and to request changes to the IEP. If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to file a complaint with the TEA or request a due process hearing.

Annual Review: The IEP must be reviewed at least annually to determine if the goals are being met and if any changes are needed. You have the right to request an ARD committee meeting at any time if you have concerns about your child's progress or the effectiveness of their IEP.

Remember, you are your child's best advocate. Understanding your rights and actively participating in the special education process will help ensure that your child receives the appropriate supports and services to reach their full potential. Don't hesitate to seek assistance from special education advocates or attorneys if you need help navigating the process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
