Tomball ISD ARD Meeting Guide for Parents

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a vital part of your child's special education journey in Tomball ISD. It's a formal meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code (TEC) Section 29.005, designed to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific educational support and services your child needs to thrive in school. The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to ensure your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) tailored to their unique needs. It's a collaborative process where you, as the parent, are a crucial member of the team.

The ARD committee consists of several key individuals who bring different perspectives and expertise to the table. The required members include you, the parent, or parents if both are involved in the child's education; at least one general education teacher (if your child is, or may be, participating in the general education environment); at least one special education teacher or provider; a representative of the school district who is knowledgeable about the general curriculum and the availability of district resources; a person who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results; and the student, when appropriate. Other individuals may be invited to the meeting at the discretion of you or the school district, such as therapists, counselors, diagnosticians, or outside experts who have specific knowledge or expertise related to your child's needs. It's important to remember that you have the right to invite anyone you believe can contribute valuable information to the discussion. These meetings are typically held annually to review progress and make necessary adjustments, but can also be called at any time if either you or the school staff believe a change to the IEP is needed.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Tomball ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the scheduled date. This is a crucial procedural safeguard ensuring you have adequate time to prepare for the meeting. This notice must include several key pieces of information, including the purpose of the meeting (for example, annual review, initial eligibility determination, or addressing a specific concern), the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of individuals who will be attending the meeting. The notice should also inform you of your right to participate in the ARD committee and to invite other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. It's essential to carefully review this notice and contact the school if any information is missing or unclear.

While the district is obligated to provide this five-day notice, you, as the parent, have the right to waive this requirement. If you agree, in writing, that the ARD meeting can be held sooner than five days from the notice, the meeting can proceed. This might be necessary or preferable in situations where a pressing issue needs to be addressed quickly, or if the originally scheduled meeting date is inconvenient for you. The school will provide a waiver form for you to sign should you choose to waive the five-day notice.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is essential for ensuring their needs are adequately addressed. Consider bringing the following items to the meeting:

First, gather any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether conducted by the school district or outside professionals. These documents provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they may need support. This includes evaluations from doctors, therapists, or educational psychologists. These evaluations can help inform the team about your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance.

Next, bring any relevant medical documentation, such as letters from doctors or therapists, outlining your child's medical condition, diagnosis, and any recommendations for accommodations or services. This is especially important if your child's disability has a medical component or requires specific medical interventions during the school day.

Gather any school records that might be relevant, such as report cards, progress reports, work samples, or discipline records. These documents can provide valuable information about your child's academic performance, behavior, and overall progress in school. This helps everyone see the full picture of your child's educational journey.

Create a written list of your specific concerns and questions you want to address during the ARD meeting. Having a prepared list ensures you don't forget anything important and allows you to clearly communicate your concerns to the ARD committee. Be as specific as possible when describing your concerns and provide examples whenever possible.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, advocate from a disability organization, or educational consultant. Having someone with you can provide emotional support, help you take notes, and ensure your voice is heard during the meeting. The advocate can ask questions, clarify information, and help you understand your rights and options.

Finally, remember that in Texas, it is legal to record the ARD meeting, provided you notify the ARD committee that you will be recording. A simple announcement at the beginning of the meeting is sufficient. Having a recording can be helpful for reviewing the discussion and ensuring accurate documentation of the decisions made during the meeting. It is recommended to use a reliable voice recorder and ensure it is functioning properly before the meeting begins. Informing the team beforehand fosters transparency and trust.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a crucial safeguard that empowers you and other members of the ARD committee to ensure thoughtful and informed decision-making. According to Texas Education Code, any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess of up to 10 school days before making final decisions regarding your child's IEP. This recess allows time to gather additional information, consult with experts, or simply reflect on the discussions and proposals made during the meeting. You can invoke the 10-day recess rule if you feel you need more time to consider the information presented or to seek outside advice before agreeing to the IEP. For example, you might want time to review new evaluation data with a therapist or advocate, or to research a specific service or accommodation discussed during the meeting.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request during the ARD meeting. There is no specific form to complete. Clearly express that you would like to recess the meeting for up to 10 school days to allow you time to gather more information or consult with someone before making a decision. The ARD committee must honor your request. The meeting will then be reconvened within those 10 school days to finalize the IEP. Remember, this rule is in place to protect your right to participate meaningfully in the IEP development process and to ensure your child's needs are met.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program, or IEP, is the cornerstone of your child's special education experience. It's a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services and supports they will receive to achieve those goals. Several key components are decided upon during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP.

First, the IEP includes a section on "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" (commonly referred to as "Present Levels"). This section describes your child's current academic skills, social-emotional functioning, and other areas relevant to their education. It's based on various sources of information, including evaluations, assessments, classroom observations, and your input as the parent. This section paints a picture of where your child currently is in their educational journey.

Based on the Present Levels, the ARD committee develops measurable annual goals for your child. These goals are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They outline what your child is expected to achieve within a year in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, behavior, or social skills. These are the targets you want your child to hit during the school year.

The IEP also specifies the special education and related services your child will receive. These services may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or assistive technology. The IEP will detail the frequency, duration, and location of these services. This section describes what the school will do to help your child reach those goals.

Placement refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. The law requires that children with disabilities be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP will specify the percentage of time your child will spend in the general education classroom and the special education setting. This section determines where the services will take place.

Finally, the IEP includes a list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom. Accommodations are changes to how the student learns the material (e.g., extended time on tests, preferential seating), while modifications are changes to what the student is expected to learn (e.g., modified assignments, reduced workload). These adjustments help your child access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. These ensure your child has what they need to succeed.

It's crucial to understand that you do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully and consult with others before providing your consent. You also have the right to agree with parts of the IEP and disagree with other parts.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is important to remember that you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. If you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions regarding your child's IEP, you have several rights and options available to you.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you disagree with the school's recommendation for special education services, you can decline to sign the consent form. In this case, the school cannot proceed with providing special education services to your child.

Second, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document, even if your child is already receiving special education services. Signing the IEP indicates that you agree with the plan as a whole. If you disagree with certain aspects of the IEP, you can refuse to sign it. This does not necessarily mean that the IEP cannot be implemented, but it does trigger certain procedural safeguards, requiring the school to attempt to resolve the disagreement.

Third, if you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can be used to inform the ARD committee's decisions. There are some limits to this; the school can challenge your request, but you are entitled to a hearing.

Finally, even if you sign the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document next to any section with which you disagree. This indicates that you do not agree with that particular aspect of the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action. This is a way to document your dissent and preserve your legal options.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee at the local level, you have several formal options available to you, governed by the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process, IDEA 2004, and 19 Texas Administrative Code Chapter 89.

First, you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process. TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of state or federal special education laws or regulations. This is a good option if you believe the school district has failed to follow proper procedures or has violated your child's rights under IDEA. You can find the complaint form and instructions on the TEA website. This is often the quickest and simplest formal avenue to take.

Second, you can request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. This is also free of charge. A trained mediator facilitates communication and helps you and the school district explore options for resolving the dispute. Mediation can be a valuable tool for building understanding and finding common ground. TEA provides a list of qualified mediators who are familiar with special education law and practice. It is a less adversarial approach and can preserve the working relationship with the school.

Third, you have the right to request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a binding decision. This process can be more complex and time-consuming than a TEA complaint or mediation, and it may be advisable to seek legal representation. Due process hearings are governed by specific timelines and procedures outlined in IDEA and Texas Education Code. It involves formal legal proceedings.

Fourth, you can file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action if a violation is found. This is a separate process from the other options and is available if you believe your child's civil rights have been violated.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
