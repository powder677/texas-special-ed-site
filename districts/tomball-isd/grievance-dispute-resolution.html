Dispute & Discipline Rights in Tomball ISD

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Tomball ISD, like all Texas public schools, can suspend a student for disciplinary reasons. A critical point to remember is the 10-day suspension rule. This rule has significant implications, particularly for students with disabilities under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

What is the 10-Day Suspension Rule? Tomball ISD can suspend a student for up to 10 cumulative school days in a single academic year without triggering certain procedural safeguards under IDEA. However, once a student reaches 10 cumulative days of suspension, IDEA protections kick in. This includes requirements related to Manifestation Determination Reviews (MDRs) and potential Changes of Placement.

Why is this important? Keeping track of your child's suspension days is vital. A seemingly minor incident could push your child over the 10-day threshold, triggering rights that can prevent further inappropriate disciplinary actions, especially if your child has an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or 504 plan.

What to do:

  • Keep Records: Maintain a detailed record of every suspension your child receives. Note the date, length of suspension, and the reason for the suspension.
  • Communicate with the School: Regularly communicate with your child's teachers, counselors, and administrators regarding any disciplinary concerns. Ask for clarification about the school's disciplinary policies and procedures.
  • Know Your Rights: If your child has an IEP or 504 plan, familiarize yourself with their rights under IDEA. Specifically, understand what happens when your child exceeds 10 cumulative suspension days.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a critical meeting required under IDEA when a student with a disability (IEP or 504) is facing disciplinary action that could result in a Change of Placement (explained below), or after they have accumulated more than 10 days of suspension in a school year.

Purpose of the MDR: The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's behavior leading to the disciplinary action was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, their disability. It also examines whether the conduct in question was the direct result of the Tomball ISD's failure to implement the student's IEP.

Who Attends the MDR Meeting? The MDR meeting typically includes:

  • Parents/Guardians
  • Relevant members of the IEP team (e.g., teachers, special education staff, administrators)
  • The student (if appropriate)
  • Other relevant school personnel who have knowledge of the student's behavior and disability.

What Happens at the MDR Meeting?

  1. Review of Information: The team reviews all relevant information, including the student's IEP, teacher observations, disciplinary records, and any other relevant documentation.
  2. Determination: The team determines whether the student's conduct was a manifestation of their disability. This is crucial.

Possible Outcomes of the MDR:

  • Manifestation: If the team determines the behavior *was* a manifestation of the disability, Tomball ISD must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA), implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) or revise the existing one. The student returns to their current placement (unless the parents and Tomball ISD agree otherwise).
  • No Manifestation: If the team determines the behavior *was not* a manifestation of the disability, Tomball ISD can discipline the student in the same manner as a student without a disability. However, the school must continue to provide special education services during any period of suspension or expulsion. This is often where schools go wrong, so carefully monitor that all services are provided according to the IEP.

Important Considerations:

  • Parental Involvement: Your active participation in the MDR is essential. Bring any relevant information that supports your position. Don't be afraid to disagree with the school if you believe their assessment is inaccurate.
  • Independent Evaluation: If you disagree with the MDR decision, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense (under certain circumstances).

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or when a series of removals constitutes a pattern. A pattern exists when the removals total more than 10 school days in a school year, the behavior is similar to previous behaviors that resulted in removals, and there are additional factors such as the length of each removal, the total amount of time the child is removed, and the proximity of the removals to each other. Suspensions exceeding 10 days can trigger a Change of Placement and require an MDR.

Examples of Change of Placement:

  • Expulsion
  • Placement in an alternative educational setting (e.g., Disciplinary Alternative Education Program - DAEP) for more than 10 days, if that placement is not the one determined by the IEP.

What to do if your child faces a Change of Placement:

  • Demand an MDR: Immediately request an MDR meeting. Do not allow Tomball ISD to proceed with a Change of Placement without completing this crucial process.
  • Review the IEP: Ensure your child's IEP is up-to-date and appropriately addresses their behavioral needs. If the IEP is inadequate, advocate for revisions.
  • Consider Legal Counsel: Consult with an education attorney. Changes of Placement can have significant long-term consequences for your child's education.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe Tomball ISD has violated your child's rights or acted inappropriately in a disciplinary matter, you have the right to file a grievance. Tomball ISD has a formal grievance policy outlined in their Student Handbook and on their website. Understanding the grievance process is critical.

Level 1 Grievance: This is the initial step in the grievance process. It involves submitting a written complaint to the appropriate administrator (usually the principal or a designated campus administrator).

How to File a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Form: Find the official Tomball ISD grievance form. It may be available on the district website or from the school administration office.
  2. Prepare Your Written Statement: Clearly and concisely describe the specific incident or issue you are grieving. Include dates, times, names of individuals involved, and any relevant documentation. Be factual and avoid emotional language.
  3. State the Violation: Clearly state which specific Tomball ISD policy, procedure, or state/federal law you believe was violated. Refer to the Student Handbook or relevant legal resources.
  4. Propose a Resolution: Clearly state what outcome you are seeking. What specific action do you want Tomball ISD to take to resolve the issue?
  5. Submit the Grievance: Submit the completed grievance form to the designated administrator within the specified timeframe (usually within 15 school days of the incident). Keep a copy for your records.

What to Expect After Filing: The administrator will investigate the grievance and provide a written response within a specified timeframe (again, usually within 10 school days). If you are not satisfied with the Level 1 response, you can appeal to Level 2.

Tips for Filing a Grievance:

  • Be Timely: Adhere to all deadlines. Late filings may not be considered.
  • Be Specific: Provide as much detail as possible.
  • Keep Records: Maintain copies of all documents, correspondence, and communications related to the grievance.
  • Seek Support: Consider consulting with an advocate or attorney to help you prepare and present your grievance.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe that Tomball ISD has violated a state or federal special education law (IDEA) and the local grievance process has failed to resolve the issue, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

How to File a TEA Complaint:

  • Gather Information: Collect all relevant documentation, including IEPs, evaluations, disciplinary records, correspondence with Tomball ISD, and grievance filings.
  • Complete the TEA Complaint Form: Obtain the official TEA complaint form from the TEA website.
  • Clearly State the Allegation: Specifically describe the alleged violation of special education law.
  • Provide Supporting Documentation: Attach copies of all relevant documents to support your complaint.
  • Submit the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA within the specified timeframe (usually one year from the date of the alleged violation).

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision. If TEA finds that Tomball ISD violated special education law, they may order corrective action.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal procedure available to parents of children with disabilities when they disagree with Tomball ISD regarding their child's identification, evaluation, educational placement, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE).

When to File for Due Process: You can file for due process when you have exhausted other dispute resolution options (like grievances and mediation) or if you believe that those options are unlikely to resolve the issue.

How to File for Due Process: Filing for due process involves submitting a written complaint to TEA, which then notifies Tomball ISD. The complaint must meet specific legal requirements.

Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding before an impartial hearing officer. Both you and Tomball ISD can present evidence, call witnesses, and argue your case.

Legal Representation: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. It is strongly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law.

Important Considerations for Due Process:

  • Statute of Limitations: There are strict deadlines for filing for due process. Don't delay.
  • Exhaustion of Remedies: In some cases, you may be required to exhaust other administrative remedies (like filing a state complaint) before filing for due process.

This guide is not exhaustive, and the legal landscape is constantly changing. Protect your child! Seek expert legal advice if you are experiencing disciplinary issues with Tomball ISD.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
