Dyslexia Services in Tomball ISD

Dyslexia Services in Tomball ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available within Tomball Independent School District (ISD) in accordance with Texas state law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. It outlines key components of the district's approach to identifying and supporting students with dyslexia. Please note that policies and procedures are subject to change, so it is always best to consult with school personnel for the most up-to-date information.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is the guiding document for dyslexia identification and intervention in Texas public schools. The 2024 update incorporates recent legal changes and best practices in dyslexia instruction. Tomball ISD adheres to the guidelines set forth in this handbook.

Key Principles of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook:

  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The handbook emphasizes a multi-tiered approach to provide increasingly intensive support to students struggling with reading. This includes universal screening, targeted intervention, and intensive intervention.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook mandates the use of structured literacy approaches in dyslexia intervention programs.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook outlines specific procedures for screening, evaluation, and identification of students with dyslexia.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the importance of parental involvement in the dyslexia identification and intervention process.
  • Professional Development: The handbook stresses the need for ongoing professional development for teachers and staff on dyslexia and related disorders.

Tomball ISD implements practices aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook to ensure that students with dyslexia receive appropriate identification, evaluation, and intervention services.

Mandatory Screening

Tomball ISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade, as required by Texas law. Additional screenings may occur in subsequent grades if concerns arise about a student's reading progress.

Screening Components:

  • Phonological Awareness: Assessing the student's ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds of language (e.g., rhyming, segmenting, blending).
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): Measuring the speed at which a student can name familiar objects, colors, letters, or numbers.
  • Letter-Sound Knowledge: Evaluating the student's ability to associate letters with their corresponding sounds.
  • Decoding Skills: Assessing the student's ability to read words, both real and nonsense words.

Students who demonstrate indicators of dyslexia on the screening measures will be referred for further evaluation to determine if they meet the criteria for a dyslexia diagnosis.

Parent Notification: Parents will be notified of the screening results and any subsequent actions taken.

Structured Literacy Programs

Tomball ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to reading instruction that focuses on the foundational skills necessary for reading success.

Key Elements of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Instruction in the sounds of language (phonemes) and how they are blended and segmented.
  • Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics): Explicit teaching of the relationships between letters and sounds.
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching the different types of syllables and how they are combined to form words.
  • Morphology: Instruction in the meaning units of language (morphemes), such as prefixes, suffixes, and roots.
  • Syntax: Instruction in sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: Instruction in vocabulary and reading comprehension strategies.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for accommodations and services through either a 504 Plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The appropriate plan depends on the severity of the student's needs and the impact of dyslexia on their access to the general education curriculum.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: 504 plans primarily provide accommodations to level the playing field and ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Accommodations may include preferential seating, extended time on tests, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation for 504 eligibility involves a review of existing data, including screening results, teacher observations, and parent input.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability, such as a Specific Learning Disability (SLD) including dyslexia, that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: IEPs provide specialized instruction and related services designed to address the student's unique needs and enable them to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Services: IEPs may include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and other related services, in addition to accommodations.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation for IEP eligibility involves a comprehensive evaluation conducted by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and specialists. This evaluation will likely include standardized assessments to measure reading, writing and spelling skills.

Key Differences:

  • Scope of Services: IEPs provide a broader range of services, including specialized instruction, while 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations.
  • Eligibility Criteria: The eligibility criteria for IEPs are more stringent than those for 504 plans. IEP eligibility requires a disability that adversely affects educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), while 504 plans are governed by Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

The decision of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the evaluation team.

Accommodations List

The following is a list of common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia through a 504 plan or an IEP. The specific accommodations provided will be determined based on the student's individual needs.

  • Extended Time: Providing additional time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length, format, or complexity of assignments.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the student to use a calculator for math assignments.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Providing visual aids to help the student organize information.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Using visual, auditory, and kinesthetic methods to teach concepts.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take breaks as needed.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet space for the student to take tests.
  • Dictation Software: Allowing the student to dictate their written responses.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check: Enabling the use of word processing software with spell check features.
  • Highlighted Textbooks: Providing textbooks with key information highlighted.
  • Chunking of Assignments: Breaking down large assignments into smaller, more manageable tasks.
  • Visual Supports: Using visuals such as pictures, charts, and diagrams to support learning.
  • Alternative Assessments: Offering alternative ways for the student to demonstrate their knowledge, such as projects or presentations.

This list is not exhaustive, and other accommodations may be appropriate depending on the student's individual needs.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from Tomball ISD.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, counselor, or principal to express your concerns about your child's reading difficulties.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education department. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a detailed description of your concerns.
  3. Provide Supporting Documentation: Include any supporting documentation, such as previous testing results, report cards, and teacher observations.
  4. Attend an ARD/504 Meeting: Once the school receives your request, they will schedule an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting (for potential IEP eligibility) or a 504 committee meeting to discuss your child's needs and determine if an evaluation is warranted.
  5. Evaluation Process: If the committee determines that an evaluation is necessary, the school will conduct a comprehensive evaluation to assess your child's reading skills.
  6. Review of Evaluation Results: After the evaluation is completed, the committee will meet again to review the results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services or a 504 plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
