What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is the cornerstone of special education services for students in Wylie ISD who have been identified as having a disability that affects their educational performance. It's a meeting, required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code (TEC) §29, where a team comes together to create an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's unique needs. The purpose of the ARD committee is to determine whether your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an IEP that outlines their specific learning needs, and to decide on the most appropriate educational placement for your child.

The ARD committee consists of several key individuals who all play a vital role in the process. You, as the parent, are the most important member of the team. Your insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and learning style are invaluable. Other members typically include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education, usually an administrator or special education director; your child's general education teacher; your child's special education teacher; an evaluation specialist, such as a diagnostician or educational psychologist, who can interpret evaluation results; and, whenever appropriate, your child. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited, such as related service providers (e.g., speech therapist, occupational therapist, counselor). You have the right to invite anyone you feel can contribute to the meeting and advocate for your child's needs.

The ARD meeting is where important decisions are made about your child's education, and it's essential that you understand your rights and responsibilities throughout the process. The team will discuss your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, meaning where they are currently performing in different areas. Based on this, the team will create measurable annual goals to help your child make progress. The IEP will also specify the special education and related services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, therapy, or accommodations. Finally, the team will determine the appropriate educational placement, which could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This notice is not just a formality; it's designed to give you adequate time to prepare, gather information, and arrange for any support you may need. The notice must include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location of the meeting, a list of individuals who will be attending, and a description of the issues that will be discussed. This allows you to understand what to expect and come prepared to participate meaningfully in the process.

It's crucial to carefully review the notice and ensure that all the information is accurate. If you have any questions or concerns about the meeting, don't hesitate to contact the school to clarify them. If the proposed meeting time doesn't work for you, you have the right to request a different time or date. The school is required to make reasonable efforts to accommodate your schedule.

In some cases, you may wish to waive the five-day notice requirement and agree to meet sooner. This might be necessary if there is an urgent issue that needs to be addressed or if you simply prefer to expedite the process. However, it's important to understand that waiving your right to notice means you're giving up some of your preparation time. You should only waive the notice if you feel fully prepared and comfortable proceeding with the meeting.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Being well-prepared for your child's ARD meeting can make a significant difference in the outcome. Gather any relevant documents and information that can help the team understand your child's needs and develop an effective IEP. This may include copies of previous evaluations, both from the school and from outside professionals, such as psychologists, therapists, or medical doctors. These reports can provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and learning style. Bring any letters or reports from doctors or other healthcare providers that document your child's medical or developmental history. This information can be helpful in understanding how any medical conditions may be affecting your child's education.

It is also a good idea to gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and work samples. These documents can provide a clear picture of your child's academic performance and areas where they may be struggling. Before the meeting, take some time to write down your specific concerns about your child's education. This could include concerns about their academic progress, behavior, social skills, or any other area that you feel needs attention. Having a written list will help you stay focused and ensure that you address all of your concerns during the meeting.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can help you understand the process, ask questions, and ensure that your voice is heard. In Texas, it is legal to record the ARD meeting, provided that you inform the other members of the team that you are doing so. A recording can be a valuable tool for reviewing the discussion and ensuring that all agreements are accurately documented.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a critical component of the ARD process in Texas, designed to ensure that all team members, including you as the parent, have sufficient time to gather information, consult with experts, or simply reflect on the discussion before making final decisions about your child's IEP. At any point during the ARD meeting, any member of the ARD committee, including you, can request a recess of up to 10 school days. This recess is not a punishment or a delay tactic; it's a tool to allow for more informed and thoughtful decision-making.

You might invoke the 10-day recess rule if you feel that you need more information before making a decision, perhaps because you don't understand a particular aspect of the proposed IEP, or because you want to seek a second opinion from an outside professional. You may also need the time to consult with an advocate to better understand your rights and options. The school cannot deny your request for a recess. To invoke the rule, simply state clearly that you are requesting a recess of up to 10 school days. It's a good idea to put this request in writing as well, to ensure that it's properly documented.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the written record of everything that is decided at the ARD meeting. It's a legally binding document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services they will receive. The IEP covers several key areas, including present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, which describe your child's current skills and challenges in different areas. Based on these present levels, the IEP will include measurable annual goals that your child is expected to achieve within a year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals).

The IEP also specifies the special education and related services that your child will receive. Special education services might include specialized instruction, modifications to the curriculum, or assistive technology. Related services might include speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. The IEP will also detail any accommodations that will be provided to your child, such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of a computer. Finally, the IEP will specify your child's educational placement, which refers to the setting where your child will receive their education. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or a self-contained classroom.

It is important to understand that you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek advice from others before making a decision. Your signature indicates that you attended the ARD meeting, and have received a copy of the IEP. You do not have to agree with every part of the IEP in order to sign it.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly acceptable to disagree with the ARD team. Your input is crucial, and you have specific rights if you feel your child's needs are not being adequately addressed. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the school's recommendation to place your child in special education, you can refuse to sign the IEP and your child will not receive special education services. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP even after your child has been receiving services. You can disagree with portions of the IEP, and you are encouraged to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document itself, specifically noting which aspects you disagree with and why.

If you disagree with the evaluations the school has conducted, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can be used to inform the ARD team's decisions about your child's IEP. It's crucial to document your disagreement clearly and concisely. This creates a record of your concerns and preserves your rights.

Escalation Options

If, after attempting to resolve your concerns with the ARD team, you still believe that your child's rights are being violated or that the IEP is not appropriate, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These rights are governed by the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process: IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89.

Your first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law.

Your second option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also free and can be a valuable way to resolve disputes without resorting to more formal legal proceedings.

Your third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's IEP. Due Process Hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is generally advisable to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that your child has been discriminated against on the basis of their disability. OCR complaints can be filed if you believe the district has violated Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act. These complaints must allege discrimination based on disability.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
