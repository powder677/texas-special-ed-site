What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting

Welcome to the world of special education in Crowley ISD! If your child has been identified as potentially needing special education services, you will be invited to what's called an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. Think of it as a gathering of important people who will work together to create a plan that will help your child succeed in school. This isn't just something the school district decided to do; it's actually required by federal law, specifically the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), and by Texas state law, specifically Texas Education Code Section 29. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine if your child has a disability that requires special education and related services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's unique needs if they do, and to review and revise that IEP at least once a year. It's also used to decide when a student no longer needs special education services and can be dismissed. The ARD committee will decide things like what specially designed instruction and related services your child needs, where those services will be provided (the "placement"), and what accommodations or modifications will help your child access the general education curriculum.

Who attends these meetings? Well, it's a team effort! The key members typically include you, the parent (you are the most important member!), at least one of your child's general education teachers, at least one of your child's special education teachers or providers, a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education (often called the "LEA representative"), and someone who can interpret the evaluation results (this could be the special education teacher or a diagnostician). Your child may also attend the meeting, especially as they get older, to participate in the discussion and share their own thoughts and goals. Others may also be invited, such as therapists, counselors, or other specialists who work with your child, if their input is relevant. You also have the right to invite anyone you feel would be beneficial to the team, such as an advocate, family member, or friend who can support you.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Before an ARD meeting takes place, Crowley ISD is required to provide you with written notice at least five calendar days in advance. This isn't just a suggestion; it's your right! This gives you time to prepare for the meeting and gather any information you want to share with the team. The notice must include several important details, such as the purpose of the meeting (is it an initial ARD, an annual review, or something else?), the date, time, and location of the meeting, who will be attending the meeting, and a statement informing you of your parental rights. It should also tell you what the district plans to discuss at the meeting. It's crucial to carefully review this notice to ensure you understand what the meeting will be about and who will be there. If you do not feel prepared for the meeting, due to the timing or lack of information on the notice, you can request the meeting be rescheduled.

Now, sometimes you might want to have the ARD meeting sooner than five days. Perhaps a situation has arisen that needs immediate attention, or maybe you simply want to get the ball rolling as quickly as possible. In that case, you can waive your right to the five-day notice in writing. This means you're agreeing to have the meeting sooner than the required timeframe. You'll likely be asked to sign a waiver indicating that you understand your right to the five-day notice and that you're voluntarily giving it up. Please note that you are never obligated to sign this waiver and can always request the full five days to prepare. It is always recommended to take the time to fully prepare for an ARD meeting.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but bringing the right information and support can make a huge difference. Think of yourself as the expert on your child, and the ARD meeting as a chance to share that expertise with the school. First, gather any prior evaluations or assessments that have been done on your child, whether they were conducted by the school district, a private psychologist, or another professional. These reports can provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they might need support. Next, collect any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other healthcare providers who work with your child. These documents can provide medical context for your child's educational needs and help the team understand any relevant medical conditions or diagnoses. Also, bring any school records that you think are relevant, such as report cards, work samples, or notes from teachers. These can give the team a clearer picture of your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom.

Beyond existing records, consider creating a written list of your specific concerns about your child's education. This could include academic challenges, social-emotional difficulties, or anything else that you believe is impacting their learning. Having a written list will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. It is also highly recommended to bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can help you understand the information presented, ask questions, and voice your concerns. An advocate can be particularly helpful if you feel intimidated or overwhelmed by the process. Finally, and this is very important, remember that Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. This means that you can legally record the ARD meeting, even without the school's permission, as long as you are a party to the conversation. However, it's always a good practice to inform the team that you will be recording the meeting out of courtesy. A recording can be invaluable for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that everyone is on the same page.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During an ARD meeting, it's possible that you or another member of the ARD committee may need more time to gather information, consider options, or consult with experts before making a final decision. Texas law provides for what's known as a "recess," which is essentially a temporary break in the ARD meeting. Any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, can request a recess of up to 10 school days. This means the meeting will be paused for up to two weeks of instruction, excluding weekends and holidays, allowing time to address outstanding questions or concerns.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request during the ARD meeting. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation, but it's helpful to briefly explain why you need the recess. For example, you might say, "I would like to request a recess of 10 school days to consult with an educational advocate about the proposed IEP goals," or "I would like a recess to obtain an independent evaluation of my child's reading skills." The ARD committee is required to grant your request for a recess, as long as it's within the 10-school-day limit. The meeting will then be reconvened after the recess period to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP. Remember that using the recess rule is your right, and it can be a valuable tool for ensuring that you have all the information and support you need to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The main outcome of an ARD meeting is the Individualized Education Program, or IEP. This is a written document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the services they will receive to meet those goals. The IEP is developed collaboratively by the ARD committee, and it's essential that you understand each section of the document. One of the first sections of the IEP is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance," often called the "Present Levels." This section describes your child's current academic and functional skills, including their strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they need support. It should be based on a variety of sources, such as evaluations, assessments, classroom observations, and parent input.

Next, the IEP will outline annual goals for your child. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). For example, a goal might be, "By the end of the school year, [child's name] will improve their reading comprehension skills from a first-grade level to a second-grade level as measured by classroom assessments and progress monitoring." The IEP will also detail the specific special education and related services that your child will receive. This could include things like specially designed instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. The IEP will also specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services. The IEP also describes your child's "placement," which refers to the educational setting where they will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom with support, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The IEP will also list any accommodations and modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, while modifications are changes to what your child learns. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. Modifications might include simplified assignments or a reduced workload. Remember that you do not have to sign the IEP the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that you are a key member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. If you disagree with the ARD committee's recommendations, you have several rights and options. For initial placement, you have the right to refuse consent. This means that the school cannot place your child in special education without your written permission. If you refuse to sign the IEP, the school district cannot implement the services in that plan. If you disagree with the evaluations used to determine your child's needs, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can be used to inform the ARD committee's decisions.

Even if you ultimately agree to sign the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document. This indicates that you have reservations about certain aspects of the IEP and that you want to document your disagreement. Writing "parent disagrees" does not prevent the school from implementing the IEP, but it preserves your right to pursue further action if necessary. It is essential to document your concerns in writing as well as verbally at the meeting. It is also helpful to request the school district document the discussion and any disagreements in the ARD meeting minutes.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee and you're still not satisfied, you have several formal options for escalation. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. This complaint must be based on violations of IDEA or Texas Education Code. Another option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third party to try to reach a mutually agreeable solution. This is also a free service provided by TEA. If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer not to pursue mediation, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a binding decision. This can be a costly process. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. It's crucial to understand your rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Chapter 89 of Title 19 of the Texas Administrative Code, which is available online in the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process. These laws outline your rights and the school district's responsibilities in providing your child with a free and appropriate public education. Navigating the special education system can be complex, but remember that you are not alone. There are many resources available to support you, including parent training and information centers, disability rights organizations, and legal aid services.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
