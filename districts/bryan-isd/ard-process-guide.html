What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their ability to learn in the general education setting, they are likely eligible for special education services within Bryan ISD. An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a critical component of providing those services. It's a meeting specifically designed to develop and review your child's Individualized Education Program, often referred to as an IEP. The ARD committee is the team that makes decisions about your child's special education. The entire process is mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code 29, ensuring that students with disabilities receive a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs.

The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to collaboratively create, review, and, when appropriate, dismiss a student from special education services. This involves determining whether a student is eligible for special education, developing appropriate educational goals, determining the supports and services needed to achieve those goals, and deciding the most appropriate educational placement for your child. Essentially, the ARD committee determines what special education and related services your child needs to thrive in school. The committee must also consider your child's strengths, parental concerns, and the results of any evaluations or assessments when making these decisions.

The ARD committee is composed of several required members. You, as the parent, are a crucial and equal member of the team. In fact, you are considered the most important member, as you know your child best. Other required members typically include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of specially designed instruction (usually an administrator or special education director), your child's special education teacher (or, if your child is being evaluated for the first time, someone knowledgeable about the student's suspected disability), a general education teacher if the child is, or may be, participating in the regular education environment, and an evaluation personnel member who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results. Depending on the circumstances, other individuals may also be invited to attend, such as related service providers (speech therapist, occupational therapist, counselor), or other specialists who can provide valuable input. Your child may also be invited to participate in the ARD meeting, particularly as they get older, to share their own perspectives and goals.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Bryan ISD, like all school districts in Texas, is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting takes place. This notice is designed to give you ample time to prepare, gather relevant information, and arrange for any support you may want to bring with you. The purpose of this notice is to ensure that you are an informed and active participant in the decision-making process regarding your child's education. Don't hesitate to reach out to the school if you do not receive this notice within the required timeframe.

This written notice must include specific information about the ARD meeting. It should clearly state the purpose of the meeting (for example, initial eligibility determination, annual review, or change of placement). It must also list the date, time, and location of the meeting. Crucially, the notice should identify who will be attending the meeting, including their roles and titles, so you know who will be present and what perspectives they represent. Furthermore, the notice must inform you of your right to invite other individuals to the meeting who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. This could include a therapist, doctor, or advocate. The notice should be written in a language you understand, and if needed, the district must provide interpretation services to ensure clear communication.

While the district is obligated to provide you with this 5-day notice, you have the option to waive this requirement in writing. This might be necessary if an urgent situation arises, such as a significant change in your child's needs or behavior, that requires an immediate ARD meeting. If you choose to waive your right to the 5-day notice, make sure you understand the reasons for doing so and that you feel adequately prepared to participate in the meeting effectively. The district cannot pressure you into waiving this right, and it's perfectly acceptable to insist on receiving the full 5-day notice if you need it.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is essential to ensure that your voice is heard and that the IEP accurately reflects your child's needs. To maximize your effectiveness, gather the following documents and materials before the meeting.

First, bring copies of any prior evaluations or assessments related to your child's disability. This includes evaluations conducted by the school district, as well as any independent evaluations you may have obtained from private psychologists, therapists, or medical professionals. Having these documents readily available will allow you to refer to specific findings and recommendations during the discussion. Second, provide any letters or reports from your child's doctors or other healthcare providers. These documents can offer valuable insights into your child's medical history, diagnoses, and any treatments they are receiving. These are useful particularly if your child's disability relates to a medical condition.

Third, assemble any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, work samples, or attendance records. These documents can provide a clear picture of your child's academic performance, strengths, and areas where they may be struggling. Sharing these records with the ARD committee can help the team develop appropriate goals and interventions. Fourth, create a written list of your specific concerns and questions regarding your child's education. This list can serve as a guide during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. It's easy to become overwhelmed in the meeting, so having a prepared list can keep you focused. Be as specific as possible when outlining your concerns.

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, educational consultant, or special education advocate. Having someone there to listen, take notes, and offer emotional support can be incredibly helpful. Sixth, remember that in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided that you notify the other members of the ARD committee that you are doing so. A recording can be invaluable for accurately documenting the discussion and ensuring that all agreements are properly implemented. You must announce your intention to record at the beginning of the meeting.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The "10-Day Recess Rule" is a crucial protection for parents (and other ARD committee members) within the special education process in Texas. It allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a temporary pause, or "recess," of the ARD meeting for up to ten school days. This recess provides an opportunity to gather more information, seek clarification, consult with an advocate, or simply to have time to thoughtfully consider the decisions being made before finalizing the IEP.

You might consider invoking the 10-day recess if you feel overwhelmed, confused, or uncertain about a particular aspect of the IEP. Perhaps you need time to review new evaluation data, research a specific service or accommodation, or consult with an outside expert. The recess allows you to make informed decisions without feeling pressured to agree to something you don't fully understand or support. To invoke the 10-day recess, simply state your request clearly during the ARD meeting. There is no specific form or procedure required. You can say something like, "I would like to request a recess of ten school days to review this information and consult with an advocate." The other members of the ARD committee must honor your request.

The ARD committee will then reconvene after the recess period, at a date and time that is mutually agreeable. During the recess, use the time wisely to gather the information or support you need. Remember, the purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop an IEP that meets your child's individual needs. The 10-day recess rule is there to empower you to be an active and informed participant in that process. It is your right, and you should not hesitate to use it if you feel it is necessary.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the written record of the decisions made at the ARD meeting. It's a comprehensive plan that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the services they will receive to achieve those goals. It is absolutely critical to understand what goes into the IEP, as it will serve as the roadmap for your child's special education program.

The IEP includes several key components. First, it describes your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section details your child's strengths, weaknesses, and how their disability affects their involvement and progress in the general education curriculum. It's based on data from evaluations, observations, and input from teachers and parents. Second, the IEP outlines annual goals that are measurable and designed to help your child make progress in specific areas. These goals should be challenging yet attainable and directly related to your child's needs identified in the present levels section. The goals are the cornerstone of the IEP, and should be very specific.

Third, the IEP specifies the special education and related services that your child will receive. This may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or other supports needed to help your child achieve their goals. The frequency, duration, and location of these services are also detailed in the IEP. Fourth, the IEP addresses your child's educational placement. This refers to the setting where your child will receive their education, ranging from the general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting. The ARD committee must consider the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning that your child should be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Fifth, the IEP includes any necessary accommodations and modifications that will be implemented in the classroom to support your child's learning. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material (e.g., extended time on tests), while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn (e.g., simplified assignments). It is important to note you do not have to sign the IEP document the same day as the meeting. You can take it home to review.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly acceptable, and often expected, that parents may not always agree with every decision made by the ARD committee. You are your child's most important advocate, and your perspective is vital. The law recognizes your right to disagree and provides several avenues for addressing those disagreements. You have the right to refuse consent for the initial placement of your child in special education. This means that the school district cannot proceed with providing special education services to your child if you do not agree. If you disagree with the IEP developed by the ARD committee, you have the right to refuse to sign the document. Signing the IEP indicates your attendance at the meeting, not necessarily your agreement with its contents. You can write "Parent Attended, Disagrees" next to your signature.

You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that its evaluation was appropriate. This is a powerful tool to ensure an independent assessment of your child's needs. Always request the IEE in writing.

Finally, you can write "Parent Disagrees" directly on the IEP document. This clearly indicates that you do not agree with certain aspects of the plan. Be sure to specify which sections you disagree with and the reasons for your disagreement. This creates a clear record of your concerns and can be helpful if you need to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you have attempted to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions and other means, but you are still unable to reach a satisfactory resolution, there are several formal escalation options available to you. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) 2004 and further specified in Texas Administrative Code, Title 19, Chapter 89, which is often referred to as "TAC 89." These laws protect your rights and ensure that your concerns are addressed.

Your first option is to file a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a formal written complaint alleging that the school district has violated IDEA or state special education laws. There is no cost to file a State Complaint, and TEA is required to investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. You must allege specific violations of special education law. You can find more information and the complaint form on the TEA website. Second, you can request Mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA provides mediation services free of charge. Mediation is a less adversarial approach than other options and can often lead to positive outcomes. Be aware that both sides must agree to go to mediation.

Third, you have the right to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your dispute. Due Process Hearings are similar to court trials, and it is highly recommended to seek legal representation if you pursue this option. The decision of the hearing officer is binding on both you and the school district, unless it is appealed to a court of law. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates allegations of discrimination based on disability. If you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child, you can file a complaint with OCR. There are strict timelines to file a complaint with OCR. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process, available online, is an excellent resource to better understand these rights and procedures.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
