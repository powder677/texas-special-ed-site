What can I do right now if Bryan ISD is ignoring my child's needs?

Submit a written evaluation request today — verbal requests do not start the legal clock. Bryan ISD has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the evaluation. If your child is being disciplined or suspended, that timeline is even more urgent. Use the guides below to take action now.

Special Education in Bryan ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Navigating the world of special education can feel overwhelming, but understanding your rights and the processes involved is the first step to ensuring your child receives the support they deserve. Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law, special education is defined as specially designed instruction, provided at no cost to parents, to meet the unique needs of a child with a disability. This can include everything from academic interventions and therapies to modifications and accommodations in the classroom.

Bryan ISD, like all school districts in Texas, operates under the oversight of the Texas Education Agency (TEA). TEA provides guidance and resources to ensure districts comply with federal and state special education laws. Further support for Bryan ISD comes from TEA Region 6 Education Service Center, one of twenty regional centers across the state that offer training and technical assistance to educators. It's crucial to remember that throughout this process, you, as a parent, have legally enforceable rights. You are an equal member of the team responsible for your child's education.

These rights are guaranteed by law and are designed to ensure that your voice is heard and your child's needs are met. Bryan ISD is obligated to uphold these rights, and you have avenues for recourse if you believe they are being violated. The information provided on this page is intended to empower you to advocate effectively for your child within Bryan ISD's special education system. Arming yourself with knowledge is the best defense and the best way to ensure your child's success.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Bryan ISD

The first step in determining if your child qualifies for special education services is to request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). It is extremely important to understand that this request must be submitted in writing. A verbal request to a teacher or principal, while a good starting point for a conversation, does not officially begin the evaluation timeline. Once Bryan ISD receives your written request and you provide written consent, the district has 45 school days to complete the FIE. This evaluation is designed to assess your child's strengths and weaknesses across various areas to determine if they have a disability that requires special education services.

Furthermore, it's important to know that Bryan ISD has a legal obligation under Child Find laws to proactively identify students who may need special education services. This means the district should be actively looking for students who are struggling and may require evaluation, even if you haven't formally requested one. However, it's always best to be proactive. If you have concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress, don't wait. Write a letter to the school principal or special education director requesting a Full Individual Evaluation. Be sure to keep a copy of the letter for your records and send it via certified mail with return receipt requested, so you have proof that Bryan ISD received it. Your next step is to draft that letter today.

ARD Meetings in Bryan ISD: Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This is the committee that determines whether your child is eligible for special education services, develops their Individualized Education Program (IEP), and makes decisions about their placement. You, as the parent, are a critical member of this committee. Bryan ISD is required to provide you with at least five calendar days' written notice before any ARD meeting. This notice should include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, location, and a list of individuals who will be attending.

During the ARD meeting, you have the right to participate fully in all discussions and decisions. If you feel overwhelmed or need more time to consider the IEP developed during the meeting, you have the right to request a 10-day recess. This means the ARD committee will reconvene within 10 calendar days to continue the discussion. You also are not required to sign the IEP the same day it is presented. You can take it home to review it carefully, consult with advocates or attorneys, and return it with your signature later. Remember, the IEP is a legally binding document, so it's crucial that you understand and agree with all of its components.

Your role in the ARD meeting is paramount. You know your child best, and your input is essential to creating an IEP that will effectively meet their needs. Dyslexia Services in Bryan ISD

Texas law mandates that Bryan ISD screen every student for dyslexia and related disorders. This screening process is designed to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties. The standards for dyslexia identification and intervention are outlined in the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook, which Bryan ISD is required to follow. This handbook provides specific guidance on screening, evaluation, and evidence-based interventions for students with dyslexia.

If your child is identified as having dyslexia, they are entitled to structured literacy intervention designed to address their specific needs. This intervention should be systematic, explicit, and multi-sensory. Depending on the severity of their dyslexia and other factors, your child may also qualify for a 504 Plan or an IEP. A 504 Plan provides accommodations to help students with disabilities access the general education curriculum, while an IEP provides more intensive services and supports. If your child has not been screened for dyslexia and you have concerns about their reading skills, request a dyslexia screening in writing from Bryan ISD today. Don't delay!

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Bryan ISD

Discipline can be a particularly challenging area for students with disabilities. It's crucial to understand that students with IEPs have specific protections when it comes to suspensions and expulsions. If your child with an IEP is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, Bryan ISD must conduct a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This review determines whether the behavior that led to the suspension was caused by or directly related to your child's disability, or whether it was the result of Bryan ISD's failure to implement the IEP.

A suspension of more than 10 cumulative days also constitutes a Change of Placement under IDEA, requiring an ARD meeting to discuss the situation. This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face as a parent of a child with an IEP. You must act quickly to ensure that your child's rights are protected. Bryan ISD must follow specific procedures during the MDR process, and you have the right to participate fully. Don't wait if your child has been suspended - use the Discipline Defense Kit to understand your rights and take action immediately.

Filing a Complaint Against Bryan ISD

If you believe that Bryan ISD is violating your child's special education rights, you have several avenues for recourse. These rights exist regardless of what Bryan ISD staff may tell you. The first is a TEA State Complaint. This is a free process where you file a written complaint with the TEA alleging specific violations of special education law. The TEA has 60 days to investigate and resolve the complaint. Another option is Mediation through TEA, also free, where a neutral third party helps you and Bryan ISD reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Both TEA State Complaints and Mediation are free services to parents.

For more formal action, you can request a Due Process Hearing, which is a more formal legal proceeding before an impartial hearing officer. This can involve legal representation and evidence presentation. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR), alleging discrimination based on disability. Choosing the right path depends on the specific circumstances of your situation. Regardless of which path you choose, remember that you have the right to advocate for your child's needs. Start by understanding each process and deciding which is the right next step for your family.

Frequently Asked Questions About Bryan ISD Special Education

The questions every Bryan ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Bryan ISD? +

Submit a written request to the Bryan ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Bryan ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Bryan ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Bryan ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Bryan ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Bryan ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Bryan ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
