Key Components of Your Child's IEP in COLUMBIA-BRAZORIA ISD

Understanding what belongs in your child's IEP helps you evaluate whether it adequately addresses their needs. Under the COLUMBIA-BRAZORIA ISD IEP process, your child's Individualized Education Program must include specific, required components.

Present Level of Academic and Functional Performance (PLAAFP)

The PLAAFP describes your child's current abilities and challenges. It should paint a clear picture of how your child learns and performs in school and community settings. This section directly informs development of COLUMBIA-BRAZORIA ISD IEP goals.

Measurable Annual Goals

COLUMBIA-BRAZORIA ISD IEP goals must be specific and measurable, with clear criteria for determining whether your child has met them. Goals should address both academic and functional needs, including communication, social skills, independence, and daily living skills when appropriate.

Effective goals follow the SMART framework:

  • Specific: Clearly defines what skill the child will learn
  • Measurable: Includes concrete measurement methods and success criteria
  • Achievable: Realistic for the student within one school year
  • Relevant: Addresses the student's actual educational needs
  • Time-bound: Specifies the timeframe for achievement

Special Education Services and Related Services

Your child's IEP specifies the frequency, duration, and location of special education and related services. For students in COLUMBIA-BRAZORIA special education programs, this might include resource room instruction, speech-language pathology, occupational therapy, counseling, or other supports.

Accommodations and Modifications

Accommodations change how your child accesses the curriculum (extended time, large print materials, small group instruction) without changing what's being taught. Modifications change the actual curriculum content or expectations. Both must be clearly documented in the IEP.

Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) Statement

COLUMBIA-BRAZORIA ISD must document how much time your child spends in general education classes versus separate settings. The law presumes students should be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.
