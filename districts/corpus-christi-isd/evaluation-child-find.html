How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Corpus Christi ISD

This guide provides information for parents and guardians in Corpus Christi ISD who believe their child may need special education services. It outlines the process of requesting a special education evaluation, the legal timelines involved, what the evaluation must cover, the district's responsibilities, what to do if your request is denied, and the steps following the evaluation. This information is based on Texas state law and regulations governing special education.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation for your child in Corpus Christi ISD is to submit a written request to the school. This is crucial for establishing a clear timeline and creating a documented record of your concerns. The request should be directed to the principal of your child's school or the Special Education Director for Corpus Christi ISD. Addressing it to both can be a good strategy to ensure it reaches the appropriate personnel quickly.

Your written request should include the following information:

  • Your child's full name and date of birth: Ensure accuracy to avoid any confusion.
  • Your child's current grade level and school: This helps the school identify your child within their system.
  • Your contact information: Include your phone number and email address for easy communication.
  • A clear statement that you are requesting a Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) for special education services. Use this specific terminology to clearly indicate your intent.
  • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress: Be specific and provide concrete examples. For instance, instead of saying "My child is struggling in math," say "My child is unable to complete basic addition problems and consistently scores below 60% on math tests."
  • Any relevant documentation you have, such as report cards, teacher notes, medical diagnoses, or previous evaluations: Include copies of these documents. Do not send originals.
  • Specify the areas you believe your child is struggling in: Be as specific as possible, such as reading comprehension, written expression, social skills, or attention.
  • A brief summary of any interventions or accommodations that have already been tried: Describe what has been attempted and the results. For example, "The teacher has provided extra help after school, but it has not improved my child's grades."
  • Your signature and the date: This confirms that you are the person making the request.

Keep a copy of your written request for your records. It's recommended to send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school received your request and when they received it. This documentation is crucial if you need to demonstrate that the school is not adhering to the timelines outlined in Texas law.

Here is a sample sentence you can include: "Based on my observations and the concerns outlined above, I believe my child may have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires special education and related services. Therefore, I am requesting a Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) to determine if my child is eligible for special education services."

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once Corpus Christi ISD receives your written request for a special education evaluation, they have a legal obligation to adhere to a strict timeline outlined in the Texas Education Code and related regulations. This timeline is crucial for ensuring that your child's potential needs are addressed promptly.

The school has 45 school days, not calendar days, from the date they receive your written request to complete the evaluation and determine if your child is eligible for special education services. School days are defined as days when students are in attendance. This means holidays, weekends, and school breaks are not included in the 45-day count. It is your responsibility to carefully track this timeline.

Within those 45 school days, the following steps must occur:

  • Review of existing data: The school must review any existing data about your child, including report cards, teacher observations, and any prior evaluations.
  • Parent consent for evaluation: The school must obtain your written consent to proceed with the evaluation. They must provide you with a clear explanation of what the evaluation will entail. You have the right to refuse consent.
  • Evaluation completion: Once you provide consent, the school must conduct the necessary evaluations to assess your child's strengths and weaknesses in all areas related to the suspected disability.
  • Eligibility determination: After the evaluation is complete, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting must be held to determine if your child meets the criteria for a disability under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas law.

It's important to note that the 45-day timeline can be extended under very specific circumstances, such as if the parent repeatedly fails or refuses to make the child available for the evaluation. The school must document any extensions and the reasons for them.

If the school fails to complete the evaluation within the 45-day timeline without a valid reason, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if your child has a disability and, if so, what specific educational needs they have. It's crucial that the FIIE is thorough and addresses all areas related to your concerns.

Texas law requires that the FIIE assess your child in all areas related to the suspected disability. This typically includes, but is not limited to:

  • Academic achievement: This assesses your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. Standardized tests are often used.
  • Intellectual functioning: This evaluates your child's cognitive abilities, such as problem-solving, reasoning, and memory.
  • Adaptive behavior: This assesses your child's ability to function independently in daily life, such as self-care, social skills, and communication.
  • Communication skills: This evaluates your child's ability to understand and express language, both verbally and nonverbally.
  • Motor skills: This assesses your child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Social-emotional functioning: This evaluates your child's emotional well-being, social skills, and behavior.
  • Sensory processing: This assesses how your child processes sensory information, such as sight, sound, touch, and movement.

The FIIE must be conducted by qualified professionals, such as licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs), educational diagnosticians, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, and physical therapists. The evaluators must use a variety of assessment methods, including standardized tests, observations, and interviews with parents, teachers, and the child.

The FIE should also consider the following:

  • Your child's educational history: This includes past grades, attendance records, and any previous interventions or accommodations.
  • Your child's medical history: This includes any relevant medical diagnoses or conditions.
  • Your child's cultural and linguistic background: This ensures that the evaluation is fair and unbiased.

You have the right to request that specific areas be assessed in the FIE. If you believe your child has a specific learning disability in reading, for example, you can request that the evaluation include specific assessments of phonological awareness, decoding skills, and reading comprehension. Make sure to document these requests in writing.

Child Find Obligations

Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas law, Corpus Christi ISD has a legal responsibility to identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within its boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This is known as "Child Find."

The Child Find mandate requires the district to actively seek out children who may have disabilities, even if they are not currently receiving special education services. This includes children who are:

  • Homeless
  • Wards of the state
  • Attending private schools
  • Homeschooled
  • Suspected of having a disability but are not yet identified

Corpus Christi ISD fulfills its Child Find obligations through various activities, such as:

  • Screening programs for all students
  • Referral processes for teachers and parents
  • Public awareness campaigns to inform the community about special education services
  • Collaboration with community agencies and organizations

If you suspect that a child you know, even if they are not your own child, may have a disability, you can contact Corpus Christi ISD's Special Education Department to report your concerns. The district is obligated to investigate and determine if an evaluation is warranted.

The Child Find mandate ensures that all children with disabilities have the opportunity to receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE).

What If They Refuse

Corpus Christi ISD cannot simply refuse to evaluate your child if you have submitted a written request for a special education evaluation. However, they may disagree that an evaluation is necessary. If the school district refuses to conduct an evaluation, they must provide you with a written notice explaining their reasons for refusing. This notice is called a Prior Written Notice (PWN).

The Prior Written Notice must include the following information:

  • A description of the action the school district proposes or refuses to take (in this case, the refusal to evaluate).
  • An explanation of why the school district proposes or refuses to take the action.
  • A description of each evaluation, procedure, assessment, record, or report the school district used as a basis for the proposed or refused action.
  • A description of other options that the school district considered and the reasons why those options were rejected.
  • A description of other factors that are relevant to the school district's proposal or refusal.
  • Information about how you can obtain a copy of the procedural safeguards under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Sources for you to contact to obtain assistance in understanding the provisions of IDEA.

If you disagree with the school district's decision not to evaluate your child, you have several options:

  • Request a meeting with the school: Schedule a meeting with the school principal, special education staff, and other relevant personnel to discuss your concerns and try to reach an agreement.
  • Request mediation: Mediation is a process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district resolve your dispute. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) provides mediation services free of charge.
  • File a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with TEA alleging that Corpus Christi ISD has violated the IDEA or Texas special education laws. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision.
  • Request a due process hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will issue a decision that is binding on both you and the school district. Due process hearings can be complex and costly, so it's recommended to seek legal counsel.
  • Obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an IEE of your child at public expense if you disagree with the school district's evaluation. However, the school district can request a due process hearing to demonstrate that its evaluation was appropriate. If the hearing officer determines that the school district's evaluation was appropriate, you may still obtain an IEE, but you will have to pay for it yourself.

It's important to consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law to understand your rights and options.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is complete, the next step is the Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting. This meeting is crucial for determining your child's eligibility for special education services and, if eligible, developing an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

The ARD committee consists of:

  • The child's parent(s) or guardian(s)
  • At least one of the child's general education teachers
  • At least one of the child's special education teachers or providers
  • A representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of special education
  • A person who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results
  • The child, if appropriate
  • Other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding the child, including related services personnel as appropriate

During the ARD committee meeting, the following will occur:

  • Review of the evaluation results: The evaluation team will present the findings of the FIIE and explain how they relate to your child's educational performance.
  • Determination of eligibility: The committee will determine if your child meets the criteria for a disability under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas law. To be eligible, your child must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires special education and related services.
  • Development of the IEP: If your child is found eligible for special education, the committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services and supports they will receive.

The IEP must include the following components:

  • Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance: This describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses.
  • Measurable annual goals: These are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year.
  • Special education and related services: This describes the specific services and supports your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, and transportation.
  • Accommodations and modifications: These are changes to the learning environment or curriculum that will help your child access the general education curriculum.
  • Participation in state and district-wide assessments: This describes how your child will participate in state and district-wide assessments, including any accommodations or modifications they will receive.
  • Dates and location of services: This specifies when and where your child will receive services.
  • Transition plan: For students age 16 and older, the IEP must include a transition plan that outlines their goals for post-secondary education, employment, and independent living.

You have the right to participate fully in the ARD committee process and to disagree with any decisions made by the committee. If you disagree with the IEP, you can request mediation or file a due process complaint. It's also important to review the IEP regularly and request revisions as needed to ensure that it continues to meet your child's evolving needs.

This guide provides a general overview of the special education evaluation process in Corpus Christi ISD. It is not a substitute for legal advice. If you have specific questions or concerns, it's important to consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
