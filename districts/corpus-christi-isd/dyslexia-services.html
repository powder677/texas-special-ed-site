Dyslexia Services in Corpus Christi ISD

Dyslexia Services in Corpus Christi ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students within the Corpus Christi Independent School District (CCISD) in accordance with Texas state law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. This information is intended to be a general guide and is not a substitute for direct consultation with school personnel and/or legal advice.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024), published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the primary guide for identifying and providing support to students with dyslexia and related disorders in Texas public schools. It outlines the legal requirements, best practices, and evidence-based interventions that school districts must follow. It is essential for parents, educators, and administrators to be familiar with this document.

Key Updates and Considerations in the 2024 Handbook: (Note: While a full summary requires reading the handbook itself, here are general areas that are often updated)

  • Structured Literacy Updates: Continued emphasis and refinement of the definition and components of Structured Literacy. More detail regarding the specific types of reading and spelling programs considered to be aligned with structured literacy.
  • Early Identification: Focus on improved and earlier identification procedures in kindergarten and first grade. This often involves universal screening processes using reliable and valid instruments.
  • Progress Monitoring: Enhanced guidance on using data from progress monitoring to make informed decisions about instruction and intervention. Emphasis on frequent data collection and analysis.
  • Technology Integration: Exploration of how technology can be used to support students with dyslexia, including assistive technology, reading software, and other digital tools.
  • Parent Involvement: Emphasis on the importance of active parent participation in the evaluation, intervention, and support process. Clarification of parental rights and responsibilities.
  • Training and Professional Development: Requirements for ongoing training and professional development for teachers and staff involved in dyslexia intervention.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): Integration of dyslexia services within an MTSS framework. Clarification on the role of general education and special education in providing services.
  • Definition of Dyslexia: Confirmation and potential updates to the definition of dyslexia and related disorders.

Accessing the Texas Dyslexia Handbook: The most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook can be found on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Search for "Texas Dyslexia Handbook." It is a critical resource for understanding your rights and the district's responsibilities.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law requires all public schools, including CCISD schools, to conduct universal screening for dyslexia and related disorders in kindergarten and first grade. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties and require further evaluation.

Screening Procedures in CCISD:

  • Screening Instruments: CCISD utilizes TEA-approved screening instruments that assess foundational reading skills such as phonological awareness, phonics, rapid naming, and reading fluency. Specific instrument names will be determined by CCISD and should be confirmed with your child's school.
  • Timeline: Screening typically occurs early in the school year. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as potentially at risk for dyslexia.
  • Parent Notification: Parents will receive timely notification of the screening results. The school will provide information about next steps, which may include additional testing, interventions, or referral for a full dyslexia evaluation.
  • Important Considerations: Screening is not a diagnosis. It is an initial step to identify students who may need further assessment. Even if a child does not meet the criteria for further evaluation based on the initial screening, parents can still request an evaluation if they have concerns.

Structured Literacy Programs

Structured Literacy is a research-based approach to reading instruction that is explicitly and systematically taught. It is considered the most effective method for teaching students with dyslexia. The Texas Dyslexia Handbook emphasizes the importance of using Structured Literacy principles in interventions for students with dyslexia.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: The study of speech sounds (phonemes) and how they are organized in language. Instruction includes phonological awareness (the ability to hear and manipulate sounds in words) and phonemic awareness (the ability to isolate and manipulate individual sounds).
  • Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics): The relationship between letters (graphemes) and sounds (phonemes). Instruction is explicit and systematic, teaching students to decode words by sounding them out.
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching students to recognize and use different syllable types (e.g., closed, open, vowel-consonant-e, r-controlled) to decode multisyllabic words.
  • Morphology: The study of word parts (morphemes), such as prefixes, suffixes, and root words. Understanding morphology helps students understand the meaning of words and improve their vocabulary.
  • Syntax: The rules that govern how words are combined to form sentences. Instruction focuses on sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: The meaning of words and sentences. Instruction focuses on vocabulary development and comprehension.

CCISD's Approach to Structured Literacy:

  • CCISD uses programs and approaches aligned with Structured Literacy principles for students identified with dyslexia or at risk of dyslexia.
  • Specific programs used within CCISD may vary by campus. Parents should contact their child's school to inquire about the specific program being used.
  • All interventions are implemented by trained professionals who have expertise in Structured Literacy methods.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and eligibility criteria.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: A 504 plan focuses on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure that the student has equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: 504 plans typically include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, and access to assistive technology. They do NOT typically include specialized instruction provided by a special education teacher.
  • Process: The 504 process involves a team of school personnel who evaluate the student's needs and develop a plan to address those needs. Parents are part of the 504 team.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability (such as dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. The process to determine eligibility involves formal comprehensive testing and an ARD committee meeting.
  • Focus: An IEP focuses on providing specialized instruction and related services to meet the student's unique needs.
  • Services: IEPs include goals and objectives, accommodations, modifications, and specialized instruction delivered by a special education teacher or other qualified professional. Students receiving special education services are eligible for specially designed instruction.
  • Process: The IEP process involves a multidisciplinary team, including parents, teachers, administrators, and specialists. The team develops an individualized plan that outlines the student's educational goals, services, and accommodations. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee makes decisions.

Key Differences Summarized:

Feature Section 504 IEP
Eligibility Substantial limitation to a major life activity Disability that adversely affects educational performance AND requires specialized instruction
Focus Equal access through accommodations Specialized instruction and related services
Instruction Primarily general education with accommodations Specialized instruction from a special education teacher

Determining the Appropriate Plan: The decision of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and evaluation results. The school team, including parents, will work together to determine the most appropriate plan.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional practices that help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. These can be provided in both 504 plans and IEPs.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location where they can easily see and hear the teacher.
  • Reduced Workload: Modifying the amount of work required, such as reducing the number of problems on a worksheet.
  • Alternative Assessments: Using alternative methods of assessment, such as oral presentations or projects, instead of written tests.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology, such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, or graphic organizers.
  • Audiobooks: Allowing the student to listen to audiobooks instead of reading printed text.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing notes or allowing the student to record lectures.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids, such as graphic organizers, charts, and diagrams, to support learning.
  • Chunking Information: Breaking down large amounts of information into smaller, more manageable chunks.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Using multi-sensory activities that engage multiple senses, such as sight, sound, and touch.
  • Modified Spelling Expectations: Focusing on content understanding rather than perfect spelling on certain assignments.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the use of a calculator for math assignments.
  • Dictation: Allowing the student to dictate answers to a scribe.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing frequent breaks to reduce fatigue and improve focus.
  • Access to a Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.

Important Considerations:

  • Accommodations should be individualized to meet the student's specific needs.
  • Accommodations should be implemented consistently across all settings.
  • The effectiveness of accommodations should be regularly reviewed and adjusted as needed.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia have the right to request a full dyslexia evaluation from the school district. The request should be made in writing to the school principal or special education director.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Write a Letter of Request: The letter should include the student's name, date of birth, grade level, and a clear statement that you are requesting a full dyslexia evaluation. Include specific concerns you have about your child's reading, spelling, or writing skills. Mention any interventions or supports that have already been tried.
  2. Submit the Letter: Send the letter to the school principal or the special education director for CCISD. You can find contact information on the CCISD website. Keep a copy of the letter for your records. Certified mail is recommended to ensure the school district receives your request.
  3. Timeline: The school district is required to respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe. The TEA provides timelines within the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, and CCISD must follow these regulations. They will either agree to evaluate your child or provide written notice explaining why they will not be evaluating your child.
  4. Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to evaluate your child, they will conduct a comprehensive assessment of their reading, spelling, and writing skills. This may involve a variety of tests and observations.
  5. ARD Committee Meeting (if applicable): If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia and requires specialized instruction, an ARD committee meeting will be held to develop an IEP.
  6. 504 Meeting (if applicable): If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia but does not require specialized instruction, a 504 meeting will be held to develop a 504 plan.

Important Reminders:

  • Parents have the right to participate in all stages of the evaluation and intervention process.
  • Parents have the right to access their child's educational records.
  • Parents have the right to seek an independent educational evaluation (IEE) if they disagree with the school district's evaluation. However, parents may be responsible for the cost of an IEE.
  • Document everything. Keep records of all communication, evaluations, and meetings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
