Dispute & Discipline Rights in Corpus Christi ISD

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Corpus Christi ISD

If your child is facing disciplinary action in Corpus Christi ISD, ACT IMMEDIATELY. Time is of the essence. This guide provides crucial information to protect your child's rights. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education or student rights as soon as possible. This is NOT legal advice.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

In Corpus Christi ISD, as in all Texas school districts, there are strict rules regarding suspensions, particularly short-term suspensions. Know your rights!

  • General Rule: Schools can generally suspend a student for up to three school days for each violation of the Student Code of Conduct.
  • Cumulative Suspensions: Be aware of cumulative suspensions. Even seemingly minor incidents can add up. Frequent, short suspensions can have a devastating impact on your child's education.
  • Documentation is Key: Request and meticulously review all documentation related to the suspension. This includes incident reports, witness statements, and any communication from the school administration. Check for inconsistencies and errors.
  • Special Education Considerations: If your child has an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or a 504 plan, the rules change dramatically. See the sections below on MDRs and Change of Placement. A suspension of more than 10 cumulative days in a school year triggers specific protections under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Question Everything: Don't accept the school's version of events without question. Speak to your child, interview witnesses (if possible), and gather your own evidence.
  • Is it discrimination? Consider if the disciplinary action is disproportionately applied to students of a certain race, ethnicity, or disability. This could be a sign of discrimination.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

This is ABSOLUTELY CRITICAL if your child has an IEP or 504 plan.

An MDR is a meeting to determine if a student's misconduct is a "manifestation" of their disability. In simpler terms, did the disability cause the behavior that led to the disciplinary action?

  • Trigger: An MDR is required whenever a student with an IEP or 504 plan faces a suspension (or other disciplinary change of placement) that will result in the student being removed from their current educational placement for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year (even if these are short suspensions added together).
  • The Team: The MDR team typically includes the parent(s), relevant members of the IEP team (or 504 committee), and school administrators.
  • The Process: The team must review all relevant information, including the student's IEP/504 plan, teacher observations, any relevant medical or psychological evaluations, and information provided by the parents.
  • The Question: The central question is: Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability? OR Was the conduct a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP/504 plan?
  • Manifestation Finding: If the answer to either of those questions is YES, then the conduct IS a manifestation of the disability.
    • Consequences of Manifestation: If the conduct is a manifestation, the school CANNOT suspend the student for more than 10 days (cumulative) and CANNOT change the student's placement without parental consent (except in very limited circumstances involving weapons, drugs, or serious bodily injury). The IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP), or revise an existing one, to address the behavior.
  • No Manifestation Finding: If the answer to both questions is NO, then the conduct is NOT a manifestation of the disability.
    • Consequences of No Manifestation: If the conduct is not a manifestation, the school can discipline the student in the same way it would discipline a student without a disability. HOWEVER, the school MUST still provide FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education). This means the student must continue to receive services as outlined in their IEP/504 plan, even while suspended.
  • Parental Rights: You have the right to participate fully in the MDR. Bring your own documentation, ask questions, and advocate fiercely for your child. Do NOT be intimidated.
  • What to Do If You Disagree: If you disagree with the outcome of the MDR, you have the right to file a complaint or request mediation. See sections below.

Change of Placement

A "change of placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a change of placement because the removals are for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, and the behavior is substantially similar to the student's behavior in previous incidents that resulted in the series of removals, and additional factors such as the length of each removal, the total amount of time the student is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another.

ANY change of placement for a student with an IEP/504 requires strict adherence to IDEA and related regulations.

  • Examples of Change of Placement: This includes suspension, expulsion, placement in an alternative educational setting (DAEP), or any other situation where the student is not receiving their typical education.
  • Emergency Removals: In rare cases, a school can remove a student to an interim alternative educational setting for up to 45 school days without regard to manifestation determination if the student has inflicted serious bodily injury upon another person; carries a weapon to school or possesses a weapon at school; or knowingly possesses or uses illegal drugs or sells or solicits the sale of a controlled substance while at school or a school function. Even in these cases, procedural safeguards apply.
  • Parental Consent: The school generally needs your consent to change your child's placement. Do NOT feel pressured to sign anything you don't understand or agree with. Consult an attorney.
  • What to Fight For: If the school is proposing a change of placement, fight to keep your child in the least restrictive environment (LRE) possible. Advocate for supports and services to address the underlying behavioral issues.
  • Extended DAEP Placement: A prolonged DAEP placement is often a sign that the school is failing to adequately address your child's needs. This is particularly concerning for students with IEPs or 504 plans. Challenge these placements aggressively.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe Corpus Christi ISD has violated your child's rights or policies, you have the right to file a grievance. The process typically starts at Level 1, which is usually with the school principal.

  • CCISD Policy Online: Find the official grievance policy in the CCISD Policy Online manual. It's likely under the "Employee & Parent Concerns" section. This document outlines the specific procedures and timelines.
  • Written Grievance: Your grievance MUST be in writing. Be clear, concise, and specific. State the policy or procedure you believe was violated, the facts of the situation, and the remedy you are seeking. Include dates, times, and names of individuals involved.
  • Deadlines: Pay close attention to deadlines. The grievance policy will specify the time frame for filing a Level 1 grievance. Missing the deadline could jeopardize your ability to pursue the grievance.
  • Documentation: Keep copies of everything. This includes your grievance, any supporting documents, and any responses from the school.
  • Meeting with the Principal: The principal will likely schedule a meeting with you to discuss the grievance. Bring a support person if possible. Be prepared to present your case clearly and calmly.
  • Principal's Response: The principal is required to provide a written response to your grievance within a specified time frame (outlined in the policy).
  • Appeal: If you are not satisfied with the principal's response, you have the right to appeal to Level 2 (typically the Superintendent or designee). Again, pay close attention to deadlines and follow the procedures outlined in the grievance policy.
  • It's about the paper trail: While Level 1 may not always bring immediate resolution, it establishes a crucial paper trail. This documentation will be essential if you need to escalate the matter to higher levels or pursue legal action.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Corpus Christi ISD has violated state or federal special education laws (like IDEA), you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

  • TEA Complaint Form: Obtain the official TEA complaint form from the TEA website. It is usually found under the Special Education section.
  • Specific Allegations: Your complaint MUST be specific. Identify the specific special education law or regulation that you believe has been violated. Describe the facts of the situation in detail.
  • Supporting Documentation: Include copies of all relevant documents, such as the IEP, evaluations, incident reports, and correspondence with the school.
  • Timeline: There are timelines for filing a TEA complaint. Typically, you must file the complaint within one year of the date of the alleged violation.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint. They may request additional information from you and from the school district.
  • TEA Findings: TEA will issue a written decision outlining their findings. If TEA finds that the school district has violated the law, they will order the district to take corrective action.
  • Corrective Action Orders: If you prevail in your complaint, make sure the corrective action ordered by TEA is actually implemented by the school district. Follow up!
  • Mediation: TEA may offer mediation as a way to resolve the complaint. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal procedure that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding their child's special education program. This is the most serious step, and you ABSOLUTELY need to consult with an attorney.

  • When to Consider Due Process: Due process is typically considered when all other attempts to resolve the dispute have failed. This might include situations where you disagree with the IEP, the school's evaluation of your child, or the school's disciplinary actions.
  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: To initiate due process, you must file a written due process complaint with the school district and TEA. The complaint must include specific information, such as the child's name, address, the nature of the problem, and the proposed resolution.
  • Resolution Period: After you file a due process complaint, there is a 30-day resolution period. During this time, the school district must convene a resolution meeting with you to try to resolve the dispute.
  • Due Process Hearing: If the dispute is not resolved during the resolution period, you have the right to a due process hearing. This is a formal hearing before an impartial hearing officer. You have the right to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine the school district's witnesses.
  • Hearing Officer's Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision. This decision is binding on both you and the school district, unless it is appealed to a court of law.
  • Legal Representation: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. It is strongly recommended that you obtain legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law.

This guide is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. You should consult with an attorney specializing in special education or student rights to discuss your specific situation. Remember, time is critical. Act now to protect your child's rights!

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
