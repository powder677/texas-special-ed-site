What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as needing special education services in Friendswood ISD, you will be invited to participate in an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. It's more than just a meeting; it's the cornerstone of your child's special education journey. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, often called an IEP. This IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific educational supports and services your child will receive to help them succeed in school. The ARD committee makes decisions regarding your child's eligibility for special education, their present levels of academic and functional performance, measurable annual goals, appropriate special education and related services, and the educational setting where those services will be provided. The entire process is driven by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, Chapter 29, which are federal and state laws that ensure children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs.

The ARD committee is a collaborative group, and you, as the parent, are a vital member. In addition to you, the team typically includes a school administrator or designee who is knowledgeable about special education services, your child's general education teacher (if applicable), a special education teacher, an educational diagnostician or Licensed Specialist in School Psychology (LSSP) who can interpret evaluation results, and other relevant professionals who can contribute to the discussion, such as a speech-language pathologist, occupational therapist, or counselor. Your child is also invited to attend the ARD meeting, especially as they get older, to participate in the discussion of their goals and aspirations. Remember, the ARD meeting is not just a formality; it's a crucial opportunity for you to actively participate in shaping your child's educational future. The decisions made in this meeting will directly impact the resources and support your child receives, ensuring they have the chance to reach their full potential. It is important to understand that ARD meetings are mandatory. The law requires that a child with a disability receives special education and related services through an appropriately developed and implemented IEP.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Friendswood ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the scheduled date. This notification is not just a courtesy; it's a legal requirement designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting. This advance notice is essential so that you can review any relevant documents, gather your thoughts, and arrange for any support you may want to bring with you. The written notice must include the purpose of the meeting (for example, initial ARD, annual review, or amendment), the date, time, and location of the meeting, who will be attending (if known), and a statement informing you of your right to participate and invite other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. The notice should also explain the topics that will be discussed during the meeting. It is very important to keep these notices in a file for future reference.

If the proposed date of the ARD meeting doesn't work for you, contact the school immediately to reschedule. The district is obligated to work with you to find a mutually agreeable time and date. However, there might be situations where a quicker meeting is necessary, or you might simply prefer to meet sooner. In such cases, you have the option to waive your right to the five-day notice. This waiver must be in writing, indicating that you understand your right to the notice period but voluntarily choose to proceed with the meeting earlier. Carefully consider whether waiving the notice is in your child's best interest, as it may limit your opportunity to adequately prepare. Remember, you are in control of this timeline and should feel comfortable with the amount of preparation time you have before the meeting.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is critical to ensuring their IEP reflects their unique needs and strengths. Think of yourself as your child's advocate and gather all the information that will help the ARD committee understand your child's perspective. Start by collecting any prior evaluations or assessments conducted on your child, whether they were done by the school district or outside professionals. These documents provide valuable data points regarding your child's academic, cognitive, or behavioral functioning. If your child has seen doctors, therapists, or specialists, bring letters or reports from them outlining your child's diagnosis, treatment plan, and any recommendations they have for the school. Official school records, such as report cards, attendance records, and previous IEPs (if applicable), will help paint a comprehensive picture of your child's academic history.

Perhaps most importantly, prepare a written list of your own concerns and observations about your child's education. What are their strengths? Where do you see them struggling? What are your goals for their future? Writing these thoughts down beforehand will help you articulate them clearly during the meeting. It's also highly recommended to bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can offer emotional support, take notes, ask clarifying questions, and help you navigate the process. In Texas, you are legally allowed to audio record the ARD meeting, as long as you provide reasonable notice to the school district before the meeting begins. Having a recording can be invaluable for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring accurate documentation of the decisions made. Having all these materials on hand ensures that the ARD committee will have a complete understanding of your child's needs, allowing them to create an IEP that truly meets those needs.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During an ARD meeting, it's sometimes necessary to pause the discussion to gather more information or seek additional input before making final decisions. This is where the "10-Day Recess Rule" comes into play. Any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess of up to 10 school days to obtain further information relevant to your child's IEP. This can be a powerful tool if you feel the team is moving too quickly, if you need more time to consider a proposal, or if you want to consult with an advocate or outside expert. For instance, if the team is proposing a specific placement or service that you are unsure about, you can request a recess to research the options, talk to other parents, or seek professional advice.

To invoke the 10-Day Recess Rule, simply state your request clearly during the ARD meeting. You don't need to provide a lengthy explanation, but it's helpful to articulate why you need the additional time and what information you hope to gather. The team must grant your request. The meeting will then be recessed, and reconvened after a period of no more than 10 school days. The team can schedule to meet sooner than 10 days if all members are available. Use this time wisely to gather the necessary information and come back to the ARD meeting feeling confident and informed. Remember, this rule is in place to protect your child's rights and ensure that IEP decisions are made thoughtfully and with a full understanding of all relevant factors.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the central document that guides your child's special education journey. It's a comprehensive plan that outlines their present levels of performance, measurable annual goals, specific services, and appropriate educational placement. At the ARD meeting, the team will discuss each section of the IEP and make decisions about what to include.

The IEP begins with a statement of your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and areas where they need support. It will include information from assessments, observations, and input from you, the parent. Next, the IEP will outline measurable annual goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The IEP will then detail the specific special education and related services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. It will also specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

The IEP will also address your child's educational placement, which refers to the setting where they will receive their special education services. This could range from a general education classroom with accommodations and supports to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or self-contained classroom. It's important to note that the law requires students with disabilities to be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning they should be educated alongside their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP will also list any accommodations and modifications your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material (e.g., extended time on tests, preferential seating), while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn (e.g., simplified assignments, reduced workload). The end of the ARD is not the end of your involvement. Even if you agree with the contents of the IEP, you are not required to sign it on the day of the meeting. You can take the document home to review, and sign later. If you disagree, refusing to sign does not mean services are stopped. Refusal just means that you don't agree with the plan.

When You Disagree With the Team

As a parent, you have the right to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions if you believe they are not in your child's best interest. It's important to remember that your voice matters, and you should never feel pressured to agree to something you're not comfortable with. One critical point is that you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you disagree with the school's recommendation for special education services, you can decline to give your permission, and your child will not be placed in special education. However, once your child is receiving special education services, refusing to sign the IEP does not stop the implementation of the plan. The school district can implement the IEP as written, even if you don't sign it.

If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document before signing it. This indicates that you do not endorse the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action. You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide valuable information to help resolve disagreements and inform the IEP process.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee but are still unable to reach a satisfactory resolution, there are several formal options available to you under the law. It's important to be aware of these options and understand the process for pursuing them. The first and often most accessible option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law. The second option is mediation, which is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is also free through TEA and can be a less adversarial way to resolve disputes.

If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer not to pursue it, you can request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a decision about the dispute. Due process hearings can be complex and may require the assistance of an attorney. The final formal option is to file a complaint with the federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. It's essential to familiarize yourself with your rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and the Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, which govern special education in Texas. These resources, often referred to as the Legal Framework, outline your rights and the school district's responsibilities, empowering you to advocate effectively for your child's educational needs. Remember, you are not alone in this process. There are resources available to help you understand your rights and navigate the special education system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
