What is an ARD meeting in Texas?

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as potentially needing special education services in Donna ISD, you'll be invited to attend an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is at the heart of how Donna ISD, and all school districts in Texas, provide special education services to children with disabilities. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine if your child is eligible for special education, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's unique needs, to review the IEP regularly, and to decide if and when special education services are no longer needed. This process is not just a suggestion; it's required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. These laws ensure that children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their individual needs.

The ARD meeting is a collaborative process. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the ARD committee. Other members typically include a school administrator, your child's general education teacher, a special education teacher or specialist, and other relevant professionals, such as a diagnostician, speech therapist, occupational therapist, or school psychologist. The specific members will depend on your child's needs and the concerns being addressed. The ARD committee works together to review evaluations and assessments, discuss your child's strengths and weaknesses, set measurable goals for your child's progress, determine the specific services your child needs to achieve those goals, and decide on the most appropriate educational setting for your child. Remember, the ARD committee's job is to design a plan that helps your child succeed in school.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Donna ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is not just a formality; it's a legal requirement designed to give you adequate time to prepare. The notice should include several key pieces of information. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting - for example, is it to determine eligibility, develop an IEP, review an existing IEP, or consider dismissal from special education services? Second, the notice must include the date, time, and location of the meeting. Third, it should list the names and titles of the individuals who will be attending the meeting. Finally, the notice should inform you of your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. This might include a therapist, doctor, or advocate.

The five-day notice is there to protect your right to participate meaningfully in the ARD process. If you do not receive proper notice, you have the right to request that the meeting be rescheduled. However, there may be times when you want to have the ARD meeting sooner than five days. In these situations, you can waive your right to the five-day notice by providing written consent to the school district. This allows the ARD meeting to proceed more quickly, but it's important to make sure you are comfortable and prepared before waiving this right. Do not feel pressured to waive the notice if you need the time to gather information or consult with others.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting can make a significant difference in ensuring your child's needs are fully addressed. It is very important that you bring anything that might help the ARD committee understand your child's strengths, weaknesses, and needs. Prior evaluations, whether they were conducted by the school district or by private professionals, are essential documents to bring. These evaluations provide valuable data about your child's academic performance, cognitive abilities, and any specific learning disabilities or challenges they may face. Doctor's letters or reports from other healthcare providers, such as therapists or counselors, are also important. These documents can provide insights into your child's medical history, any diagnoses they have received, and any recommendations for treatment or support.

Gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and samples of your child's work. These documents can give the ARD committee a clear picture of your child's academic performance over time. It is helpful to prepare a written list of your concerns about your child's education and well-being. This list can serve as a guide during the meeting and ensure that you don't forget to address any important issues. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you understand the information being presented, and ensure that your voice is heard. Finally, remember that in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided you inform the other attendees that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for capturing the details of the meeting and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the discussions and decisions made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides an important safeguard in the ARD process: the 10-day recess rule. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a recess of up to 10 school days before any final decisions are made regarding your child's IEP. The purpose of this recess is to provide time for gathering additional information, consulting with experts or advocates, or simply taking time to carefully consider the information presented at the ARD meeting. The recess can be invoked if you feel that the team is missing information, not adequately considering your concerns, or moving too quickly toward decisions that you are not comfortable with.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, you simply need to verbally state your request during the ARD meeting. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation, but it is helpful to state the reason for your request. For example, you might say, "I would like to request a 10-day recess to consult with an advocate before finalizing the IEP goals." Once you have made the request, the ARD committee must grant it. The 10-day period starts the day after the ARD meeting and counts only school days. During this time, the ARD committee cannot finalize the IEP or implement any changes without your agreement. Use this time wisely to get the information you need to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the cornerstone of your child's special education. It is a written document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports they will receive to achieve those goals. The IEP is developed during the ARD meeting and is based on a thorough assessment of your child's strengths and weaknesses. Several key areas are decided upon during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP. First, the IEP will include a section called "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance." This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, and social skills. It also identifies any areas where your child is struggling.

Second, the IEP will outline annual goals that are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). These goals are designed to help your child make progress in the areas where they need the most support. Third, the IEP will specify the services your child will receive to help them achieve their goals. These services may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or assistive technology. Fourth, the IEP will determine your child's placement, which refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom with supports, a resource room, or a self-contained special education classroom. Finally, the IEP will list any accommodations or modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as extended time on tests or preferential seating. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as modified assignments or a reduced workload. It's important to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully and consult with others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee about certain aspects of their child's IEP. It's important to know your rights and how to advocate for your child in these situations. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can decline to give your consent, and your child will not be placed in special education. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP, even if your child is already receiving special education services. Refusing to sign the IEP does not necessarily mean that the IEP will not be implemented, but it does indicate that you disagree with some or all of its provisions.

If you disagree with the ARD committee's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the district believes its own evaluation was appropriate, they have the right to pursue a due process hearing to defend their evaluation. If you disagree with any part of the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it. This indicates that you have reservations about the IEP and allows you to preserve your right to pursue further action if needed. "Parent Disagrees" written on an IEP protects you and does not prevent your child from getting needed services.

Escalation Options

If you have attempted to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee and are still not satisfied, there are several formal escalation options available to you. These options are outlined in the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process, which is based on IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89, both of which govern special education in Texas.

Your first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. You can find the complaint form on the TEA website. Your second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the school district meet with a neutral third-party mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA offers free mediation services to parents of children with disabilities. Your third option is to request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process in which you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer, who will then issue a binding decision. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation. Your fourth option is to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. Each of these processes requires strict timelines and procedures. Do not hesitate to seek assistance to learn about these options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
