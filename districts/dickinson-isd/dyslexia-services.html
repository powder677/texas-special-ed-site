Dyslexia Services in Dickinson ISD

Dyslexia Services in Dickinson ISD

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Dickinson ISD adheres to the guidelines and procedures outlined in the updated Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This handbook provides comprehensive information regarding dyslexia, including identification, evaluation, and intervention strategies. Parents and educators are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the most current version of the handbook, which can be found on the TEA website (search "Texas Dyslexia Handbook"). Key updates often include clarification on screening practices, expanded intervention options, and refined definitions related to dyslexia and related disorders. Familiarizing yourself with the handbook will ensure a better understanding of your rights and the district's responsibilities regarding dyslexia support.

Mandatory Screening

Dickinson ISD conducts universal screening for dyslexia risk factors in kindergarten and first grade, as mandated by Texas law. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia early on. The screening process typically involves assessing phonological awareness, phonemic awareness, rapid naming, and letter-sound knowledge. It's important to remember that screening is a preliminary step, and a positive screening result does not automatically mean a child has dyslexia. Rather, it indicates a need for further evaluation or intervention. Parents will be notified if their child shows signs of dyslexia risk during these screenings, and the school will discuss next steps.

Structured Literacy Programs

Dickinson ISD utilizes structured literacy approaches for dyslexia intervention. Structured literacy is a research-based approach that explicitly teaches phonology, sound-symbol association, syllable instruction, morphology, syntax, and semantics. These programs are systematic, cumulative, and multisensory. While the specific program may vary depending on the campus and student needs, Dickinson ISD strives to implement interventions that are aligned with the principles of structured literacy and proven to be effective in supporting students with dyslexia. Contact your child's school to inquire about the specific structured literacy program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may qualify for support through a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to ensure equal access to the learning environment for students with disabilities. Students are eligible under Section 504 if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. An IEP, on the other hand, is for students who require specialized instruction due to a disability that adversely affects their educational performance. To qualify for an IEP, a student must meet the criteria for one of the specific disability categories outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), and require specially designed instruction. The determination of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of educators and parents, based on the student's individual needs and evaluation results.

Accommodations List

Common accommodations provided to students with dyslexia in Dickinson ISD may include, but are not limited to:

  • Extended time on assignments and tests
  • Use of assistive technology (e.g., text-to-speech software, audiobooks)
  • Preferential seating
  • Modified assignments
  • Multi-sensory learning activities
  • Reading aloud accommodations
  • Use of graphic organizers
  • Frequent breaks
  • Copies of notes
The specific accommodations will be determined based on the student's individual needs and outlined in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from Dickinson ISD. To initiate the process, contact your child's school counselor, teacher, or principal and express your concerns in writing. The school will then schedule a meeting to discuss your concerns and determine if an evaluation is warranted. The evaluation process typically involves a comprehensive assessment of reading, writing, and related skills. Parents are active participants in the evaluation process and will receive a copy of the evaluation report. Remember to clearly state your concerns in writing and document all communication with the school. The school will provide you with the necessary forms and information regarding the evaluation process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
