Requesting an Evaluation in Ector County ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Ector County ISD

This guide outlines the process of requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Ector County Independent School District (ECISD) under Texas law. Understanding your rights and the school district's obligations is crucial to ensuring your child receives the appropriate support and services. This guide covers the steps involved in requesting an evaluation, the timelines the district must adhere to, what the evaluation should include, the district's responsibility to identify students with disabilities, what to do if your request is denied, and what happens after the evaluation is completed. This guide is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law for specific legal guidance related to your situation.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating the special education evaluation process is submitting a formal, written request to ECISD. This request serves as official notification that you suspect your child may have a disability that affects their educational performance and requires special education services. The written request should be clear, concise, and contain specific information about your child and your concerns. While there isn't a prescribed form you must use, including the following details will ensure your request is comprehensive: * **Child's Full Name:** Include your child's legal name as it appears on school records. * **Child's Date of Birth:** This helps the school accurately identify your child's records. * **Child's Current Grade Level:** State your child's current grade. * **Child's Current School:** Specify the ECISD school your child attends. * **Your Name(s) and Contact Information:** Provide your full name(s), address, phone number, and email address. Include the best method and time to contact you. * **Statement of Concern:** Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services. * **Specific Areas of Concern:** Detail the specific areas of concern that lead you to believe your child may have a disability. These concerns should relate to your child's academic, behavioral, social-emotional, or physical development, and how these issues affect their ability to learn in the general education environment. Provide concrete examples. For instance, you might write: "My child struggles with reading comprehension and frequently misses key details in assigned texts," or "My child exhibits significant difficulty focusing in class, disrupting their learning and the learning of others." * **Supporting Documentation:** If you have any supporting documentation, such as medical records, previous evaluations, report cards, teacher observations, or work samples, include copies with your request. While you don't need a formal diagnosis to make a request, any information that strengthens your case will be helpful. * **Request for All Areas of Suspected Disability:** Explicitly state that you are requesting an evaluation in *all* areas of suspected disability. This is important because it prevents the school from limiting the scope of the evaluation to only the areas they deem necessary. For example, if you suspect both a learning disability and an emotional disturbance, make sure you request an evaluation for both. * **Signature and Date:** Sign and date the request. **How to Submit the Request:** The written request should be delivered to the appropriate school official. The best option is usually the school principal or the special education director/coordinator for your child's campus. It is highly recommended that you send the request via certified mail with a return receipt requested. This provides you with proof that the school received your request. Keep a copy of the request and the certified mail receipt for your records. **Example Request:** [Your Name] [Your Address] [Your Phone Number] [Your Email Address] [Date] [Principal's Name] [School Name] [School Address] Subject: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Name] Dear [Principal's Name], I am writing to request a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services for my child, [Child's Name], date of birth [Child's Date of Birth], who is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. I am concerned that [Child's Name] may have a disability that is impacting their educational performance. Specifically, [Child's Name] struggles with [Specific Area of Concern 1] and [Specific Area of Concern 2]. For example, [Child's Name] [Provide Specific Example]. I am requesting that [Child's Name] be evaluated in all areas of suspected disability. I have attached [List any attached documents, e.g., report cards, doctor's notes]. Thank you for your time and consideration. I look forward to hearing from you soon to discuss this matter further. Sincerely, [Your Signature] [Your Typed Name]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once ECISD receives your written request for a special education evaluation, a strict 45-school-day timeline begins. This timeline is mandated by Texas Education Code and ensures that the evaluation process moves forward in a timely manner. It's crucial to understand this timeline and hold the district accountable for adhering to it. **Day 1: Receipt of the Written Request:** This is the official start date of the 45-school-day clock. Make sure you have documentation (certified mail receipt) confirming the date the district received your request. **Within 15 School Days (of receiving parental consent to evaluate):** The Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee must meet to determine if an evaluation is warranted. This meeting is called a "Review of Existing Evaluation Data" (REED) meeting. You are a crucial member of this ARD committee. The school *must* obtain your informed consent before conducting any evaluations. The ARD committee, including you, reviews existing data about your child, including academic records, teacher observations, parent input, and any other relevant information. The committee will then decide whether or not to proceed with a full and individual evaluation. *Importantly, you are not limited to the school's 15 day timeframe to provide consent to evaluate. The clock does not start until you consent. Also, the request for consent to evaluate starts a 15 day timeline to hold the REED meeting.* **If the ARD committee decides to evaluate:** The district must complete the full and individual evaluation within the remaining time of the 45-school-day timeline. **If the ARD committee decides *not* to evaluate:** The district must provide you with written notice explaining the reasons for the refusal and inform you of your right to appeal this decision. This notice must be provided within a reasonable timeframe after the REED meeting. The process of appealing a refusal to evaluate is discussed later in this guide. **The Full and Individual Evaluation:** If the ARD committee decides to proceed with an evaluation, the district must conduct a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and needs. This evaluation, called the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE), must be conducted by qualified professionals. **Within the 45-School-Day Timeline:** After the evaluation is completed, another ARD committee meeting must be held to review the evaluation results, determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services, and, if eligible, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). **School Days vs. Calendar Days:** It's important to note that the 45-day timeline refers to *school days*, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted. This means the actual timeframe may be longer than 45 calendar days. **Monitoring the Timeline:** It is your responsibility to monitor the 45-school-day timeline and ensure the district is meeting its obligations. Keep detailed records of all communications with the school, including dates, times, and the content of conversations. If you believe the district is not adhering to the timeline, document your concerns in writing and send them to the school principal, special education director, and superintendent. You can also contact the Texas Education Agency (TEA) for assistance. **Extending the Timeline:** Under certain circumstances, the ARD committee, including you, can agree to extend the 45-school-day timeline. However, this should only be done if it is truly necessary to ensure a thorough and accurate evaluation. The extension must be documented in writing and signed by you.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a cornerstone of the special education process in Texas. It's a comprehensive assessment designed to identify your child's specific educational needs and determine whether they qualify for special education services. To be effective, the FIE must cover all areas of suspected disability. **Key Components of a Comprehensive FIE:** * **Comprehensive Assessment:** The FIE should not be limited to standardized tests. It should include a variety of assessment methods, such as observations, interviews, record reviews, and informal assessments, to gain a complete picture of your child's strengths and weaknesses. * **All Areas of Suspected Disability:** As mentioned earlier, the FIE must address all areas where a disability is suspected. This includes, but is not limited to, academic skills (reading, writing, math), speech and language, social-emotional development, motor skills, and adaptive behavior. If you suspect your child has multiple disabilities, the FIE must evaluate each area. * **Qualified Professionals:** The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals who have the expertise and training to assess the specific areas of concern. This may include educational diagnosticians, speech-language pathologists, psychologists, occupational therapists, and other specialists. * **Parent Input:** Your input as a parent is crucial to the FIE process. The evaluators should actively seek your input regarding your child's history, strengths, weaknesses, and any concerns you have about their development and learning. This input should be considered in the evaluation process and reflected in the FIE report. * **Educational Impact:** The FIE must determine how your child's disability, if present, impacts their educational performance. This includes how the disability affects their ability to access the general education curriculum and participate in school activities. * **Specific Recommendations:** The FIE report should include specific recommendations for interventions, accommodations, and modifications that could help your child succeed in the classroom. These recommendations should be based on the evaluation findings and tailored to your child's individual needs. **Specific Areas to Consider:** * **Cognitive Functioning:** Assessments of intellectual ability, problem-solving skills, and information processing. * **Academic Achievement:** Evaluation of reading, writing, math, and other academic areas using standardized tests and classroom-based assessments. * **Speech and Language:** Assessment of articulation, fluency, language comprehension, and expressive language skills. * **Social-Emotional Functioning:** Evaluation of social skills, emotional regulation, behavior, and mental health. * **Motor Skills:** Assessment of gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, cutting). * **Adaptive Behavior:** Evaluation of daily living skills, such as dressing, eating, and hygiene. **Reviewing the FIE Report:** Once the FIE is completed, you have the right to receive a copy of the report. Review the report carefully to ensure that it is accurate, comprehensive, and addresses all of your concerns. If you have any questions or disagree with any of the findings, discuss them with the evaluation team at the ARD meeting. You have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation, under certain circumstances.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law mandates that all school districts, including ECISD, have a "Child Find" obligation. This means they have a legal responsibility to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether the child is enrolled in public school, private school, or homeschooled. This applies to children from birth to age 21. **Key Aspects of Child Find:** * **Proactive Identification:** School districts cannot simply wait for parents to request evaluations. They must actively seek out children who may have disabilities through various means, such as screenings, community outreach, and collaboration with other agencies. * **Public Awareness:** Districts must inform the public about the availability of special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation. This information should be readily accessible on the district's website, in school handbooks, and through other communication channels. * **Referral Process:** School personnel, including teachers, counselors, and administrators, are responsible for referring students for evaluation when they suspect a disability. Parents, doctors, and other professionals can also make referrals. * **Timely Evaluation:** Once a referral is made, the district must conduct an evaluation in a timely manner, following the 45-school-day timeline outlined earlier in this guide. * **Confidentiality:** All information related to Child Find activities must be kept confidential and protected under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA). **What This Means for Parents:** If you suspect your child has a disability, you don't have to wait for the school to initiate the evaluation process. You have the right to request an evaluation at any time. Your request triggers the district's Child Find obligation, and they must respond to your request in a timely manner. Even if your child is not enrolled in public school, ECISD is still responsible for evaluating your child if you live within the district boundaries. **If You Suspect the District is Not Meeting Its Child Find Obligations:** If you believe ECISD is not fulfilling its Child Find responsibilities, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). The TEA will investigate your complaint and take corrective action if necessary.

What If They Refuse

Despite the legal requirements, ECISD may refuse to conduct a special education evaluation for your child. This refusal can be frustrating and disheartening, but it's important to understand your rights and the steps you can take to challenge the district's decision. **Reasons for Refusal:** ECISD may refuse to evaluate your child if they believe there is insufficient evidence to suggest a disability that significantly impacts their educational performance. They might argue that your child is performing adequately in school, that their difficulties are due to other factors (e.g., lack of motivation, attendance issues), or that they have already tried interventions in the general education classroom. **Your Rights When a Request is Denied:** If ECISD refuses to evaluate your child, they must provide you with a written notice explaining the reasons for the refusal. This notice must also inform you of your right to appeal the decision. This written notice is extremely important, as it details your next steps and outlines the reasons for the denial. **Steps to Take If Your Request is Denied:** 1. **Request a Copy of the Documentation Used for the Decision:** Ask the district for a copy of all the documentation they used to make the decision not to evaluate. This will help you understand their reasoning and prepare your response. 2. **Request Mediation:** Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third party to try to resolve the dispute. The mediator helps facilitate communication and explore potential solutions. Mediation is often a good first step because it can be less adversarial than other dispute resolution options. 3. **Request a Due Process Hearing:** A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about whether the district should have evaluated your child. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. Due process hearings are often complex and time-consuming, so it's important to seek legal advice before proceeding. 4. **File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA):** You can file a complaint with the TEA alleging that ECISD violated the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or other applicable laws. The TEA will investigate your complaint and take corrective action if necessary. **Important Considerations:** * **Gather Evidence:** To strengthen your case, gather as much evidence as possible to support your belief that your child has a disability that requires special education services. This may include medical records, previous evaluations, teacher observations, work samples, and your own observations of your child's behavior and learning. * **Seek Legal Advice:** Special education law is complex, and it's important to seek legal advice from an attorney specializing in this area. An attorney can help you understand your rights, navigate the dispute resolution process, and advocate for your child's needs. * **Document Everything:** Keep detailed records of all communications with the school district, including dates, times, the content of conversations, and any documents you exchange.

After the Evaluation

After the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is completed, the next step is to hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to review the evaluation results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. **The ARD Committee Meeting:** The ARD committee is composed of you (the parent), school administrators, teachers, evaluators, and other relevant professionals. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to: * **Review the FIE Report:** The evaluation team will present the findings of the FIE, explaining your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas of need. * **Determine Eligibility:** The ARD committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law. To be eligible, your child must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. * **Develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP):** If your child is found eligible, the ARD committee will develop an IEP tailored to their individual needs. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific special education and related services your child will receive. **Key Components of an IEP:** * **Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance:** This section describes your child's current academic and functional abilities. * **Measurable Annual Goals:** These are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. * **Special Education and Related Services:** This section lists the specific special education and related services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, and transportation. * **Accommodations and Modifications:** This section describes the accommodations and modifications that will be provided to help your child access the general education curriculum and participate in school activities. * **Placement:** This section specifies where your child will receive their special education services (e.g., general education classroom, resource room, special education classroom). * **Dates and Duration of Services:** This section indicates when the IEP will be implemented and how long the services will be provided. **Your Role in the ARD Committee:** As a parent, you are an essential member of the ARD committee. You have the right to participate fully in all decisions regarding your child's special education program. Your input is invaluable, as you know your child better than anyone else. **If You Disagree with the IEP:** If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to refuse to consent to its implementation. You can also request mediation or a due process hearing to resolve the dispute. It is important to document your concerns in writing and communicate them to the school district. **Annual Reviews:** The IEP must be reviewed and updated at least once a year to ensure it continues to meet your child's needs. You have the right to participate in these annual reviews and make recommendations for changes to the IEP. **Re-Evaluations:** Your child must be re-evaluated at least every three years to determine if they continue to be eligible for special education services. You can also request a re-evaluation at any time if you believe your child's needs have changed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
