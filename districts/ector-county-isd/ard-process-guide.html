What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. ARD Meetings in Ector County ISD: A Parent's Guide to Advocating for Your Child

Navigating the world of special education can feel overwhelming, but as a parent of a child with a disability in Ector County ISD (ECISD), you are your child's strongest advocate. This guide is designed to empower you with the knowledge you need to actively participate in Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meetings, ensuring your child receives the Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) they are entitled to under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting is a formal process used to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's unique educational needs, the specific services they will receive, and the goals they will work toward throughout the school year. It is the cornerstone of their special education. The ARD committee is comprised of individuals who play a role in your child's education. Under IDEA (34 CFR § 300.321) and the Texas Education Code (TEC § 29.005), the ARD committee must include: * **The parent(s) of the child:** You are a critical member of the ARD committee. Your insights and perspectives on your child's strengths, needs, and learning style are invaluable. * **At least one regular education teacher of the child (if the child is, or may be, participating in the regular education environment):** This teacher can provide valuable information about your child's performance in the general education setting and how they interact with their peers. * **At least one special education teacher, or where appropriate, at least one special education provider of the child:** This teacher is responsible for providing specialized instruction and support to your child. * **A representative of the local educational agency (LEA) who is qualified to provide, or supervise the provision of, specially designed instruction to meet the unique needs of children with disabilities, is knowledgeable about the general education curriculum, and is knowledgeable about the availability of resources of the LEA:** This person is usually an administrator or special education coordinator and has the authority to commit resources to support your child's IEP. * **An individual who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results:** This person may be a diagnostician, educational psychologist, or other qualified professional. They can explain the results of assessments and how they impact your child's learning. * **The child, if appropriate:** It's beneficial to involve your child in the ARD process, especially as they get older, to help them understand their IEP and advocate for their own needs. * **Other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding the child, including related services personnel as appropriate:** You or the school can invite other individuals who have knowledge or expertise about your child, such as therapists, doctors, or advocates. The ARD committee works together to make decisions about your child's educational program. The purpose of the meeting is to determine: * **Eligibility for Special Education Services:** If this is an initial ARD meeting, the committee will review evaluation data to determine if your child meets the criteria for a specific disability category under IDEA and Texas law. * **Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP):** This section of the IEP describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in all areas relevant to their education. It's crucial that the PLAAFP is accurate and comprehensive, as it forms the basis for setting appropriate goals. * **Measurable Annual Goals:** The ARD committee will develop specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child to achieve during the school year. These goals should be based on the PLAAFP and address your child's identified needs. * **Special Education and Related Services:** The IEP will outline the specific services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and assistive technology. * **Placement:** The ARD committee will determine the most appropriate educational setting for your child. This may include a general education classroom, resource room, self-contained classroom, or a combination of settings. IDEA requires that students with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE) to the maximum extent appropriate. * **Accommodations and Modifications:** The IEP will list any accommodations and modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum and participate in school activities. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, while modifications are changes to what a student learns.

5-Day Notice Rule

You are entitled to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five school days before the meeting, unless you agree to waive this requirement (Texas Education Code § 29.005(c)). This notice must include: * The purpose of the meeting * The date, time, and location of the meeting * A list of the individuals who are expected to attend * Your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child * A copy of the evaluation report, if applicable This five-day notice gives you time to prepare for the meeting, gather any necessary information, and consider your child's needs and goals. If you don't receive adequate notice, you have the right to request that the meeting be rescheduled.

What to Bring to the ARD Meeting

Being prepared for the ARD meeting is essential for ensuring that your child's needs are met. Here are some things you should bring: * **Your child's IEP (if it's a review meeting):** Review the IEP carefully before the meeting and make notes about what's working well and what needs to be changed. * **Evaluation reports and other relevant documents:** Bring copies of any evaluation reports, medical records, or other documents that support your child's needs. * **A list of your child's strengths and weaknesses:** This will help you communicate your understanding of your child's abilities and areas where they need support. * **A list of your child's goals:** Think about what you want your child to achieve academically, socially, and emotionally during the school year. * **A list of questions you want to ask:** This will help you stay focused and ensure that you get all the information you need. * **Notes from previous meetings:** Reviewing past discussions can help you track progress and identify any recurring issues. * **A friend, family member, or advocate:** Having someone with you can provide support and help you remember important details. An advocate can be particularly helpful if you feel intimidated or overwhelmed by the ARD process. Many disability rights organizations can provide free or low-cost advocacy services.

The 10-Day Recess (and Other Recesses)

Under Texas Education Code § 29.005(e), if, after holding an ARD committee meeting, a parent disagrees with the IEP developed for their child, the parent can request a recess of not more than 10 school days, excluding weekends and holidays. This "10-day recess" provides time for the parent to: * Consider the IEP and gather additional information * Consult with experts or advocates * Negotiate with the school district to reach an agreement It's important to understand that the 10-day recess is a powerful tool for parents who feel that their child's IEP is not adequate. During the recess, the school district cannot implement the IEP without your consent. Additionally, the ARD committee can call for a recess at any time if more information is needed to make informed decisions. For example, if the committee needs to observe your child in the classroom or conduct additional assessments, a recess can be called to allow time for these activities. Parents also have the right to request a recess if they feel that they need more time to consider the information being presented.

Disagreeing With the Team

Despite your best efforts, you may find yourself disagreeing with the ARD committee about your child's IEP. It is important to remember that you have rights and options for resolving disagreements. * **Document your concerns:** Keep a written record of your concerns and the reasons why you disagree with the committee's decisions. * **Request another ARD meeting:** You can request another ARD meeting to further discuss your concerns and try to reach an agreement. * **Consider mediation:** Mediation is a process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district resolve disagreements. It can be a less adversarial way to reach a mutually agreeable solution. ECISD must offer mediation as an option (Texas Education Code § 29.005(h)). * **Request a State Complaint Investigation:** A State Complaint is a formal written allegation that a school district has violated requirements under Part B of the IDEA or state law. See below for details on filing a State Complaint. * **Request a Due Process Hearing:** A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you can present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will make a decision about your child's IEP. Due process hearings are more formal and complex than other dispute resolution options, so it's advisable to seek legal representation.

Filing a State Complaint

Filing a State Complaint is a formal process for alleging that Ector County ISD has violated federal or state special education laws. Here's a general overview of the process, but it's important to consult the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website and seek legal advice for specific guidance. * **Who Can File:** Any individual or organization can file a State Complaint if they believe ECISD has violated the IDEA or related state laws with respect to a student with a disability. This can include parents, guardians, advocates, or even other concerned citizens. * **Time Limit:** You generally have one year from the date of the alleged violation to file a State Complaint. * **Content of the Complaint:** The complaint must be in writing and signed. It must include the following information: * The name and address of the student. * The name of the school the student attends. * A description of the alleged violation, including specific facts and dates. * The requirements of the IDEA or state law that you believe have been violated. * Proposed resolution of the problem to the extent known and available to the party at the time of filing the complaint. * **Filing the Complaint:** The complaint should be submitted to the Texas Education Agency (TEA). The TEA's website (tea.texas.gov) provides information on how to submit a complaint, including the mailing address and any online submission options. * **TEA Investigation:** Once the TEA receives a complaint, they will conduct an investigation. This may involve reviewing documents, interviewing witnesses, and gathering other relevant information. The TEA will typically complete the investigation within 60 days, but this timeline can be extended in certain circumstances. * **TEA Decision:** After the investigation, the TEA will issue a written decision. If the TEA finds that ECISD has violated special education laws, they will order corrective action. This may include: * Compensatory education services for the student * Training for school staff * Changes to ECISD policies and procedures * **Right to Appeal:** If you are not satisfied with the TEA's decision, you may have the right to appeal. Remember, you are not alone in advocating for your child. Numerous resources are available to support you, including disability rights organizations, parent training and information centers, and special education attorneys. By understanding your rights and actively participating in the ARD process, you can ensure that your child receives the education and support they need to reach their full potential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
