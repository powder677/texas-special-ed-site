Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Ector County ISD

If your child is facing disciplinary action in Ector County ISD, especially a suspension or potential expulsion, act *immediately*. Time is of the essence! This guide provides a rapid overview of your rights and how to protect your child. Don't delay seeking legal counsel or advocacy if you feel overwhelmed.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Ector County ISD, like all Texas school districts, has limitations on how long a student can be suspended. Generally, a student can be suspended for up to three school days for a single disciplinary infraction. However, the critical point is the cumulative suspension days. Once a student accumulates more than 10 days of suspension in a school year (even for seemingly minor offenses), significant changes occur. These can trigger what's known as a "Change of Placement" and require special consideration, particularly if your child has an IEP or 504 plan.

What to Watch Out For:

  • Tracking Suspension Days: Keep a detailed record of every suspension your child receives. Note the date, reason, length of suspension, and the name of the administrator who issued it. This is your responsibility. Do not rely on the school district to accurately track this for you.
  • Notice of 10th Day: The district is required to provide notice before or at the point the 10th day of suspension is assigned. Often, this notice is provided alongside a Notice of Parent Rights. Do NOT ignore these documents!
  • Informal Suspensions: Be wary of "informal suspensions" where your child is sent home but not officially suspended. These days can still contribute to the 10-day threshold. Document them carefully. If the child is removed, even for an afternoon, document the time of removal and the reason for removal.
  • Consequences Beyond Suspension: Even if the suspension is less than 10 days, the school might impose other disciplinary measures like detention, Saturday school, or removal from extracurricular activities. Be aware of these and challenge them if you believe they are unfair or disproportionate.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

If your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or a Section 504 plan, and the school is considering a suspension that would result in a cumulative total of more than 10 school days of suspension, or is considering a Change of Placement due to a disciplinary matter, a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) MUST be conducted. This is a critical safeguard to protect students with disabilities from being unfairly disciplined for behaviors that are related to their disability.

Purpose of the MDR:

The MDR team, which includes you as the parent, relevant school staff, and potentially your child (depending on their age and abilities), must determine if:

  1. The conduct in question was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability; OR
  2. The conduct in question was the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP.

Key Considerations During the MDR:

  • Review the IEP: Carefully review your child's IEP to ensure it is being followed correctly. Look for accommodations, modifications, and behavioral interventions that should have been in place.
  • Present Evidence: Bring any evidence that supports your claim that the behavior is related to your child's disability. This could include doctor's notes, therapist reports, previous IEP meeting minutes, or examples of the school's failure to implement the IEP.
  • Challenge Assumptions: Be prepared to challenge any assumptions made by the school staff about your child's behavior. Advocate for your child and clearly explain how their disability impacts their actions.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the school's assessment of your child's disability or the relationship between their disability and the behavior, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense (under certain circumstances).

What Happens If The Behavior IS a Manifestation?

If the MDR team determines that the behavior *was* a manifestation of the child's disability, the school MUST:

  • Return the child to their original placement (unless you and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the behavioral intervention plan).
  • Conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA), unless one has already been conducted.
  • Develop or revise a Behavioral Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The BIP *must* be tailored to your child's specific needs and address the underlying causes of the behavior.

What Happens If The Behavior IS NOT a Manifestation?

If the MDR team determines that the behavior *was not* a manifestation of the child's disability, the school can proceed with disciplinary measures, including suspension or expulsion, to the same extent that it would apply to a student without a disability. However, the school is still required to provide educational services to your child during the period of removal so that they can continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals.

Change of Placement

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when a series of removals constitutes a pattern (e.g., frequent short suspensions that add up to a significant amount of time). This can include placement in a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP) or expulsion.

When Does a Change of Placement Trigger IDEA Protections?

For students with IEPs, a Change of Placement due to disciplinary reasons triggers significant rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), including the right to an MDR, as discussed above.

What to Do if a Change of Placement Is Proposed:

  • Demand an MDR: If your child has an IEP and a Change of Placement is being considered, immediately demand a Manifestation Determination Review. Do this in writing.
  • Request a Full Explanation: Ask the school for a detailed explanation of why the Change of Placement is necessary, what the alternative placement will look like, and how it will meet your child's educational needs.
  • Document Everything: Keep meticulous records of all communication with the school, including emails, phone calls, and meeting minutes.
  • Consider Legal Counsel: A Change of Placement can have serious consequences for your child's education. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law to understand your rights and options.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe that Ector County ISD has violated your child's rights or that the school's disciplinary actions are unfair or discriminatory, you have the right to file a grievance. The grievance process is typically outlined in the district's policy manual (search for "Grievance Policy" on the Ector County ISD website).

Level 1 Grievance: This is the initial step in the grievance process. Typically, it involves submitting a written complaint to the school principal or another designated administrator. The complaint should:

  • Clearly state the specific issue you are grieving.
  • Provide all relevant facts and dates.
  • Identify the specific policy or law that you believe has been violated.
  • State the remedy you are seeking (e.g., reinstatement, removal of a suspension from the record, changes to the IEP).
  • Include copies of any relevant documents.

Timeline: The district usually has a specific timeline for responding to a Level 1 grievance. Follow up if you do not receive a response within the designated timeframe. Often, you have a limited amount of time to file the grievance. Act quickly!

Document Your Submission: Keep a copy of your grievance and proof of delivery (e.g., certified mail receipt).

Appeal: If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the Level 1 grievance, you can typically appeal to the next level of administration, such as the superintendent or the school board. The process for appealing is outlined in the district's grievance policy.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Ector County ISD has violated a federal special education law (IDEA), you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a school district grievance.

Requirements for a TEA Complaint:

  • Timeliness: The complaint must generally be filed within one year of the alleged violation.
  • Specificity: The complaint must clearly state the specific violation of IDEA and provide supporting facts.
  • Signatures: The complaint must be signed and dated.

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and determine whether the district has violated IDEA. If TEA finds a violation, it can order the district to take corrective action.

Filing a Complaint: Information on how to file a TEA complaint can be found on the TEA website.

Due Process

For students with IEPs, the IDEA provides the right to "due process" - a formal legal procedure to resolve disputes with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability.

When to Consider Due Process:

Due process is a more serious step than a grievance or a TEA complaint. It is typically considered when:

  • You have exhausted other avenues for resolving the dispute.
  • The dispute involves significant issues, such as a change in placement, a denial of services, or a failure to implement the IEP.
  • You are willing to engage in a legal process and potentially go to a hearing.

Due Process Hearing:

A due process hearing is similar to a court trial. You will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine the school district's witnesses. A hearing officer will then make a decision based on the evidence presented.

Mediation:

Prior to a due process hearing, you are typically required to participate in mediation with the school district. Mediation is a process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution.

Important Note: Due process is a complex legal process. It is highly recommended that you consult with an attorney specializing in special education law before filing a due process complaint.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
