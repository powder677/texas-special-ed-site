Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Edinburg CISD

Your child's education and well-being are paramount. If your child in Edinburg CISD is facing disciplinary action, particularly a suspension or potential change of placement, ACT IMMEDIATELY. This guide provides critical information about your rights and how to protect them. Time is of the essence!

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Know Your Limits

Texas law, and particularly the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) for students with disabilities, places limits on how long a student can be suspended. The general rule is that a student can be suspended for up to 10 school days for a single disciplinary incident. This is crucial information because exceeding this limit triggers specific legal protections, especially for students with Individualized Education Programs (IEPs) or Section 504 plans.

Why is this important? If your child is suspended for more than 10 school days, either consecutively or cumulatively throughout the school year for separate incidents that constitute a "pattern," it can be considered a Change of Placement, triggering specific procedural safeguards under IDEA. This is a serious situation and requires immediate action.

What to do if your child is facing a suspension:

  • Document Everything: Keep meticulous records of all communication with the school, including dates, times, names of individuals involved, and the specifics of the alleged infraction. Save emails, texts, and handwritten notes.
  • Ask Questions: Demand a clear explanation of the reason for the suspension. Request copies of any incident reports or disciplinary referrals. Don't accept vague explanations.
  • Know the Number of Days: Keep a running tally of all suspension days your child has served during the school year. Be vigilant about exceeding that 10-day threshold.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a critical process under IDEA that must be conducted when a school district is considering a disciplinary action that would result in a Change of Placement for a student with a disability. This includes suspensions exceeding 10 cumulative days in a school year that demonstrate a pattern.

The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's misconduct was:

  • Caused by or directly and substantially related to the student's disability; or
  • The direct result of the school district's failure to implement the student's IEP.

Who participates in the MDR? Typically, the MDR team includes:

  • The student's parents/guardians
  • Relevant members of the IEP team (e.g., special education teacher, general education teacher, school psychologist)
  • A representative of the school district with knowledge of the student's disability and disciplinary history.

What happens during the MDR? The team will review all relevant information, including:

  • The student's IEP
  • Evaluation data
  • Observations of the student
  • Disciplinary records

What if the MDR finds the behavior WAS a manifestation of the disability or a result of IEP implementation failure? If the team determines that the student's misconduct was a manifestation of their disability or the result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP, the school district cannot proceed with the disciplinary action that would constitute a Change of Placement. The IEP team must then conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA), implement a behavior intervention plan (BIP), or revise the existing BIP if one is already in place to address the behavior.

What if the MDR finds the behavior was NOT a manifestation of the disability? If the team determines that the student's behavior was not a manifestation of their disability, the student can be disciplined in the same manner as students without disabilities. HOWEVER, the school district is still responsible for providing special education and related services to the student during the period of disciplinary removal.

CRITICAL: If the school is proposing a disciplinary action that could lead to a Change of Placement for your child with an IEP or 504 plan, DEMAND an MDR immediately. Do not let them proceed without it.

Change of Placement: What It Means and Why You Should Be Concerned

A Change of Placement occurs when a disciplinary action results in a significant alteration of the student's educational environment. This can include:

  • Suspensions exceeding 10 consecutive school days.
  • A series of suspensions that total more than 10 school days and constitute a "pattern" of removal.
  • Expulsion.
  • Placement in an Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES) for more than 45 school days (for certain serious offenses).

Why is Change of Placement so important? Because it triggers significant procedural safeguards under IDEA. These safeguards ensure that students with disabilities are not unfairly penalized and that their educational needs continue to be met.

Your rights during a Change of Placement:

  • Right to an MDR: As described above, you have the right to an MDR to determine if the behavior was related to your child's disability.
  • Right to Continued Services: Even if your child is removed from their regular classroom, the school district is still obligated to provide them with special education and related services that will enable them to continue to participate in the general education curriculum, although in another setting, and to progress toward meeting the goals set out in the student's IEP.
  • Right to Challenge the Decision: You have the right to challenge the school district's decision through a due process hearing (see section below).

Beware of IAES placements! An Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES) is a temporary placement for students who have violated a code of conduct. While IAES placements can sometimes be necessary, they should not be used as a long-term solution or as a way to avoid providing appropriate special education services. Parents must be involved in the decision-making process regarding IAES placements.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in Edinburg CISD

If you believe your child's rights have been violated or that the school district has failed to follow proper procedures, you have the right to file a grievance. Edinburg CISD, like all Texas school districts, has a formal grievance process. This section outlines the Level 1 grievance process, which is the first step in addressing your concerns.

What is a grievance? A grievance is a formal written complaint alleging a violation, misinterpretation, or misapplication of school district policy, regulations, or a student's rights.

How to file a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Form: Contact the principal's office or the Edinburg CISD central administration office to obtain a copy of the district's grievance form. You may also find it on the district's website (search "Edinburg CISD Grievance Policy").
  2. Complete the Form Carefully: Provide a clear and concise description of the issue you are grieving. Include dates, times, names of individuals involved, and specific details of the alleged violation. Be as specific as possible. Refer to specific policies or procedures that you believe were violated.
  3. Gather Supporting Documentation: Attach any relevant documents to your grievance form, such as emails, letters, incident reports, or IEP documents.
  4. Submit the Grievance Form: Submit the completed grievance form to the appropriate administrator. At Level 1, this is typically the campus principal or the immediate supervisor of the individual you are grieving against. Make sure to obtain a receipt or confirmation that your grievance was received.
  5. Deadlines: Pay close attention to the deadlines for filing a Level 1 grievance. These deadlines are typically outlined in the district's grievance policy and are often very short (e.g., 10-15 days from the date of the incident). Missing the deadline could result in your grievance being dismissed.
  6. Meet with the Administrator: The administrator will typically schedule a meeting with you to discuss your grievance. Prepare to clearly articulate your concerns and provide any additional information that may be relevant.
  7. Receive a Written Response: The administrator is required to provide you with a written response to your grievance within a specified timeframe (again, check the district's policy).

What if you are not satisfied with the Level 1 response? If you are not satisfied with the administrator's response at Level 1, you have the right to appeal to Level 2, which typically involves a higher-level administrator (e.g., the superintendent or a designee). The district's grievance policy will outline the procedures for appealing to the next level. Don't delay! The timeframe to appeal is also short.

TEA State Complaint: An External Option

In addition to the district's grievance process, you also have the option of filing a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). A TEA complaint is an external review of your concerns and can be particularly effective if you believe the school district is violating state or federal laws related to special education or other student rights.

When to file a TEA Complaint: You may file a TEA complaint if you believe the school district has violated:

  • The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
  • Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act
  • Other state or federal laws related to education

How to file a TEA Complaint:

  1. Obtain the Complaint Form: You can download the complaint form from the TEA website (search "TEA Special Education Complaint").
  2. Complete the Form Thoroughly: Provide a detailed description of the alleged violation, including dates, times, names of individuals involved, and specific details. Attach any supporting documentation, such as IEP documents, disciplinary records, emails, or letters.
  3. Specific Requirements: The TEA complaint form requires specific information. You need to clearly state the specific law or regulation that you believe the school district has violated.
  4. Submit the Complaint Form: Submit the completed complaint form to the TEA. The instructions for submitting the form are available on the TEA website.
  5. Deadlines: TEA complaints must typically be filed within one year of the date of the alleged violation.

What happens after you file a TEA Complaint? The TEA will review your complaint and determine whether to investigate. If the TEA decides to investigate, they will typically contact the school district and request information. The TEA may also conduct interviews with relevant individuals. After completing its investigation, the TEA will issue a written report outlining its findings and any corrective actions that the school district must take.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Protection

Due Process is the ultimate legal protection afforded to parents of students with disabilities under IDEA. It is a formal legal process that allows you to challenge the school district's decisions regarding your child's special education services or disciplinary actions.

When to Request a Due Process Hearing: You can request a due process hearing if you disagree with the school district regarding:

  • Your child's identification, evaluation, or educational placement.
  • The provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE) to your child.
  • Disciplinary actions that constitute a Change of Placement.

How to Request a Due Process Hearing:

  1. File a Due Process Complaint: You must file a written due process complaint with the TEA and the school district. The complaint must contain specific information, including:
    • The name and address of your child.
    • A description of the nature of the problem, including the facts relating to the problem.
    • A proposed resolution of the problem to the extent known and available to you at the time.
  2. Statute of Limitations: There is a statute of limitations for filing a due process complaint. You must typically file the complaint within two years of the date you knew or should have known about the alleged violation.

What happens after you file a Due Process Complaint?

  • Resolution Session: The school district must convene a resolution session with you within 15 days of receiving your due process complaint. The purpose of the resolution session is to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  • Mediation: You may also choose to participate in mediation, a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement.
  • Due Process Hearing: If the dispute is not resolved through the resolution session or mediation, a due process hearing will be conducted before an impartial hearing officer. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at the hearing, present evidence, and cross-examine witnesses.

Important Considerations:

  • Legal Representation: Due process hearings can be complex legal proceedings. It is strongly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney who specializes in special education law. Organizations like Disability Rights Texas can sometimes provide assistance.
  • Stay Put: During the pendency of a due process hearing, your child generally has the right to remain in their current educational placement (the "stay put" provision). This can be crucial to ensuring that your child's education is not disrupted while the dispute is being resolved. There are exceptions to "stay put," especially concerning discipline for certain behaviors (weapons, drugs, serious bodily injury). Consult with an attorney.

Don't wait! Seek legal advice immediately if you believe your child's rights are being violated. The information in this guide is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Consult with an attorney to discuss your specific situation and understand your legal options. Protect your child!

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
