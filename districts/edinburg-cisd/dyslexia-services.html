Dyslexia Services in Edinburg CISD

Dyslexia Services in Edinburg CISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Edinburg Consolidated Independent School District (CISD), aligning with the requirements and recommendations outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is the guiding document for dyslexia identification and intervention in Texas public schools. The 2024 update reflects ongoing research and best practices in supporting students with dyslexia and related disorders. Edinburg CISD adheres to the principles and procedures outlined in this handbook.

Key Updates and Emphasis in the 2024 Handbook:

  • Early Identification and Intervention: The handbook stresses the importance of early identification through universal screening and targeted interventions at the earliest possible grade levels, often starting in kindergarten and first grade.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook emphasizes the use of Structured Literacy approaches for instruction. Structured Literacy is explicit, systematic, cumulative, diagnostic, and multisensory.
  • Comprehensive Assessment: The handbook outlines procedures for comprehensive assessment to identify students with dyslexia, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook highlights the crucial role of parents in the dyslexia identification and intervention process and emphasizes open communication and collaboration between school staff and parents.
  • Professional Development: The handbook emphasizes the need for ongoing professional development for teachers and staff in dyslexia identification, intervention, and Structured Literacy approaches.
  • Technology Integration: The handbook encourages the use of technology to support students with dyslexia, including assistive technology and digital learning tools.
  • Progress Monitoring: The handbook stresses the importance of ongoing progress monitoring to track student growth and adjust interventions as needed.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): Dyslexia intervention is implemented within a multi-tiered system of supports (MTSS) framework.

Edinburg CISD staff are trained on the principles and practices outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 to ensure that students with dyslexia receive appropriate and effective support.

Mandatory Screening

Edinburg CISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade, as required by Texas law. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. Screening typically includes measures of:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds in spoken language (e.g., rhyming, segmenting, blending).
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly name a series of familiar objects, colors, or letters.
  • Letter Knowledge: The ability to recognize and name letters of the alphabet.
  • Phonological Memory: The ability to hold phonological information in working memory.
  • Sound Symbol Correspondence: Connecting speech sounds (phonemes) to the letters that represent them (graphemes).

Students who demonstrate characteristics of dyslexia during the initial screening process may receive further diagnostic testing to determine if they have dyslexia. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as potentially at risk for dyslexia and about the next steps in the evaluation process.

It is important to note that screening is not a diagnosis. It is a way to identify students who may need further evaluation.

Structured Literacy Programs

Edinburg CISD utilizes Structured Literacy programs that align with the evidence-based practices recommended in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024. Structured Literacy is an explicit, systematic, cumulative, diagnostic, and multisensory approach to reading instruction. This approach is designed to address the specific learning needs of students with dyslexia by providing direct instruction in the foundational skills necessary for reading and spelling.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Instruction in the sound structure of language, including phonemes, syllables, and morphemes.
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Systematic teaching of the relationships between letters and sounds.
  • Syllable Instruction: Explicit instruction in syllable types and syllable division rules.
  • Morphology: Instruction in meaningful parts of words (prefixes, suffixes, roots).
  • Syntax: Understanding the rules for how words combine to form sentences.
  • Semantics: Comprehension of the meaning of words and sentences.

Examples of Structured Literacy programs that Edinburg CISD may utilize (please note that this list may change based on district adoption and is intended for illustrative purposes only):

  • Orton-Gillingham-based programs: These programs are designed to be highly individualized and intensive.
  • Evidence-based reading interventions: These are programs specifically designed to support students who struggle with reading.

Instruction is delivered in small groups or one-on-one to provide individualized attention and support. Progress monitoring is conducted regularly to track student progress and adjust instruction as needed.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) through an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of eligibility depends on the severity of the student's dyslexia and the level of support required to access the general education curriculum.

Section 504:

  • Provides accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Eligibility is based on having a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading).
  • Accommodations are designed to level the playing field and provide students with the support they need to succeed in the general education classroom.
  • Does not provide specialized instruction.

IEP (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act):

  • Provides specialized instruction and related services for students with disabilities who require a more intensive level of support.
  • Eligibility is based on meeting specific criteria for one of the 13 disability categories outlined in IDEA, and demonstrating a need for specially designed instruction. Dyslexia itself is not a specific disability category; however, students with dyslexia may qualify under the category of Specific Learning Disability (SLD).
  • An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the student's individualized goals, objectives, and services.
  • Provides specialized instruction, which may include instruction delivered using a structured literacy approach.

Key Differences:

  • Eligibility: Section 504 has broader eligibility criteria than IDEA.
  • Services: Section 504 provides accommodations, while IDEA provides specialized instruction and related services.
  • Documentation: Section 504 requires a 504 plan, while IDEA requires an IEP.
  • Legal Requirements: IDEA has more stringent legal requirements than Section 504.

The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee determines whether a student qualifies for special education services under IDEA. The 504 committee determines whether a student qualifies for accommodations under Section 504. Both committees include parents as active members.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional practices that help students with dyslexia access the general education curriculum. Accommodations do not change the content of the curriculum but rather provide students with the support they need to demonstrate their knowledge and skills. Edinburg CISD provides a range of accommodations for students with dyslexia, which may include:

  • Reading Accommodations:
    • Extended time on reading assignments and tests.
    • Text-to-speech software.
    • Audiobooks.
    • Preferential seating.
    • Highlighted text.
    • Use of a reading guide or ruler.
    • Reduced reading workload.
  • Writing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on writing assignments and tests.
    • Use of a keyboard or word processor.
    • Speech-to-text software.
    • Graphic organizers.
    • Pre-writing activities.
    • Reduced writing workload.
    • Copy of notes.
  • Spelling Accommodations:
    • Use of a spell checker.
    • Allowing phonetic spelling on drafts.
    • Providing a list of frequently misspelled words.
  • Testing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on tests.
    • Testing in a quiet environment.
    • Read-aloud accommodations.
    • Use of assistive technology.
    • Breaking tests into smaller chunks.
  • Classroom Accommodations:
    • Preferential seating.
    • Frequent breaks.
    • Clear and concise instructions.
    • Multisensory teaching methods.
    • Visual aids.
    • Opportunities for repetition and review.

The specific accommodations provided to a student are determined based on their individual needs and documented in their 504 plan or IEP. It's important to remember accommodations should be individualized and based on data.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia should contact their child's teacher, school counselor, or the campus administrator to request an evaluation. The request should be made in writing and should include specific concerns about the child's reading and spelling skills.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or campus administrator to express your concerns.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation. This request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth.
    • Your contact information.
    • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading and spelling skills.
    • Any supporting documentation, such as report cards or previous evaluations.
  3. School Response: The school is required to respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe, as outlined by Texas law.
  4. Evaluation Process: If the school determines that an evaluation is warranted, they will conduct a comprehensive evaluation to assess your child's reading, spelling, and related skills.
  5. Evaluation Results: You will receive a copy of the evaluation report and be invited to a meeting to discuss the results and determine if your child is eligible for dyslexia services.

Important Contact Information:

Contact your child's school directly for campus-specific procedures and contact information. You can find school contact information on the Edinburg CISD website: https://www.ecisd.us/ (Replace with actual Edinburg CISD website).

Parents are encouraged to actively participate in the evaluation and intervention process. Open communication between parents and school staff is essential to ensure that students with dyslexia receive the support they need to succeed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
