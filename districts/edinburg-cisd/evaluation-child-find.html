Requesting an Evaluation in Edinburg CISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Edinburg CISD

This guide provides information for parents and legal guardians seeking a special education evaluation for their child within the Edinburg Consolidated Independent School District (CISD) in Texas. It outlines the process, timelines, and legal obligations of the school district as defined by Texas law, particularly the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Texas Education Code (TEC).

How to Submit a Written Request

The first, and arguably most crucial, step in initiating the special education evaluation process is submitting a formal, written request to Edinburg CISD. This request acts as a trigger, setting in motion a series of legal requirements for the district. It's vital that the request is clear, concise, and properly submitted to the appropriate personnel.

Who to Address the Request To: The request should be addressed to the campus principal. Sending a copy to the special education director is also a good practice. If your child attends a pre-K program, contact the Early Childhood Special Education Coordinator or designated campus contact.

Content of the Written Request: Your written request should include the following information:

  • Date: The date the request is being submitted.
  • Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: Accurate identification is essential.
  • Child's School and Grade Level: This helps the district locate your child's records quickly.
  • Your Name(s), Address, Phone Number(s), and Email Address(es): Provide multiple ways for the district to contact you.
  • A Clear Statement of Request: Explicitly state that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services. For example, "I am writing to formally request a full and individual initial evaluation to determine my child's eligibility for special education services under IDEA."
  • Specific Concerns and Reasons for the Request: Be as specific as possible about your concerns regarding your child's academic, behavioral, social, emotional, or physical development. Provide examples of specific difficulties your child is experiencing. For example: "My child is struggling with reading comprehension, consistently scoring below grade level on reading assessments and requiring significant support with homework. They also seem easily frustrated and have difficulty focusing in class." Mention any relevant medical diagnoses or conditions.
  • Any Supporting Documentation: Include any reports, evaluations, medical records, or other documentation that supports your concerns. This might include previous evaluations from doctors, therapists, or educational specialists.
  • Preferred Method of Communication: Indicate how you prefer to be contacted (e.g., phone, email, mail).

Sample Request Letter Template:

[Date]
[Principal's Name]
[School Name]
[School Address]

RE: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Full Name]

Dear Principal [Principal's Last Name],

I am writing to formally request a full and individual initial evaluation to determine my child, [Child's Full Name], born on [Date of Birth], who is in [Grade Level] at [School Name],'s eligibility for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.

I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s [Specify areas of concern: e.g., reading skills, math skills, behavior in class]. Specifically, [Child's Name] is [Provide specific examples: e.g., struggling to read grade-level text, having difficulty completing math assignments, exhibiting disruptive behavior in the classroom]. [Add any relevant medical or diagnostic information].

I have attached [List attached documents, if any].

I can be reached at [Your Phone Number] or [Your Email Address]. My preferred method of communication is [Your Preferred Method of Communication].

Thank you for your prompt attention to this matter. I look forward to hearing from you soon.

Sincerely,
[Your Name(s)]

Submitting the Request: Send the written request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides you with proof that the district received your request. Keep a copy of the request and the return receipt for your records.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Upon receiving your written request for a special education evaluation, Edinburg CISD is legally obligated to adhere to a strict timeline dictated by Texas Education Code. This timeline is typically 45 school days, not calendar days.

What the 45 Days Encompasses: The 45-school-day timeline encompasses several key steps:

  • Review of Existing Data (RED): The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, including parents, reviews existing data on your child. This includes classroom performance, grades, attendance records, teacher observations, and any previous evaluations.
  • Decision Regarding Evaluation: Based on the RED process, the ARD committee decides whether to proceed with a full and individual evaluation. The school district *must* conduct an evaluation if they suspect that a child has a disability and needs special education services, regardless of parent consent (though parental consent is generally sought and highly encouraged).
  • Obtaining Parental Consent for Evaluation: If the ARD committee decides to evaluate, the district must obtain your written consent before proceeding. They will provide you with an Evaluation Consent Form. It's important to read this form carefully and understand what the evaluation will entail.
  • Conducting the Evaluation (FIE): Once parental consent is received, the district must conduct a comprehensive Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) to assess your child's strengths and weaknesses in various areas relevant to their suspected disability.
  • ARD Committee Meeting: After the FIE is completed, the ARD committee must meet to review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services.

Important Considerations Regarding the Timeline:

  • School Days vs. Calendar Days: Remember that the timeline is based on school days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted.
  • Documenting the Timeline: Keep meticulous records of all communication with the school district, including dates of correspondence, meetings, and any delays encountered. This documentation can be crucial if you believe the district is not adhering to the timeline.
  • Extensions: Under certain circumstances, the ARD committee can request an extension of the 45-day timeline. However, this requires mutual agreement between the parent and the school district and must be documented.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability and, if so, what specific educational needs the child has. The scope of the FIE must be tailored to the individual child and the specific areas of concern identified in the referral.

Areas Typically Assessed in an FIE:

  • Cognitive Ability: This assesses intellectual functioning and learning potential. It often involves standardized tests like the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC).
  • Academic Achievement: This evaluates a child's performance in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. Standardized achievement tests are commonly used.
  • Adaptive Behavior: This assesses a child's ability to function independently in everyday life, including self-care, social skills, and communication. Assessments like the Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales are often used.
  • Communication Skills: This evaluates a child's receptive and expressive language skills, including articulation, fluency, and understanding of language. A speech-language pathologist typically conducts this assessment.
  • Motor Skills: This assesses a child's gross and fine motor skills, including coordination, balance, and dexterity. An occupational or physical therapist typically conducts this assessment.
  • Social and Emotional Functioning: This evaluates a child's emotional regulation, social skills, and behavior. This may involve observations, interviews, and rating scales completed by parents, teachers, and the child.
  • Health and Medical History: The FIE should consider any relevant medical history or diagnoses that may impact the child's educational performance.
  • Vision and Hearing Screening: This is to rule out any vision or hearing impairments that may be contributing to the child's difficulties.

Texas Specific Considerations: Texas law requires that the FIE be conducted by appropriately qualified professionals. This means that the individuals conducting the assessments must have the necessary certifications and training to administer and interpret the tests. The ARD committee, including the parent, has the right to determine which assessments are necessary and appropriate for the child. Parents also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if they disagree with the district's evaluation. See "What If They Refuse" below.

Child Find Obligations

Edinburg CISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under IDEA to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public schools. This is known as "Child Find."

What Child Find Entails:

  • Public Awareness Activities: The district must conduct public awareness campaigns to inform parents and the community about special education services and how to access them.
  • Screening and Referral Programs: The district must have procedures in place to screen children for potential disabilities and to refer them for evaluation if necessary.
  • Early Intervention Services: The district should provide early intervention services for young children who are at risk for developmental delays.
  • Collaboration with Other Agencies: The district should collaborate with other agencies, such as healthcare providers and social service organizations, to identify children with disabilities.

Parent's Role in Child Find: If you suspect that your child has a disability, you have the right to contact Edinburg CISD and request an evaluation, even if your child is not currently enrolled in a public school. The district is obligated to respond to your request and initiate the evaluation process if there is reason to suspect a disability. If you believe the district is not fulfilling its Child Find obligations, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What If They Refuse

Edinburg CISD can't simply refuse to evaluate your child if you've submitted a written request. Under IDEA, they must either conduct an evaluation or provide you with a written explanation of why they refuse. This is a critical point. They cannot ignore your request.

Reasons for Refusal: The school district may refuse to evaluate if they believe there is insufficient evidence to suggest that your child has a disability that requires special education services. However, this determination must be based on a review of existing data and not simply a subjective opinion.

Your Options When Refused:

  • Request a Written Explanation: If the district refuses to evaluate, demand a written explanation of the reasons for the refusal. This explanation must include the data used to make the decision.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district work with a neutral third party to try to resolve your dispute. The TEA offers free mediation services to parents of children with disabilities.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You have the right to file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe the district has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Texas law. The TEA will investigate your complaint and take corrective action if necessary.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a more formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will make a decision based on the evidence presented. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the district's refusal to evaluate or with the results of the district's evaluation (if they eventually conduct one). If you request an IEE at public expense, the district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. The IEE can provide a valuable second opinion and can be used as evidence in a due process hearing.

Documentation is Key: Throughout this process, it is essential to document everything, including dates of communication, summaries of conversations, and copies of all documents. This documentation will be crucial if you need to pursue further action.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is completed, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee must meet to review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services. You are a crucial member of this ARD committee.

The ARD Committee Meeting:

  • Review of Evaluation Results: The ARD committee will carefully review the FIE report, discussing the strengths and weaknesses identified, and the implications for your child's education.
  • Eligibility Determination: The committee will determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for one or more of the specific disability categories defined under IDEA and Texas law. These categories include, but are not limited to: Specific Learning Disability, Autism, Emotional Disturbance, Intellectual Disability, and Speech Impairment.
  • Development of the Individualized Education Program (IEP): If your child is found eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and services. You, as the parent, have the right to actively participate in the development of the IEP and to provide input on the goals and services included.
  • Placement Decision: The ARD committee will determine the appropriate educational placement for your child. This placement should be in the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE), meaning that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Placement options may include general education with support, resource room, self-contained classroom, or, in rare cases, a more restrictive setting.

What to Expect in the IEP: An IEP in Texas will contain, among other things, measurable annual goals (both academic and functional), a statement of how progress will be measured, specially designed instruction to meet unique needs, related services (such as speech therapy or occupational therapy), accommodations, and modifications to the general education curriculum, if needed.

Your Rights After the Evaluation:

  • You have the right to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions: If you disagree with the eligibility determination, the IEP, or the placement decision, you have the right to request mediation, file a complaint with the TEA, or request a due process hearing.
  • You have the right to request changes to the IEP: You can request an ARD committee meeting at any time to review and revise the IEP.
  • You have the right to receive regular progress reports: The school district must provide you with regular progress reports on your child's progress toward their IEP goals.

Navigating the special education process can be complex and overwhelming. It is highly recommended to connect with parent support organizations, legal aid services, or special education advocates in the Edinburg area for assistance and guidance. Understanding your rights and actively participating in the process are essential to ensuring that your child receives the appropriate education and support they need to succeed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
