The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Know Your Rights!

United ISD, like all school districts, is subject to limitations on out-of-school suspensions, especially for students receiving special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The "10-Day Suspension Rule" is critical. A student with a disability cannot be suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year without triggering specific IDEA protections.

What does this mean for you?

  • Tracking is Key: You need to meticulously track every day your child is suspended, even partial days. The school district should be doing this, but do not rely on them. Keep your own records!
  • Beyond 10 Days: Once a student with a disability has been suspended for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year, a "Change of Placement" may be occurring, and IDEA protections kick in. This includes a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).
  • Short-Term Suspensions Accumulating: Even seemingly minor suspensions can add up quickly. Don't dismiss a one or two-day suspension as inconsequential. It could be the one that pushes your child over the 10-day limit.
  • In-School Suspension: While In-School Suspension (ISS) generally doesn't count toward the 10-day limit if the student is receiving their educational services, it's vital to ensure your child is actually receiving those services in ISS. If ISS is essentially just isolating your child without instruction, it could be argued as an unofficial suspension, potentially impacting the 10-day count.

Act Immediately: If your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 days of suspension, contact the school principal, special education director, and your child's ARD committee members in writing, demanding they convene a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) if they haven't already. Document everything!

What Is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR)?

An MDR is a crucial process under IDEA designed to determine if a student's misconduct is a "manifestation" of their disability. In other words, the ARD committee must determine if the behavior that led to the suspension was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability; or was the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the student's IEP.

Why is the MDR so important?

  • Protects Your Child: If the ARD committee determines the behavior was a manifestation of the disability, the school cannot suspend your child beyond the initial 10 days (unless certain exceptions apply, such as weapons or drugs).
  • IEP Review: A manifestation determination requires the ARD committee to review the student's IEP and consider if it was appropriate and being implemented correctly. This could lead to necessary changes and improvements to the IEP.
  • Focus on Behavior: Even if the behavior is deemed a manifestation, the ARD committee must develop a plan to address the behavior, potentially involving a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP).

How the MDR Works:

  1. Convening the MDR: The MDR must be convened within 10 school days of the decision to change the student's placement due to disciplinary action (which, again, is triggered after exceeding 10 cumulative suspension days).
  2. ARD Committee Review: The ARD committee (including you, the parent) will review all relevant information, including the student's IEP, teacher observations, and any relevant medical or psychological evaluations.
  3. Determining Manifestation: The ARD committee will answer the question: "Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?" and "Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP?"
  4. Outcomes:
    • Manifestation: The student returns to their previous placement (unless parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the modification of the behavioral plan). The ARD committee must conduct an FBA (unless one has already been conducted) and implement a BIP. If a BIP already exists, it must be reviewed and modified, as necessary, to address the behavior.
    • No Manifestation: The school can proceed with disciplinary action as they would for a student without a disability. However, the school must continue to provide educational services so the student can continue to participate in the general education curriculum (although in another setting) and progress toward meeting the goals set out in the student's IEP.

Be Prepared for the MDR: Gather all documentation relevant to your child's disability and the incident leading to the suspension. Be prepared to advocate strongly for your child and to explain how their disability impacted their behavior. Bring an advocate or attorney if possible!

Change of Placement: What It Really Means

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a disciplinary action removes a student with a disability from their current educational setting for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, or when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a change of placement. This includes suspensions exceeding 10 days, expulsions, and potentially repeated short-term suspensions if they demonstrate a pattern.

Why is this significant?

  • Triggers IDEA Protections: As mentioned earlier, a Change of Placement triggers the requirement for an MDR.
  • Continuity of Services: Even if the disciplinary action is allowed to proceed (i.e., the MDR determines the behavior was not a manifestation), the school district is still obligated to provide educational services to your child so they can continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals.
  • Protections Against Discriminatory Discipline: IDEA aims to prevent students with disabilities from being disciplined more harshly than their non-disabled peers for similar behaviors. If you believe your child is being unfairly targeted due to their disability, document every instance and seek legal advice.

Document Everything Regarding Placement: Keep detailed records of your child's educational setting, any changes made, and the reasons for those changes. This documentation will be invaluable if you need to file a grievance or a TEA complaint.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in United ISD

If you believe your child's rights have been violated concerning discipline or special education services, you have the right to file a grievance. United ISD likely has a formal grievance policy outlining the process. The first step is typically filing a Level 1 grievance at the campus level.

Steps to Filing a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Policy: Request a copy of United ISD's grievance policy from the school principal or the district's central office. It should also be available on the district's website. Read it carefully.
  2. Identify the Grievance: Clearly define what you are grieving. Be specific about the dates, incidents, and individuals involved. State the policy or law you believe was violated. For example, "I am grieving the principal's decision to suspend my child for three days without conducting an MDR after they had already been suspended for eight days this school year, violating IDEA regulations regarding Change of Placement."
  3. Write Your Grievance: Prepare a written grievance that includes:
    • Your child's name and student ID number
    • Your name and contact information
    • The date of the incident(s)
    • A detailed description of the incident(s)
    • The specific policy or law you believe was violated
    • The remedy you are seeking (e.g., removal of the suspension from the student's record, a new MDR, changes to the IEP)
  4. Submit the Grievance: Submit your written grievance to the appropriate school official (usually the principal). Obtain a dated receipt confirming your submission.
  5. Timeline: Be aware of the timelines outlined in the district's grievance policy. Typically, the school official has a limited time to respond to your grievance (e.g., 10 school days).
  6. Keep Records: Keep copies of your grievance, the school's response, and any other relevant documentation.

Do Not Delay: Grievance policies often have strict deadlines. Missing the deadline can mean your grievance will be dismissed. Start the process immediately!

TEA State Complaint: Escalating Your Concerns

If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the Level 1 grievance (or subsequent levels of the grievance process), you have the right to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). A TEA complaint alleges that United ISD has violated state or federal laws or regulations related to special education.

Filing a TEA Complaint:

  • Eligibility: You must file the TEA complaint within one year of the date of the violation.
  • TEA Complaint Form: Obtain the official TEA complaint form from the TEA website.
  • Required Information: The TEA complaint form requires detailed information, including:
    • Your child's information
    • United ISD's information
    • A clear and concise description of the alleged violation
    • Supporting documentation (IEP, disciplinary records, correspondence with the school)
  • Submitting the Complaint: Submit the completed TEA complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA according to their instructions.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and determine if a violation occurred.
  • Remedies: If TEA finds a violation, they can order United ISD to take corrective action, which may include providing compensatory services to your child, revising policies, or providing training to staff.

TEA Complaint Assistance: Consider seeking assistance from disability rights organizations or attorneys to help you prepare and file your TEA complaint effectively.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Right

Due process is a legal right under IDEA that allows parents to challenge decisions made by the school district regarding their child's special education services. This is typically the final step in resolving disputes.

When to Consider Due Process:

  • Disagreement on IEP: You disagree with the content of your child's IEP.
  • Denial of Services: The school district has denied your child necessary special education services.
  • Disciplinary Actions: You believe your child's disciplinary actions violate IDEA.

Due Process Hearing:

  • Filing a Request: You must file a written request for a due process hearing with TEA and United ISD.
  • Mediation: Before a due process hearing, you may be required to participate in mediation with the school district to attempt to resolve the dispute informally.
  • Hearing Officer: A due process hearing is conducted by an impartial hearing officer.
  • Evidence and Testimony: You and the school district will present evidence and testimony to support your positions.
  • Hearing Officer Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision, which is binding on both parties (unless appealed).

Due Process Requires Legal Expertise: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. You should strongly consider hiring an attorney with experience in special education law to represent you. The school district will almost certainly have legal representation.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.
