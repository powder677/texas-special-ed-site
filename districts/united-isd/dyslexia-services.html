Dyslexia Services in United ISD

Dyslexia Services in United ISD

This guide provides information regarding dyslexia services available to students within United ISD (UISD). It is intended for parents, guardians, educators, and anyone seeking to understand the identification and support processes for students with dyslexia within the framework of Texas law and UISD policy.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook serves as the definitive guide for addressing dyslexia in Texas public schools. The 2024 update, released by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), provides comprehensive guidelines for screening, identification, and instruction of students with dyslexia. Key areas covered include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook clearly defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Screening Procedures: The handbook outlines mandatory screening procedures for students in kindergarten through second grade. These screenings aim to identify students at risk for dyslexia early on. Specific screening instruments and timelines are detailed.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook provides guidance on how to comprehensively evaluate a student suspected of having dyslexia, including assessments of phonological awareness, decoding, reading fluency, reading comprehension, and spelling. It emphasizes the importance of using multiple sources of data, including classroom observations, parent input, and standardized test scores.
  • Instructional Approaches: The handbook promotes the use of Structured Literacy programs, which are evidence-based instructional approaches that systematically and explicitly teach reading skills. It provides guidance on the essential components of Structured Literacy and how to implement these approaches effectively.
  • Accommodation and Modification Strategies: The handbook offers a range of accommodations and modifications that can be used to support students with dyslexia in the classroom. These strategies aim to address the specific needs of each student and help them access the curriculum.
  • Legal Requirements: The handbook outlines the legal requirements related to dyslexia services, including the requirements of state law and federal law (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act - IDEA).
  • Parental Involvement: The handbook stresses the importance of parental involvement in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. It encourages schools to communicate regularly with parents and to involve them in the development of individualized education plans (IEPs) or 504 plans.

Accessibility of the Handbook: The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook is available for free download on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Parents and educators are strongly encouraged to review the handbook to understand the current guidelines and requirements.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten, first grade, and second grade. United ISD adheres to these requirements using TEA-approved screening instruments and procedures.

Purpose of Screening: The primary goal of the mandatory screening is to identify students who are at risk for developing dyslexia. Early identification allows for early intervention, which can significantly improve reading outcomes.

Screening Instruments: UISD utilizes standardized screening tools that assess key skills related to reading development, including:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds in spoken language.
  • Letter Knowledge: Knowing the names and sounds of letters.
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly and accurately name letters, numbers, or objects.
  • Phonological Memory: The ability to hold and manipulate sounds in working memory.
  • Decoding Skills: The ability to apply letter-sound knowledge to read words.

Screening Timeline: Screenings are typically conducted during the fall and winter of each school year. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia. Notification will include information about next steps, such as further evaluation and/or intervention.

What Happens After Screening? If a student is identified as being at risk, further assessment may be recommended. This assessment may include a comprehensive evaluation to determine whether the student meets the criteria for a diagnosis of dyslexia. Parents have the right to request an evaluation at any time, regardless of screening results.

Structured Literacy Programs

United ISD utilizes Structured Literacy programs to provide evidence-based instruction for students with dyslexia and students at risk for dyslexia. Structured Literacy is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading that focuses on the underlying structure of language.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Systematic instruction in phonological awareness, including phoneme awareness, blending, segmenting, and manipulation.
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Explicit teaching of the relationships between letters and sounds (phonics).
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching the different types of syllables and how to decode multisyllabic words.
  • Morphology: Instruction in morphemes (meaningful parts of words, such as prefixes, suffixes, and roots) to enhance vocabulary and reading comprehension.
  • Syntax: Explicit teaching of sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: Focus on vocabulary development and understanding the meaning of words and sentences.

Characteristics of Structured Literacy Programs:

  • Systematic: Skills are taught in a logical and sequential order.
  • Explicit: Concepts are directly taught and modeled for students.
  • Cumulative: New skills are built upon previously learned skills.
  • Diagnostic: Instruction is tailored to meet the individual needs of each student.
  • Multisensory: Learning activities engage multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile).

UISD's Implementation of Structured Literacy: UISD utilizes specific Structured Literacy programs that have been vetted and approved by the district. These programs are implemented by trained teachers who have expertise in dyslexia intervention. The specific programs used may vary by campus, so parents should inquire with their child's school about the specific program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determining factor is the level of support needed and whether the student's dyslexia "substantially limits a major life activity." Understanding the differences between these two plans is crucial.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Legal Basis: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading, writing).
  • Focus: Provides accommodations and modifications to ensure equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation is conducted by a team, typically including teachers, administrators, and parents.
  • IEP Not Required: Does not require an IEP.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Legal Basis: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability (including a specific learning disability like dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: Provides specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: Services may include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling.
  • Evaluation: Requires a comprehensive evaluation conducted by a multidisciplinary team, including teachers, administrators, specialists (e.g., educational diagnostician, speech therapist), and parents.
  • IEP Required: Requires a written IEP that outlines the student's goals, objectives, and services.

Which Plan is Right for My Child? The decision of whether a 504 plan or an IEP is appropriate for a student with dyslexia is made on a case-by-case basis. If a student's needs can be met with accommodations within the general education classroom, a 504 plan may be sufficient. However, if a student requires specialized instruction and intensive interventions, an IEP may be necessary. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, in the case of an IEP, or the 504 committee will make this determination based on the evaluation data and the student's individual needs.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material. They are designed to provide access to the curriculum without changing the content being taught. Accommodations can be provided within a 504 plan or an IEP.

Here is a list of common accommodations that may be used for students with dyslexia in United ISD:

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Seating the student in a location that minimizes distractions and allows for optimal learning.
  • Reduced Workload: Modifying assignments to reduce the amount of work required.
  • Use of Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, audiobooks, and graphic organizers.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the format or complexity of assignments to meet the student's needs.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet space for the student to take tests.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing the student with copies of notes or allowing them to use a note-taker.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids such as graphic organizers, charts, and diagrams to support learning.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Access to a Word Processor: Allowing the student to use a word processor for writing assignments.
  • Highlighted Text: Providing texts that have been highlighted to emphasize key information.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Engaging multiple senses during instruction to enhance learning.
  • Directions Repeated: Providing repeated or simplified directions.
  • Check for Understanding: Frequently checking the student's comprehension of the material.
  • Use of Manipulatives: Allowing the use of hands-on materials.

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations provided will depend on the individual needs of the student. The ARD committee (for IEPs) or the 504 committee will determine the appropriate accommodations based on the student's evaluation data and their specific learning challenges.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents have the right to request an evaluation for dyslexia at any time. If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, it is important to contact your child's school to begin the evaluation process.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to express your concerns about your child's reading difficulties.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a brief description of your concerns. It is helpful to include specific examples of your child's difficulties with reading, writing, or spelling.
  3. School Response: The school is required to respond to your request in a timely manner. They will typically schedule a meeting to discuss your concerns and determine whether an evaluation is warranted.
  4. Evaluation Process: If an evaluation is deemed necessary, the school will obtain your consent before proceeding. The evaluation will be conducted by a qualified professional, such as an educational diagnostician or a licensed specialist in school psychology (LSSP). The evaluation will assess your child's reading skills, phonological awareness, decoding abilities, spelling skills, and other relevant areas.
  5. Evaluation Report: Once the evaluation is complete, you will receive a written report summarizing the findings. The report will indicate whether your child meets the criteria for a diagnosis of dyslexia.
  6. ARD or 504 Committee Meeting: If your child is diagnosed with dyslexia, the school will convene an ARD committee (for IEPs) or a 504 committee to develop a plan to address your child's needs.

Important Considerations:

  • Documentation: Keep copies of all correspondence related to your child's evaluation and services.
  • Advocacy: You have the right to advocate for your child's needs throughout the evaluation and intervention process.
  • Resources: Utilize resources such as the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website and the International Dyslexia Association (IDA) to learn more about dyslexia and available services.

United ISD is committed to providing appropriate support and services to students with dyslexia. By working together, parents, educators, and students can ensure that all students have the opportunity to reach their full potential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
