How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in United ISD

This guide outlines the process for requesting a special education evaluation for your child within United ISD in Texas. Understanding your rights and responsibilities under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law is crucial for ensuring your child receives the support they need to succeed. This guide covers everything from submitting a written request to understanding your options if the school district refuses to evaluate.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first, and arguably most important, step is to submit a written request for a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE). This request should be clear, concise, and provide specific details about your child's challenges. A verbal request is not sufficient to trigger the timelines and protections afforded under the law. Here's a breakdown of what your written request should include:

1. Child's Information:

  • Full name of the child
  • Date of birth
  • Current grade level
  • School and teacher
  • Your address and phone number(s)

2. Specific Concerns:

  • Clearly describe the specific academic, behavioral, social, or emotional challenges your child is experiencing. Avoid vague statements like "my child is struggling." Provide concrete examples. For instance: "My child struggles with reading comprehension. They consistently score below grade level on reading assessments and have difficulty summarizing stories." Or, "My child has difficulty focusing in class. They are frequently out of their seat, fidgeting, and interrupting the teacher."
  • Detail any interventions or strategies that have already been tried, both at home and at school, and their outcomes. For example, "We have tried extra reading practice at home, but it has not improved their comprehension skills." Or, "The teacher has implemented a behavior chart, but it has not significantly reduced the number of times my child gets out of their seat."
  • If applicable, mention any diagnoses or medical conditions that might be relevant. Include any relevant reports or assessments from doctors or therapists.

3. Why You Believe a Special Education Evaluation is Necessary:

  • Explain why you believe your child's challenges warrant a special education evaluation. Connect their difficulties to potential disabilities as defined under IDEA. For example: "Based on these difficulties, I believe my child may have a learning disability that is impacting their academic progress. I request a full and individual evaluation to determine if they are eligible for special education services."

4. Explicit Request for Evaluation:

  • State clearly that you are requesting a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) to determine eligibility for special education services under IDEA. Use this specific language.

5. Delivery Method:

  • Send your written request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school district received your request. Keep a copy of the request for your records. Alternatively, you can hand-deliver the request to the school principal or Special Education Director and obtain a signed and dated receipt as proof of delivery.

To Whom to Send: The letter should be addressed to the school principal and/or the Special Education Director of United ISD. Contact the school or district office to confirm the correct name and address for the Special Education Director.

Sample Sentence Starters: "I am writing to request a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], to determine their eligibility for special education services." "I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s challenges with [specific area of concern] and believe they may have a disability that is impacting their educational progress."

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once the school district receives your written request, a strict 45 school day timeline is triggered under Texas law (Texas Administrative Code §89.1011(c)). This timeline dictates the timeframe within which the school district must complete the following:

1. Decision to Evaluate: Within 15 school days of receiving your written request, the school district must decide whether or not to conduct an FIE. They will notify you of their decision in writing. If they decide to evaluate, the 45-day clock continues ticking. If they refuse, they must provide you with written notice explaining their reasons and inform you of your rights to appeal this decision.

2. Obtain Parental Consent: If the district decides to evaluate, they must obtain your written consent before beginning the evaluation process. They should provide you with an "Assessment Plan" outlining the specific areas that will be assessed and the types of assessments that will be used.

3. Conduct the Evaluation: After receiving your written consent, the school district must complete the FIE. This includes administering various assessments, reviewing your child's records, observing your child in the classroom, and consulting with you.

4. Eligibility Determination: After completing the evaluation, a group of qualified professionals (including you as the parent) will meet to determine if your child meets the criteria for one or more of the thirteen disability categories defined under IDEA and Texas law, and if, due to that disability, they need special education and related services. This meeting is often referred to as the Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting.

5. ARD Meeting and IEP Development (If Eligible): If your child is found eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services that will be provided to help them achieve those goals.

Important Considerations Regarding the 45-Day Timeline:

  • School Days vs. Calendar Days: The timeline refers to "school days," not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted.
  • Documentation is Key: Keep detailed records of all communication with the school district, including dates of letters sent and received, phone calls, and meetings. This documentation will be crucial if you need to advocate for your child's rights.
  • Breaks in the Timeline: There are very limited exceptions that allow for extensions to the 45-day timeline. Ensure the school district follows proper procedures if they request an extension. The school district must seek your written consent for any extensions of the 45-day timeline.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to identify your child's strengths and weaknesses, determine if they have a disability, and identify the specific educational needs that result from that disability. The scope of the FIE must be broad enough to address all areas of suspected disability. The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals, including:

  • Licensed Educational Diagnostician
  • Speech-Language Pathologist (if speech or language impairment is suspected)
  • School Psychologist
  • Other specialists as needed

The FIE should include the following components:

1. Review of Existing Data: The evaluation team will review your child's academic records, including report cards, standardized test scores, previous evaluations, and teacher observations. They will also review any medical records or reports from outside therapists or doctors that you provide.

2. Parent Input: Your input is crucial to the evaluation process. The evaluation team must consider your concerns and perspectives regarding your child's strengths, weaknesses, and educational needs. You should be given opportunities to share your observations and insights. Be prepared to describe your child's developmental history, any challenges they have faced, and what has worked for them in the past.

3. Classroom Observation: The evaluation team will observe your child in their classroom setting to assess their academic performance, behavior, and social interactions.

4. Individual Assessments: The evaluation team will administer a variety of standardized and informal assessments to evaluate your child's skills in various areas, including:

  • Cognitive Abilities: Intelligence tests (e.g., WISC-V) to assess intellectual functioning.
  • Academic Achievement: Tests of reading, writing, and math skills (e.g., WIAT-III, Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Achievement).
  • Language Skills: Assessments of receptive and expressive language skills (e.g., CELF-5).
  • Speech and Articulation: If speech or language impairment is suspected.
  • Motor Skills: Assessments of fine and gross motor skills.
  • Social-Emotional Functioning: Assessments of behavior, emotional regulation, and social skills (e.g., BASC-3).
  • Adaptive Behavior: Assessments of daily living skills (e.g., Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales).

5. Determination of Eligibility: After completing the evaluation, the evaluation team will prepare a written report summarizing their findings and recommendations. This report will be shared with you prior to the ARD committee meeting. At the ARD committee meeting, the team will determine whether your child meets the criteria for one or more of the thirteen disability categories under IDEA and Texas law. These categories include:

  • Autism
  • Deaf-Blindness
  • Deafness
  • Emotional Disturbance
  • Hearing Impairment
  • Intellectual Disability
  • Multiple Disabilities
  • Orthopedic Impairment
  • Other Health Impairment
  • Specific Learning Disability
  • Speech or Language Impairment
  • Traumatic Brain Injury
  • Visual Impairment

The FIE must provide a clear and comprehensive picture of your child's strengths, weaknesses, and educational needs. It should inform the development of an effective IEP, if your child is found eligible for special education services.

Child Find Obligations

United ISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under IDEA to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within its boundaries who are in need of special education and related services. This is known as "Child Find." This obligation applies to all children, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or homeschooled. It also applies to children suspected of having a disability, even if they are succeeding academically.

What Child Find Means for You:

  • Proactive Responsibility: The school district cannot simply wait for parents to request evaluations. They must have systems in place to proactively identify children who may have disabilities.
  • Referral Processes: Schools should have clear and accessible referral processes for parents, teachers, and other professionals to raise concerns about a child's development or learning.
  • Screening and Monitoring: Schools may conduct screenings and monitor student progress to identify potential concerns.
  • Public Awareness: The school district must engage in public awareness activities to inform the community about the availability of special education services.

If you suspect your child has a disability, even if they are not struggling significantly in school, you have the right to request an evaluation. The school district cannot deny your request simply because your child is passing their classes. Child Find requires them to investigate any potential disability, regardless of academic performance.

Texas Transition and Guide to the Admission, Review, and Dismissal Process: The Texas Education Agency (TEA) provides a document titled "Texas Transition and Guide to the Admission, Review, and Dismissal Process." This guide provides detailed information about special education laws and procedures in Texas, including Child Find obligations. It is available on the TEA website.

What If They Refuse

If United ISD refuses to conduct a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) after you have submitted a written request, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for the refusal. This written notice must also inform you of your rights to challenge the school district's decision. This written refusal is known as Prior Written Notice.

Your Options When the School District Refuses:

1. Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third party (a mediator) to try to resolve the disagreement. The mediator helps facilitate communication and explore possible solutions. Mediation is confidential and non-binding, meaning that any agreement reached must be mutually agreed upon by both parties.

2. Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a more formal legal process where you present evidence and arguments before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will then make a decision about whether the school district should conduct an evaluation. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. The decision of the hearing officer is binding on both you and the school district.

3. File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe the school district has violated IDEA or state special education laws. The TEA will investigate your complaint and may issue a corrective action plan if they find that the school district has violated the law. This process is less adversarial than a due process hearing, but it may not provide you with the same level of individual relief.

4. Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school district's evaluation. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The school district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate. If the school district pays for the IEE, the results must be considered by the ARD committee.

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines: There are strict timelines for filing complaints and requesting due process hearings. Be sure to familiarize yourself with these timelines and act promptly.
  • Documentation: Keep thorough records of all communication with the school district and any actions you take to challenge their decision.
  • Legal Assistance: Consider seeking legal assistance from an attorney specializing in special education law. An attorney can provide you with guidance and representation throughout the process. Organizations like Disability Rights Texas can be helpful resources.

After the Evaluation

Following the completion of the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE), the next steps depend on the outcome of the evaluation:

1. Child is Not Eligible for Special Education Services: If the ARD committee determines that your child does not meet the criteria for any of the disability categories under IDEA and Texas law, or that they do not need special education services, the school district is not required to provide special education services. However, you still have the right to disagree with this decision and pursue options such as requesting an IEE or filing a complaint with the TEA. The school is still obligated to provide general education interventions to support your child.

2. Child is Eligible for Special Education Services: If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education services, the committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services that will be provided to help them achieve those goals.

Key Components of the IEP:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance: A description of your child's current academic and functional abilities.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the special education and related services that will be provided to your child, including the frequency, duration, and location of those services. These services may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, and transportation.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom to help them access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning.
  • Placement: The location where your child will receive special education services. IDEA emphasizes educating children with disabilities in the least restrictive environment (LRE). This means that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.
  • Progress Monitoring: A description of how your child's progress towards their goals will be monitored and reported to you.

Your Role in the IEP Process: As a parent, you are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in the IEP development process. Your input is invaluable in ensuring that the IEP meets your child's individual needs. Don't hesitate to ask questions, express your concerns, and advocate for your child's best interests.

Annual Review: The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. This is an opportunity to assess your child's progress, make any necessary adjustments to the IEP, and set new goals for the upcoming year. You can request an ARD meeting at any time if you have concerns about your child's IEP.

Triennial Re-evaluation: Every three years, the school district must conduct a re-evaluation to determine if your child continues to be eligible for special education services. This re-evaluation may involve administering new assessments and reviewing your child's progress. You have the right to waive the triennial evaluation if you and the school district agree that it is not necessary.

By understanding your rights and responsibilities under IDEA and Texas law, you can effectively advocate for your child and ensure that they receive the special education services they need to reach their full potential. Remember to document all communication, seek legal assistance if needed, and actively participate in the ARD committee process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
