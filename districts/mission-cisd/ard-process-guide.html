What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their ability to learn in the general education setting, you will be invited to participate in an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting. ARD is an acronym that stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is the cornerstone of special education services in Texas, designed to create an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored specifically to meet your child's unique needs. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the special education and related services your child will receive to make progress in school. The ARD meeting is required by both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29.

The purpose of the ARD meeting is multifaceted. First, the team discusses whether your child meets the criteria for special education services based on evaluation data. If so, the ARD committee then collaboratively develops the IEP. This involves determining your child's current academic and functional performance levels (present levels), setting measurable annual goals for your child to achieve, identifying the specific special education and related services necessary to help them reach those goals, determining the appropriate educational placement (the environment where your child will receive services), and outlining any necessary accommodations and modifications to support their learning. The ARD committee also meets regularly to review your child's progress and revise the IEP as needed to ensure it continues to meet their evolving needs. Finally, the ARD committee determines when a child no longer requires special education services and can be dismissed from special education programs.

The ARD committee is comprised of various individuals who contribute their expertise to the IEP development process. Required members include you, the parent (or legal guardian); at least one of your child's general education teachers; at least one of your child's special education teachers or providers; a representative of the school district who is knowledgeable about special education, the general curriculum, and the availability of district resources (often an administrator or special education coordinator); and a person who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results. Your child may also be invited to attend the ARD meeting, especially as they get older. You have the right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, such as therapists, doctors, or advocates.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Mission CISD, like all school districts in Texas, is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is designed to give you sufficient time to prepare for the meeting, gather relevant information, and consider your thoughts and concerns. This is a critical procedural safeguard designed to empower you as an active and informed participant in your child's education.

The written notice must include specific information about the ARD meeting, including the purpose of the meeting (e.g., initial evaluation, annual review, manifestation determination), the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of the individuals who will be attending. It should also inform you of your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise about your child to participate in the meeting. The notice will also explain what topics will be discussed at the meeting, such as present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, goals, services, and placement. The notice should be clear and easy to understand, avoiding jargon or technical language.

While the five-day notice is a legal requirement, you have the option to waive this right if you choose. For example, if a situation arises where your child needs immediate support or services, you might agree to hold an ARD meeting sooner than five days. However, any waiver of the five-day notice must be provided in writing, indicating your informed consent to proceed with the meeting on a shorter timeframe. Understand that you are NEVER required to waive this right and it exists to benefit you and your ability to prepare.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparation is key to a successful ARD meeting. Bringing relevant documents and information can help you advocate effectively for your child's needs and ensure that the IEP is comprehensive and tailored to their specific strengths and challenges. Consider gathering the following items:

First, any prior evaluations, whether conducted by the school district or by independent professionals, are crucial. These evaluations provide valuable data about your child's academic, cognitive, and functional abilities, helping the ARD committee understand their learning profile. This includes psychological evaluations, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other assessments relevant to your child's needs.

Second, bring any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other medical professionals who have worked with your child. These documents can provide important insights into your child's medical history, diagnoses, and any treatments or interventions that might impact their educational needs. For example, a doctor's letter might explain the impact of ADHD on your child's attention and learning, or a therapist's report might detail strategies for managing anxiety in the classroom.

Third, gather your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, attendance records, and any work samples that demonstrate their academic performance. These records can provide a clear picture of your child's strengths and weaknesses in different subject areas, as well as any patterns of difficulty or success.

Fourth, it is highly recommended that you prepare a written list of your concerns and questions about your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. Be as specific as possible, outlining your observations about your child's learning, behavior, or social-emotional well-being. This is especially important if your child cannot communicate these concerns themselves.

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, special education advocate, or anyone who can provide emotional support and help you articulate your concerns. Having another person present can be invaluable, especially if you feel overwhelmed or intimidated by the ARD process.

Finally, remember that it is legal in Texas to record ARD meetings, provided that you give prior notice to all participants. A voice recorder can be a helpful tool for documenting the discussion and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions made during the meeting. This can be particularly useful if you need to refer back to the meeting later or if you disagree with any of the decisions made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During the ARD meeting, if you or any other member of the ARD committee feels that additional information or time is needed to make informed decisions, you have the right to request a recess. This is often referred to as the "10-day recess rule," although the actual recess can be shorter than 10 days if the team agrees. This is a valuable safeguard that ensures decisions are not rushed and that all relevant information is considered.

The purpose of the recess is to allow time for gathering additional data, consulting with experts, or seeking clarification on specific issues. For example, you might request a recess to obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) if you disagree with the school district's evaluation, or to consult with a special education advocate if you need assistance understanding your rights and options. Any ARD team member can invoke this rule, not just the parent.

To request a recess, simply state your need for additional time or information during the ARD meeting. The ARD committee will then discuss the request and determine whether a recess is necessary. If a recess is granted, the meeting will be adjourned, and a new meeting date will be scheduled within 10 school days. During the recess, you should use the time to gather the necessary information or consult with the appropriate individuals.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the central document that guides your child's special education services. It is developed collaboratively by the ARD committee and outlines your child's present levels of performance, annual goals, specific services, placement, and accommodations.

The IEP begins with a statement of your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in various areas, based on evaluation data, classroom observations, and input from parents and teachers. The present levels statement serves as a baseline for measuring your child's progress and developing appropriate goals.

Next, the IEP includes measurable annual goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). For example, a goal might be that your child will improve their reading comprehension skills to a specific grade level, or that they will learn to manage their behavior in the classroom more effectively. Each goal is further broken down into smaller, short-term objectives or benchmarks to track progress more frequently.

The IEP also specifies the special education and related services that your child will receive to help them reach their goals. These services might include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or other supports. The IEP will detail the frequency, duration, and location of each service.

The IEP will also determine your child's educational placement, which refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. Placement options range from the general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or a self-contained classroom. The IEP must justify the placement decision and explain why it is the least restrictive environment (LRE) that meets your child's needs.

Finally, the IEP outlines any necessary accommodations and modifications that will be implemented to support your child's learning. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as providing extra time on tests or allowing them to use assistive technology. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as reducing the number of assignments or simplifying the content. Accommodations and modifications are designed to help your child access the general education curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge.

Remember, you are not obligated to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home to review it carefully and consider whether you agree with the decisions made. You can then schedule another meeting to discuss any concerns or request changes to the IEP.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is important to remember that you are an equal and vital member of the ARD committee. You have the right to express your concerns and disagree with the team's recommendations if you believe they are not in your child's best interest. There are several specific rights you have in these situations.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you disagree with the ARD committee's determination that your child requires special education services, you can refuse to allow your child to be placed in a special education program.

Second, even if you initially consent to services, you do not have to sign the IEP document at the ARD meeting. You are entitled to take it home, review it, and return it with your signature later. Taking it home and reviewing it thoroughly is highly recommended.

Third, if you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can be used to challenge the district's evaluation and inform the IEP development process. The district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate.

Fourth, even if you ultimately sign the IEP document, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document before signing it. This indicates that you do not fully agree with all of the decisions made during the ARD meeting and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, there are several formal options available to you to address your concerns. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89.

Your first option is to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege violations of IDEA or state special education laws. You can find information and forms for filing a complaint on the TEA website.

Your second option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution to your dispute. Mediation is also free of charge and can be a valuable tool for resolving conflicts without resorting to more formal legal proceedings.

Your third option is to request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a binding decision regarding your dispute. This is a more complex and time-consuming process than filing a complaint or requesting mediation, and it is generally recommended to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue a due process hearing.

Finally, you have the option to file a complaint with the federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability in educational settings. If OCR finds that the school district has discriminated against your child, they can order the district to take corrective action.

Remember, the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process is a great resource for understanding your rights and responsibilities in the special education process. It provides a comprehensive overview of IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89 and can help you navigate the ARD process effectively. You can find this resource online. Knowing your rights and advocating effectively for your child's needs is essential to ensuring that they receive a free and appropriate public education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
