What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a crucial meeting for parents of students with disabilities in Birdville ISD. It's a formal process, mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, Chapter 29, designed to create, review, and revise a student's Individualized Education Program, often referred to as an IEP. Think of the ARD meeting as the place where educators, specialists, and most importantly, you, the parent, come together to discuss your child's unique educational needs and determine the best way to support their learning journey.

The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop an IEP that is tailored to your child's specific strengths and weaknesses. This IEP outlines annual goals, the special education and related services that will be provided, the appropriate educational placement, and any necessary accommodations or modifications to ensure your child has access to a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) in the least restrictive environment (LRE). This means that your child will be educated with students without disabilities to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP is a legally binding document, which ensures that the school district is committed to providing the services and supports outlined within it.

Several people typically attend an ARD meeting. Required members include you, the parent; at least one regular education teacher of the child (if the child is, or may be, participating in the regular education environment); at least one special education teacher or special education provider; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services and is knowledgeable about the general curriculum and the availability of resources (often an administrator or special education coordinator); a person who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results, which may be a member of the team already listed; and the student, when appropriate. Other individuals who may be invited include related service providers such as speech therapists, occupational therapists, counselors, and diagnosticians, depending on your child's needs. The school district also has the right to invite people with specific knowledge or expertise regarding your child. You, as a parent, have the right to invite anyone you feel has important information to contribute regarding your child.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Birdville ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the scheduled date. This five-day notice is not just a formality; it's a crucial part of your rights under IDEA and Texas Education Code. The purpose of this notice is to give you adequate time to prepare for the meeting, gather relevant information, and consider your child's needs and goals.

The written notice must include several key pieces of information. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting, such as initial admission, annual review, or a specific issue requiring discussion. Second, it must specify the date, time, and location of the meeting. Third, it must inform you of who will be in attendance, including their titles and roles at the ARD meeting. It should also inform you of your right to invite other individuals to attend the ARD meeting on your behalf. Finally, the notice should inform you of your procedural safeguards, which are your rights and protections under IDEA. Keep the notice handy during the meeting as a reference.

While the district must provide this five-day notice, there are situations where you might want to have the ARD meeting sooner. In these cases, you have the option to waive your right to the five-day notice in writing. This means you would sign a document indicating that you understand your right to the notice but are choosing to proceed with the meeting earlier than required. Waiving the notice can be helpful if there's an urgent issue that needs to be addressed quickly, or if scheduling conflicts make it difficult to attend a meeting within the usual timeframe. However, carefully consider whether you have enough time to prepare before waiving your right to the five-day notice.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but bringing the right materials can make a significant difference in ensuring your child's needs are fully addressed. Think of the ARD meeting as a collaborative effort, and these items can help you effectively participate in the discussion.

First, bring any prior evaluations or assessments that are relevant to your child's educational needs. This could include psychological evaluations, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, or any other assessments conducted by professionals outside of the school district. These reports provide valuable information about your child's strengths and weaknesses, and can help the ARD team develop appropriate goals and services.

Also, bring any letters or reports from your child's doctors or other healthcare providers. These documents can provide medical context for your child's learning challenges and can help the ARD team understand any physical or medical conditions that may be impacting their education. For example, if your child has ADHD, a letter from their pediatrician outlining their diagnosis and treatment plan can be helpful.

Collect and bring any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, work samples, and attendance records. These documents can provide a comprehensive picture of your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom. Highlighting specific areas of concern or success can help focus the ARD team's discussion.

Before the meeting, take the time to create a written list of your concerns and questions. This will help you stay focused and ensure that you address everything that is important to you. Include specific examples of challenges your child is facing, as well as any goals or aspirations you have for their education. Writing this list out ensures that you cover all of your points, even if you become nervous during the meeting.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who is familiar with special education laws and procedures. Having someone there to support you can be invaluable, especially if you feel intimidated or overwhelmed by the process. Your advocate can take notes, ask questions, and provide emotional support.

Finally, it is legal in Texas to record the ARD meeting, provided you give the school district prior notice. A simple email a day or two before stating you intend to record the meeting for your personal records fulfills this requirement. Recording the meeting can be helpful for several reasons. It allows you to review the discussion later at your own pace, ensuring that you don't miss any important details. It also provides a record of what was agreed upon, which can be useful if there are any disagreements or misunderstandings later on. Be sure to bring a reliable voice recorder and test it beforehand to ensure it is working properly.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a powerful tool that you, as a parent, can use during an ARD meeting. It allows any member of the ARD committee, including you, to request a temporary pause, or "recess," of up to 10 school days before making any final decisions about your child's IEP. This is invaluable when you feel overwhelmed, need more information, or want to consult with an advocate before agreeing to the proposed IEP.

You can invoke the 10-day recess rule at any point during the ARD meeting if you feel that you need more time to consider the information being presented or to seek outside advice. For example, if the team proposes a change in placement that you are unsure about, or if you disagree with the proposed goals and services, you can request a recess. There is no specific reason you must provide beyond stating that you would like to recess the meeting.

To invoke the recess, simply state clearly that you are requesting a recess of the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days. It is helpful to make this request in writing, but it is not required. The ARD committee is then obligated to grant the recess. During this time, you can gather additional information, consult with experts, or simply take time to process the information presented at the meeting. The ARD meeting will then reconvene after the recess period to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP (Individualized Education Program) is the heart of your child's special education. It's a written document that outlines your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, annual goals, the specific special education and related services they will receive, their educational placement, and any necessary accommodations or modifications. Understanding each component of the IEP is critical to ensuring that your child receives the support they need to succeed.

The present levels section describes your child's current academic and functional performance, based on assessments, observations, and information provided by parents and teachers. This section should clearly identify your child's strengths and weaknesses, as well as any areas where they are struggling. The present levels serve as the foundation for setting appropriate goals and determining the services needed.

The annual goals section outlines measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within a one-year timeframe. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They should be aligned with your child's present levels and address their individual needs. The IEP also includes short-term objectives or benchmarks, which are smaller steps that lead towards achieving the annual goals.

The services section describes the specific special education and related services that your child will receive. Special education services may include specially designed instruction, modifications to the curriculum, and assistive technology. Related services may include speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and other supports necessary to help your child benefit from their education. The frequency, duration, and location of these services must be clearly specified in the IEP.

The placement section determines the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. The goal is to place your child in the least restrictive environment (LRE), which means they should be educated with students without disabilities to the maximum extent appropriate. Placement options may include general education classrooms, resource rooms, self-contained classrooms, or even homebound instruction, depending on your child's needs.

The accommodations section lists any modifications or adjustments that will be made to the classroom environment, curriculum, or assessments to help your child access the general education curriculum. Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, or modified assignments. The IEP should clearly specify which accommodations will be provided and in what settings.

Remember, you do not have to sign the IEP at the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek advice from others before making a decision. The district will set a reasonable timeline for you to respond, but you are never obligated to sign on the spot.

When You Disagree With the Team

Disagreements during ARD meetings are not uncommon. As a parent, you have the right to advocate for your child's needs, and that may sometimes mean disagreeing with the school district's recommendations. It's important to know your rights and options when these disagreements arise.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for the initial placement of your child in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD team's decision to place your child in special education, you can refuse to sign the IEP, which would prevent the school district from providing special education services. However, this decision should be made carefully, as it could impact your child's access to needed support.

You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document at any point, even after your child has been receiving special education services. Refusing to sign the IEP does not necessarily mean that the IEP will not be implemented. The school district may still proceed with implementing the IEP if they believe it is in your child's best interest, but you have the right to document your disagreement.

If you disagree with the evaluation results used to develop your child's IEP, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can be used to inform the ARD team's decisions and may lead to revisions of the IEP. The district is obligated to consider the results of the IEE.

You always have the right to write "Parent Disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it. This simple notation clearly documents your disagreement with specific aspects of the IEP, even if you are willing to sign the document to allow services to begin while you pursue other options to resolve the disagreement.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve disagreements with the ARD team through communication and collaboration, but you're still not satisfied with the outcome, there are several formal escalation options available to you. These options are designed to protect your child's rights and ensure that they receive a free and appropriate public education. These rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code, Title 19, Chapter 89.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a formal written complaint that alleges violations of IDEA or state special education laws. The complaint must be filed within one year of the alleged violation and must include specific details about the issues you are raising. TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This is a free process.

Mediation is another option that can help you resolve disputes with the school district. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party facilitates communication between you and the school district to reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is confidential and can be a less adversarial way to resolve disagreements than other formal processes. TEA offers free mediation services to parents and school districts. Agreeing to mediate does not remove your ability to file a state complaint or request due process.

If mediation is unsuccessful or not appropriate for your situation, you can request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you and the school district present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a binding decision. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at the due process hearing, and the school district will also be represented by legal counsel. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, so it's important to seek legal advice if you're considering this option.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. An OCR complaint alleges violations of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). These laws prohibit discrimination based on disability in programs and activities that receive federal funding. OCR will investigate the complaint and may order the school district to take corrective action if they find evidence of discrimination.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
