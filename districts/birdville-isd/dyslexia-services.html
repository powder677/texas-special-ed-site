Dyslexia Services in Birdville ISD

Dyslexia Services in Birdville ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Birdville Independent School District (BISD). It is designed to help parents, educators, and students understand the district's approach to identifying and supporting students with dyslexia, aligning with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) guidelines and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024, serves as the primary resource for dyslexia identification, intervention, and services in Texas public schools. BISD adheres to the guidelines and procedures outlined in this handbook. It is crucial for parents and educators to familiarize themselves with this document.

Key updates and important aspects of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a clear and consistent definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurobiological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The handbook emphasizes the importance of MTSS, which is a framework for providing targeted support to students based on their individual needs. This includes universal screening, progress monitoring, and increasingly intensive interventions.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook mandates the use of Structured Literacy as the instructional approach for students with dyslexia. This approach is explicit, systematic, cumulative, and diagnostic.
  • Screening Requirements: Detailed guidelines are provided for universal screening of students to identify those at risk for dyslexia.
  • Evaluation Procedures: The handbook outlines the procedures for conducting comprehensive evaluations to determine if a student has dyslexia.
  • IEP and 504 Plans: The handbook clarifies the differences between IEPs (Individualized Education Programs) and 504 plans and when each is appropriate for students with dyslexia.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook stresses the importance of parent involvement in the identification, evaluation, and intervention process.
  • Professional Development: The handbook emphasizes the need for ongoing professional development for educators in dyslexia and related disorders.

BISD is committed to providing all students with the resources and support they need to succeed, and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 is a critical tool in achieving this goal.

Mandatory Screening

BISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening in accordance with Texas Education Code §38.003. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and require further evaluation.

The following are key aspects of the mandatory screening process in BISD:

  • Grade Levels: Screening is typically conducted for all students in kindergarten and first grade. BISD may also screen students in other grade levels based on teacher observation, parent request, or other indicators of potential reading difficulties.
  • Screening Instruments: BISD uses TEA-approved screening instruments that are designed to assess key literacy skills, such as phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, and reading comprehension.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia based on the screening results.
  • Follow-Up: Students who are identified as being at risk receive additional support and interventions through the MTSS framework. This may include targeted small-group instruction, progress monitoring, and referral for a full evaluation if necessary.

If you have concerns about your child's reading development, even if they have not been identified through mandatory screening, you are encouraged to contact your child's teacher or the school principal to discuss your concerns.

Structured Literacy Programs

BISD utilizes Structured Literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia and those at risk. Structured Literacy is an explicit, systematic, cumulative, and diagnostic approach to teaching reading, spelling, and writing. It is based on the science of reading and is designed to address the underlying language deficits that characterize dyslexia.

Key components of Structured Literacy programs used in BISD include:

  • Phonology: Teaching the sounds of language (phonemes) and how they relate to letters (graphemes).
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Explicitly teaching the relationship between sounds and letters.
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaching the different types of syllables and how to decode and encode words.
  • Morphology: Teaching the meaning of prefixes, suffixes, and root words.
  • Syntax: Teaching the rules of grammar and sentence structure.
  • Semantics: Teaching the meaning of words and how they are used in context.

BISD ensures that educators providing Structured Literacy instruction are properly trained and have the necessary expertise to effectively implement these programs. Progress monitoring is regularly conducted to track student progress and adjust instruction as needed.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Understanding the difference between a 504 plan and an IEP (Individualized Education Program) is crucial for parents of students with dyslexia. Both plans provide accommodations and support to students with disabilities, but they differ in eligibility criteria and the scope of services provided.

Section 504 Plan

A 504 plan is based on Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, which prohibits discrimination based on disability. To be eligible for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.

For students with dyslexia, a 504 plan typically provides accommodations to help them access the general education curriculum. These accommodations may include:

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Preferential seating
  • Audiobooks
  • Use of assistive technology
  • Modified assignments

Individualized Education Program (IEP)

An IEP is based on the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), which is a federal law that provides special education and related services to students with disabilities. To be eligible for an IEP, a student must have a disability that falls under one of the 13 categories specified in IDEA and must require specially designed instruction to benefit from education. Dyslexia can fall under the category of Specific Learning Disability.

An IEP provides more comprehensive services than a 504 plan, including:

  • Specially designed instruction in reading, writing, and spelling
  • Related services, such as speech therapy or occupational therapy
  • Modifications to the curriculum
  • Assistive technology
  • Progress monitoring

Key Differences

Here's a table summarizing the key differences:

Feature 504 Plan IEP
Legal Basis Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
Eligibility Physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities Disability that falls under one of the 13 IDEA categories and requires specially designed instruction
Services Accommodations to access the general education curriculum Specially designed instruction and related services
Focus Removing barriers to access Providing specialized instruction to address learning needs

The decision of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and parents. The team will consider the student's individual needs and the level of support required to ensure their academic success. In BISD, both 504 and Special Education services follow the guidelines laid out in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered or assessed that do not fundamentally alter the content or expectations of the curriculum. They are designed to help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge.

Common accommodations for students with dyslexia in BISD may include:

  • Reading Accommodations:
    • Audiobooks or text-to-speech software
    • Read-aloud tests and assignments
    • Preferential seating near the teacher
    • Use of a reading guide or highlighter
    • Chunking of text
  • Writing Accommodations:
    • Use of a computer or other assistive technology
    • Scribe or note-taker
    • Extended time for writing assignments
    • Use of graphic organizers
    • Alternative forms of assessment (e.g., oral reports)
  • Spelling Accommodations:
    • Use of a spell checker
    • Word banks
    • Reduced spelling expectations on some assignments
  • Testing Accommodations:
    • Extended time
    • Quiet testing environment
    • Read-aloud tests
    • Use of assistive technology
  • Organizational Accommodations:
    • Use of a planner or calendar
    • Assistance with organization
    • Checklists

The specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will depend on their individual needs and the recommendations of the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee (for IEPs) or the 504 committee (for 504 plans). BISD provides professional development and resources to educators to ensure the effective implementation of accommodations.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from BISD. The process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to express your concerns. Be prepared to provide specific examples of your child's reading difficulties.
  2. Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a brief summary of your concerns.
  3. Review of Existing Data: The school will review your child's existing educational records, including grades, test scores, and teacher observations.
  4. Evaluation Planning: If the school determines that an evaluation is warranted, they will convene a team to develop an evaluation plan. The team will include you, the school principal, and other relevant professionals.
  5. Evaluation Process: The evaluation process will typically involve a variety of assessments to evaluate your child's reading, writing, spelling, and language skills. These assessments may be administered by a diagnostician, educational psychologist, or other qualified professional.
  6. Evaluation Report: Once the evaluation is complete, you will receive a written report summarizing the findings and recommendations.
  7. ARD/504 Meeting: If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia and requires special education or related services, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting will be held to develop an IEP. If an IEP is not deemed necessary but the student requires accommodations, a 504 meeting will be held to develop a 504 plan.

BISD is committed to working collaboratively with parents to ensure that students with dyslexia receive the support they need to succeed. Do not hesitate to contact your school or the district's special education department if you have any questions or concerns.

Disclaimer: This guide provides general information about dyslexia services in Birdville ISD. It is not intended to be a substitute for professional advice. Please contact your school or the district's special education department for specific information about your child's individual needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
