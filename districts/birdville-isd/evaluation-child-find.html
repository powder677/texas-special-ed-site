How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?

This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Birdville ISD

This guide provides information on how to request a special education evaluation for your child in Birdville Independent School District (BISD) in Texas. It outlines the process, timelines, your rights, and what to do if the school district refuses your request. Understanding these procedures can empower you to advocate effectively for your child's educational needs.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating the special education evaluation process is submitting a formal, written request to BISD. This request should be clear, concise, and provide sufficient information to warrant the evaluation. While a verbal request might prompt action, a written request creates a documented record and ensures accountability.

Who to Send the Request To: The request should ideally be sent to your child's campus principal and the Special Education Director for Birdville ISD. You can typically find contact information for the Special Education Director on the BISD website. Sending the request to both parties ensures it reaches the appropriate individuals promptly.

What to Include in the Request: Your written request should include the following information:

* Date: Include the date you are writing the letter. * Your Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: This ensures accurate identification of your child. * Your Contact Information: Provide your phone number, email address, and mailing address. * Your Child's School and Grade Level: Indicate which BISD school your child attends and their current grade. * Specific Concerns: Clearly describe the specific academic, behavioral, or developmental concerns you have about your child. Be as specific as possible. Instead of simply saying "My child is struggling in reading," provide examples such as "My child struggles to decode words, frequently skips words when reading aloud, and has difficulty comprehending grade-level reading materials." Likewise, rather than "My child has behavior problems," describe specific behaviors, their frequency, and the context in which they occur. For example, "My child frequently disrupts class by talking out of turn, gets out of their seat without permission, and has difficulty following instructions. This occurs multiple times per day." * Documentation of Interventions (if any): If your child has received any interventions or accommodations in the classroom, describe them and their effectiveness (or lack thereof). This could include things like Response to Intervention (RTI) strategies, tutoring, modified assignments, or preferential seating. Indicate how long these interventions have been in place. * Request for a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE): Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation to determine if your child is eligible for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). * Parent Signature: Your signature validates the request.

Example Request Letter Excerpt:

"...I am writing to request a full and individual initial evaluation for my child, [Child's Full Name], to determine if they are eligible for special education services. [Child's Name] is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. I am concerned about [his/her/their] [Specific Concerns, e.g., reading comprehension, math skills, attention span]. [He/She/They] has/have been receiving [Describe Interventions, if any] since [Date]. Despite these interventions, [he/she/they] continue/continues to struggle significantly. Therefore, I believe an evaluation is necessary to determine if special education services are needed..."

Delivery Method: Send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school district received your request. Keep a copy of the request and the certified mail receipt for your records.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a specific timeline for the special education evaluation process. Upon receiving your written request, BISD has a limited amount of time to respond and complete the evaluation process. Understanding this timeline is crucial for ensuring your child receives timely support.

The 15 School Day Timeline (Consent for Evaluation): Within 15 school days of receiving your written request, the school district must provide you with a notice of action regarding your request. This notice will inform you whether the school district intends to conduct an evaluation or if they are refusing to evaluate. If they intend to evaluate, they must also provide you with an Assessment Plan that outlines the specific areas they plan to assess. You must provide written consent for the evaluation to proceed. The 45-day timeline only begins AFTER you provide written consent.

The 45 School Day Timeline (Evaluation and ARD Meeting): From the date the school district receives your signed consent for the evaluation, BISD has 45 school days to complete the evaluation and hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. It's important to note that this is 45 school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted.

What Happens During the 45 Days: During this period, the school district will conduct the necessary assessments to evaluate your child's strengths and weaknesses in various areas, as outlined in the Assessment Plan. These assessments may include academic testing, psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy assessments, and other relevant evaluations. After the assessments are complete, the evaluation team will compile a comprehensive report summarizing the findings.

Requesting an Extension: While rare, the school district may request an extension of the 45-day timeline. This is typically only granted in exceptional circumstances, such as when a specialist is needed for a specific assessment or when there are significant delays beyond the school district's control. Any request for an extension must be made in writing and must explain the reason for the delay. You have the right to refuse an extension.

What to Do If the Timeline Is Violated: If BISD fails to meet the 45-day timeline without a valid extension, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). You can also pursue other legal remedies to ensure your child receives a timely evaluation.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability that qualifies them for special education services under IDEA. The FIE must be thorough and address all areas of suspected disability. It's crucial to understand what the FIE should cover to ensure your child receives a complete and accurate assessment.

Areas of Suspected Disability: The FIE must evaluate all areas related to the child's suspected disability. This means the evaluation team must consider all the concerns you raised in your initial request and any other concerns identified during the evaluation process. This may include:

* Academic Skills: Reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. * Cognitive Abilities: Intellectual functioning, problem-solving skills, and learning capacity. * Communication Skills: Speech, language, and communication abilities. * Social-Emotional Development: Social skills, emotional regulation, and behavior. * Motor Skills: Gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning). * Adaptive Behavior: Daily living skills, such as dressing, eating, and hygiene. * Vision and Hearing: These areas should be screened to rule out sensory impairments that may be contributing to the child's difficulties. * Medical History: The evaluation team should consider relevant medical information that may impact the child's educational performance.

Specific Assessment Tools: The evaluation team should use a variety of assessment tools, including standardized tests, observations, interviews, and work samples. Standardized tests should be age-appropriate, culturally sensitive, and administered by qualified professionals. Observations should be conducted in multiple settings, such as the classroom, playground, and cafeteria. Interviews should be conducted with the child, parents, and teachers. Work samples should be reviewed to assess the child's academic performance.

Addressing Parental Concerns: The FIE must specifically address the concerns raised by the parents in their written request. The evaluation team should provide a clear explanation of how they addressed each concern and the findings related to each concern.

Determining Eligibility: The ultimate goal of the FIE is to determine if the child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under IDEA. In Texas, a child must have a disability in one of the following categories to be eligible for special education:

* Autism * Deaf-Blindness * Emotional Disturbance * Hearing Impairment (including deafness) * Intellectual Disability * Multiple Disabilities * Orthopedic Impairment * Other Health Impairment * Specific Learning Disability * Speech or Language Impairment * Traumatic Brain Injury * Visual Impairment (including blindness)

In addition to having a disability, the child must also demonstrate a need for special education and related services in order to benefit from their education. The FIE should provide evidence to support the determination of eligibility.

Child Find Obligations

The "Child Find" mandate is a critical component of IDEA. It requires school districts, including BISD, to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or homeschooled. This obligation extends to children who are suspected of having a disability, even if they are not currently receiving special education services.

Proactive Identification: BISD is required to implement procedures to identify children with disabilities. This may include screening programs, awareness campaigns, and collaboration with community agencies. School personnel, such as teachers, counselors, and nurses, are also responsible for identifying students who may be in need of special education services.

Referral Process: If a child is suspected of having a disability, a referral for evaluation can be made by parents, teachers, or other professionals. The referral should be in writing and should include specific information about the child's suspected disability and the reasons for the referral. Even without a formal referral, if BISD personnel suspect a disability, they have a duty to investigate.

Parental Rights: Parents have the right to request an evaluation for their child if they suspect a disability. The school district cannot refuse to evaluate a child simply because they are performing well academically. The Child Find mandate applies to all children with suspected disabilities, regardless of their academic performance.

Private School and Homeschool Students: BISD has a responsibility to identify and evaluate children with disabilities who attend private schools or are homeschooled within the district. These students are entitled to the same protections and rights as students who attend public schools.

Consequences of Non-Compliance: Failure to comply with the Child Find mandate can have serious consequences for the school district. The TEA can impose sanctions on the district, and parents can pursue legal remedies to ensure their child receives a timely and appropriate evaluation.

What If They Refuse

Despite your written request and the Child Find obligations, BISD may refuse to conduct a special education evaluation. This can be frustrating and disheartening, but it's important to understand your rights and the steps you can take to challenge the school district's decision.

Prior Written Notice: If BISD refuses to evaluate your child, they must provide you with prior written notice explaining the reasons for their refusal. This notice must be clear and specific and must explain the basis for the school district's decision. The notice must also inform you of your right to appeal the school district's decision.

Challenging the Refusal: You have several options for challenging the school district's refusal to evaluate your child:

* Requesting Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the school district meet with a neutral third party to try to resolve the dispute. The mediator can help you and the school district communicate effectively and reach a mutually agreeable solution. * Filing a Complaint with the TEA: You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe that BISD has violated IDEA or state special education laws. The TEA will investigate your complaint and take appropriate action if they find that the school district has violated the law. * Requesting a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about whether the school district is required to evaluate your child. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. * Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school district's evaluation or refusal to evaluate. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you request an IEE at public expense, the school district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate.

Document Everything: It is crucial to document all communications with the school district, including dates, times, and the content of conversations. Keep copies of all documents related to your request for evaluation, including the initial request, the school district's response, and any other relevant information. This documentation will be essential if you need to pursue legal remedies.

After the Evaluation

Once the FIE is complete, BISD will schedule an ARD committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. This meeting is a crucial opportunity to advocate for your child's needs and ensure they receive the appropriate support.

The ARD Committee: The ARD committee is responsible for developing your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP) if they are found eligible for special education services. The ARD committee typically includes the following members:

* You (the parent) * Your child (when appropriate) * A representative of the school district who is knowledgeable about special education * Your child's teacher(s) * An evaluation specialist who can interpret the evaluation results

Reviewing the Evaluation Results: At the ARD meeting, the evaluation specialist will present the findings of the FIE. You will have the opportunity to ask questions and discuss your concerns about the evaluation results.

Determining Eligibility: The ARD committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services based on the evaluation results. If your child is found eligible, the ARD committee will develop an IEP.

Developing the IEP: The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's educational goals, the special education services they will receive, and the accommodations and modifications they will need to succeed in school. The IEP should be tailored to your child's individual needs and should be developed collaboratively by the ARD committee.

Key Components of the IEP: The IEP must include the following components:

* Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance * Measurable annual goals * Special education and related services * Accommodations and modifications * Participation in state and district-wide assessments * Dates for beginning and ending services * How progress will be measured

Parental Rights: You have the right to participate fully in the ARD process and to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to request mediation or a due process hearing.

Regular Review and Revision: The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. This ensures that the IEP continues to meet your child's changing needs.

Advocating for your child throughout the special education process can be challenging, but understanding your rights and the procedures involved will empower you to ensure they receive the support they need to succeed in school. Don't hesitate to seek assistance from advocacy groups, attorneys, or other professionals who can provide guidance and support.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
