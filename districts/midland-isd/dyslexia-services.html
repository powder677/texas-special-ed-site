Dyslexia Services in Midland ISD

Dyslexia Services in Midland ISD

This guide provides an overview of dyslexia services offered within Midland Independent School District (MISD), aligning with Texas state law and the latest updates from the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024, serves as the foundational guide for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia in Texas public schools. It outlines the legal requirements and best practices for dyslexia identification, evaluation, and intervention. Key components of the handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a clear definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Screening and Identification: The handbook details the screening process for students who exhibit characteristics of dyslexia. This includes universal screening measures in early grades, as well as more targeted screening for students referred by teachers, parents, or other professionals. The handbook emphasizes the importance of using valid and reliable screening tools.
  • Structured Literacy Interventions: The handbook emphasizes the importance of providing evidence-based structured literacy interventions to students identified with dyslexia. These interventions are systematic, explicit, multi-sensory, and cumulative.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook underscores the crucial role of parents in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. Schools are required to provide parents with information about dyslexia, their child's progress, and strategies for supporting their child at home.
  • Legal Requirements: The Texas Education Code (TEC) §38.003 and the Texas Administrative Code (TAC) §74.28 outline the legal requirements for dyslexia identification and intervention in Texas public schools. These laws are based on House Bill 3 (HB 3) passed by the 86th Texas Legislature. The handbook clarifies these legal obligations for school districts.
  • Updates for 2024: While specifics should be confirmed with the TEA (Texas Education Agency) website and the full handbook, generally updates focus on:
    • Enhanced guidance on early literacy practices in the general education classroom to prevent reading difficulties.
    • Clarification of the roles and responsibilities of school staff in the dyslexia identification and intervention process.
    • Strengthened emphasis on progress monitoring and data-based decision making.
    • Guidance on using technology to support students with dyslexia.

Accessing the Handbook: The Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 is available on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. It is essential for parents and educators to familiarize themselves with this document.

Mandatory Screening

Midland ISD adheres to the state mandate for dyslexia screening. Here's what you need to know about the screening process:

  • Universal Screening: All students in kindergarten and first grade are screened for risk factors associated with dyslexia. The screening process typically involves assessments of phonological awareness, letter-sound knowledge, rapid naming, and reading fluency.
  • Screening Timelines: Screening usually occurs within the first few months of the school year. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia.
  • Targeted Screening: Students in higher grades (2nd and above) who are struggling with reading may be referred for targeted dyslexia screening. Referrals can come from teachers, parents, or other school staff.
  • Screening Tools: Midland ISD utilizes TEA-approved screening instruments that are designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. Specific tools used might vary between campuses. Contact your child's school for details on screening tool specifics.
  • Follow-Up: If a student is identified as being at risk for dyslexia, the school will conduct further evaluation to determine if the student has dyslexia and requires intervention.
  • Parent Notification: Parents will be notified in writing of the results of their child's dyslexia screening and any subsequent evaluations.

Structured Literacy Programs

Midland ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide evidence-based intervention for students with dyslexia. These programs are characterized by the following features:

  • Systematic and Explicit Instruction: Instruction is sequential and builds upon previously learned skills. Concepts are taught directly and explicitly, rather than implicitly.
  • Phonological Awareness: A strong emphasis is placed on developing phonological awareness skills, such as identifying and manipulating sounds in words.
  • Phonics: Students learn the relationships between letters and sounds (graphemes and phonemes) and how to decode words.
  • Fluency: Instruction focuses on developing reading fluency through repeated reading and other strategies.
  • Vocabulary: Vocabulary development is an integral part of the structured literacy program.
  • Comprehension: Students are taught comprehension strategies to improve their understanding of what they read.
  • Multi-Sensory Approach: Instruction incorporates visual, auditory, and kinesthetic/tactile modalities to engage students and enhance learning.
  • Progress Monitoring: Student progress is monitored regularly to ensure that the intervention is effective. Adjustments are made to the instruction as needed.

Examples of Structured Literacy Programs: Midland ISD may use various structured literacy programs. Contact your child's school to learn about the specific program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The choice depends on the severity of the student's needs and the type of support required.

  • 504 Plan: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to the learning environment to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to education. Students with dyslexia who are able to access the general education curriculum with accommodations may be served through a 504 plan.
    • Eligibility: To be eligible for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning, reading, or writing.
    • Focus: 504 plans focus on removing barriers to learning in the general education classroom.
    • Examples of Accommodations: Examples of accommodations in a 504 plan for dyslexia include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and access to assistive technology.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program): An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specialized instruction and related services that a student with a disability needs to access a free and appropriate public education (FAPE). Students with dyslexia who require significant individualized instruction and support may be served through an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability (SLD).
    • Eligibility: To be eligible for an IEP, a student must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
    • Focus: IEPs focus on providing individualized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
    • Examples of Services: Examples of services in an IEP for dyslexia include specialized reading instruction, speech-language therapy, and occupational therapy.
  • Key Differences:
    • Level of Support: IEPs generally provide a higher level of support than 504 plans.
    • Eligibility Criteria: The eligibility criteria for an IEP are more stringent than those for a 504 plan.
    • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), while 504 plans are governed by Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

Determining the Appropriate Plan: The decision of whether a student should receive support through a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and specialists, in collaboration with the parents. This decision is based on a comprehensive evaluation of the student's needs.

Accommodations List

The following is a list of common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia. The specific accommodations provided will vary depending on the individual student's needs.

  • Reading Accommodations:
    • Extended time on reading tasks and tests.
    • Use of audiobooks or text-to-speech software.
    • Preferential seating near the teacher.
    • Access to a quiet testing environment.
    • Use of a reading guide or highlighter.
    • Modified reading materials (e.g., larger font size, increased spacing).
  • Writing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on writing tasks and tests.
    • Use of word processing software with spell check and grammar check.
    • Use of assistive writing technology (e.g., speech-to-text software).
    • Modified writing assignments (e.g., shorter assignments, alternative formats).
    • Provision of a scribe or note-taker.
    • Allowance for oral responses instead of written responses.
  • Spelling Accommodations:
    • Use of a spell checker.
    • Provision of a word bank.
    • Allowance for phonetic spelling.
    • Focus on content rather than spelling accuracy in certain assignments.
  • Organization Accommodations:
    • Use of an assignment notebook or planner.
    • Assistance with organization skills.
    • Provision of a structured classroom environment.
  • Testing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on tests.
    • Small group testing.
    • Read aloud of test questions (as appropriate).
    • Use of assistive technology during testing.

This list is not exhaustive, and other accommodations may be appropriate depending on the student's individual needs.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from Midland ISD. Here's how:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, counselor, or principal to express your concerns. They can provide you with information about the district's dyslexia evaluation process.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for a dyslexia evaluation to the school principal or special education department. The request should include:
    • Your child's name, date of birth, and grade level.
    • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading and writing skills.
    • Any relevant information about your child's academic history, medical history, or family history of dyslexia.
    • Your contact information.
  3. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, they will initiate the evaluation process. This may involve a review of your child's academic records, observations of your child in the classroom, and administration of standardized tests to assess your child's reading, writing, and spelling skills.
  4. Evaluation Report: After the evaluation is complete, the school will provide you with a written report of the findings. The report will indicate whether your child meets the criteria for dyslexia.
  5. ARD Meeting (if applicable): If the evaluation results indicate that your child has dyslexia and requires special education services, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting will be held to develop an IEP for your child. You have the right to participate in all ARD committee meetings and to provide input into the development of your child's IEP.

Your Rights: You have the right to request an independent educational evaluation (IEE) if you disagree with the results of the school's evaluation. You also have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe that the school is not providing your child with a free and appropriate public education (FAPE).

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
