What can I do right now if Midland ISD is ignoring my child's needs?

Submit a written evaluation request today — verbal requests do not start the legal clock. Midland ISD has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the evaluation. If your child is being disciplined or suspended, that timeline is even more urgent. Use the guides below to take action now.

Special Education in Midland ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Special education, as defined under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and corresponding Texas law, is specially designed instruction to meet the unique needs of a child with a disability. This means that your child, if eligible, will receive an individualized education program (IEP) tailored to their specific learning challenges. This could involve specialized teaching methods, assistive technology, related services such as speech therapy or occupational therapy, and modifications to the general education curriculum. All of these services are designed to help your child access the general education curriculum to the greatest extent possible alongside their non-disabled peers.

Midland ISD is overseen by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), which ensures that the district complies with all state and federal special education laws. Midland ISD also receives support from TEA Region 18 Education Service Center, which provides professional development and technical assistance to help the district effectively serve students with disabilities. Understanding this relationship is crucial because it highlights the multiple layers of support and oversight in place to ensure your child receives a quality education. Ultimately, Midland ISD is responsible for the appropriate implementation of special education services.

Crucially, as a parent, you have enforceable legal rights throughout the entire special education process. These rights are designed to ensure you are an active and informed participant in all decisions regarding your child's education. From requesting an evaluation to participating in ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meetings, you have the right to be heard, to access information, and to challenge decisions you believe are not in your child's best interest. Remember, you are your child's most important advocate. Your voice matters, and the law protects your right to use it. Start learning about your rights today, so you are prepared to advocate effectively for your child in Midland ISD.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Midland ISD

If you suspect your child has a disability that is impacting their educational performance, you have the right to request a full and individual evaluation (FIE) to determine if they are eligible for special education services. It is absolutely critical to understand that this request must be submitted in writing to the school. A verbal request, while helpful for opening the conversation, does not officially start the timeline required by law. The written request can be as simple as a letter stating, "I am requesting a full and individual evaluation for my child, [Child's Name], due to concerns about [specific concerns, e.g., reading comprehension, attention, etc.]." Be sure to include your child's name, date of birth, grade, and your contact information.

Once Midland ISD receives your written consent to evaluate, the district has 45 school days to complete the FIE. This evaluation will assess your child's strengths and weaknesses in all areas related to the suspected disability. It's also important to know that under Child Find laws, Midland ISD has a proactive responsibility to identify students who may need special education services, even if you haven't made a specific request. If you believe your child should have been identified sooner, it is still essential to submit your written request for an evaluation immediately. Delays can impact your child's progress, so time is of the essence. Don't wait. Write a letter today requesting a full and individual evaluation for your child, keep a copy for your records, and send it to the school principal or special education director via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery.

ARD Meetings in Midland ISD: Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, and these meetings are the cornerstone of the special education process. The ARD committee, which includes you as a vital member, is responsible for determining your child's eligibility for special education, developing their individualized education program (IEP), and making decisions about their educational placement. These meetings are where crucial decisions impacting your child's education in Midland ISD are made, so preparation and understanding your rights are essential.

Midland ISD must provide you with written notice at least five calendar days before any ARD meeting. This notice will include the date, time, location, and purpose of the meeting. It's crucial to review this notice carefully. During the ARD meeting itself, you have the right to request a 10-day recess at any time. This allows you to take a break to review information presented, seek outside consultation, or simply gather your thoughts before making a decision. You also have the right to disagree with the IEP and do not have to sign it on the same day it is presented. Taking time to review the document and seek input is strongly encouraged if you have any concerns.

Your active participation is key to ensuring your child receives the appropriate supports and services in Midland ISD. Remember, you know your child best, and your insights are invaluable to the ARD committee. Advocate for your child's needs, ask questions, and don't hesitate to express your concerns. Dyslexia Services in Midland ISD

Texas law mandates that Midland ISD screen all students for dyslexia. This screening is crucial for early identification and intervention, as dyslexia is a common learning difference that can significantly impact reading and writing skills. If the screening indicates potential risk factors for dyslexia, further evaluation and intervention are required. Remember, early intervention is key to helping your child overcome reading challenges.

The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook sets the standards that Midland ISD must follow when identifying and supporting students with dyslexia. If your child is identified as having dyslexia, they are entitled to structured literacy intervention, which is a research-based approach that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. Depending on the severity of your child's dyslexia, they may also qualify for a 504 Plan, which provides accommodations in the classroom, or an IEP, which provides more intensive special education services. If your child has not been screened for dyslexia, request the screening in writing today. Early detection can make a significant difference.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Midland ISD

It is critical to understand that if a student with a disability is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year within Midland ISD, certain protections under IDEA are triggered. Specifically, a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) must be conducted. This process involves an ARD meeting to determine if the student's behavior was a manifestation of their disability or a result of the district's failure to implement the IEP. Furthermore, such a suspension can constitute a Change of Placement under IDEA, requiring a thorough review of the student's educational needs and placement.

This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face as a parent of a special education student. You must act within days. If your child has been suspended or is facing disciplinary action, immediately contact the school and request an ARD meeting to conduct a Manifestation Determination Review. Document all communications and be prepared to advocate strongly for your child's rights. Don't delay. Use the Discipline Defense Kit to understand your rights and how to respond effectively if your child has been suspended from Midland ISD.

Filing a Complaint Against Midland ISD

As a parent, you have several avenues for addressing concerns about special education services in Midland ISD. It is important to know that these rights exist regardless of what you may be told by school staff. One option is to file a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate and resolve the complaint within 60 days. Another free option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation brings you and Midland ISD representatives together with a neutral third-party mediator to attempt to resolve the issues collaboratively.

If these initial steps do not resolve your concerns, you have the right to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal, legal process similar to a court case, and it typically requires legal representation. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe Midland ISD has discriminated against your child based on their disability. Understanding these options is crucial for protecting your child's rights. Familiarize yourself with the complaint processes today so you are prepared to take action if necessary.

Frequently Asked Questions About Midland ISD Special Education

The questions every Midland ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Midland ISD? +

Submit a written request to the Midland ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Midland ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Midland ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Midland ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Midland ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Midland ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Midland ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
