Requesting an Evaluation in Midland ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Midland ISD: A Parent's Guide

This guide is designed to help parents in Midland ISD navigate the process of requesting a special education evaluation for their child. Understanding your rights under Texas law, specifically the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) as implemented in Texas, is crucial. This guide provides information on submitting a written request, the timeline the district must follow, what the evaluation (known as a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation, or FIE) should cover, the district's "Child Find" obligations, what to do if the district refuses your request, and what happens after the evaluation is complete.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first, and arguably most important, step is to submit a written request for a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) to Midland ISD. This request sets the wheels in motion for the special education process. A verbal request, while it might be acknowledged, does not trigger the same legal obligations as a written request.

Who to Address Your Request To: The best practice is to address the request to the campus principal and the Special Education Director for Midland ISD. This ensures the request is properly received and routed to the appropriate personnel. You can find the contact information for the Special Education Director on the Midland ISD website.

What to Include in Your Written Request: Your request should be clear, concise, and include the following information:

  • Your Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: Accurate identification is essential.
  • Your Child's Current School and Grade Level: This helps the district locate your child's records.
  • Your Contact Information: Include your phone number, email address, and mailing address.
  • A Clear Statement Requesting a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE): Be explicit. Use the exact phrase "Full and Individual Initial Evaluation" or "special education evaluation" to leave no room for misinterpretation.
  • A Detailed Explanation of Your Concerns: This is the most crucial part of the request. Describe in detail why you believe your child may have a disability that is affecting their ability to succeed in school. Be specific. For example, instead of saying "My child is struggling in reading," say "My child is significantly behind their peers in reading. They struggle to decode simple words, have difficulty comprehending what they read, and consistently score below grade level on reading assessments. They have been receiving reading interventions, but have not made adequate progress." Include specific examples of difficulties your child is experiencing in the classroom, at home, and in social situations. Include any relevant information from doctors, therapists, or other professionals.
  • Copies of Supporting Documentation: Attach any relevant reports from doctors, therapists, or other professionals who have evaluated or treated your child. Include report cards, work samples, and any other documentation that supports your concerns.
  • Your Signature and the Date: This validates the document.

Example Language for Your Request:

"Dear [Principal's Name] and Special Education Director,

I am writing to request a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Full Name], date of birth [Date of Birth], who is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name].

[Child's Name] is experiencing significant difficulties in [Specific area of concern, e.g., reading, math, writing, behavior]. For example, [Describe specific examples and observations]. [Include any supporting documentation attached].

Based on these concerns, I believe [Child's Name] may have a disability that is impacting their educational performance. I am requesting that Midland ISD conduct a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation to determine if [Child's Name] is eligible for special education services.

Thank you for your time and consideration. Please contact me at [Your Phone Number] or [Your Email Address] to discuss this matter further.

Sincerely,

[Your Signature]

[Your Printed Name]

[Date]

Submitting Your Request: Keep a copy of your request for your records. Send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the district received your request. You can also hand-deliver the request and ask the school to sign and date a copy acknowledging receipt.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once Midland ISD receives your written request for an FIE, they are legally required to adhere to a specific timeline outlined in Texas Education Code §29.004 and the Texas Administrative Code (TAC). This timeline is crucial for ensuring your child receives a timely evaluation. The district has 45 school days to complete the evaluation and hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to discuss the results. It is imperative to understand that this is 45 school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks do not count towards the 45-day timeline.

Timeline Breakdown:

  • Receipt of Request (Day 0): The date the district officially receives your written request.
  • Review of Existing Data (RED): Within a reasonable timeframe after receiving the request, the ARD committee, including you as the parent, must review existing data on your child. This includes grades, test scores, teacher observations, medical records, and any other relevant information. The purpose of the RED is to determine whether an evaluation is warranted. Important: Even if the school believes they have enough information, you, as the parent, can still insist on a full evaluation.
  • Consent to Evaluate: After the RED, if the ARD committee determines that an evaluation is necessary, the district must provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) and seek your written consent to conduct the FIE. The 45-school-day timeline begins once you provide your consent.
  • Conducting the FIE: Once consent is received, the district must conduct the evaluation within the remaining time. This involves administering various assessments to determine if your child has a disability and the nature and extent of their educational needs.
  • ARD Meeting: Within the 45-school-day timeline, the district must hold an ARD meeting to discuss the evaluation results, determine eligibility for special education services, and, if eligible, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). You are a crucial member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in all decisions.

Monitoring the Timeline: It is essential to track the timeline and communicate with the school if you believe they are not adhering to it. Document all communications with the school, including dates, times, and the names of the people you spoke with.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine whether a child has a disability and, if so, to identify the child's educational needs. The evaluation must be tailored to the individual child and must assess all areas of suspected disability. According to Texas law (Texas Education Code §29.004 and TAC), the FIE must:

  • Assess all areas related to the suspected disability: The evaluation must cover all areas of concern that you raised in your written request and any other areas the school suspects may be impacted. This may include academic skills (reading, writing, math), cognitive abilities, communication skills, social-emotional functioning, adaptive behavior, and motor skills.
  • Use a variety of assessment tools and strategies: The evaluation should not rely on a single test or assessment. It should include a variety of standardized tests, observations, interviews, and work samples to provide a comprehensive picture of the child's strengths and weaknesses.
  • Be conducted by qualified professionals: The evaluators must be qualified and trained to administer and interpret the specific assessments used. This may include educational diagnosticians, school psychologists, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, and other specialists.
  • Be non-discriminatory: The evaluation must be conducted in the child's native language or mode of communication, unless it is clearly not feasible to do so. The assessments used must be culturally and linguistically appropriate and must not discriminate against the child based on their race, ethnicity, gender, or socioeconomic status.
  • Provide educationally relevant information: The evaluation must provide information that is useful for developing an effective IEP. This includes identifying the child's strengths and weaknesses, determining the impact of the disability on their educational performance, and recommending appropriate accommodations and services.

Specific Areas of Assessment: Depending on the suspected disability, the FIE may include assessments in the following areas:

  • Cognitive Functioning: Intelligence tests (e.g., Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children - WISC) to assess overall cognitive abilities and specific cognitive strengths and weaknesses.
  • Academic Achievement: Standardized tests to assess reading, writing, and math skills (e.g., Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Achievement).
  • Language and Communication: Assessments of receptive and expressive language skills, articulation, fluency, and social communication skills.
  • Social-Emotional Functioning: Assessments of behavior, emotional regulation, social skills, and adaptive behavior. This may include behavior rating scales, observations, and interviews.
  • Motor Skills: Assessments of gross motor and fine motor skills, sensory processing, and motor coordination.
  • Adaptive Behavior: Assessments of daily living skills, such as self-care, communication, and social skills.

Reviewing the Evaluation Plan: Before the evaluation begins, you have the right to review the evaluation plan and ask questions about the assessments that will be used. If you have concerns about the appropriateness of the assessments, you should discuss them with the school.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law, in accordance with IDEA, places a "Child Find" obligation on all school districts, including Midland ISD. This means that the district has an affirmative duty to identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within its boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This obligation extends to children who are suspected of having a disability, even if they are not currently receiving special education services.

What Child Find Means for Parents:

  • The district cannot wait for you to request an evaluation: If the district suspects that your child may have a disability, they have a responsibility to initiate the evaluation process, even if you have not requested it. This is particularly important for students who are struggling academically or behaviorally, but whose parents may not be aware of their rights or the availability of special education services.
  • Referrals from various sources: Referrals for evaluation can come from teachers, administrators, parents, doctors, or any other person who has reason to believe that a child may have a disability.
  • Public Awareness Activities: Midland ISD is required to conduct public awareness activities to inform parents about the availability of special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation. This may include information on the district website, brochures, and presentations at school events.

What to Do If You Believe the District is Not Meeting Its Child Find Obligations:

  • Document your concerns: Keep records of any instances where you believe the district failed to identify or evaluate your child despite evidence of a potential disability.
  • Contact the school principal or Special Education Director: Express your concerns in writing and request that the district fulfill its Child Find obligations by evaluating your child.
  • File a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): If the district fails to respond to your concerns or refuses to evaluate your child, you can file a formal complaint with the TEA. Information on how to file a complaint can be found on the TEA website.

What If They Refuse

If Midland ISD refuses your written request for a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE), they are required to provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) explaining the reasons for their refusal. This PWN must include:

  • A description of the action the agency proposed or refused to take: In this case, the refusal to conduct an evaluation.
  • An explanation of why the agency proposes or refuses to take the action: This is a crucial part of the PWN. The district must provide a clear and understandable explanation of their reasoning. General statements or vague explanations are not sufficient.
  • A description of each evaluation procedure, assessment, record, or report the agency used as a basis for the proposed or refused action: The district must identify the specific data they relied upon to make their decision.
  • A description of other options that the agency considered and the reasons why those options were rejected: This demonstrates that the district considered alternative approaches before refusing the evaluation.
  • A description of other factors that are relevant to the agency's proposal or refusal: This could include any other information that the district considered in making its decision.
  • Information about where you can obtain assistance in understanding the provisions of IDEA: The district must provide information on resources that can help you understand your rights and options.
  • Information on how to pursue dispute resolution options: The district must inform you of your right to mediate, request a due process hearing, or file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

Your Options When the District Refuses:

  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the district meet with a neutral third party to try to resolve the dispute. Mediation can be a less adversarial and more cost-effective way to reach an agreement.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision on whether the district violated your child's rights under IDEA. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation.
  • File a Complaint with the TEA: You can file a complaint with the TEA alleging that the district violated IDEA. The TEA will investigate the complaint and determine whether the district is in compliance with the law.
  • Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to request an IEE at public expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation. The district may either pay for the IEE or request a due process hearing to show that their evaluation was appropriate.

Important Considerations: It is crucial to act quickly if the district refuses your request for an evaluation. There are deadlines for filing complaints and requesting due process hearings. Consulting with an attorney or advocate experienced in special education law is highly recommended.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) is complete, Midland ISD must hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to discuss the results. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in all decisions. The ARD committee will review the evaluation reports, discuss your child's strengths and weaknesses, and determine whether your child meets the criteria for eligibility for special education services under IDEA.

Key Outcomes of the ARD Meeting:

  • Eligibility Determination: The ARD committee will determine whether your child has a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires special education and related services. The committee must consider all relevant information, including the evaluation reports, your input, and teacher observations.
  • IEP Development (If Eligible): If your child is found eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services and supports that will be provided to help them achieve those goals.
  • Placement Decisions (If Eligible): The ARD committee will determine the most appropriate placement for your child, which is the least restrictive environment (LRE) where your child can receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE). The LRE is generally the general education classroom, with supports and accommodations as needed. Other placement options may include resource rooms, self-contained classrooms, or special schools.

Your Role in the ARD Meeting:

  • Be Prepared: Review the evaluation reports carefully before the meeting and make a list of any questions or concerns you have.
  • Share Your Perspective: Provide your input on your child's strengths, weaknesses, and needs. Share your observations of your child's performance at home and in the community.
  • Actively Participate: Ask questions, express your opinions, and advocate for your child's needs. Remember that you are an equal member of the ARD committee.
  • Review the IEP Carefully: Ensure that you understand all aspects of the IEP before signing it. If you disagree with any part of the IEP, you have the right to request changes or refuse to consent to the IEP.

What If You Disagree With the ARD Committee's Decisions?

  • Request Another ARD Meeting: If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the ARD meeting, you can request another meeting to discuss your concerns further.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation can be used to resolve disputes between you and the district.
  • File a Complaint with the TEA: You can file a complaint with the TEA alleging that the district violated IDEA.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: You can request a due process hearing to challenge the ARD committee's decisions.

Ongoing Monitoring and Review: The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to participate in all IEP meetings and to request changes to the IEP if your child's needs change. Continuous communication and collaboration between you and the school are essential to ensure that your child receives the appropriate services and supports to succeed.

This guide provides general information and should not be considered legal advice. It is important to consult with an attorney or advocate experienced in special education law for guidance specific to your child's situation. Resources such as Disability Rights Texas and the Texas Parent to Parent organization can provide valuable information and support.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
