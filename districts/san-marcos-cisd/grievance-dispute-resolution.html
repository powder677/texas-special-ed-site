Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

San Marcos CISD, like all Texas school districts, can issue suspensions. However, for students with disabilities covered under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), there are crucial limitations. A student cannot be suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single academic year without triggering a "Change of Placement" (discussed below) and certain procedural safeguards.

Track your child's suspensions meticulously. Keep a record of every suspension, including the dates, duration, and reason. Even short suspensions add up quickly. If your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 days, understand your rights and demand a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).

What Is an MDR?

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a meeting required under IDEA when a school seeks to suspend a student with a disability for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, or when there is a pattern of removals from the classroom (even for shorter periods). The purpose is to determine if the student's behavior leading to the suspension was a manifestation of their disability, or the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP (Individualized Education Program).

DEMAND AN MDR IMMEDIATELY if your child is nearing or has exceeded the 10-day suspension limit. The MDR team MUST include you, the parent, and relevant members of the IEP team. The team will review all relevant information, including the IEP, teacher observations, and any relevant diagnostic information.

What if the behavior IS a manifestation of the disability? The student cannot be further suspended. The school MUST conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA) and implement or revise a behavior intervention plan (BIP) to address the underlying causes of the behavior. They MUST ensure the IEP is properly implemented.

What if the behavior IS NOT a manifestation of the disability? The school can discipline the student in the same manner as a non-disabled student. However, they STILL must provide FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education), ensuring your child continues to participate in the general education curriculum and receives IEP services during the suspension.

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that total more than 10 school days in a school year and these removals constitute a significant change in the student's educational program. This triggers additional due process rights.

Keep detailed records of all disciplinary actions. A seemingly minor string of "in-school suspensions" or short removals might trigger a Change of Placement. A Change of Placement requires a formal process, including an MDR and potentially a revised IEP.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe San Marcos CISD has violated your child's rights, you can file a grievance. Start at Level 1. This typically involves submitting a written complaint to the principal or relevant administrator.

Document EVERYTHING. Keep copies of all correspondence, emails, and documents related to the incident. Clearly state the specific violation you believe occurred, cite relevant laws or policies (IDEA, San Marcos CISD policy), and propose a specific remedy. For example, you might request the suspension be expunged from your child's record, or demand a revised IEP.

Meet all deadlines. San Marcos CISD likely has specific timelines for filing and responding to grievances. Missed deadlines can invalidate your claim. Find the grievance policy on the San Marcos CISD website and adhere to it meticulously.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe San Marcos CISD has violated IDEA, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). A TEA complaint must be filed within one year of the alleged violation.

TEA will investigate your complaint and determine if the district is in compliance with IDEA. A TEA complaint can be a powerful tool, but it's often a lengthy process. Gather all supporting documentation before filing.

Due Process

IDEA provides parents with significant due process rights. You have the right to challenge the school's decisions regarding your child's education, including disciplinary actions. This may involve mediation or a due process hearing.

Seek Legal Counsel. Navigating the due process system is complex. An education lawyer or advocate can provide invaluable assistance in protecting your child's rights. Don't wait until it's too late. Your child's future is at stake.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
