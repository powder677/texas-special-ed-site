Discipline Rights & Grievances in Harmony Public Schools (Combined) - URGENT GUIDE

This guide is for parents facing disciplinary action against their child in Harmony Public Schools. Act quickly! Time is often of the essence. This is a basic overview, and you should seek legal advice from a special education attorney or advocate as soon as possible.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Harmony Public Schools, like all Texas public schools, operates under Texas Education Agency (TEA) guidelines regarding student discipline. A crucial rule to understand is the 10-day suspension rule. This rule states that schools can suspend a student for up to 10 cumulative school days in a single academic year without triggering certain procedural safeguards, especially for students with disabilities protected under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Why is this important? Because once your child reaches this 10-day threshold (or exhibits a pattern of shorter suspensions that suggest a strategy to avoid the 10-day rule), additional rights and protections are activated. This is where things can get tricky and where school districts often try to skirt their responsibilities.

What to do:

  • Keep meticulous records: Track every suspension, even if it's only for a portion of a day. Note the date, the reason for the suspension, and the length of the suspension.
  • Know the reasons: Understand exactly why your child was suspended. Demand written documentation outlining the specific infraction and the school's justification for the disciplinary action. Vague descriptions are unacceptable.
  • Watch for patterns: Are the suspensions for similar behaviors? Are they escalating in severity? This could indicate a need for a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP).
  • Immediately contact the ARD Committee (if applicable): If your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP), immediately notify the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) Committee. Discuss the suspensions and their potential impact on your child's access to their education.

What Is an MDR? (Manifestation Determination Review)

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a crucial process under IDEA when a student with an IEP is facing a suspension that constitutes a "change of placement" (more on that later). The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's behavior that led to the disciplinary action was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, their disability; or if the behavior was the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

When is an MDR required?

  • When a student with an IEP has been removed from their current placement for more than 10 consecutive school days.
  • When a student with an IEP has been subjected to a series of removals that constitute a pattern because they total more than 10 school days in a school year; and because of factors such as the length of each removal, the total amount of time the student is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another. The key here is "pattern." The school might try to argue there is no pattern, so you must document meticulously!

What happens during an MDR?

The ARD Committee (including you, the parent) will review all relevant information, including:

  • The student's IEP
  • Teacher observations
  • Relevant information provided by the parents
  • Any relevant medical or psychological evaluations

What are the possible outcomes of an MDR?

  • Manifestation: If the ARD Committee determines that the behavior was a manifestation of the student's disability, the school must take immediate steps to address the issue. This might include revising the IEP, implementing a BIP, providing additional supports and services, or changing the student's placement. The student must be returned to their previous placement unless the parents and school agree otherwise.
  • No Manifestation: If the ARD Committee determines that the behavior was not a manifestation of the student's disability, the school can proceed with disciplinary action as they would for a student without an IEP. However, the school is still required to provide services to the student so they can continue to participate in the general education curriculum, although in another setting, and to progress toward meeting the goals set out in the student's IEP.

Important points about MDRs:

  • Your voice matters! As a parent, your input is crucial. Be prepared to advocate for your child and provide any relevant information that supports your position.
  • Document everything! Keep records of all communication with the school, including emails, phone calls, and meeting notes.
  • Don't be intimidated! School districts often try to control the narrative. Stand your ground and assert your child's rights.
  • Seek outside expertise! If you disagree with the school's findings, consider obtaining an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at the school's expense.

Change of Placement

A "change of placement" occurs when a disciplinary action results in the student being removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or constitutes a pattern of removals as described above. This is a critical point because it triggers the MDR process and other procedural safeguards under IDEA.

Examples of a Change of Placement:

  • Suspension for more than 10 consecutive school days.
  • Placement in an alternative educational setting (AES) for more than 10 consecutive school days (or a pattern of shorter placements).
  • Expulsion.

Why is "Change of Placement" important? Because it activates specific legal protections for students with IEPs, including the right to an MDR and the continuation of educational services.

What to do if you suspect a Change of Placement:

  • Immediately request an MDR: Do not wait for the school to initiate the MDR process. Write a letter or email to the school principal and special education director demanding an MDR.
  • Document the removals: As mentioned earlier, meticulous record-keeping is essential.
  • Ensure continuation of services: Even during a suspension or placement in an AES, your child is entitled to receive services outlined in their IEP. Demand that the school provide these services.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe that Harmony Public Schools has violated your child's rights or failed to follow proper procedures, you have the right to file a grievance. Generally, Harmony Public Schools has a multi-level grievance policy, with Level 1 being the initial step. Consult the Harmony Public Schools student handbook or website for the specific grievance policy applicable to your school.

General steps for filing a Level 1 Grievance:

  • Obtain the grievance policy: This document will outline the specific procedures, timelines, and requirements for filing a grievance.
  • Put it in writing: Your grievance must be submitted in writing. Use a clear and concise format.
  • Identify the issue: Clearly state the specific action or decision you are grieving. Be as detailed as possible.
  • Explain the impact: Explain how the action or decision has negatively impacted your child.
  • Propose a solution: State what you want the school to do to resolve the issue.
  • Include supporting documentation: Attach any relevant documents, such as emails, letters, suspension notices, or IEP documents.
  • Submit the grievance: Submit the grievance to the designated person (usually the principal or campus administrator).
  • Keep a copy: Retain a copy of the grievance and all supporting documentation for your records.

Important considerations:

  • Timelines are critical! The grievance policy will specify strict deadlines for filing a grievance. Missed deadlines can result in the grievance being dismissed.
  • Follow the chain of command: Start at Level 1 and follow the established procedures.
  • Document everything! Keep records of all communication related to the grievance.

TEA State Complaint

If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the grievance process, or if you believe that Harmony Public Schools has violated IDEA or other federal or state laws, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than filing a grievance and can result in a TEA investigation.

How to file a TEA State Complaint:

  • Obtain the TEA complaint form: You can find the complaint form on the TEA website.
  • Complete the complaint form: Provide all required information, including the name of the school, the specific violation, and the impact on your child.
  • Include supporting documentation: Attach any relevant documents that support your complaint.
  • Submit the complaint: Submit the complaint to TEA according to their instructions.

Key considerations:

  • Timelines: TEA has specific timelines for filing complaints. Ensure that you meet these deadlines.
  • Specificity: Be as specific as possible in your complaint. Provide dates, names, and details of the alleged violations.
  • Evidence: The more evidence you can provide, the stronger your complaint will be.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and may conduct interviews, review documents, and visit the school.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal procedure under IDEA that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district. It is a more adversarial process than mediation or other forms of dispute resolution. You have the right to request a due process hearing if you disagree with the school's decisions regarding your child's identification, evaluation, placement, or the provision of special education services.

When to consider Due Process:

  • When you have exhausted other avenues of dispute resolution.
  • When the school is refusing to provide necessary services.
  • When the school is violating your child's rights under IDEA.
  • When you disagree with the results of an evaluation.
  • When you disagree with the IEP.

Important Information About Due Process:

  • Statute of Limitations: There are strict deadlines (statute of limitations) for filing for due process. Missing the deadline will bar your claim. Consult with an attorney immediately!
  • Legal Representation is Highly Recommended: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. It is highly recommended that you seek legal representation from a special education attorney.
  • Mediation is Often Required: Before proceeding to a due process hearing, you may be required to participate in mediation.
  • Impartial Hearing Officer: The hearing will be conducted by an impartial hearing officer who is trained in special education law.
  • Legal Decision: The hearing officer will make a decision based on the evidence presented.
  • Appeals: If you are not satisfied with the hearing officer's decision, you may have the right to appeal to state or federal court.

Disclaimer: This guide provides general information and is not a substitute for legal advice. You should consult with a qualified special education attorney or advocate to discuss your specific situation. Time is critical, so act quickly to protect your child's rights.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
