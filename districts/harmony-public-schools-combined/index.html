Special Education in Harmony Public Schools (Combined): What Every Parent Needs to Know

Navigating special education can feel overwhelming, but you're not alone. Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), and Texas law, special education is designed to provide a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) to eligible children with disabilities. This means your child is entitled to receive specialized instruction and related services tailored to their unique needs, at no cost to you. This might include things like speech therapy, occupational therapy, assistive technology, or modifications to the curriculum.

Harmony Public Schools (Combined), like all public school districts in Texas, operates under the oversight of the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Harmony Public Schools (Combined) also receives valuable support and resources from TEA Region 4 Education Service Center, which provides training, technical assistance, and guidance to help ensure students with disabilities receive the services they need. The goal is always to help your child reach their full potential in the least restrictive environment possible.

It's crucial to remember that as a parent, you have enforceable legal rights throughout the entire special education process. From initial evaluation to developing an Individualized Education Program (IEP), you have the right to participate fully, ask questions, and advocate for your child's needs. Don't hesitate to exercise those rights. Harmony Public Schools (Combined) must respect them. Right now, familiarize yourself with the Parent's Guide to the Admission, Review, and Dismissal Process provided by TEA; it will be an invaluable resource for you.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)

If you suspect your child may have a disability that requires special education services, the first step is to request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in writing from Harmony Public Schools (Combined). It's absolutely essential that you make this request in writing because a verbal request does not trigger the legal timelines under IDEA. The school cannot refuse to evaluate your child simply because they don't think there's a problem. They must evaluate if you make a written request.

Once Harmony Public Schools (Combined) receives your written consent to evaluate, the district has 45 school days to complete the FIE. This evaluation will assess your child's strengths and weaknesses in various areas, such as academic skills, communication, motor skills, and social-emotional development. Remember, the Child Find mandate requires Harmony Public Schools (Combined) to proactively identify, locate, and evaluate children within their jurisdiction who may have disabilities, regardless of whether they attend public school. This means Harmony Public Schools (Combined) cannot wait for you to ask; they must be actively looking for children who need help. However, initiating the process yourself is always a good first step. Write a letter to the Special Education Director at Harmony Public Schools (Combined) today requesting an evaluation for your child. Keep a copy for your records and send the letter via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure it's received.

ARD Meetings in Harmony Public Schools (Combined): Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. It refers to the committee that makes decisions about your child's special education services. The ARD committee includes you (the parent), school administrators, teachers, and other professionals who have knowledge or expertise related to your child's needs. These meetings are critically important and it is essential that you attend and actively participate in the process.

Harmony Public Schools (Combined) is required to provide you with at least five calendar days written notice before any ARD meeting. This notice should include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location, and a list of the individuals who will be attending. During the ARD meeting, the committee will discuss your child's evaluation results, determine eligibility for special education services, and develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). You have the right to request a 10-day recess (a break) at any time during the ARD meeting if you need more time to consider the information being presented or to consult with an advocate or attorney.

Remember, you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your input is essential. You do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully before making a decision. Never feel pressured to sign something you're not comfortable with. Dyslexia Services in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)

Texas law mandates that Harmony Public Schools (Combined) screen all students for dyslexia. This screening is typically conducted in the early grades to identify students who may be at risk. If your child is identified as potentially having dyslexia, Harmony Public Schools (Combined) must provide appropriate interventions based on the guidelines outlined in the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook, which sets the standards for dyslexia services across the state. The Handbook ensures all districts, including Harmony Public Schools (Combined), use evidence-based practices.

Students identified with dyslexia are entitled to structured literacy intervention, which is a specific approach to reading instruction designed to address the unique challenges faced by individuals with dyslexia. These interventions might take place within the general education classroom or in a small group setting. In some cases, students with dyslexia may also qualify for a 504 Plan or an IEP, which would provide additional accommodations and supports. If your child has not been screened for dyslexia, request that Harmony Public Schools (Combined) conduct the screening in writing immediately.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)

If your child with disabilities is suspended from Harmony Public Schools (Combined) for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year, this triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This review is a critical process to determine whether your child's behavior that led to the suspension was a manifestation of their disability or whether it was the direct result of the school's failure to implement their IEP. Suspensions beyond this threshold constitute a Change of Placement under IDEA, requiring an ARD meeting to review the circumstances and consider possible modifications to your child's IEP or behavior plan.

This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face as a parent of a special education student. You must act quickly to protect your child's rights. Harmony Public Schools (Combined) must hold the MDR ARD meeting promptly, typically within 10 school days of the decision to change the student's placement due to disciplinary reasons. The consequences of failing to act can be severe. Filing a Complaint Against Harmony Public Schools (Combined)

If you believe Harmony Public Schools (Combined) has violated your child's rights under IDEA, you have several avenues for recourse. The first is filing a TEA State Complaint. This is a free process, and TEA is required to resolve the complaint within 60 days. Another option is to request Mediation through TEA, which is also free and provides a neutral third party to facilitate communication and resolve disagreements. If these options are unsuccessful, you can pursue a Due Process Hearing, which is a more formal legal proceeding before an administrative law judge. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe Harmony Public Schools (Combined) has discriminated against your child based on their disability.

Regardless of what any staff member at Harmony Public Schools (Combined) might tell you, these rights are enshrined in federal and state law. No one can take them away from you. Your ability to advocate effectively for your child depends on knowing these rights and being prepared to exercise them if necessary. Consider these options as tools in your toolkit. Choose the one that best fits your specific situation.

Frequently Asked Questions About Harmony Public Schools (Combined) Special Education

The questions every Harmony Public Schools (Combined) parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)? +

Submit a written request to the Harmony Public Schools (Combined) Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Harmony Public Schools (Combined) have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Harmony Public Schools (Combined) to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Harmony Public Schools (Combined) must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Harmony Public Schools (Combined) suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Harmony Public Schools (Combined) must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
