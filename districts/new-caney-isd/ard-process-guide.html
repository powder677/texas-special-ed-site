What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in New Caney ISD. As a parent of a child with a disability, you are a vital part of your child's educational journey. The Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting is the cornerstone of that journey. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, and it's a meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, often referred to as an IEP. This document is like a roadmap for your child's education, outlining the specific supports and services they need to succeed in school.

The ARD meeting isn't just a formality; it's a collaborative process where you, as the parent, work together with school professionals to make decisions about your child's education. The team typically includes you, the parent; one or more of your child's teachers; a special education administrator or designee who is knowledgeable about special education programs; an evaluation specialist, such as a diagnostician or educational psychologist; and other professionals who have specific expertise related to your child's needs. Your child may also be invited to attend, depending on their age and ability to participate meaningfully. The goal is to create an IEP that is tailored to your child's unique strengths, needs, and learning style. The ARD committee will discuss your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, develop measurable annual goals, determine the appropriate supports and services your child needs, and decide on the most appropriate educational placement for your child. Remember, you are an equal partner in this process, and your voice matters.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

New Caney ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is not five school days, but five calendar days, including weekends and holidays. This notice is designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting, gather relevant information, and consider your input. The notice must include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of the individuals who will be attending. It should also inform you of your right to participate in the meeting and to bring anyone you choose, such as an advocate or family member, to support you.

If you wish to have the ARD meeting sooner than five days, you can waive your right to the five-day notice in writing. This is entirely your choice. Perhaps you have availability sooner or the team needs to meet quickly to address an urgent issue. If you choose to waive the notice, be sure you are comfortable doing so and that you have had adequate time to prepare for the meeting. Never feel pressured to waive this right if you need more time to gather your thoughts, consult with others, or collect necessary documentation. Your informed consent is essential throughout the ARD process.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Being prepared for your child's ARD meeting can make a significant difference in the outcome. Here's a practical list of items you may want to bring: Any prior evaluations, whether from the school district or from independent professionals. This information provides valuable insights into your child's strengths and areas where they may need support. Don't hesitate to bring doctor's letters or medical reports that are relevant to your child's educational needs. For example, if your child has a diagnosis of ADHD, a letter from their doctor outlining their symptoms and recommended interventions can be helpful. Gathering any school records you have, such as report cards, progress reports, or work samples, can help illustrate your child's academic performance over time. It's also wise to create a written list of your concerns and questions. This will ensure that you address all of your concerns during the meeting and that nothing gets overlooked. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you take notes, and ensure that your voice is heard. Finally, in Texas, it is legal to audio record the ARD meeting, provided that you inform the ARD committee that you are doing so before the meeting begins. A recording can be a valuable tool for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The ARD process is designed to be collaborative and to ensure that decisions are made in the best interest of your child. If, at any point during the ARD meeting, you or any other member of the ARD committee feels that more information is needed or that a recess would be beneficial, you have the right to request a recess of up to 10 school days. This recess allows time to gather additional information, consult with experts, or simply to process the information that has been presented. Perhaps you want to seek input from an outside therapist or have a specific evaluation conducted. The recess period provides that opportunity.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request to the ARD committee. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation, although you can if you choose. Be clear that you are requesting a recess of the meeting to gather further information or seek consultation. The ARD committee must then grant your request and schedule a date to reconvene the meeting. Remember, this is your right, and the team is obligated to honor it. Don't hesitate to use this option if you feel it would help ensure that your child's IEP is comprehensive and appropriate.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the central document that guides your child's special education. It is developed during the ARD meeting and outlines the specific supports and services that your child will receive. The IEP includes several key components. First, it describes your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section summarizes your child's strengths and areas where they may need support, based on assessments, observations, and input from parents and teachers. Second, the IEP includes measurable annual goals. These goals are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals) and are designed to help your child make progress in their education. Third, the IEP outlines the specific special education and related services that your child will receive. This could include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or other supports. Fourth, the IEP specifies the educational placement for your child. This refers to the setting where your child will receive their education, such as a general education classroom, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. Placement decisions are based on your child's individual needs and should be made in the least restrictive environment. Finally, the IEP includes accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as extended time on tests or preferential seating. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as modified assignments or a reduced workload. It is important to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and consult with others before making a decision. You can reconvene the ARD team if you have questions or concerns.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD team from time to time. You are the expert on your child, and your perspective is valuable. If you disagree with the team's recommendations regarding your child's IEP, it's important to know your rights. First, you have the right to refuse consent for initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the team's decision to place your child in special education, you can decline to give your consent, and your child will not be placed in special education at that time. Second, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with any of its components. Signing the IEP indicates that you were present at the ARD meeting, not that you agree with every aspect of the document. If you choose not to sign, the school district may implement the IEP, but you retain the right to pursue other options, such as requesting an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at district expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an IEE, and the district is generally required to pay for it. Even if you sign the IEP, you can write "parent disagrees" on the document next to any area where you disagree. This serves as a record of your dissent and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you have tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD team and have not been successful, there are several formal options available to you. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. The second option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district work with a trained mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is also free and can be a helpful way to resolve disputes without resorting to more formal legal proceedings. The third option is to request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a decision about your child's IEP. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. All of these rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code, Title 19, Chapter 89. These resources, also known as the Legal Framework, are available online and detail your rights and the school district's responsibilities. Remember, you are your child's best advocate, and you have the right to ensure that they receive a free and appropriate public education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
