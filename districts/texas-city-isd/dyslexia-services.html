Dyslexia Services in Texas City ISD

Dyslexia Services in Texas City ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Texas City Independent School District (TCISD). TCISD is committed to identifying and supporting students with dyslexia, adhering to state mandates and best practices.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is the guiding document for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia in Texas public schools. TCISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the most recent version, the 2024 update. This handbook provides comprehensive information on:

  • The definition of dyslexia
  • Identification procedures
  • Instructional practices
  • Assessment methods
  • Parent involvement

Parents are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024, which can be accessed on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. It provides a clear understanding of the district's responsibilities and your rights as a parent.

Mandatory Screening

TCISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening for students in accordance with Texas law. Screening typically begins in kindergarten and continues through the early elementary grades. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. This may include looking at early literacy skills like phonological awareness, letter knowledge, and rapid naming. A screening does not provide a definitive diagnosis of dyslexia but rather flags students who may need further evaluation.

If a student is identified as potentially at risk for dyslexia through the screening process, further evaluation and interventions will be provided.

Structured Literacy Programs

TCISD utilizes Structured Literacy approaches for students identified with dyslexia or those at risk. Structured Literacy is a research-based approach that systematically teaches foundational reading skills. Key components of Structured Literacy include:

  • Phonology: Understanding the sound structure of language.
  • Orthography: Understanding the rules and patterns of written language.
  • Morphology: Understanding the meaning of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, roots).
  • Syntax: Understanding sentence structure.
  • Semantics: Understanding word meaning.

TCISD employs trained educators who are proficient in delivering Structured Literacy instruction. Specific programs and interventions used within the district may vary based on individual student needs.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's specific needs and how those needs impact their access to education.

  • 504 Plan: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to ensure that a student with a disability has equal access to the general education curriculum. This is for students whose dyslexia significantly limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program): An IEP is a legally binding document for students with disabilities who require specially designed instruction to make progress in their education. Students eligible for an IEP typically require more intensive support and interventions than those served under a 504 plan. This is reserved for students with dyslexia and another disability that significantly impacts their ability to learn.

The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, which includes parents, determines whether a student qualifies for an IEP. The 504 committee, which also includes parents, determines eligibility for a 504 plan.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns that do not change the content being taught. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia may include:

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Use of assistive technology (e.g., text-to-speech software)
  • Preferential seating
  • Oral administration of tests
  • Note-taking assistance
  • Reduced workload
  • Access to audiobooks

The specific accommodations provided will be tailored to the individual needs of the student, as determined by the 504 or ARD committee.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, you should contact your child's teacher or the school principal. You can also contact the TCISD Special Education Department. A written request for evaluation is typically required. This request should include:

  • Your child's name and date of birth
  • Your contact information
  • A description of your concerns about your child's reading and spelling skills

TCISD will respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe, typically outlined in district policy. The district will then determine if an evaluation is warranted based on the information provided and the child's school history.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
