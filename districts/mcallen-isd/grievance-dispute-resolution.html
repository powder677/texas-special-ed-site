The 10-Day Suspension Rule

The "10-Day Suspension Rule" is a cornerstone of protecting students, especially those with disabilities, from unfair or excessive disciplinary measures. McAllen ISD, like all Texas school districts, is bound by this rule, which is derived from federal and state laws, including the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

What does it mean? Simply put, if your child is removed from their current educational placement (through suspension or other means) for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single school year, it triggers specific protections and processes, particularly if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or a 504 plan. These removals don't have to be consecutive; they add up over the entire year.

Why is it important? Beyond 10 days, the school district must determine if the student's behavior is a manifestation of their disability. This determination can dramatically affect the consequences your child faces. It ensures that students aren't punished for behaviors stemming from their disability.

What to do IMMEDIATELY if your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 days of suspension:

  • Track the days: Keep a meticulous record of every day your child has been suspended or otherwise removed from school. Don't rely on the school's record alone.
  • Contact the school: Immediately notify the principal, special education director (if applicable), and your child's teacher that you are aware of the 10-day rule and expect the district to comply with IDEA. Document this notification (email is preferred).
  • Request an ARD/504 meeting: If your child has an IEP or 504 plan, formally request an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting or a 504 meeting immediately. This meeting is crucial to discuss the behavior and conduct a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).
  • Know your rights: Familiarize yourself with IDEA and the Texas Education Agency's (TEA) guidelines regarding discipline for students with disabilities.

Warning: Do not assume the school district will automatically inform you of your rights or conduct the necessary reviews. You must be proactive! Advocate fiercely for your child.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a critical process mandated by IDEA to determine whether a student's misconduct is a manifestation of their disability. This process applies to students with IEPs when they face disciplinary action that could result in a Change of Placement (see below).

How does it work?

  1. Trigger: The MDR is triggered when a student with an IEP faces a suspension or expulsion that would result in a Change of Placement (more than 10 consecutive school days, or a series of removals that constitute a pattern).
  2. ARD Committee Review: The ARD committee (including you, the parent) reviews all relevant information, including the student's IEP, evaluations, teacher observations, and any relevant information provided by you.
  3. Two Key Questions: The ARD committee must answer these two crucial questions:
    • Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
    • Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP?
  4. The Outcome:
    • If the answer to either question is YES: The behavior is considered a manifestation of the disability. The student cannot be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The ARD committee must conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA), implement a behavior intervention plan (BIP), or revise an existing one. The student must be returned to their previous placement, unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the BIP.
    • If the answer to both questions is NO: The behavior is not considered a manifestation of the disability. The student can be disciplined in the same manner as a student without a disability. However, the school district is still required to provide services to the student so they can participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals.

What to do during an MDR:

  • Prepare thoroughly: Gather all relevant information about your child's disability and the circumstances surrounding the incident. Bring documentation, evaluations, and any evidence that supports your position.
  • Actively participate: Express your concerns and opinions. Don't be afraid to challenge the school district's perspective.
  • Document everything: Keep detailed notes of the meeting, including who attended, what was discussed, and any decisions made.
  • Consult with an advocate or attorney: Consider seeking legal advice or representation to ensure your child's rights are protected.

Key Point: The MDR is not just a formality. It's a critical safeguard to protect students with disabilities from discriminatory discipline.

Change of Placement

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or when a series of removals constitutes a pattern of exclusion. This pattern might involve short suspensions adding up to more than 10 days, or changes to the student's learning environment that significantly disrupt their education.

Why is it significant? A Change of Placement triggers heightened protections under IDEA, particularly the MDR process. It signals that the student's behavior is significantly impacting their educational experience, and the school district must carefully consider whether the behavior is related to the student's disability.

Examples of Change of Placement:

  • Long-term suspension: Suspension exceeding 10 consecutive school days.
  • Expulsion: Removal from school for a significant period of time.
  • Frequent short-term suspensions: A pattern of short suspensions that add up to more than 10 days and indicate a significant disruption to the student's education.
  • Placement in a more restrictive setting: Moving a student from a general education classroom to a self-contained special education classroom, or from a public school to a residential treatment facility.

What to do if a Change of Placement is proposed:

  • Immediately invoke your rights: Inform the school district that you are aware of your child's rights under IDEA and that you expect them to follow the proper procedures.
  • Request an ARD meeting: Demand an ARD meeting to discuss the proposed Change of Placement and conduct an MDR, if applicable.
  • Review the IEP: Carefully review your child's IEP to ensure it is being properly implemented and that it addresses their behavioral needs.
  • Consider an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child's needs, you have the right to request an IEE at the district's expense.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe McAllen ISD has violated your child's rights or has failed to follow proper procedures, you have the right to file a grievance. The grievance process is typically multi-tiered, starting with a Level 1 grievance at the campus level.

What is a Level 1 Grievance? This is the initial formal complaint filed with the school principal or designated administrator. It outlines the specific issue, the date it occurred, the individuals involved, and the resolution you are seeking.

Steps to File a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Form: Request a copy of the McAllen ISD grievance form from the school principal or the district's website.
  2. Complete the Form: Fill out the form completely and accurately. Be specific about the following:
    • The specific issue: Clearly describe the problem or violation you are grieving.
    • The date and time of the incident: Provide accurate details about when the incident occurred.
    • The individuals involved: Identify all individuals involved in the incident.
    • The policy or procedure violated: If possible, cite the specific school district policy or procedure that was violated.
    • The resolution you are seeking: Clearly state what you want the school district to do to resolve the issue.
  3. Gather Supporting Documentation: Attach any relevant documents to your grievance form, such as emails, letters, incident reports, or witness statements.
  4. Submit the Grievance: Submit the completed grievance form and supporting documentation to the school principal or designated administrator.
  5. Keep a Copy: Make a copy of the grievance form and all supporting documentation for your records.
  6. Track the Timeline: The school district typically has a limited time frame to respond to your grievance (usually within 10-15 business days). Track the timeline and follow up if you do not receive a response within the allotted time.

Important Considerations:

  • Be professional and factual: While emotions are understandable, keep your grievance focused on the facts and avoid personal attacks.
  • Document everything: Maintain a detailed record of all communications with the school district.
  • Escalate if necessary: If you are not satisfied with the response to your Level 1 grievance, you can typically escalate the grievance to the next level (Level 2), which usually involves the superintendent or a designated district administrator.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe McAllen ISD has violated federal or state special education laws (IDEA) and the grievance process has failed to resolve the issue, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What is a TEA State Complaint? This is a formal complaint filed with TEA alleging that a school district has failed to comply with special education requirements. TEA will investigate the complaint and, if a violation is found, will order the district to take corrective action.

How to File a TEA State Complaint:

  1. Obtain the Complaint Form: Download the TEA complaint form from the TEA website (search for "TEA Special Education Complaint").
  2. Meet the Deadlines: The complaint must be filed within one year of the date you knew or should have known about the alleged violation.
  3. Complete the Form: Fill out the complaint form completely and accurately. Be specific about the following:
    • The specific violation: Clearly describe the special education law or regulation that you believe the school district has violated.
    • The facts of the case: Provide detailed information about the events that led to the complaint.
    • Supporting documentation: Attach any relevant documents, such as IEPs, evaluations, letters, emails, and grievance responses.
    • Proposed resolution: State what you want TEA to order the school district to do to correct the violation.
  4. Submit the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA. The TEA website will provide the correct address.
  5. Cooperate with TEA's Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and may contact you for additional information. Be prepared to cooperate fully with TEA's investigation.

Important Considerations:

  • Gather Strong Evidence: The more evidence you can provide to support your complaint, the stronger your case will be.
  • Consult with an Advocate or Attorney: Filing a TEA complaint can be complex. Consider seeking legal advice or representation to ensure your complaint is properly prepared and presented.
  • Understand TEA's Authority: TEA's authority is limited to enforcing compliance with special education laws. It cannot award monetary damages or resolve all types of disputes.

Due Process

Due process is a legal term that refers to the fundamental fairness of legal proceedings. In the context of special education, due process provides parents with the right to challenge the school district's decisions regarding their child's identification, evaluation, placement, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE).

When to Invoke Due Process: You can request a due process hearing if you disagree with the school district's actions and have been unable to resolve the issue through other means, such as the grievance process or mediation. Common reasons for requesting a due process hearing include:

  • Disagreement with the IEP: You disagree with the goals, services, or placement outlined in your child's IEP.
  • Failure to Provide FAPE: You believe the school district is failing to provide your child with a free appropriate public education.
  • Inadequate Evaluation: You believe the school district's evaluation of your child's needs was inadequate or biased.
  • Discipline Issues: You believe the school district's disciplinary actions violate your child's rights under IDEA.

The Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding before an impartial hearing officer. Both you and the school district will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and argue your case.

Steps to Request a Due Process Hearing:

  1. File a Due Process Complaint: You must file a written due process complaint with the school district and the Texas Education Agency (TEA). The complaint must include specific information, such as:
    • Your child's name and address.
    • The name of the school your child attends.
    • A description of the problem and the proposed solution.
    • A statement that you are requesting a due process hearing.
  2. Timeline: There are strict timelines for filing a due process complaint. You must file the complaint within two years of the date you knew or should have known about the alleged violation.
  3. Resolution Session: Within 15 days of receiving your due process complaint, the school district must convene a resolution session to attempt to resolve the dispute. You are not required to participate in the resolution session, but it is often a valuable opportunity to discuss the issues and potentially reach an agreement.
  4. Due Process Hearing: If the resolution session is unsuccessful, the due process hearing will be scheduled. The hearing officer will issue a written decision within 45 days of the end of the hearing.

Important Considerations:

  • Seek Legal Representation: Due process hearings can be complex and adversarial. It is strongly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney experienced in special education law.
  • Gather Strong Evidence: The outcome of the due process hearing will depend on the evidence presented. Gather all relevant documents, such as IEPs, evaluations, letters, emails, and witness statements.
  • Prepare for the Hearing: Work closely with your attorney to prepare for the hearing. This includes identifying witnesses, preparing exhibits, and developing a legal strategy.

Warning: Due process is a serious undertaking and should not be entered into lightly. However, it is a powerful tool to protect your child's rights and ensure they receive the education they deserve.
