Dyslexia Services in McAllen ISD

Dyslexia Services in McAllen ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in McAllen Independent School District (McAllen ISD) in compliance with Texas state law and the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is the primary resource guiding dyslexia identification and intervention practices in Texas public schools. The 2024 update brings several important changes and clarifications that McAllen ISD implements. Key aspects of the Handbook that families and educators should be aware of include:

  • Legal Foundation: The Handbook outlines the legal mandates related to dyslexia screening, identification, and instruction as defined in the Texas Education Code (TEC) and Texas Administrative Code (TAC).
  • Definition of Dyslexia: The Handbook provides the state's definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Screening Procedures: The Handbook details the mandatory screening procedures for dyslexia for students in kindergarten and first grade, and outlines procedures for identifying students in subsequent grades who may exhibit characteristics of dyslexia.
  • Instructional Practices (Structured Literacy): The Handbook emphasizes the use of structured literacy approaches for students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to teaching reading that focuses on phonology, sound-symbol association, syllable instruction, morphology, syntax, and semantics.
  • Progress Monitoring: The Handbook highlights the importance of ongoing progress monitoring to ensure the effectiveness of intervention and to make data-driven decisions about instructional adjustments.
  • Parent Involvement: The Handbook emphasizes the importance of active parent involvement in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. Parents are entitled to information about their child's progress and the services being provided.
  • Assistive Technology: The Handbook acknowledges the potential benefits of assistive technology for students with dyslexia and provides guidance on selecting and implementing appropriate technology.

McAllen ISD adheres to the guidelines and procedures outlined in the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook to ensure that students with dyslexia receive appropriate identification, support, and instruction. The full Handbook can be accessed on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law requires all students in kindergarten and first grade to be screened for dyslexia. McAllen ISD implements the following screening procedures:

  • Kindergarten Screening: All kindergarten students are screened for early literacy skills that are predictive of future reading difficulties. These screenings typically occur in the fall and spring.
  • First Grade Screening: All first grade students are screened for dyslexia in the fall and, if necessary, again in the spring.
  • Screening Instruments: McAllen ISD uses TEA-approved screening instruments that assess key areas such as phonological awareness, rapid automatized naming (RAN), letter knowledge, and phonological memory. Specific instruments used are determined by district curriculum specialists and comply with the Handbook.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified of the results of the screening. If a student is identified as being at risk for dyslexia, parents will receive information about intervention options and support services available within McAllen ISD.
  • Referral Process: If the screening results indicate a potential risk for dyslexia, the student may be referred for further evaluation and consideration for dyslexia intervention.
  • Upper Grades: Students in grades 2 and above may be screened for characteristics of dyslexia if concerns arise from teachers, parents, or the student themselves.

It's important to note that screening is only the first step. A screening result indicating a potential risk does not necessarily mean the student has dyslexia. A comprehensive evaluation is required to make a definitive diagnosis.

Structured Literacy Programs

McAllen ISD utilizes structured literacy approaches to provide effective reading instruction for students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is research-based and proven to be effective in addressing the core deficits associated with dyslexia. Key features of structured literacy programs include:

  • Explicit Instruction: Skills are taught directly and systematically, with clear explanations and modeling.
  • Systematic and Cumulative: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Multisensory Instruction: Instruction involves the use of multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance learning and memory.
  • Phonological Awareness: Instruction focuses on developing phonological awareness skills, such as rhyming, segmenting, and blending sounds in words.
  • Phonics: Instruction emphasizes the relationship between letters and sounds, teaching students how to decode words accurately.
  • Fluency: Instruction focuses on improving reading fluency, including accuracy, rate, and prosody.
  • Vocabulary: Instruction focuses on building vocabulary knowledge, including word meanings and relationships.
  • Reading Comprehension: Instruction focuses on developing reading comprehension strategies, such as summarizing, questioning, and making inferences.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support services through either a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is most appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the severity of their dyslexia.

  • Section 504 Plan: A Section 504 plan is designed to provide accommodations and modifications to the general education environment to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to education. A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. For students with dyslexia, a 504 plan might include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. The focus of a 504 plan is to level the playing field and provide access to the general education curriculum.
  • Individualized Education Program (IEP): An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines a student's special education and related services. To be eligible for an IEP, a student must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specially designed instruction. Students with dyslexia who require intensive, individualized intervention may be eligible for an IEP under the specific learning disability (SLD) category. An IEP includes specific goals, objectives, and services, such as direct instruction in reading, speech therapy, or occupational therapy. The IEP team, including parents, develops the IEP based on the student's individual needs.

Key Differences:

  • Eligibility: 504 eligibility is broader than IEP eligibility.
  • Services: IEPs provide specially designed instruction, while 504 plans primarily provide accommodations.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs have more extensive legal requirements and procedural safeguards than 504 plans.
  • Focus: 504 focuses on access; IEP focuses on specially designed instruction to address the academic deficit.

The decision of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis by a team of professionals, including the student's teacher, school psychologist, and parents. The team will consider the student's academic performance, assessment results, and individual needs to determine the most appropriate level of support.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional practices that help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Accommodations do not change the content of the curriculum but rather change how it is presented, practiced, or assessed.

Examples of accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia in McAllen ISD include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location in the classroom that minimizes distractions and allows for optimal learning.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology, such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, or audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Oral Administration: Reading test questions or instructions aloud to the student.
  • Use of Manipulatives: Allowing the student to use manipulatives, such as letter tiles or counters, to support learning.
  • Visual Aids: Providing visual aids, such as graphic organizers or charts, to support understanding.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take frequent breaks to avoid fatigue and maintain focus.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet, distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Word Prediction Software: Using software to assist with spelling and writing.
  • Access to Notes: Providing copies of teacher notes or allowing the student to take notes using a laptop or tablet.
  • Alternative Assessments: Using alternative assessment methods, such as oral presentations or projects, to assess understanding.
  • Reading Materials in Alternate Formats: Providing materials in larger print, audio format, or digital format.

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations provided to a student will be determined based on their individual needs as outlined in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia should take the following steps to request an evaluation in McAllen ISD:

  1. Contact the Teacher: Begin by contacting your child's teacher to discuss your concerns. Share any observations you have made about your child's reading difficulties, as well as any relevant medical or educational history.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or Principal: If you continue to have concerns after speaking with the teacher, contact the school counselor or principal. They can provide you with additional information about the district's dyslexia services and the evaluation process.
  3. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for a dyslexia evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The request should include your child's name, grade level, school, and a detailed explanation of your concerns. Be specific about the reading difficulties you have observed. You can often find a form to use on the McAllen ISD website or by contacting the special education department.
  4. Participate in the Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to conduct an evaluation, you will be asked to provide consent. The evaluation process may involve a variety of assessments, including standardized tests of reading, spelling, and phonological awareness. You will be an important part of the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee if an IEP evaluation is pursued.
  5. Attend the ARD or 504 Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, a meeting will be held to discuss the results and determine whether your child is eligible for dyslexia services. If your child is found to have dyslexia, the team will develop a 504 plan or IEP to address their individual needs.

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines: Texas law sets specific timelines for the evaluation process. The school district must complete the evaluation within a specified timeframe after receiving parental consent.
  • Parent Rights: Parents have the right to be involved in all aspects of the dyslexia identification and intervention process. You have the right to access your child's educational records, participate in meetings, and disagree with the school's decisions.
  • Advocacy: If you have concerns about the evaluation process or the services being provided to your child, you may wish to seek assistance from an advocate or attorney specializing in special education law. Several organizations in Texas provide resources and support to parents of children with dyslexia.

McAllen ISD is committed to providing comprehensive dyslexia services to students who need them. By working together, parents, educators, and students can ensure that all students with dyslexia have the opportunity to reach their full potential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
