Requesting an Evaluation in Spring Branch ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Spring Branch ISD

Introduction

This guide outlines the process for requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Spring Branch Independent School District (SBISD), in accordance with Texas special education law. Understanding your rights and the proper procedures can significantly improve the likelihood of a timely and appropriate evaluation. This guide covers submitting a written request, the mandated timeline, required components of the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE), Child Find obligations, what to do if your request is refused, and the steps following the evaluation.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first and most crucial step in initiating the special education evaluation process is submitting a formal, written request to SBISD. This request serves as official notification to the district that you believe your child may have a disability and requires special education and related services. While verbal requests might be acknowledged, a written request creates a documented record and triggers the district's legal obligations under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code.

Your written request should be clear, concise, and contain the following information:

  • Date: Include the date you are submitting the request.
  • Your Contact Information: Provide your full name, address, phone number, and email address. Be sure to specify how you prefer to be contacted.
  • Child's Information: Include your child's full name, date of birth, grade level, and the name of the school they attend.
  • Statement of Concern: Clearly state that you are requesting a special education evaluation for your child. Use language like, "I am writing to formally request a full and individual evaluation to determine if my child, [Child's Name], is eligible for special education services."
  • Reasons for Concern: Provide specific examples of your child's difficulties in school. These examples should be factual and descriptive, focusing on observable behaviors and academic performance. Avoid vague statements. For instance, instead of saying "My child is struggling in math," provide examples like, "My child consistently scores below 70 on math tests, has difficulty completing multi-step problems, and struggles to understand concepts like fractions." Include specific examples of difficulties with reading, writing, social skills, communication, or behavior.
  • Supporting Documentation (Optional): If you have any documentation that supports your concerns, such as doctor's reports, previous evaluations, report cards, work samples, or teacher communications, you can include copies with your request. While not mandatory at this stage, this can strengthen your case.
  • Specific Areas of Concern (Optional): If you have specific areas you believe should be assessed (e.g., reading comprehension, speech therapy, occupational therapy), explicitly mention them in your request. This is helpful but not required; the ARD committee will ultimately determine the scope of the evaluation.
  • Delivery Method and Recipient: Address the request to the appropriate school official. In SBISD, this is usually the school principal, special education director, or the campus Section 504 coordinator. Verify the correct contact person with the school beforehand. Send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery. Retain a copy of the request for your records.

Example Letter Snippet:

Date: October 26, 2023

To: [Principal's Name], Principal, [School Name]

From: [Your Name]

Subject: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Name]

I am writing to formally request a full and individual evaluation to determine if my child, [Child's Name], is eligible for special education services. [Child's Name] is in the [Grade] grade at [School Name]. I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s progress in [Specific Subject or Area]. For example, [Child's Name] struggles with [Specific example of difficulty]. I have also attached [Mention any attached documents].

Remember to tailor this example to your specific circumstances and concerns.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a strict timeline for SBISD to respond to your written request for a special education evaluation. Understanding this timeline is crucial for advocating for your child's rights.

Upon receiving your written request, SBISD has 15 school days to respond with one of the following actions:

  • Decision to Evaluate: The district agrees to conduct a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE).
  • Refusal to Evaluate: The district refuses to conduct an FIE and provides you with a written explanation of the reasons for the refusal. This explanation must include a description of the data reviewed by the district, any alternative options considered, and your right to appeal the decision.
  • Request for More Information: The district may request additional information from you before making a decision. However, this request does not restart the 15-day clock. The district must still make a decision within the original timeframe.

If the district agrees to evaluate, the following timeline applies:

Once the district decides to conduct an FIE, they have an additional 45 school days to complete the evaluation and hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the results and determine eligibility for special education services. It is important to note that these are school days, meaning weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not included in the calculation. Therefore, the entire process can take significantly longer than 45 calendar days.

The ARD committee meeting must be scheduled in a timely manner to ensure compliance with the 45-school-day deadline. You, as the parent, are a critical member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in all discussions and decisions.

Important Considerations:

  • School Days vs. Calendar Days: Carefully track the timeline, keeping in mind the distinction between school days and calendar days.
  • Documentation: Keep meticulous records of all communication with the school district, including dates of letters, emails, phone calls, and meetings.
  • Breaks in the Timeline: The timeline can be paused under very specific circumstances (e.g., if you are repeatedly unavailable to attend meetings). Ensure you understand the reasons for any pauses and document them.
  • Extended Evaluations: In rare circumstances, the ARD committee may agree to extend the evaluation timeline. This requires your written consent and must be based on a legitimate need for additional assessment.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and weaknesses in all areas related to the suspected disability. The FIE is used to determine if your child has a disability under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and to identify the specific educational and related services your child needs to access a free and appropriate public education (FAPE).

Texas Education Code outlines the specific areas that the FIE must cover, depending on the suspected disability. Generally, the FIE should include assessments in the following areas, as relevant:

  • Intellectual Functioning: This assesses your child's cognitive abilities, including reasoning, problem-solving, and learning potential.
  • Academic Achievement: This evaluates your child's performance in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. Standardized achievement tests are typically used.
  • Adaptive Behavior: This assesses your child's ability to function independently in daily life, including self-care, social skills, and communication.
  • Communication Skills: This evaluates your child's expressive and receptive language abilities, including speech, articulation, and understanding of language.
  • Motor Skills: This assesses your child's gross and fine motor skills, including coordination, balance, and dexterity.
  • Social and Emotional Functioning: This evaluates your child's emotional regulation, social skills, and ability to interact with others.
  • Medical History: A review of your child's medical history, including any relevant diagnoses or treatments.
  • Sensory Processing: An evaluation of how your child processes sensory information (e.g., touch, sound, sight, movement).
  • Hearing and Vision Screenings: These screenings are essential to rule out hearing or vision impairments as contributing factors to your child's difficulties.

The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in assessing children with disabilities. This may include educational diagnosticians, psychologists, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, and other specialists.

Parental Input: Your input is crucial throughout the evaluation process. You have the right to provide information about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and developmental history. The evaluation team must consider your perspective when interpreting the results and making recommendations.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the district's evaluation, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense. SBISD can either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. Understanding your rights regarding IEEs is crucial if you disagree with the district's findings.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law, in accordance with IDEA, places a legal obligation on SBISD to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within its boundaries who may be in need of special education and related services. This obligation is known as "Child Find." This applies to all children, including those who are homeschooled, attend private schools, or are suspected of having a disability even if they are succeeding academically.

SBISD fulfills its Child Find obligation through various activities, including:

  • Screening Programs: The district may conduct screenings for hearing, vision, speech, and other developmental areas.
  • Referral Processes: SBISD must have clear and accessible procedures for referring students who are suspected of having a disability.
  • Public Awareness Campaigns: The district may conduct outreach efforts to inform parents and the community about special education services.
  • Collaboration with Community Agencies: SBISD may work with local doctors, therapists, and other professionals to identify children with disabilities.

Your Role in Child Find: As a parent, you play a vital role in the Child Find process. If you suspect your child may have a disability, it is your responsibility to notify SBISD. Do not assume that the district will automatically identify your child. By submitting a written request for an evaluation, you are fulfilling your role in the Child Find process.

What If You Suspect a Disability but the School Does Not? If you have concerns about your child's development or academic progress, but the school does not share your concerns, you still have the right to request an evaluation. Document your concerns in writing and follow the steps outlined above for submitting a written request. The district is obligated to consider your request and conduct an evaluation if there is reason to suspect a disability.

What If They Refuse

If SBISD refuses to conduct a special education evaluation after you have submitted a written request, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for the refusal. This explanation must include:

  • A description of the data reviewed by the district.
  • Any alternative options considered.
  • A statement that you have the right to appeal the decision.

Your Options When Refused: You have several options if SBISD refuses to evaluate your child:

  • Request a Meeting: Schedule a meeting with the school principal, special education director, or Section 504 coordinator to discuss their concerns and try to reach a resolution. Bring documentation of your concerns and be prepared to advocate for your child's needs.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) offers mediation services.
  • File a Complaint with TEA: You can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe that SBISD has violated state or federal special education laws. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the dispute. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing.

Documentation is Key: Throughout this process, it is essential to maintain detailed records of all communication with the school district, including dates of letters, emails, phone calls, and meetings. This documentation will be crucial if you decide to pursue mediation, file a complaint, or request a due process hearing.

Seek Legal Advice: Consider consulting with an attorney specializing in special education law. An attorney can advise you on your rights and options and represent you in negotiations, mediation, or due process hearings. Organizations like Disability Rights Texas can also provide valuable information and support.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is completed, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to discuss the results. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of this committee.

The ARD Committee Meeting:

At the ARD meeting, the evaluation team will present their findings and recommendations. The committee will discuss whether your child meets the criteria for a specific disability under IDEA and Texas law. If your child is determined eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

The Individualized Education Program (IEP):

The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports that SBISD will provide to help your child succeed. The IEP must include:

  • Your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance.
  • Measurable annual goals.
  • A description of the special education and related services your child will receive.
  • A statement of any accommodations or modifications your child needs to access the general education curriculum.
  • A description of how your child's progress will be measured and reported to you.
  • Dates for when services will begin and end, the frequency and location of services.

Your Rights at the ARD Meeting: You have the right to:

  • Participate fully in all discussions and decisions.
  • Ask questions and seek clarification about the evaluation results and IEP recommendations.
  • Provide input about your child's needs and goals.
  • Disagree with the committee's recommendations.
  • Request additional assessments or services.
  • Bring an advocate or attorney to the meeting.

If You Disagree with the IEP: If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to refuse to consent to its implementation. You can then pursue mediation, file a complaint with TEA, or request a due process hearing. It's crucial to document your concerns and the reasons for your disagreement.

Ongoing Monitoring and Review: The IEP must be reviewed and updated at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to request an ARD meeting at any time if you have concerns about your child's progress or the effectiveness of the IEP.

This guide provides a general overview of the special education evaluation process in Spring Branch ISD. It is essential to consult with legal professionals or advocacy organizations for personalized advice and support. Understanding your rights and advocating for your child's needs will ensure they receive the appropriate special education and related services to thrive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
