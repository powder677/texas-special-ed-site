What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, and an ARD meeting is a very important part of your child's special education journey in Little Elm ISD. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to make decisions about your child's Individualized Education Program, often called an IEP. These meetings are required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and also by the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which outlines special education services in our state. In essence, the ARD meeting is where a team of people, including you as the parent, get together to decide if your child is eligible for special education services, to review their progress, and to plan for their future educational needs. The team carefully considers your child's strengths, weaknesses, and what supports they need to succeed in school.

The ARD committee includes a variety of people who have a stake in your child's education. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the team. Other required members include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services, your child's general education teacher (if your child is, or may be, participating in the general education environment), a special education teacher, and someone who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results. Depending on your child's specific needs, other individuals may also be invited, such as therapists (speech, occupational, physical), counselors, diagnosticians, or even your child themselves, especially as they get older. The goal is to bring together everyone who has relevant knowledge and expertise to create the best possible plan for your child. Remember, the ARD committee works together to develop and implement an IEP that meets your child's unique needs and helps them make progress in school.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Little Elm ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting takes place. This notice is not just a formality; it's a crucial safeguard to ensure you have adequate time to prepare and gather your thoughts before making important decisions about your child's education. The purpose of this notice is to give you enough time to review any documents, arrange for childcare, or invite a support person to attend the meeting with you.

The written notice must include several key pieces of information. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting - for example, is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility for special education, an annual ARD to review and update the IEP, or a meeting to address a specific concern? The notice will also include the date, time, and location of the meeting. Most importantly, the notice must list the individuals who will be attending the meeting. This allows you to know who will be present and to consider who else you might want to invite to provide additional support or perspective. Keep in mind that you have the right to request that other individuals be invited to the ARD meeting if you believe they have important information to contribute. Finally, the notice should clearly state your rights as a parent in the ARD process. If you do not receive a notice at least five calendar days before the meeting, you have the right to request that the meeting be rescheduled. However, if you are comfortable meeting sooner, you can waive the 5-day notice requirement in writing. This might be helpful if you want to address an urgent issue or if you and the school district agree that meeting sooner would be beneficial for your child.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but coming prepared can significantly improve the experience and ensure your child's needs are fully addressed. It's essential to gather relevant information and organize your thoughts beforehand. One of the most helpful things you can bring is any prior evaluations of your child, whether conducted by the school district or by independent professionals. These evaluations provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they may need support. This includes psychological evaluations, speech-language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, or any other assessments that shed light on your child's learning profile.

Another important item to bring is any letters or reports from doctors or other medical professionals who have treated your child. These documents can provide important context regarding your child's medical history and any relevant diagnoses that may impact their education. Bringing copies of your child's school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and attendance records, can also be helpful. These records provide a snapshot of your child's academic performance and can highlight any areas of concern or progress. Perhaps most importantly, prepare a written list of your specific concerns and questions regarding your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. You can also write down what goals and objectives you have for your child.

Consider inviting a trusted advocate or support person to attend the ARD meeting with you. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you understand the information being presented, and ensure your voice is heard. Finally, in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided that you notify the other members of the ARD committee that you will be recording. This can be a valuable tool for documenting the meeting and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a valuable tool for parents and other ARD committee members to ensure that decisions are made thoughtfully and with adequate information. This is the "10-Day Recess Rule," which allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a temporary pause in the ARD meeting, giving everyone time to gather additional information or seek outside counsel before finalizing decisions. This recess can last up to 10 school days, providing a valuable opportunity to ensure that the IEP is truly tailored to your child's individual needs.

You might consider invoking the 10-day recess rule if, for example, new information is presented at the meeting that you need time to process. Perhaps you disagree with an assessment or recommendation made by the school district, and you want to consult with an independent expert for a second opinion. Or perhaps you simply feel overwhelmed by the amount of information being discussed and need time to gather your thoughts and formulate your questions. To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state during the ARD meeting that you would like to request a recess of up to 10 school days to gather more information or consult with an advocate. You do not need to provide a specific reason for your request, although it can be helpful to briefly explain why you feel the recess is necessary. Once you make the request, the ARD committee is obligated to grant it. The meeting will then be paused, and reconvened within 10 school days. Remember, this is your right, and it is a valuable tool for ensuring that your child's IEP is truly individualized and meets their unique needs.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the heart of your child's special education services. It's a written plan that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the services they will receive to help them succeed. Several key areas are decided during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP.

First, the IEP will include a section on "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance." This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in all areas relevant to their education, including academic subjects, social skills, and behavior. It should be based on a variety of information, including assessments, observations, and input from parents and teachers. The IEP will also include annual goals, which are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be based on your child's present levels and should address their areas of need. The IEP also specifies the services your child will receive to help them achieve their annual goals. These services may include specialized instruction, related services such as speech therapy or occupational therapy, and accommodations and modifications to the curriculum or classroom environment. The IEP document will specify the amount, frequency, and duration of these services.

Placement refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom with supports, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The ARD committee must consider the least restrictive environment (LRE) for your child, meaning they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP also includes accommodations, which are changes to the way your child learns, and modifications, which are changes to what your child learns. Accommodations might include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. Modifications might include simplifying assignments or reducing the number of questions on a test. It is vital to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home to review it carefully and to seek advice from others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD committee from time to time. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your voice is important. If you disagree with the team's decisions, it's crucial to understand your rights and the options available to you.

You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you disagree with the ARD committee's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can refuse to give your consent, and the school district cannot proceed with providing those services. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with its contents. Signing the IEP indicates that you attended the meeting, not necessarily that you agree with everything in the document. If you disagree with the IEP, you can write "parent disagrees" on the document before signing it. This indicates that you do not fully support the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you request an IEE, the school district must either pay for the evaluation or initiate a due process hearing to show that their evaluation was appropriate. Requesting an IEE can provide valuable information and a fresh perspective on your child's needs.

Escalation Options

If you have tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions and other means, but you are still unable to reach a satisfactory resolution, you have several formal options available to you. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and in the Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process provides guidance on these rights. It is important to pursue these options in the proper order.

The first formal option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. A TEA complaint is most appropriate for addressing procedural violations of IDEA or state special education laws. The second option is mediation, which is also a free service offered through TEA. Mediation involves a neutral third party who helps you and the school district work together to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is a voluntary process, and both you and the school district must agree to participate. The third option is to request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a binding decision. Due process hearings can be complex and may require the assistance of an attorney. Finally, you also have the option to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. These escalation options are in place to ensure your child receives a Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE).

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
