What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Sherman ISD! As a parent of a child with a disability, you are a critical member of the team that designs and implements your child's educational program. This team meets formally in a meeting called an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This might sound complicated, but it really just refers to the decisions made at the meeting. The "Admission" part refers to initially determining eligibility for special education services. The "Review" part refers to the regular meetings that take place to monitor progress and update your child's plan. The "Dismissal" part refers to ending special education services when your child no longer needs them.

The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services they will receive to help them succeed in school. These meetings are required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which outlines the rights of students with disabilities in Texas public schools. The ARD committee, of which you are a vital member, works together to make decisions about your child's education.

The ARD committee includes several key players. You, as the parent, are the most important member. You know your child best and your input is essential. Other members typically include a school administrator, at least one of your child's general education teachers, at least one of your child's special education teachers, an assessment personnel who can interpret evaluation results (like a diagnostician or educational psychologist), and sometimes related service providers such as a speech therapist, occupational therapist, or counselor. Depending on your child's needs and age (starting at age 14), your child may also be invited to attend and participate in the meeting. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited by you or the school district. It's important to remember that everyone at the table is there to work together to support your child's success.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Texas law ensures that you are given adequate time to prepare for your child's ARD meeting. Sherman ISD is required to provide you with written notice of the ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This "5-day notice" is designed to give you sufficient time to review the agenda, gather your thoughts, and prepare any questions or information you want to share with the ARD committee.

This written notice must include several important pieces of information. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting - whether it's for initial eligibility, annual review, or something else. Second, it must tell you the date, time, and location of the meeting. Third, it must list the names and titles of the individuals who will be attending the meeting, to the extent that they are known. Finally, the notice must inform you of your right to invite other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child to attend the ARD meeting.

It's important to understand that the 5-day notice requirement is there to protect your rights as a parent. If you feel that you need more time to prepare for the meeting, don't hesitate to ask for a postponement. However, you can also waive your right to the 5-day notice if you wish to hold the meeting sooner. For example, if an urgent situation arises or if you and the school district are both available and agreeable to meeting earlier, you can sign a written waiver to proceed with the meeting. The waiver demonstrates that you knowingly and willingly agree to forgo the 5-day notice period. Remember, the ultimate goal is to schedule the ARD meeting at a time and in a manner that best supports your active participation.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming to an ARD meeting prepared can make a significant difference in the outcome. You are your child's biggest advocate, and your input is invaluable. To ensure that your voice is heard and that the ARD committee has a complete picture of your child's needs, consider bringing the following items to the meeting:

First, bring any prior evaluations or assessments of your child. This includes evaluations conducted by the school district, as well as independent evaluations you may have obtained from private professionals. These evaluations provide valuable data about your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they need support. Sharing these documents will help the ARD committee develop a comprehensive understanding of your child's educational needs. Second, bring any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other professionals who work with your child. Medical documentation can provide important context for your child's learning challenges and help the ARD committee determine appropriate supports and services.

Third, gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and work samples. These documents can provide evidence of your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom. Reviewing these records beforehand can help you identify areas where your child is struggling and areas where they are succeeding. Fourth, prepare a written list of your concerns and questions. Writing down your concerns beforehand will ensure that you don't forget anything important during the meeting. It can also help you stay focused and organized when discussing your child's needs with the ARD committee. Be specific and provide examples whenever possible. For example, instead of saying "My child is struggling in math," you could say "My child is struggling with multiplication and division problems, and has difficulty completing math homework independently."

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person to the meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns. Having someone else present can be especially helpful if you feel overwhelmed or intimidated by the ARD process. Finally, under Texas law, you have the right to audio record the ARD meeting, provided you give the school district advance notice of your intent to do so. Recording the meeting can be helpful for your own reference and can serve as a record of the decisions that were made. It is highly recommended that you provide the school district with written notice prior to the meeting. Using a voice recorder allows you to focus on the conversation at hand without needing to take detailed notes.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Sometimes, ARD meetings can be overwhelming, and you might feel like you need more time to process information or gather additional information before making important decisions about your child's education. Fortunately, Texas law provides a "recess" option, which allows any member of the ARD committee, including you, to request a temporary pause in the ARD meeting. This pause can last up to 10 school days.

The purpose of the 10-day recess is to give the ARD committee, or individual members, time to gather more information, consult with experts, or simply reflect on the information that has been presented. For example, you might request a recess to consult with an advocate, review evaluation reports more thoroughly, or observe your child in the classroom. The school district might request a recess to conduct additional assessments or gather input from other professionals.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request during the ARD meeting. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation, but it's helpful to be clear about what you hope to accomplish during the recess. For example, you could say, "I would like to request a 10-day recess to allow me time to review the proposed IEP with my advocate." The ARD committee must grant your request. The meeting will then be adjourned and reconvened within 10 school days. During the recess, it is important to use the time wisely to gather the information you need and prepare for the reconvened meeting.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program, or IEP, is the centerpiece of the ARD process. It is a written document that outlines your child's unique educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports they will receive to help them succeed. The IEP is developed collaboratively by the ARD committee, with your input being essential.

Several key elements are decided at the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP. First, the ARD committee will discuss your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths, weaknesses, and how their disability affects their involvement and progress in the general education curriculum. It includes information from evaluations, teacher observations, and your input as a parent.

Second, the ARD committee will develop annual goals for your child. These goals are measurable statements that describe what your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be ambitious but realistic and should be aligned with your child's present levels of performance. Third, the ARD committee will determine the specific special education and related services that your child will receive. This may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or other supports. The IEP will specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

Fourth, the ARD committee will determine your child's educational placement. This refers to the setting in which your child will receive their education. Placement options range from the general education classroom with supports to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or a self-contained classroom. The goal is to place your child in the least restrictive environment where they can make meaningful progress. Fifth, the IEP will outline any accommodations or modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as extra time on tests or preferential seating. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as modified assignments or a reduced workload.

It's crucial to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek advice from others before making a decision. Signing the IEP indicates that you agree with the plan, but you can also partially agree and disagree with specific parts. This allows you to implement the portions of the IEP you support while pursuing resolution on the areas of disagreement.

When You Disagree With the Team

Disagreements can sometimes arise during the ARD process, and it's important to know your rights if you find yourself in this situation. As a parent, you have the right to disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you disagree with the evaluation results or the proposed IEP, you do not have to sign the document. Instead, you can write "Parent Disagrees" on the signature line. This indicates that you do not agree with the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action.

One important right you have is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation, or IEE, at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you can request an IEE to obtain a second opinion. The school district must either agree to pay for the IEE or demonstrate through a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate. While the district may have specific evaluators they prefer to use, the evaluator must meet specific professional requirements.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, you have several formal options available to you. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, which is part of the Legal Framework for Special Education in Texas.

The first option is to file a formal State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process and TEA is required to resolve the complaint within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law. The second option is Mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the school district meet with a trained, impartial mediator to try to resolve your disagreements. Mediation is also provided free of charge through TEA. The mediator helps facilitate communication and explore possible solutions. Mediation is often a helpful way to find common ground and avoid a more adversarial process.

The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. A Due Process Hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's educational program. This is a more formal and adversarial process than mediation. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a Due Process Hearing. Due Process Hearings can be expensive and time-consuming, but they may be necessary if you are unable to resolve your disagreements through other means.

Finally, if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates allegations of discrimination based on disability, race, color, national origin, sex, or age. Filing a complaint with OCR is a separate process from the other dispute resolution options available under IDEA. Understanding your rights and options is essential to ensuring that your child receives the free and appropriate public education (FAPE) to which they are entitled.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
