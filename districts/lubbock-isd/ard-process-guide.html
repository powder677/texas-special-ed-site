What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is essentially a meeting of experts, all focused on creating the best possible educational plan for your child. These meetings are required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code Section 29. These laws ensure that children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs. Think of the ARD meeting as the central hub for developing and maintaining your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a written document that outlines everything from your child's current academic performance to the specific supports and services the school district will provide.

The ARD meeting is attended by a team of individuals who all play a critical role. First and foremost, you, the parent, are a crucial member of the team. Your input and perspectives are invaluable, as you know your child best. Other essential members typically include a school administrator (or their designee), one or more of your child's teachers, an assessment personnel member who can explain the implications of evaluation results, and, as appropriate, your child. Other specialists may also attend, depending on your child's needs. These could include a speech therapist, occupational therapist, physical therapist, counselor, or a diagnostician. The purpose of the meeting is to collaboratively develop, review, and revise your child's IEP. This includes determining eligibility for special education services, developing appropriate goals and objectives, and deciding on the right educational placement for your child. The ARD committee must consider your child's strengths, needs, and parental concerns. Remember, this is a collaborative process, and your voice is essential in ensuring your child receives the support they need to succeed.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Lubbock ISD, like all school districts, is required to provide you with written notice at least five calendar days before an ARD meeting takes place. This is more than just a courtesy; it's a legal right designed to give you adequate time to prepare for the meeting. The purpose of this notice is to ensure that you are fully informed about the meeting's purpose, who will be attending, and what topics will be discussed. The notice must include the date, time, and location of the meeting. Critically, it must also clearly state the purpose of the meeting; for example, is it an initial evaluation, an annual review, or a manifestation determination? The notice should also include a list of individuals who are expected to attend the meeting. This allows you to anticipate who will be at the table and consider whether you need to bring additional support or information.

The 5-day notice is designed to give you time to review any relevant documents, gather your thoughts, and arrange for childcare or other supports you may need to attend the meeting without distractions. However, there may be times when you want to hold the ARD meeting sooner than five days. For example, if there is an urgent situation or concern about your child's well-being, you can waive your right to the 5-day notice. This waiver must be in writing, indicating that you understand your right to the notice but voluntarily choose to proceed with the meeting sooner. The district cannot pressure you into waiving your right; it must be a voluntary decision on your part. Remember that you always have the right to take the full five days to prepare if you prefer.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparation is key to a successful ARD meeting. Bringing relevant documents and information can help you advocate effectively for your child's needs. Start by gathering any prior evaluations or assessments that have been conducted, whether by the school district or independent professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, speech therapy reports, occupational therapy assessments, and any other relevant data. Doctor's letters or medical reports can provide valuable insight into your child's medical conditions or disabilities and how they impact their learning. Any previous school records, including report cards, progress reports, and attendance records, can help paint a comprehensive picture of your child's academic history.

It is also highly recommended that you create a written list of your concerns and priorities for your child's education. This list can serve as a roadmap for the meeting, ensuring that you address all of your key concerns. Think about what you want your child to achieve, what challenges they are facing, and what supports you believe they need to succeed. Don't hesitate to be specific and detailed in your list. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or educational advocate who can provide emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns. In Texas, it is legal to record ARD meetings, but you must provide the school district with notice that you intend to do so. A recording can be helpful for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that everyone is on the same page. Remember to organize all your documents and information in a clear and accessible way so you can easily refer to them during the meeting.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a powerful tool available to all members of the ARD committee, including you, the parent. This rule allows for a "pause" in the ARD process to gather additional information or consult with experts before making final decisions about your child's IEP. Any member of the ARD committee, not just the parents, can request a recess if they feel they need more time or information to make informed decisions.

For example, perhaps you are presented with new assessment data during the ARD meeting that you need time to review and understand. Or, you might want to consult with an independent educational expert or advocate to get their opinion on the proposed IEP. Maybe you feel overwhelmed by the information presented and need time to process it all. In any of these situations, you can invoke the 10-day recess rule. To do so, simply state that you would like to recess the meeting for up to 10 school days. The ARD committee must grant your request. The recess allows you to gather additional information, consult with experts, and prepare for the next meeting. It is crucial to remember that the 10-day period refers to school days, not calendar days, so weekends and holidays are not included in the calculation. When the ARD meeting reconvenes, you will have the opportunity to share your additional information and continue the discussion to develop an IEP that truly meets your child's needs.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the written result of the ARD meeting. It outlines all of the important decisions made about your child's special education. Understanding the different sections of the IEP is essential for advocating for your child's needs. The IEP begins with a section on "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance," often abbreviated as "Present Levels." This section describes your child's current academic and functional abilities, based on assessments, observations, and parent input. It details your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they need support.

Next, the IEP includes annual goals, which are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be based on the present levels and should address your child's individual needs. The IEP will also outline the specific special education services that your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling), and supplementary aids and services. This section will specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services. The IEP will also describe your child's educational placement, which is the setting where they will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom with supports, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. Accommodations are changes to the learning environment or instructional methods that help your child access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning. Examples include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and assistive technology. Finally, remember that you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully and consult with others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that you have rights, even if you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. One of the most important rights is the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the IEP or the proposed placement, you can refuse to sign the consent form, which would prevent the district from placing your child in special education services. Additionally, you are never obligated to sign the IEP document on the day of the meeting. Take it home, review it carefully, and consult with others if needed.

Even if you agree with some parts of the IEP, you can still disagree with others. In this case, you have the right to write "Parent Disagrees" on the IEP document next to the specific sections with which you disagree. This documents your dissent and allows you to pursue further action. You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide valuable information to help resolve disagreements. Furthermore, you are not required to implement anything in the IEP that you disagree with.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, there are formal escalation options available to you. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically governed by Legal Framework: IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. This is a good option for addressing procedural violations of IDEA or Texas special education law.

Another option is mediation, which is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also offered free through TEA. If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer to pursue a more formal legal route, you can request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the dispute. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation if you choose this option. Finally, you can file a complaint with the federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. Each of these options has specific timelines and procedures that must be followed, so it is important to familiarize yourself with the relevant laws and regulations. Remember, you are your child's best advocate, and you have the right to pursue these options if you believe it is necessary to ensure that your child receives a free and appropriate public education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
