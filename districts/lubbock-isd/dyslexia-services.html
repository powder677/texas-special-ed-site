Dyslexia Services in Lubbock ISD

Dyslexia Services in Lubbock ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services and support available for students in Lubbock Independent School District (Lubbock ISD), based on Texas state law and best practices. This information is for informational purposes only and should not be considered legal advice. Please consult with Lubbock ISD staff or legal counsel for specific guidance.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024, is the primary resource for understanding dyslexia and related disorders in Texas schools. Lubbock ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in this handbook for identification, assessment, and instruction of students with dyslexia. This handbook offers guidance on best practices, legal requirements, and resources for educators and parents.

Key updates and information from the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a clear definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction.
  • Response to Intervention (RtI): RtI is emphasized as a crucial component of identifying and supporting students with reading difficulties, including those who may have dyslexia. The handbook outlines tiers of support and the types of interventions that should be implemented at each tier.
  • Screening Requirements: The handbook details the requirements for universal screening of students for reading difficulties in kindergarten and first grade. It also provides guidance on screening for students in higher grades who are suspected of having dyslexia.
  • Evaluation Procedures: The handbook outlines the comprehensive evaluation process for determining if a student has dyslexia, including the types of assessments that should be used and the qualifications of the professionals who should conduct the evaluations.
  • Instructional Requirements: The handbook describes the characteristics of effective dyslexia intervention programs, emphasizing structured literacy approaches. It provides guidance on the components of structured literacy instruction, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and reading comprehension.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook stresses the importance of parent involvement in the identification, evaluation, and instruction of students with dyslexia. It outlines the rights and responsibilities of parents in the special education process.
  • Technology: The handbook includes information on assistive technology that can support students with dyslexia, such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and graphic organizers.
  • Legal Framework: The handbook references relevant state and federal laws and regulations related to dyslexia, including the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

Parents and educators in Lubbock ISD are encouraged to review the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 to gain a comprehensive understanding of dyslexia and the services available to students.

Mandatory Screening

Lubbock ISD follows the state mandates regarding dyslexia screening. Specifically:

  • Kindergarten and First Grade: All students in Kindergarten and First Grade receive universal screening for reading difficulties, as required by Texas law. This screening typically focuses on key foundational skills, such as phonological awareness, letter-sound correspondence, and rapid automatized naming (RAN). The specific screening tools used by Lubbock ISD are determined by the district and are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.
  • Other Grades: Students in grades 2 and above who exhibit characteristics of dyslexia or who are struggling with reading may be screened upon referral from a teacher, parent, or other school staff member. The screening process for older students may include more comprehensive assessments of reading, spelling, and writing skills.
  • Screening Process: The screening process generally involves administering standardized assessments to identify students who are at risk for reading difficulties. The results of the screening are used to determine whether a student needs further evaluation or intervention.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified of the results of the screening and any recommendations for further action.

If a screening indicates that a student may be at risk for dyslexia, Lubbock ISD will take appropriate steps to provide support and intervention, which may include referral for a full evaluation.

Structured Literacy Programs

Lubbock ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia or at risk for reading difficulties. Structured literacy is an approach to reading instruction that is explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multisensory. It focuses on the foundational skills necessary for reading success, including:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to hear and manipulate the individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words.
  • Phonics: The relationship between letters and sounds, and how to use this knowledge to decode words.
  • Fluency: The ability to read accurately, quickly, and with expression.
  • Vocabulary: The knowledge of word meanings and how to use words effectively.
  • Reading Comprehension: The ability to understand and interpret what is read.

Specific programs used in Lubbock ISD may vary by campus and grade level. Contact your child's school for detailed information regarding the specific structured literacy programs implemented. Examples of structured literacy programs that may be used include (but are not limited to):

  • Reading by Design: A program focused on structured literacy principles.
  • Orton-Gillingham based programs: These are methodologies that are applied using specific program material.

These programs typically involve intensive, individualized or small-group instruction delivered by trained professionals. The goal of structured literacy instruction is to provide students with the skills and strategies they need to become proficient readers.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP), depending on the severity of their disability and the types of services they require.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: The 504 plan focuses on providing accommodations and modifications to the general education environment to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to education.
  • Services: 504 plans may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Funding: 504 plans are not federally funded.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that falls under one of the categories defined by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), such as specific learning disability (including dyslexia), and the disability adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: The IEP focuses on providing specialized instruction and related services to meet the unique needs of the student.
  • Services: IEPs may include specialized instruction in reading, writing, or math, as well as related services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling. They also include accommodations and modifications.
  • Funding: IEPs are partially federally funded.

Key Differences:

  • Severity of Disability: Students with more significant reading difficulties who require intensive, specialized instruction are more likely to be eligible for an IEP. Students with less severe difficulties who primarily need accommodations to access the general education curriculum may be eligible for a 504 plan.
  • Specialized Instruction: IEPs provide for specialized instruction, while 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations.
  • Legal Protections: IEPs provide more extensive legal protections for students with disabilities than 504 plans.

The decision of whether a student qualifies for a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and parents. The team will consider the student's individual needs and the impact of their disability on their educational performance.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the way a student learns or is assessed that do not change the content of what is being taught. They are designed to provide students with equal access to education. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia in Lubbock ISD may include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing students more time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing students in a location where they can focus and minimize distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing students with tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and graphic organizers.
  • Audiobooks: Allowing students to listen to audio recordings of textbooks and other reading materials.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments to meet the student's needs.
  • Notes Provided: Providing students with copies of notes from lectures or presentations.
  • Oral Testing: Allowing students to answer test questions orally instead of in writing.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing students to use a calculator for math assignments and tests.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing students to take short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids such as graphic organizers, pictures, and diagrams to support learning.
  • Dictation Software: Allowing students to use dictation software to complete writing assignments.
  • Highlighted Text: Providing students with highlighted text to help them focus on key information.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing students with a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Multi-sensory Instruction: Providing instruction that engages multiple senses (e.g., visual, auditory, kinesthetic).

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will depend on their individual needs. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, in the case of an IEP, or the 504 committee will determine the appropriate accommodations for each student.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from Lubbock ISD. Here are the steps to take:

  1. Contact your child's teacher: Discuss your concerns with your child's teacher and provide any relevant information, such as observations or previous assessments.
  2. Contact the school principal or counselor: If you are not satisfied with the initial response from the teacher, or if you have additional concerns, contact the school principal or counselor.
  3. Submit a written request for evaluation: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a description of your concerns about their reading skills. It is advisable to keep a copy of this request for your records.
  4. Evaluation Process: Lubbock ISD will conduct an evaluation to determine if your child has dyslexia and is eligible for special education services. The evaluation may include assessments of reading, spelling, writing, phonological awareness, and other related skills.
  5. ARD Meeting: If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia and is eligible for special education services, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting will be held to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The ARD committee includes parents, teachers, administrators, and other relevant professionals.
  6. 504 Plan Consideration: If the evaluation shows that your child has a disability affecting their ability to access education but does not require special education, a 504 plan may be considered. A 504 meeting, similar to the ARD, will determine eligibility and necessary accommodations.

Lubbock ISD is committed to providing appropriate support and services to students with dyslexia. If you have any questions or concerns, please contact your child's school or the Lubbock ISD Special Education Department.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
