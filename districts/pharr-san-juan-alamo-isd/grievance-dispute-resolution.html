Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Discipline Rights & Grievances in Pharr-San Juan-Alamo ISD - URGENT GUIDE

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Pharr-San Juan-Alamo ISD

If your child is facing disciplinary action in PSJA ISD, especially suspension or potential expulsion, ACT IMMEDIATELY. Time is of the essence. This guide provides a crucial overview of your rights and the steps you need to take. Do not delay in seeking legal counsel or advocating for your child. This guide is NOT a substitute for legal advice.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Understand Your Child's Rights

PSJA ISD, like all Texas school districts, operates under specific rules regarding student suspensions. The "10-day suspension rule" is a critical aspect of this, particularly for students with disabilities and those suspected of having disabilities.

What is it? A student can be suspended for up to 3 school days for certain disciplinary infractions determined by the school principal or their designee. A student can be suspended for up to 10 school days (in-school or out-of-school suspension) without triggering a formal "Change of Placement," which has significant implications under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Why is this important? If a student is suspended for *more than* 10 cumulative school days in a school year (either in a single incident or through a series of shorter suspensions) AND the behavior leading to the suspension is substantially similar, it could be considered a "Change of Placement." A Change of Placement triggers specific procedural safeguards under IDEA. PSJA ISD has to provide an educational program and follow specific procedures.

What should you do if your child is facing suspension?

  • Document everything. Keep detailed records of all communication with the school, including dates, times, names of individuals involved, and the specific reasons given for the suspension. Save all emails, letters, and text messages.
  • Request a meeting with the principal (or designee) and your child's teacher(s). Understand the specific allegations against your child. Ask for concrete evidence supporting the suspension.
  • Inquire about the school's discipline policy. Ensure the school is following its own published disciplinary procedures. Is the punishment proportionate to the offense?
  • If your child has an IEP or 504 plan, immediately inform the school of this. The school MUST consider whether the behavior is related to the disability. Request an immediate IEP team or 504 team meeting.
  • If you suspect your child has a disability but has not been evaluated, request a full and individual evaluation in writing immediately. This can potentially trigger protections under IDEA.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

The Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is a CRITICAL process under IDEA for students with IEPs or 504 plans who are facing disciplinary action that constitutes a "Change of Placement" (e.g., suspension exceeding 10 days in a school year for similar behaviors). The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's behavior was caused by or directly and substantially related to the student's disability, or if the behavior was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

When is an MDR required? An MDR *must* be conducted if a student with a disability is facing a suspension that would result in a Change of Placement.

Who participates in an MDR? The IEP team (including you, the parent), the school principal or designee, and relevant school personnel (e.g., teachers, counselors).

What happens during an MDR?

  • The IEP team reviews all relevant information in the student's file, including the IEP, teacher observations, any relevant medical or psychological evaluations, and information provided by the parents.
  • The team must determine if the conduct in question was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability.
  • The team must also determine if the conduct was the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

What are the possible outcomes of an MDR?

  • Manifestation: If the team determines the behavior *was* a manifestation of the disability OR the result of the school's failure to implement the IEP, the student cannot be disciplined in the same manner as a student without a disability. The IEP team must then conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA) and implement or revise a behavior intervention plan (BIP), unless the school had already conducted an FBA and BIP prior to the behavior that resulted in the suspension. The student must also be returned to their original placement unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the modification of the BIP.
  • No Manifestation: If the team determines the behavior *was not* a manifestation of the disability AND the school properly implemented the IEP, the student can be disciplined in the same manner as students without disabilities, *except* that the school must still provide educational services to the student during the period of suspension to allow the student to continue to participate in the general education curriculum and to progress toward meeting the goals set out in the IEP.

WARNING: Schools sometimes try to rush through MDRs or downplay the role of the disability. Be prepared to advocate fiercely for your child. Bring any independent evaluations or medical records you have. If you disagree with the MDR outcome, you have the right to challenge it through a Due Process hearing.

Change of Placement: Know Your Rights Under IDEA

As mentioned above, a "Change of Placement" occurs when a student with a disability is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year for similar behaviors. This triggers significant protections under IDEA.

What constitutes a Change of Placement?

  • Suspension exceeding 10 school days in a school year for similar behaviors (as determined by the school district).
  • Expulsion.
  • Any change in the student's educational setting (e.g., moving a student from general education to a self-contained classroom) that is not agreed upon by the IEP team.

What are your rights when a Change of Placement is proposed?

  • Right to Prior Written Notice: The school MUST provide you with written notice of the proposed change of placement, including the reasons for the change.
  • Right to Participate in the IEP Team Meeting: You have the right to be actively involved in the IEP team meeting where the Change of Placement is discussed.
  • Right to a Manifestation Determination Review (if the Change of Placement is due to disciplinary reasons): As outlined above, the school must conduct an MDR to determine if the behavior is related to the student's disability.
  • Right to Challenge the Change of Placement: If you disagree with the proposed Change of Placement, you have the right to challenge it through mediation or a Due Process hearing.
  • Right to "Stay Put": While you are challenging a proposed Change of Placement, the student generally has the right to "stay put" in their current educational setting until the dispute is resolved. This is known as the "stay-put" provision. However, there are exceptions to this rule, particularly if the student's behavior poses a danger to themselves or others.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in PSJA ISD

If you believe PSJA ISD has violated your child's rights, you can file a grievance. The PSJA ISD website should have its grievance policy, usually under the "School Board" or "Policies" section. Seek it out and become familiar with the timelines and procedures. Typically, a grievance begins at Level 1.

What is a Level 1 Grievance? This is the initial step in the grievance process. It involves filing a formal written complaint with the principal or administrator most directly involved in the issue.

How to file a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the grievance form. PSJA ISD likely has a specific form for filing grievances. Find it on the district website or request it from the school.
  2. Complete the form thoroughly. Clearly and concisely describe the incident or issue that led to the grievance. Include dates, times, names of individuals involved, and specific details. Attach any supporting documentation (e.g., emails, letters, witness statements).
  3. State the specific relief you are seeking. What do you want the school to do to resolve the issue? Be specific.
  4. Submit the grievance form to the appropriate administrator. Usually, this is the principal of the school.
  5. Keep a copy of the grievance form and all supporting documents for your records.

What happens after you file a Level 1 Grievance? The administrator is typically required to investigate the grievance and provide a written response within a specific timeframe (check the PSJA ISD policy - it's usually within 10 school days). If you are not satisfied with the response at Level 1, you can appeal to the next level (usually the superintendent or a designee).

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines are crucial. Missed deadlines can invalidate your grievance. Be aware of the deadlines at each level of the grievance process and adhere to them strictly.
  • Document everything. Keep records of all communication with the school regarding the grievance.
  • Seek legal advice. If the issue is serious or complex, consider consulting with an attorney experienced in education law.

TEA State Complaint: An Alternative Avenue

A TEA (Texas Education Agency) State Complaint is a formal complaint filed with the TEA alleging that a school district has violated a federal or state law or regulation related to special education. This is a separate process from the district's grievance procedure.

When to file a TEA State Complaint:

  • When you believe PSJA ISD has violated IDEA or other special education laws.
  • When the district's internal grievance process has failed to resolve the issue.
  • When you need an independent investigation of the district's actions.

How to file a TEA State Complaint:

  • Obtain the complaint form. You can find the complaint form on the TEA website (search for "TEA Special Education Complaint").
  • Complete the form thoroughly. Clearly and concisely describe the alleged violation. Include dates, times, names of individuals involved, and specific details.
  • Attach supporting documentation. Include any relevant documents (e.g., IEPs, evaluations, correspondence).
  • Submit the complaint form to the TEA. The instructions for submitting the complaint are on the form.

What happens after you file a TEA State Complaint? The TEA will investigate the complaint and determine if a violation occurred. If the TEA finds that a violation occurred, it can order the district to take corrective action.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Legal Safeguard

Due Process is a formal legal proceeding available to parents of students with disabilities under IDEA. It is the most serious and formal way to challenge a school district's decisions regarding your child's special education.

When to request a Due Process hearing:

  • When you disagree with the school district about any aspect of your child's IEP, evaluation, placement, or the provision of special education services.
  • When you believe the school district has violated IDEA and has not resolved the issue through other means (e.g., mediation, TEA State Complaint).
  • When you are challenging a Manifestation Determination Review outcome.

How to request a Due Process hearing:

  • You must file a written complaint with the TEA and provide a copy to the school district. The complaint must include specific information, such as the child's name and address, a description of the problem, and the proposed resolution.

What happens in a Due Process hearing?

  • A hearing officer (an impartial third party) will conduct the hearing.
  • You and the school district will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine witnesses.
  • The hearing officer will issue a written decision.

Important Considerations:

  • Due Process hearings are complex and can be expensive. You should strongly consider consulting with an attorney experienced in special education law before requesting a Due Process hearing.
  • There are strict timelines for filing a Due Process complaint. Be aware of the deadlines and adhere to them strictly.
  • You have the right to legal representation at the hearing.
  • The decision of the hearing officer is binding on both you and the school district, unless it is appealed to a court of law.

This guide is intended to provide a general overview of your rights. It is NOT a substitute for legal advice. If your child is facing disciplinary action in PSJA ISD, you should consult with an attorney or advocate experienced in special education law as soon as possible. Time is of the essence. Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
