Dyslexia Services in Pharr-San Juan-Alamo ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services offered within the Pharr-San Juan-Alamo (PSJA) Independent School District (ISD) in accordance with Texas state law. This information is intended for parents, educators, and anyone interested in understanding how PSJA ISD supports students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

All dyslexia services within Texas public schools, including those in PSJA ISD, are guided by the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, which is updated periodically by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). The latest version, the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook, is the controlling document for identification, evaluation, and instructional practices for students with dyslexia. It is essential for parents and educators to be familiar with this handbook.

Key Updates and Information in the 2024 Handbook:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The Texas Dyslexia Handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Response to Intervention (RTI): The handbook outlines the role of Response to Intervention (RTI) in identifying students who may be at risk for dyslexia. RTI is a multi-tiered system of support designed to provide increasingly intensive interventions to students who are struggling academically.
  • Screening Procedures: The handbook details the mandatory screening procedures for dyslexia in Texas public schools (explained further below).
  • Evaluation Procedures: The Texas Dyslexia Handbook describes the evaluation process for determining if a student has dyslexia. This process involves a comprehensive assessment of various reading and language skills.
  • Instructional Programs: The handbook emphasizes the importance of providing students with dyslexia with explicit, systematic, and multisensory structured literacy instruction (explained further below).
  • Accommodations and Modifications: The handbook provides guidance on appropriate accommodations and modifications that can be provided to students with dyslexia (explained further below).
  • Legal Framework: The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is aligned with state and federal laws, including the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.

Where to Find the Handbook:

The most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook is available on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Parents and educators are strongly encouraged to download and review this document.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates that all students in kindergarten and first grade be screened for dyslexia. PSJA ISD adheres to these requirements.

Screening Process:

  • Kindergarten Screening: Kindergarten students are typically screened at the beginning and middle of the school year.
  • First Grade Screening: First-grade students are screened at the beginning of the school year.
  • Screening Tools: PSJA ISD utilizes TEA-approved screening instruments to assess students' skills in areas such as phonological awareness, phonological memory, rapid automatized naming, and letter identification. These screenings are designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia.
  • Parent Notification: Parents will be notified if their child's screening results indicate a potential risk for dyslexia. This notification will include information about the screening results and recommendations for further action.
  • Screening in Other Grades: While mandatory screening is only required for kindergarten and first grade, students in other grades can be screened if concerns arise about their reading skills. Parents or teachers can request a screening if they observe characteristics of dyslexia.

It is important to understand that a screening is not a diagnosis of dyslexia. A screening only identifies students who may be at risk and require further evaluation.

Structured Literacy Programs

Students who are identified with dyslexia in PSJA ISD receive specialized instruction using structured literacy programs. These programs are evidence-based and aligned with the principles of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Explicit Instruction: Skills are taught directly and systematically, with clear explanations and modeling.
  • Systematic and Cumulative: Instruction progresses in a logical sequence, building on previously learned skills.
  • Multisensory: Instruction incorporates visual, auditory, and kinesthetic-tactile modalities to enhance learning.
  • Phonological Awareness: Students develop an understanding of the sound structure of language.
  • Phonics: Students learn the relationships between letters and sounds.
  • Spelling: Spelling instruction is integrated with reading instruction.
  • Fluency: Students develop the ability to read accurately, quickly, and with expression.
  • Vocabulary: Students expand their vocabulary knowledge.
  • Reading Comprehension: Students learn strategies to understand and interpret text.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive services under either Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the individual student's needs.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: The focus of a 504 plan is to provide accommodations and modifications to ensure that the student has equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services under a 504 plan may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, and assistive technology.
  • Dyslexia and 504: Many students with dyslexia are served under Section 504. If a student's dyslexia substantially limits their ability to learn, they may be eligible for a 504 plan.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that is specifically listed under IDEA (such as a specific learning disability, which includes dyslexia) and that disability adversely affects their educational performance, requiring specialized instruction.
  • Focus: The focus of an IEP is to provide specialized instruction and related services to address the student's individual needs and help them make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services under an IEP may include specialized reading instruction, speech-language therapy, and occupational therapy, in addition to accommodations and modifications.
  • Dyslexia and IEP: Some students with dyslexia require more intensive intervention and support than can be provided through a 504 plan. These students may be eligible for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability.

Key Differences:

  • Scope of Services: IEPs provide a broader range of services, including specialized instruction, while 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations.
  • Evaluation Requirements: The evaluation requirements for an IEP are more extensive than those for a 504 plan.
  • Legal Protections: IEPs provide greater legal protections for students with disabilities.

The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, which includes parents, educators, and other professionals, will determine whether a student is eligible for a 504 plan or an IEP based on the student's individual needs and the results of the evaluation.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material. They do not change what the student is expected to learn. Accommodations are crucial for supporting students with dyslexia and allowing them to access the curriculum.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Allowing students more time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing students in a location in the classroom that minimizes distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks.
  • Audiobooks: Allowing students to listen to audiobooks instead of reading print.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Software that reads text aloud to students.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Software that allows students to dictate their writing.
  • Modified Assignments: Providing assignments with fewer problems or simplified instructions.
  • Alternative Assessments: Using alternative methods to assess student learning, such as oral presentations or projects.
  • Graphic Organizers: Using graphic organizers to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Highlighted Text: Highlighting key information in textbooks and other materials.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing students with notes or allowing them to record lectures.
  • Reading Guides: Providing students with reading guides to help them focus on key concepts.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Breaks: Allowing students to take breaks during long tasks.
  • Use of Manipulatives: Using hands-on materials to support learning.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids, such as charts, graphs, and pictures, to support learning.

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will be determined based on their individual needs. The ARD committee will discuss and determine the appropriate accommodations for students with IEPs. The 504 committee will determine appropriate accommodations for students with 504 plans.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading skills and suspect they may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from PSJA ISD.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact Your Child's Teacher: The first step is to contact your child's teacher to discuss your concerns. The teacher can provide information about your child's academic performance and may have already observed characteristics of dyslexia.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or Principal: If you are not satisfied with the response from your child's teacher, or if you feel that an evaluation is necessary, contact the school counselor or principal. They can provide you with the necessary forms and information about the evaluation process.
  3. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a detailed explanation of your concerns. Be specific about the reading difficulties you have observed.
  4. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, the school has a timeline to respond and initiate the evaluation process. The evaluation will be conducted by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians and speech-language pathologists. The evaluation will assess your child's reading, spelling, and language skills.
  5. ARD/504 Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, the ARD committee (for IEPs) or the 504 committee (for 504 plans) will meet to discuss the evaluation results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services or accommodations. You, as the parent, are a vital member of this committee.

Important Considerations:

  • Parent Rights: You have the right to request an evaluation at any time if you suspect that your child has a disability.
  • Timelines: Texas law requires school districts to follow specific timelines for conducting evaluations. Be sure to ask about these timelines when you submit your request.
  • Documentation: Keep copies of all documents related to the evaluation process, including your written request, evaluation reports, and ARD/504 meeting minutes.
  • Advocacy: You have the right to advocate for your child's needs throughout the evaluation and service delivery process. Consider bringing an advocate to ARD/504 meetings if you feel it would be helpful.

By following these steps, you can ensure that your child receives the support they need to succeed in school. Please contact PSJA ISD Special Education Department for any further questions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
