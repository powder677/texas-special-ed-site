What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome! If you're reading this, you're likely preparing for or attending an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting for your child in the Pharr-San Juan-Alamo ISD. The ARD meeting is a cornerstone of special education, and it's absolutely vital that you, as a parent, understand your rights and the process. The ARD meeting is essentially a meeting of experts who come together to discuss and make decisions about your child's educational program if they have been identified as needing special education services. It's called Admission, Review, and Dismissal because the team will consider whether your child needs special education services (Admission), review your child's progress and the effectiveness of their current program (Review), and determine if special education services are still needed (Dismissal). This entire process is required by both the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which outlines special education in Texas. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop an Individualized Education Program, or IEP, that is specifically tailored to meet your child's unique needs.

Who attends the ARD meeting? The ARD committee is composed of several individuals who bring different perspectives and expertise to the table. The required members include you, the parent; at least one of your child's general education teachers (if your child is participating in general education); at least one special education teacher or provider; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services and is knowledgeable about the general curriculum and the availability of resources; a person who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results (this could be one of the other members or a separate person); and, when appropriate, your child themselves. Other individuals may be invited to the meeting based on their knowledge or expertise related to your child's needs. This could include therapists, counselors, diagnosticians, or anyone else you or the school district believes can contribute to the discussion and IEP development. The presence of all these individuals ensures a well-rounded and informed approach to creating your child's educational plan. Remember, you are a critical and equal member of this team, and your input and perspectives are essential.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

One of your key rights as a parent is the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the scheduled meeting. This is a crucial requirement designed to give you ample time to prepare, gather information, and ensure you can attend. The notice is not just a simple announcement; it must contain specific information. The notice must include the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location of the meeting, a list of the individuals who will be attending, and a clear explanation of your parental rights. This comprehensive notice is designed to ensure that you are fully informed about the meeting and can actively participate in the decision-making process. This five-day notice allows you time to review any relevant documents, speak with teachers or therapists, gather your thoughts and concerns, and arrange for childcare or transportation if needed.

It is important to note that you have the option to waive this five-day notice requirement. If you and the school district agree that it is necessary to meet sooner, you can provide written consent to waive the notice period. This might be beneficial if there is an urgent issue that needs to be addressed or if everyone involved has flexible schedules. However, it is crucial to carefully consider whether waiving the notice is in your child's best interest. Do not feel pressured to waive your rights if you need the full five days to prepare. The school district should respect your decision to exercise your right to the full notice period.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but being well-prepared will empower you to advocate effectively for your child. Bringing the right information and support can make a significant difference in the outcome of the meeting. First and foremost, bring any prior evaluations or assessments related to your child's educational needs. This includes evaluations from private psychologists, therapists, or specialists. These evaluations provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and specific needs. Next, gather any relevant medical documentation, such as letters from doctors or therapists outlining diagnoses, treatment plans, or recommendations. This information can help the ARD committee understand the medical factors that may be impacting your child's learning.

It is also wise to collect your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, and any communication you've had with teachers or other school staff. These records can provide a comprehensive picture of your child's academic performance and behavior in the school setting. Critically, prepare a written list of your concerns and priorities for your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your questions and concerns are addressed. Having this written down helps ensure you don't forget anything in the moment. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns. Remember, you don't have to go through this alone. Finally, in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided you inform the other members of the ARD team that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be invaluable for reviewing the meeting later and ensuring that you accurately remember all the decisions that were made. It can also serve as a helpful record if any disputes arise later on. Be sure to announce at the start of the meeting that you are recording for your personal use.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a powerful tool that you, as a parent, can use to ensure that you have adequate time to consider the information presented at the ARD meeting and make informed decisions. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you, to request a recess of up to 10 school days before decisions are finalized. This recess provides time to gather additional information, consult with experts, or simply reflect on the proposed IEP and its implications for your child.

How do you invoke the 10-day recess rule? If at any point during the ARD meeting you feel that you need more time to consider the information or seek additional input, you can simply state that you are requesting a recess. It is helpful to be clear about the reason for your request, such as wanting to consult with an advocate or obtain a second opinion. The ARD committee must grant your request for a recess of up to 10 school days. During this recess, you can use the time to review the proposed IEP, research specific services or accommodations, speak with other professionals, or seek advice from a special education advocate. Once you have had time to gather the information you need, you can reconvene the ARD committee to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP. The 10-day recess rule is a valuable safeguard that empowers you to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the written plan that outlines the specific educational services and supports your child will receive. It is crucial to understand the different sections of the IEP and the decisions that are made during the ARD meeting that inform the document. One of the first sections of the IEP is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance," often called "Present Levels." This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic areas, as well as their functional performance, such as social skills, communication, and behavior. It is based on a variety of information, including evaluations, assessments, teacher observations, and your input as a parent. The Present Levels section serves as the foundation for developing the rest of the IEP.

Based on the Present Levels, the ARD committee will develop annual goals for your child. These goals are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They outline what your child is expected to achieve within one year. The IEP will also specify the services that will be provided to help your child reach those goals. These services may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or other supports. The ARD committee will determine the frequency, duration, and location of these services. Placement refers to where your child will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom with supports, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The goal is to place your child in the least restrictive environment (LRE) that meets their needs. Finally, the IEP will outline any accommodations that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, or assistive technology. It is very important to know that you do not have to sign the IEP document the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully and consider your decision. Do not feel pressured to sign if you are not comfortable with the plan.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD committee. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your voice matters. You have specific rights when you disagree with the team's recommendations. One of your most fundamental rights is the right to refuse consent for the initial placement of your child in special education. If you do not agree with the ARD committee's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can refuse to sign the IEP. This means that your child will not receive special education services at this time. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document even if your child is already receiving special education services, if you disagree with any aspect of the plan. Refusing to sign the IEP does not necessarily mean that the IEP cannot be implemented, but it does trigger certain procedural safeguards.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide valuable information to the ARD committee and may lead to revisions in the IEP. Finally, you always have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it. This indicates that you do not agree with all or part of the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action. It is important to clearly specify which parts of the IEP you disagree with.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all attempts to resolve disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are designed to ensure that your child's rights are protected and that their educational needs are met. First, you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate the complaint and issue a resolution within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law. This process is often the best first step in formal escalation.

The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is also free through TEA. It is a less adversarial process than a due process hearing and can often be a successful way to resolve disputes. If mediation is not successful, or if you prefer to pursue a more formal legal process, you can request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the dispute. This is similar to a court case and usually requires an attorney. Be mindful of strict deadlines in a due process hearing. Finally, you always have the option to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. Remember that your rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Texas Administrative Code 19 TAC Chapter 89. By understanding your rights and the available escalation options, you can advocate effectively for your child's educational needs and ensure that they receive the services and supports they deserve.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
