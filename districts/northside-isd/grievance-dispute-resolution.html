Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Northside ISD

WARNING: Time is of the essence when dealing with school discipline. This guide provides critical information but is NOT a substitute for legal advice. Consult with an attorney specializing in education law immediately if your child faces suspension, expulsion, or a potential Change of Placement.

This guide is written from the perspective of a concerned parent navigating the Northside ISD disciplinary system. It is designed to equip you with the knowledge to protect your child's rights and ensure they receive a fair and appropriate education.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: A Critical Threshold

Northside ISD, like all Texas school districts, operates under the Texas Education Code. Understanding the 10-day suspension rule is crucial. If your child is facing suspension for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, SIGNIFICANT rights are triggered, especially if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or a Section 504 plan. Even if the suspensions are for different incidents, they count towards that 10-day total.

What to do NOW if your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 days of suspension:

  • Keep meticulous records: Track every suspension, the reason for it, and the dates. A calendar is your friend.
  • Demand documentation: Immediately request written documentation for each suspension, including the specific violation of the Student Code of Conduct.
  • Notify the school (in writing) if your child has a suspected disability, even if undiagnosed: If you suspect your child has a disability (e.g., ADHD, learning disability, emotional disturbance) but they haven't been formally diagnosed, inform the school administration in writing. This can trigger protections under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). Send this communication via certified mail, return receipt requested, and keep a copy.
  • Contact an attorney: Do NOT wait. The earlier you involve legal counsel, the better protected your child's rights will be.

Important Note: Students with disabilities have additional protections under IDEA. A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative days can trigger a Change of Placement, requiring a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This is a critical safeguard.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a meeting held to determine if a student's behavior that led to disciplinary action (e.g., suspension, expulsion) was a manifestation of their disability. In other words, did the disability cause or contribute to the behavior?

When is an MDR required? An MDR is required when a student with an IEP or 504 plan faces a suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days in a school year, or when the school proposes a Change of Placement.

Who attends the MDR? The IEP team, relevant school personnel (including administrators and teachers), and YOU, the parent! Your participation is crucial. Bring any relevant documentation, including medical records, evaluations, and prior communication with the school.

What happens during the MDR? The team reviews the student's IEP, relevant assessment data, and any information provided by you and the school staff. They will discuss whether:

  • The behavior was caused by or had a direct and substantial relationship to the student's disability.
  • The behavior was the direct result of the school's failure to implement the student's IEP.

What are the possible outcomes of the MDR?

  • If the behavior IS a manifestation of the disability: The student MUST be returned to their current placement, unless you and the school agree otherwise. The IEP team must also conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and implement or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The school must also ensure that the IEP was properly implemented.
  • If the behavior IS NOT a manifestation of the disability: The student can be disciplined in the same manner as students without disabilities. However, the school is still required to provide educational services to ensure the student continues to participate in the general education curriculum and make progress toward their IEP goals.
  • If the school failed to implement the IEP: This is a serious violation. The behavior is considered a manifestation, and the school must immediately rectify the IEP implementation failures and develop strategies to prevent future occurrences.

CRITICAL ACTION: If you disagree with the MDR outcome, you have the right to challenge it through due process (explained below). Don't hesitate to seek legal counsel immediately.

Change of Placement: The Ultimate Intervention

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days OR when a series of removals constitutes a pattern because they total more than 10 school days in a school year, and because of factors such as:

  • The length of each removal
  • The total amount of time the student is removed
  • The proximity of the removals to one another.

A Change of Placement can involve:

  • Expulsion
  • Placement in a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP)
  • Placement in a more restrictive special education setting.

A Change of Placement triggers the MDR process. It is a serious action that can significantly impact your child's education and future. Fight for your child's right to remain in their least restrictive environment.

IMMEDIATE STEPS if the school proposes a Change of Placement:

  • Demand a written explanation: Request a detailed explanation of why the school believes a Change of Placement is necessary.
  • Request all supporting documentation: Obtain copies of all incident reports, witness statements, and other documents related to the proposed Change of Placement.
  • Prepare for the MDR: Gather all relevant information, including medical records, evaluations, and communication with the school.
  • Consult with an attorney IMMEDIATELY: A Change of Placement is a high-stakes situation. Legal representation is essential.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance: Your First Line of Defense

Northside ISD has a formal grievance process outlined in its policies. Filing a Level 1 grievance is often the first step in challenging a disciplinary action or a school's decision. Review Northside ISD's board policy on their website. Look for the grievance policy to get the most current and specific information.

When to file a Level 1 grievance: File a grievance if you believe the school has violated your child's rights, misapplied a policy, or acted unfairly in a disciplinary matter. This could include disagreements about the MDR outcome, the IEP implementation, or the appropriateness of a disciplinary action.

How to file a Level 1 grievance:

  • Obtain the grievance form: The school should provide you with the official grievance form.
  • Complete the form thoroughly: Clearly and concisely explain the basis of your grievance. Include specific dates, times, names, and details. Attach any supporting documentation (e.g., suspension notices, IEP documents, emails).
  • Submit the grievance: Submit the completed grievance form to the designated school official (usually the principal or a designated administrator) within the timeframe specified in the school's policy. Pay close attention to deadlines! Missing the deadline can result in your grievance being dismissed.
  • Keep a copy: Retain a copy of the grievance form and all supporting documentation for your records.
  • Follow up: After submitting the grievance, follow up with the school to inquire about the status of your grievance. Document all communication.

What to expect after filing a Level 1 grievance:

The school official will investigate your grievance and provide a written response within a specified timeframe (as outlined in the school's policy). If you are not satisfied with the response, you may have the option to appeal to the next level of the grievance process (Level 2), as specified in the Northside ISD board policy.

TEA State Complaint: An External Review

If you believe Northside ISD has violated state or federal law regarding special education, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a formal process that can lead to an investigation by TEA.

When to file a TEA complaint: File a TEA complaint when you believe the school has violated the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, or other state or federal laws related to special education. This could include instances of:

  • Failure to provide a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)
  • Failure to properly implement the IEP
  • Denial of due process rights
  • Discrimination based on disability

How to file a TEA complaint:

  • Obtain the TEA complaint form: The complaint form is available on the TEA website.
  • Complete the form accurately and thoroughly: Provide a detailed description of the alleged violation, including specific dates, times, names, and supporting documentation.
  • Submit the complaint: Submit the completed complaint form to TEA according to the instructions provided on the form.
  • Be aware of deadlines: TEA complaints typically must be filed within one year of the alleged violation.

What to expect after filing a TEA complaint:

TEA will investigate your complaint and determine whether a violation has occurred. If TEA finds a violation, it may order the school to take corrective action. While a TEA complaint can be helpful, it is not a substitute for legal representation. An attorney can advise you on your legal rights and options.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Legal Recourse

Due process is a formal legal process that allows you to challenge the school's decisions regarding your child's special education services. It is a powerful tool, but it is complex and requires the assistance of an attorney specializing in special education law.

When to request a due process hearing: You can request a due process hearing if you disagree with the school's decisions regarding your child's identification, evaluation, educational placement, or the provision of FAPE. This could include disagreements about the MDR outcome, the IEP, or the appropriateness of the disciplinary action.

How to request a due process hearing:

  • File a written request: You must file a written request for a due process hearing with the school district and the TEA. The request must include specific information, as outlined in the IDEA regulations.

What to expect in a due process hearing:

A due process hearing is similar to a court trial. You will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine the school's witnesses. An impartial hearing officer will preside over the hearing and issue a written decision.

Important Considerations:

  • Legal Representation is Essential: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. You should never attempt to navigate the due process system without the assistance of an experienced special education attorney.
  • Timelines are Strict: There are strict timelines for filing a due process request and for each stage of the due process process. Missing a deadline can jeopardize your case.
  • Mediation: Before proceeding to a due process hearing, you may be required to participate in mediation with the school district in an attempt to resolve the dispute.

Final Thoughts:

Navigating the Northside ISD disciplinary system can be overwhelming, especially when your child's rights are at stake. Remember to document everything, communicate in writing, and seek legal advice immediately. As a parent, you are your child's best advocate. Don't be afraid to fight for their right to a fair and appropriate education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
