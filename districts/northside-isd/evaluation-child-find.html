Requesting an Evaluation in Northside ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Northside ISD

This guide provides information for parents and legal guardians seeking a special education evaluation for their child within Northside Independent School District (NISD) in Texas. This information is based on Texas Education Agency (TEA) guidelines and federal regulations under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). This guide is intended for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Parents should seek legal counsel from a qualified attorney regarding specific situations.

How to Submit a Written Request

The process for initiating a special education evaluation begins with a formal, written request submitted to the appropriate school personnel. A verbal request is not sufficient to trigger the timelines and obligations outlined by IDEA and state law. It is crucial that the request be in writing to establish a clear record.

Who to Address the Request To: The written request should be directed to one of the following individuals at your child's school:

  • The principal
  • The special education director or coordinator
  • Your child's teacher(s)
  • The school counselor

Sending the request to multiple individuals ensures that it reaches the appropriate personnel promptly. It is highly recommended to keep a copy of the request for your records, along with proof of delivery (e.g., certified mail receipt, email delivery confirmation). In Northside ISD, it is beneficial to contact the Special Education department directly if you are unsure of who to contact at the campus level. Their contact information can be found on the NISD website.

Content of the Written Request: The written request should clearly and concisely state your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress and the reasons why you believe a special education evaluation is necessary. The letter should include the following information:

  • Your child's full name and date of birth
  • Your child's current grade level and school
  • Your contact information (address, phone number, email)
  • A clear statement that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services under IDEA
  • A description of your concerns about your child's performance, including specific examples of difficulties observed at home and/or school
  • Any interventions or supports that have already been tried and their effectiveness (or lack thereof)
  • Any relevant medical, psychological, or educational history that you believe may be pertinent to the evaluation
  • Your signature and the date

Example Request:

[Date]

To: [Principal's Name/Special Education Coordinator's Name]

[School Name]

[School Address]

Re: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Full Name]

Dear [Principal's Name/Special Education Coordinator's Name],

I am writing to formally request a full and individual initial evaluation to determine eligibility for special education services for my child, [Child's Full Name], who is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. [Child's Name] was born on [Date of Birth].

I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s [specific academic areas of concern, e.g., reading, writing, math] and [specific behavioral concerns, e.g., difficulty focusing, impulsivity]. For example, [Child's Name] struggles with [specific examples, e.g., reading comprehension, completing homework assignments, staying on task in class]. These difficulties have been observed both at home and at school.

[Optional: Describe any interventions already tried, e.g., "We have tried providing [Child's Name] with extra help at home and have also communicated with his/her teacher, but these efforts have not resulted in significant improvement."]

[Optional: Include relevant medical or educational history, e.g., "[Child's Name] was diagnosed with [Medical Condition] in [Year], which may be contributing to these difficulties."]

I believe that [Child's Name]'s difficulties warrant a comprehensive evaluation to determine if he/she is eligible for special education services under IDEA. Thank you for your prompt attention to this matter. I look forward to hearing from you soon regarding the next steps in the evaluation process.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

[Your Address]

[Your Phone Number]

[Your Email Address]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Upon receiving your written request for an initial evaluation, Northside ISD (and all Texas school districts) is bound by a strict timeline established by state and federal law. The district has a maximum of 45 school days (not calendar days) to complete the following steps:

  1. Review Existing Data (RED): The school must convene a team, which includes parents, to review existing data regarding your child. This data includes academic records, teacher observations, medical information (if provided), and any previous interventions or evaluations.
  2. Decision to Evaluate: Based on the review of existing data, the team must decide whether or not to conduct a full and individual initial evaluation. This decision must be made without unreasonable delay.
  3. Notice and Consent: If the team decides to evaluate, the school must provide you with written notice of the proposed evaluation and obtain your written consent. This notice must include a description of the evaluation procedures and the types of assessments that will be used. Your consent must be informed and voluntary.
  4. Conduct the Evaluation: Once you provide consent, the school must conduct the evaluation.
  5. Eligibility Determination: After the evaluation is complete, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, which includes you, will meet to review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under one of the thirteen disability categories outlined in IDEA.

Important Considerations Regarding the Timeline:

  • The 45-school day timeline begins the date the school receives your written request.
  • School breaks, such as summer vacation and holidays, are not counted as school days.
  • If the ARD committee determines that additional evaluations are needed, the timeline can be extended, but only with your written consent.
  • You have the right to request an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation. This IEE must meet the same standards as the school's evaluation.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and needs in all areas related to the suspected disability. The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals and must use a variety of assessment tools and strategies. The FIE must be sufficiently comprehensive to identify all of the child's special education and related services needs, whether or not commonly linked to the disability category in which the child has been identified as having a disability.

Key Components of the FIE: The specific areas covered in the FIE will depend on your child's individual needs and the suspected disability. However, a comprehensive FIE typically includes assessments in the following areas:

  • Cognitive Functioning: Assesses intellectual abilities, such as problem-solving, reasoning, and memory.
  • Academic Achievement: Assesses skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas.
  • Communication Skills: Assesses receptive and expressive language skills, articulation, fluency, and social communication.
  • Motor Skills: Assesses gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Social and Emotional Functioning: Assesses social skills, emotional regulation, and behavior.
  • Adaptive Behavior: Assesses skills needed for daily living, such as self-care, communication, and social interaction.
  • Observation: Observations of the child in various settings, such as the classroom and playground.
  • Parent and Teacher Input: Information gathered from parents and teachers regarding the child's strengths, needs, and performance.

Texas-Specific Requirements: In addition to the federal requirements under IDEA, Texas law requires that the FIE include specific assessments depending on the suspected disability. For example, if a learning disability is suspected, the FIE must include assessments of phonological processing, reading fluency, and written expression.

Importance of a Comprehensive FIE: A thorough and comprehensive FIE is essential for determining eligibility for special education services and for developing an individualized education program (IEP) that meets your child's unique needs. If you believe that the FIE is incomplete or inadequate, you have the right to request additional evaluations or an independent educational evaluation (IEE).

Child Find Obligations

Northside ISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under IDEA to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within its boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This is known as "Child Find." This obligation extends to children who are suspected of having a disability, even if they are not currently receiving special education services.

How Child Find Works: NISD utilizes various methods to fulfill its Child Find obligations, including:

  • Screening Activities: Conducting screenings of students to identify those who may be at risk for academic or developmental delays.
  • Referral Process: Establishing a clear referral process for parents, teachers, and other professionals to refer children who are suspected of having a disability.
  • Public Awareness Campaigns: Conducting public awareness campaigns to inform parents and the community about special education services and the Child Find process.
  • Collaboration with Community Agencies: Collaborating with community agencies, such as healthcare providers and social service organizations, to identify children who may need special education services.

Your Role in Child Find: As a parent, you play a crucial role in the Child Find process. If you have concerns about your child's development or academic performance, you should contact the school to discuss your concerns and request an evaluation. NISD is obligated to respond to your request and initiate the evaluation process if there is reason to suspect that your child has a disability.

What If They Refuse

If Northside ISD refuses to conduct an initial evaluation after receiving your written request, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for the refusal. This explanation must be provided within a reasonable timeframe.

Your Options if the School Refuses: If you disagree with the school's decision not to evaluate, you have several options:

  • Request a Meeting: Request a meeting with the school's special education staff to discuss your concerns and try to resolve the issue informally.
  • Mediation: Request mediation with the school district. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school reach an agreement.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): File a formal complaint with the TEA. The TEA will investigate your complaint and determine whether the school district has violated IDEA or state law.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: Request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and argue your case before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will make a decision that is binding on both you and the school district.

Important Considerations:

  • There are timelines for filing complaints and requesting due process hearings, so it is important to act promptly.
  • You have the right to be represented by an attorney at any stage of the process.
  • The burden of proof is on the school district to demonstrate that its decision not to evaluate was appropriate.

After the Evaluation

Once the FIE is completed, the ARD committee, which includes you, will meet to review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under one of the thirteen disability categories outlined in IDEA. This determination must be based on a preponderance of the evidence.

If Your Child is Eligible: If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education services, the committee will develop an individualized education program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and services. You are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate in all decisions regarding your child's IEP.

Key Components of the IEP:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): A description of your child's current academic and functional performance.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound goals for your child's progress.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the special education and related services that your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling.
  • Placement: The setting in which your child will receive special education services.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: Accommodations and modifications that will be provided to help your child access the general education curriculum.
  • Progress Monitoring: How your child's progress will be monitored and reported to you.

If Your Child is Not Eligible: If the ARD committee determines that your child is not eligible for special education services, you have the right to disagree with the decision and pursue options such as an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) or mediation as outlined previously.

Ongoing Review and Revision: The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed, to ensure that it continues to meet your child's changing needs. You have the right to request an ARD meeting at any time to discuss your child's progress or make changes to the IEP.

Parental Rights: Throughout the entire special education process, you have important rights as a parent. These rights include the right to:

  • Participate in all decisions regarding your child's education.
  • Receive written notice of all proposed actions.
  • Inspect and review your child's educational records.
  • Obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation.
  • Request mediation or a due process hearing to resolve disputes with the school district.

It is highly recommended that parents familiarize themselves with their rights under IDEA and state law to ensure that their child receives the appropriate special education services.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
