How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD

This guide provides information about requesting a special education evaluation for your child within the Hurst-Euless-Bedford Independent School District (HEB ISD) in Texas. It outlines the steps involved, the timeline, your rights, and what to do if the school district denies your request. This information is based on the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating the special education process is submitting a written request for an evaluation to the school district. This request must be in writing to formally trigger the district's legal obligations under IDEA and Texas Education Code Chapter 29. Sending an email is acceptable, but retain a copy for your records. A phone call alone is not sufficient.

Here's what your written request should include:

  • Your Child's Full Name: Include your child's legal name as it appears on school records.
  • Your Child's Date of Birth: Include the date of birth to ensure accurate identification.
  • Your Child's Current School and Grade Level: Specify the campus your child attends within HEB ISD and their current grade.
  • Your Contact Information: Provide your name, address, phone number, and email address for communication.
  • A Clear Statement Requesting an Evaluation: State explicitly that you are requesting a Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) for special education services. Use language like, "I am requesting a full individual and initial evaluation to determine if my child has a disability that qualifies them for special education services."
  • A Brief Description of Your Concerns: Outline the reasons you believe your child may have a disability. Be specific as possible. Mention any academic, behavioral, social, or emotional difficulties your child is experiencing. Provide examples. For instance, "My child is struggling significantly with reading comprehension. They are two grade levels behind in reading, and their grades have been declining. They also frequently express frustration with reading assignments." Or, "My child has difficulty focusing in class and often disrupts other students. They have received numerous disciplinary referrals for inattention and impulsivity. I suspect they may have ADHD."
  • Any Supporting Documentation: Include any reports, evaluations, medical diagnoses, or other relevant information that supports your concerns. This may include doctor's notes, private therapy reports, previous testing results, or examples of your child's schoolwork.

Where to Send the Request: The best practice is to send the written request to multiple parties to ensure it is received. Send it to:

  • The School Principal: This is the primary point of contact at the school.
  • The Special Education Director: Contact information for the HEB ISD Special Education Director can be found on the district website.
  • Your Child's Teacher(s): Sending it to your child's teacher(s) helps ensure they are aware of the request and can contribute information to the evaluation process.

Sample Language for Your Request:

Subject: Request for Full Individual and Initial Evaluation for [Child's Name]

Dear [Principal's Name/Special Education Director's Name/Teacher's Name],

I am writing to request a full individual and initial evaluation for my child, [Child's Name], born on [Date of Birth]. [Child's Name] is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name].

I am concerned that [Child's Name] may have a disability that requires special education services. [Specifically describe your concerns and provide examples. Be as detailed as possible.]

I am attaching [list any supporting documentation].

Please contact me at [Phone Number] or [Email Address] to discuss this matter further. Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a specific timeline for the special education evaluation process. Once the school district receives your written request, they have a maximum of 45 school days to complete the following:

  • Obtain Parental Consent for Evaluation: The school district must provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) and a consent form for the evaluation. Do not sign the consent form until you have reviewed it carefully and understand what evaluations will be conducted. You have the right to ask questions and request clarification about the evaluation plan.
  • Complete the Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE): This comprehensive evaluation must assess all areas related to your child's suspected disability.
  • Hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) Committee Meeting: The ARD committee, which includes you as a crucial member, must meet to review the evaluation results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services.

Important Considerations Regarding the Timeline:

  • School Days vs. Calendar Days: The 45-day timeline refers to school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted. This means the actual time it takes to complete the evaluation process may be longer than 45 calendar days.
  • "Clock Starts" When the District Receives the Request: The 45-day clock begins when the district receives your written request. This underscores the importance of sending the request via email and keeping a copy, or sending it via certified mail with return receipt requested to verify delivery.
  • Parental Delays: Any delays caused by you, such as repeatedly rescheduling ARD meetings or failing to provide necessary information, may extend the timeline.
  • Legal Recourse: If the school district fails to complete the evaluation process within the 45-day timeline without a valid reason, you have legal recourse. You may consider filing a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) or pursuing other legal options.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if your child has a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires special education services. The FIIE must assess all areas related to the suspected disability. It should be conducted by qualified professionals and use a variety of assessment tools and techniques.

Areas Typically Covered in an FIIE:

  • Cognitive Abilities: Assesses intellectual functioning, problem-solving skills, and learning potential. This often involves standardized intelligence tests (IQ tests).
  • Academic Achievement: Evaluates performance in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. This may include standardized achievement tests and classroom-based assessments.
  • Language and Communication Skills: Assesses receptive and expressive language abilities, articulation, fluency, and social communication skills. This is particularly important if language is a primary concern.
  • Motor Skills: Evaluates gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Social-Emotional Functioning: Assesses emotional regulation, social skills, behavior, and mental health. This may involve observations, interviews, and rating scales.
  • Adaptive Behavior: Evaluates daily living skills such as self-care, communication, and social skills. This is often assessed using standardized adaptive behavior scales.
  • Sensory Processing: If sensory issues are suspected, the evaluation may include assessments of sensory processing and integration.
  • Medical Information: The evaluation team will review any relevant medical information, including diagnoses, medications, and medical history. They may request additional information from your child's physician.

Parental Input: Your input is crucial to the FIIE process. Share your observations, concerns, and any relevant information about your child's development, medical history, and educational experiences with the evaluation team. The evaluation team should consider your perspective when interpreting the evaluation results.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school district's FIIE, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense. The school district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate in a due process hearing that its evaluation was appropriate. You are entitled to receive the results of the IEE and have them considered by the ARD committee.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law and IDEA impose a "Child Find" obligation on school districts. This means that HEB ISD has a legal responsibility to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public schools or not. This includes children who are suspected of having a disability but have not yet been diagnosed.

What Child Find Means for You:

  • Proactive Identification: HEB ISD should have systems in place to screen and identify students who may be at risk for disabilities. This may include universal screenings, teacher referrals, and parent outreach programs.
  • No Excuses: The school district cannot deny an evaluation simply because your child is doing well academically in some areas. If you have concerns about your child's development or learning, the district must evaluate them to determine if they have a disability that requires special education services.
  • Private School Students: The Child Find obligation extends to children attending private schools within HEB ISD boundaries. If you suspect that your child attending a private school has a disability, you can request an evaluation from HEB ISD.
  • Home Schooled Students: Similarly, the Child Find obligation applies to students who are home schooled within the district.

If You Suspect Your Child Needs Help: If you have concerns about your child's development or learning, do not hesitate to contact HEB ISD and request an evaluation. Even if the school has not proactively identified your child as potentially having a disability, you have the right to request an evaluation.

What If They Refuse

The school district cannot arbitrarily refuse to evaluate your child if you have made a written request and have raised reasonable concerns about a possible disability. However, the district may disagree that an evaluation is warranted. If the school district refuses to conduct an evaluation, they must provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) explaining the reasons for their refusal. The PWN must be clear, concise, and understandable. It must also explain your rights and the options available to you.

Your Options if the District Refuses to Evaluate:

  • Request a Meeting: Schedule a meeting with the school district to discuss their reasons for refusing the evaluation and to present your concerns. Bring any supporting documentation you have to the meeting.
  • Mediation: Request mediation with the school district. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the district reach an agreement. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) offers free mediation services.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with TEA if you believe the school district has violated IDEA or Texas special education law. TEA will investigate your complaint and may order the district to take corrective action.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about whether the school district has violated your child's rights. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing.
  • Consider an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): As mentioned previously, you have the right to an IEE. Even if the district refuses their own, obtaining an IEE may give you more information to either address the district's concerns or build your case for a due process hearing.

Important Considerations:

  • Document Everything: Keep detailed records of all communications with the school district, including dates, times, and the content of conversations. Save all emails, letters, and other documents.
  • Seek Legal Advice: If you are unsure about your rights or the best course of action, consult with an attorney specializing in special education law.

After the Evaluation

After the evaluation is completed, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to discuss the results. The ARD committee includes you (the parent), school administrators, teachers, and evaluators. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine:

  • Eligibility for Special Education: Based on the evaluation results, the ARD committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under IDEA and Texas law. The committee must find that your child has a disability and that the disability adversely affects their educational performance.
  • Development of an Individualized Education Program (IEP): If your child is found eligible for special education, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports they will receive.

Key Components of an IEP:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): A description of your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic and functional areas.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A list of the specific services and supports your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, therapy, counseling, and assistive technology.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: Changes to the curriculum, instruction, or assessment procedures that will help your child succeed.
  • Placement: The setting in which your child will receive special education services. This could be a general education classroom, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom.
  • Progress Monitoring: How your child's progress towards their annual goals will be measured and reported to you.

Your Rights During the ARD Process:

  • Equal Participation: You are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in all discussions and decisions.
  • Access to Information: You have the right to access all of your child's educational records, including evaluation reports, IEPs, and progress reports.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): As mentioned previously, you have the right to request an IEE if you disagree with the school district's evaluation.
  • Prior Written Notice (PWN): The school district must provide you with PWN whenever they propose to change or refuse to change your child's IEP or placement.
  • Dispute Resolution Options: If you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing.

Reviewing the IEP:

  • Read Carefully: Before agreeing to the IEP, review it carefully to ensure that it accurately reflects your child's needs and goals.
  • Ask Questions: Don't hesitate to ask questions if you don't understand something in the IEP.
  • Suggest Changes: You have the right to suggest changes to the IEP.
  • Do Not Feel Pressured: You are not obligated to agree to the IEP if you don't think it is appropriate for your child.

Ongoing Monitoring: Your child's IEP should be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. Stay involved in your child's education and communicate regularly with their teachers and other service providers to ensure that their IEP is being implemented effectively and that they are making progress.

This guide provides general information and is not a substitute for legal advice. If you have specific legal questions or concerns, consult with an attorney specializing in special education law.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
