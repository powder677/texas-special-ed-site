What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a very important meeting about your child's special education services in Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD. This meeting is required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code Section 29. These laws guarantee that students with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's specific needs, and to decide where your child will receive those services. It is also used to review and, if necessary, revise the IEP at least annually to ensure it continues to meet your child's evolving needs. Ultimately, the goal is to help your child succeed in school.

The ARD committee is comprised of several individuals who are essential in the development and implementation of your child's IEP. Required members include: you, the parent or legal guardian; at least one of your child's general education teachers (if applicable); at least one special education teacher or provider; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education (often an administrator or special education director); a person who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results (this could be a diagnostician, educational specialist, or other qualified professional); and, whenever appropriate, your child. Other individuals may also be invited to the meeting, such as related service providers (speech therapist, occupational therapist, counselor), advocates, or anyone else who has knowledge or expertise about your child. Your input as a parent is critical. You know your child best, and your insights into their strengths, weaknesses, needs, and aspirations are invaluable to the ARD committee. Your active participation ensures that the IEP truly reflects your child's unique circumstances and promotes their academic and personal growth.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is not five school days, but five calendar days, meaning weekends and holidays count. This notice is crucial because it gives you time to prepare for the meeting, gather relevant information, and consider your child's needs and goals. The purpose of this notice is to ensure you are not caught off guard and have adequate time to actively participate in the decision-making process.

The written notice must include specific information. It must tell you the purpose of the meeting, such as initial eligibility, annual review, or a requested change to the IEP. It must also include the date, time, and location of the meeting. It is essential that the notice lists the individuals who will be attending the meeting, including their roles. This allows you to anticipate the perspectives that will be represented and to bring along any individuals of your own to support you. Finally, the notice must inform you of your right to participate in the ARD meeting and to bring other people who have knowledge or expertise about your child. If the notice is missing any of this information, it is essential to bring it to the school's attention, as it impacts your ability to be fully prepared and engaged.

While the five-day notice is in place to protect your rights, you can waive this requirement in writing if you choose to meet sooner. This might be beneficial if there is an urgent situation or if you and the school agree that an earlier meeting would be in your child's best interest. However, before waiving your right to the five-day notice, make sure you have had enough time to adequately prepare for the meeting and are comfortable moving forward.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting is vital to ensuring your child receives the best possible educational support. There are several items that you should consider bringing to the meeting to help inform the discussion and advocate for your child's needs. Gathering these resources ahead of time will allow for a smoother and more productive meeting.

If your child has had any prior evaluations, assessments, or testing results, bring copies to the ARD meeting. This includes previous IEPs, psychological evaluations, educational assessments, and any other relevant reports. These documents provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they may need support. Doctor's letters or reports from other professionals who work with your child, such as therapists or counselors, can also provide essential information about your child's medical or therapeutic needs. Sharing these documents helps the ARD committee understand the whole picture of your child's development and well-being.

Bringing your child's school records, including report cards, attendance records, and any examples of your child's work, can be incredibly useful. These documents provide a snapshot of your child's academic progress and areas where they may be struggling. If you have specific concerns about your child's performance, behavior, or social-emotional well-being, write them down in a list and bring it with you. Having a written list ensures that you don't forget any important points you want to discuss during the meeting. This list can also help you stay organized and focused during the conversation.

It's highly recommended that you bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who is familiar with special education laws and procedures. An advocate can provide emotional support, help you understand the information being presented, and ensure that your voice is heard. Having another person present can also help you remember key details from the meeting and provide a different perspective.

Texas is a one-party consent state when it comes to recording conversations. This means that you are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting as long as you inform the ARD committee that you will be recording it. The audio recording can serve as a valuable reference tool, helping you remember the discussion and review the decisions made during the meeting. It can also be useful if there are any disagreements or misunderstandings later on. Be sure to state clearly at the beginning of the meeting that you are recording. Some districts also have their own policies about recording, so it's good to check with them ahead of time.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

One of the most empowering rights you have in the ARD process is the ability to request a recess. Any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, can request a recess of up to ten school days to gather more information, seek outside consultation, or simply have more time to consider the decisions being made. This is especially useful when complex issues are being discussed, or when you feel that the team is moving too quickly without fully addressing your concerns.

The purpose of the 10-day recess is to ensure that all members of the ARD committee have adequate time to make informed decisions. This could involve consulting with medical professionals, obtaining independent evaluations, or simply taking time to reflect on the proposed IEP and its implications for your child. It's important to note that these are school days, not calendar days. So weekends and holidays don't count.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state that you would like to recess the meeting for up to 10 school days to further consider the information presented or gather additional information. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation or justification for your request; it is your right to invoke this rule. It is generally a good idea to request the recess formally and in writing, documenting the date and time of the request. This can help prevent misunderstandings or disputes later on.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP (Individualized Education Program) document is the cornerstone of your child's special education services. This document outlines the specific supports and services that will be provided to help your child achieve their educational goals. It's essential to understand the key components of the IEP to ensure that it accurately reflects your child's needs and promotes their success.

The IEP includes several key sections. The first section is Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance. This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, and social skills. It should also include information about any challenges your child is facing and how those challenges affect their ability to learn and participate in school. This section informs the rest of the IEP and is based on evaluations, observations, and input from teachers and parents.

Next are the Annual Goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). They should be directly related to your child's needs as identified in the present levels section. The IEP also outlines the specific services that will be provided to help your child achieve their annual goals. This may include specialized instruction, related services (such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling), accommodations, and modifications to the curriculum or classroom environment. The amount of time that services will be provided should be clearly stated in the IEP.

Placement refers to where your child will receive their special education services. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or self-contained classroom. The IEP must explain why the chosen placement is the most appropriate for your child and how it will allow them to access the general education curriculum to the maximum extent appropriate. Accommodations are changes to the way your child learns or is assessed. These can include things like extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. The IEP must clearly list the accommodations that will be provided to your child. Remember, you do not have to sign the IEP document the same day as the ARD meeting. You can take the IEP home to review it, consult with others, and ensure you fully understand and agree with its contents before signing.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee's recommendations at some point in their child's special education journey. It is crucial to understand your rights when disagreements arise and how to advocate for your child's needs effectively. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the team's recommendation for special education services, you can refuse to give your consent, and your child will not be placed in special education. However, once your child is receiving services, it becomes more complicated to unilaterally withdraw them.

Even if you attend the ARD meeting, participate in the discussion, and the IEP is developed, you are not required to sign the IEP document on the same day. You can take the IEP home to review it thoroughly, consult with advocates or other professionals, and ensure that you fully understand and agree with all of its provisions. If you disagree with certain aspects of the IEP but still want to move forward with some services, you can sign the IEP but write "Parent Disagrees" next to the specific sections you disagree with. This indicates that you do not agree with those particular aspects of the IEP but are willing to allow the other services to be implemented. This ensures your concerns are documented.

You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you request an IEE, the school district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that its evaluation was appropriate. The results of the IEE must be considered by the ARD committee when making decisions about your child's IEP.

Escalation Options

If disagreements with the ARD committee persist and you feel that your child's needs are not being adequately met, you have several formal options for resolving the dispute. These options offer different levels of intervention and should be pursued in a logical order.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process and involves submitting a written complaint outlining your concerns and the specific violations of special education law that you believe have occurred. TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This is a relatively quick and informal way to address systemic issues or violations of procedural safeguards. Information on how to file a TEA State Complaint can be found on the TEA website.

Mediation is another free option offered through TEA. Mediation involves a neutral third party who helps facilitate communication and negotiation between you and the school district. The mediator does not make decisions but helps both parties reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is a voluntary process, and both parties must agree to participate. It can be a valuable way to resolve disagreements amicably and collaboratively.

If mediation is unsuccessful or not appropriate, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal and adversarial process that involves presenting evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will make a binding decision based on the evidence presented. Due Process Hearings are governed by specific timelines and procedures, so it is essential to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option. Due Process Hearings can be expensive and time-consuming but may be necessary to protect your child's rights.

Finally, you have the option of filing a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. This option is most appropriate if you believe that your child has been discriminated against by the school district based on their disability. The Legal Framework for the IEP and ARD process is governed primarily by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89. Understanding these laws and regulations is essential for advocating effectively for your child's special education needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
