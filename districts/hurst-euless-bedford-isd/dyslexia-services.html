Dyslexia Services in Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services within the Hurst-Euless-Bedford Independent School District (HEB ISD) in Texas. It outlines the district's procedures and programs for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia, based on the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

HEB ISD adheres to the guidelines and requirements outlined in the most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the foundational document for understanding dyslexia, its characteristics, and the appropriate interventions and supports required for students in Texas public schools. The 2024 edition represents the most up-to-date guidance on best practices and legal mandates related to dyslexia.

Key elements of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 that inform HEB ISD's dyslexia services include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: Dyslexia is defined as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Early Identification and Intervention: The Handbook emphasizes the importance of early identification and intervention to prevent academic struggles and build foundational reading skills. HEB ISD implements screening procedures to identify students at risk for dyslexia.
  • Structured Literacy Approach: The Handbook advocates for the use of structured literacy as the primary instructional approach for students with dyslexia. HEB ISD utilizes structured literacy programs that are evidence-based and aligned with the Handbook's recommendations.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The Handbook promotes a MTSS framework for providing support to all students, with increasingly intensive interventions for students with dyslexia. HEB ISD utilizes MTSS to differentiate instruction and provide targeted support based on student needs.
  • Parent Involvement: The Handbook stresses the importance of parent involvement in the identification, assessment, and intervention process. HEB ISD actively communicates with parents and provides resources to support their child's learning.
  • Legal Requirements: The Handbook outlines the legal requirements of Texas Education Code related to dyslexia identification and services, including requirements for screening, assessment, instruction, and accommodations. HEB ISD ensures compliance with all applicable laws and regulations.
  • Professional Development: The Handbook underscores the need for ongoing professional development for educators to effectively identify and support students with dyslexia. HEB ISD provides professional development opportunities for teachers and staff on dyslexia awareness, identification, and intervention strategies.

Parents and guardians are encouraged to review the full Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024, which is available on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Understanding the Handbook is essential for advocating for your child's educational needs.

Mandatory Screening

HEB ISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening in accordance with Texas Education Code §38.003. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and require further evaluation.

Screening Procedures:

  • Grade Levels Screened: Typically, HEB ISD screens students in kindergarten through first grade, and sometimes extends into second grade, depending on individual student progress and needs. Specific grade levels screened may vary slightly year to year based on district data analysis. Contact your child's campus for the most up-to-date information on screening procedures.
  • Screening Instruments: HEB ISD utilizes TEA-approved screening instruments that are designed to assess key reading skills, including phonological awareness, letter knowledge, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and phonological memory.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified in writing if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia based on the screening results. This notification will include information about the screening results, the next steps in the process, and resources for parents.
  • Follow-Up: Students who are identified as at-risk through screening will receive targeted interventions and progress monitoring. If the interventions are not successful in addressing the student's reading difficulties, a full evaluation for dyslexia may be recommended.

It is important to note that screening is just one step in the identification process. A student who is not identified as at-risk during screening may still have dyslexia. If you have concerns about your child's reading development, you should communicate with their teacher and request further evaluation.

Structured Literacy Programs

HEB ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide explicit, systematic, and multi-sensory instruction to students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an evidence-based approach that focuses on teaching the foundational skills necessary for reading and spelling.

Key Characteristics of Structured Literacy Programs Used in HEB ISD:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and explicitly, with clear explanations and modeling.
  • Systematic and Sequential: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building from simple to complex.
  • Multi-Sensory: Instruction incorporates visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile modalities to engage different learning styles.
  • Phonological Awareness: Instruction focuses on developing awareness of the sounds of language and the ability to manipulate those sounds.
  • Phonics: Instruction teaches the relationship between letters and sounds, enabling students to decode words.
  • Fluency: Instruction emphasizes developing reading fluency, including accuracy, speed, and prosody.
  • Vocabulary: Instruction expands vocabulary knowledge through explicit teaching and contextual learning.
  • Comprehension: Instruction focuses on developing reading comprehension strategies, such as summarizing, predicting, and questioning.
  • Spelling: Instruction provides explicit rules for spelling, taking into account phonological patterns and common spelling conventions.

While specific program names may vary across campuses and grade levels, HEB ISD ensures that all programs used for students with dyslexia adhere to the principles of structured literacy and are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Contact your child's campus to inquire about the specific structured literacy program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the individual student's needs and the severity of their dyslexia.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. Dyslexia can qualify as an impairment under Section 504.
  • Focus: 504 plans focus on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: 504 plans may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • General Education Setting: Students with 504 plans typically receive their education in the general education classroom.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. Dyslexia, when it significantly impacts reading development despite interventions, can qualify a student for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability.
  • Focus: IEPs focus on providing specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: IEPs may include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and other related services.
  • Placement: Students with IEPs may receive instruction in a variety of settings, including general education classrooms, resource rooms, and self-contained classrooms, depending on their individual needs.

Key Differences:

  • Specialized Instruction: IEPs provide for specialized instruction tailored to the student's specific needs, while 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations within the general education setting.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), while 504 plans are governed by Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. IDEA provides more extensive procedural safeguards for parents and students.
  • Evaluation Process: The evaluation process for an IEP is more comprehensive and involves a multidisciplinary team, while the evaluation process for a 504 plan may be less formal.

The decision of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis by a team of educators and parents. The team will consider the student's strengths and weaknesses, the impact of dyslexia on their educational performance, and the type of services and supports needed to ensure their success.

Accommodations List

HEB ISD provides a range of accommodations to students with dyslexia to help them access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning. These accommodations are designed to address the specific challenges that students with dyslexia may face, such as difficulties with decoding, fluency, and spelling.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Providing extra time on tests and assignments to allow students to process information and complete tasks without time pressure.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing students in a location in the classroom where they can easily see and hear the teacher and minimize distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Using tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks to support reading and writing.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length, complexity, or format of assignments to make them more accessible.
  • Reading Aloud: Allowing students to have tests and assignments read aloud to them.
  • Use of a Calculator: Providing access to a calculator for math tasks.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing students to use a word processor with spell check for writing assignments.
  • Graphic Organizers: Providing graphic organizers to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Chunking: Breaking down large tasks into smaller, more manageable steps.
  • Multi-Sensory Techniques: Using visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile methods to reinforce learning.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing students to take short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Oral Testing: Allowing students to answer questions orally instead of in writing.
  • Highlighting: Allowing students to highlight key information in texts.
  • Copy of Notes: Providing students with a copy of the teacher's notes.

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations provided to a student will depend on their individual needs. The ARD committee (for IEPs) or the 504 committee will determine the appropriate accommodations based on the student's evaluation data and input from parents and educators.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading development and suspect they may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from HEB ISD.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Communicate with the Teacher: The first step is to communicate your concerns to your child's teacher. Discuss your observations and provide any relevant information about your child's reading difficulties.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or Principal: If you are not satisfied with the initial response from the teacher, or if you believe that further action is needed, contact the school counselor or principal. They can provide guidance and support in navigating the evaluation process.
  3. Submit a Written Request: To formally request an evaluation for dyslexia, you must submit a written request to the school principal or special education director. The written request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your name and contact information
    • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading development
    • Any relevant information about your child's academic history and previous interventions
  4. Evaluation Process: Upon receiving your written request, HEB ISD will initiate the evaluation process. This process may include:
    • Review of your child's academic records
    • Observations of your child in the classroom
    • Administration of standardized assessments to evaluate reading skills, including phonological awareness, decoding, fluency, and comprehension
    • Assessment of cognitive abilities to rule out other potential causes of reading difficulties
  5. Evaluation Report: Once the evaluation is complete, the evaluation team will prepare a written report outlining the findings. The report will include information about your child's strengths and weaknesses, whether or not they meet the criteria for dyslexia, and recommendations for intervention.
  6. ARD Meeting (if applicable): If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia and requires specialized instruction, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting will be scheduled to develop an IEP. At the ARD meeting, the team will discuss the evaluation results, determine appropriate goals and objectives for your child, and develop a plan for providing specialized instruction and related services. If the evaluation indicates that an IEP is not required but accommodations are, a 504 meeting will be scheduled.

HEB ISD is committed to providing timely and appropriate evaluations for students suspected of having dyslexia. If you have any questions or concerns about the evaluation process, contact the school counselor, principal, or special education director at your child's campus.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
