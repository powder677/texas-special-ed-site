Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

HEB ISD, like all Texas school districts, must adhere to state and federal laws regarding student discipline. A crucial point to understand is the "10-Day Suspension Rule," especially if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or a Section 504 plan. This rule is vital because it triggers certain procedural safeguards.

What it means: If your child is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single academic year, it's considered a Change of Placement. This is SIGNIFICANT, especially for students with IEPs because it triggers their special education rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Why it matters: Accumulating more than 10 days of suspension can lead to a more restrictive educational environment, potentially hindering your child's progress. If the suspensions are related to their disability, it could be a violation of IDEA.

What you need to do:

  • Track Suspension Days: Keep a detailed record of every suspension, no matter how short. Note the date, length of suspension, and the reason given by the school.
  • Request Records: Immediately request your child's disciplinary records from the school. Verify the accuracy of the reported suspension days.
  • If Approaching 10 Days: If your child is nearing 10 days of suspension, IMMEDIATELY contact the school's principal, special education director (if applicable), and your child's case manager or 504 coordinator. Express your concerns about exceeding the 10-day threshold and the implications for your child's education.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a critical process under IDEA that MUST occur when a school district contemplates a Change of Placement for a student with an IEP due to disciplinary reasons. This is triggered when a student is facing suspension for more than 10 school days or is considered for more restrictive placements due to disciplinary infractions.

Purpose of the MDR: The MDR aims to determine if the student's misbehavior was:

  1. Caused by or directly and substantially related to the child's disability, OR
  2. The direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

Who Participates in the MDR? The MDR is conducted by the IEP team, including:

  • Parents/Guardians
  • Relevant school personnel (e.g., teachers, special education staff, administrators)
  • The student (when appropriate)

Key Considerations During the MDR:

  • Review of IEP: The team meticulously examines the IEP to ensure it's appropriate and being implemented correctly.
  • Behavioral Intervention Plan (BIP): If a BIP exists, it's assessed for effectiveness. Is it being followed? Is it addressing the specific behaviors leading to disciplinary action?
  • Evaluation Data: All relevant evaluation data, including psychological evaluations, behavioral assessments, and academic records, are reviewed.
  • Parent Input: Your input is crucial. You know your child best. Share your insights into the reasons behind their behavior, any relevant medical or psychological factors, and your concerns about the proposed disciplinary action.

What Happens if the Behavior IS a Manifestation of the Disability? If the MDR team determines that the behavior IS a manifestation of the disability (or due to IEP implementation failure), the school CANNOT impose further disciplinary action (like expulsion or long-term suspension) that would result in a Change of Placement, *unless* the parent agrees. Instead, the IEP team MUST:

  • Conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) if one hasn't been done already.
  • Develop (or revise) a BIP to address the behavior.
  • Implement the BIP effectively.
  • Return the student to their previous placement (unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the BIP).

What Happens if the Behavior IS NOT a Manifestation of the Disability? If the MDR team determines that the behavior is NOT a manifestation of the disability, the school can apply the same disciplinary actions it would apply to students without disabilities. HOWEVER, the school MUST still provide Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) to the student, even during the disciplinary period. This might involve providing instruction in an alternative setting.

What if you DISAGREE with the MDR decision? You have the right to challenge the MDR decision through due process (discussed later). Don't hesitate to seek legal counsel if you believe the school is not properly considering your child's disability.

Change of Placement

As mentioned previously, a "Change of Placement" is a significant event, especially for students with IEPs or 504 plans. It's crucial to understand what constitutes a Change of Placement and your rights in such situations.

What constitutes a Change of Placement?

  • Suspension for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year. This triggers the MDR process for students with IEPs.
  • Expulsion.
  • Placement in an Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES) for more than 45 school days for disciplinary reasons (especially involving drugs, weapons, or serious bodily injury).
  • Any significant alteration of the student's IEP or 504 plan that changes the educational setting, services, or supports. This could include moving a student from a general education classroom to a self-contained classroom, or significantly reducing the amount of specialized instruction.

Your Rights When a Change of Placement is Proposed:

  • Notice: The school MUST provide you with written notice of the proposed Change of Placement. This notice must explain the reasons for the change, your rights, and how to access procedural safeguards.
  • Right to Participate: You have the right to participate in all meetings and discussions related to the Change of Placement, including the MDR (if applicable) and IEP meetings.
  • Right to Review Records: You have the right to review all records related to the proposed Change of Placement.
  • Right to Consent (or Refuse): In many cases, the school needs your consent before implementing a Change of Placement, especially for students with IEPs. You have the right to refuse consent if you disagree with the proposed change.
  • Stay Put: This is a CRITICAL right under IDEA. If you disagree with the proposed Change of Placement, you have the right to invoke "stay put." This means your child remains in their current placement (the placement at the time the dispute arose) while the dispute is being resolved through mediation or due process. You MUST formally invoke "stay put" in writing.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe that HEB ISD has violated your child's rights, whether related to discipline, special education, or any other issue, you have the right to file a grievance. HEB ISD has a formal grievance policy, and it's essential to follow the proper procedures.

Level 1 Grievance: The first step in the grievance process is usually filing a Level 1 grievance. This is typically addressed to the principal or immediate supervisor of the person involved in the issue.

Steps to File a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Policy: Locate the HEB ISD's grievance policy. This document outlines the specific procedures, timelines, and requirements for filing a grievance. You can usually find it on the HEB ISD website or by requesting it from the school or district office.
  2. Prepare a Written Grievance: Your grievance MUST be in writing. Clearly and concisely state the following:
    • Your child's name and school.
    • The date(s) of the incident(s) that led to the grievance.
    • A detailed description of the problem or violation. Be specific and factual. Include names, dates, and any relevant details.
    • The specific policy or law that you believe was violated.
    • The remedy you are seeking. What do you want the school to do to resolve the issue?
  3. Submit the Grievance: Submit the written grievance to the appropriate person (usually the principal or immediate supervisor) within the timeframe specified in the grievance policy. Keep a copy of the grievance for your records.
  4. Follow Up: The school is required to respond to your grievance within a certain timeframe (usually outlined in the policy). If you don't receive a response within that timeframe, follow up with the school to inquire about the status of your grievance.

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines: Pay close attention to the deadlines for filing grievances and appeals. Missing a deadline can result in your grievance being dismissed.
  • Documentation: Gather and organize all relevant documentation, such as emails, letters, disciplinary records, IEP documents, and witness statements.
  • Escalation: If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the Level 1 grievance, you can typically appeal to the next level (Level 2), which may involve the superintendent or a designated administrator. The grievance policy will outline the procedures for appealing.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe that HEB ISD has violated federal or state laws related to special education, you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). A TEA complaint can be a powerful tool for holding the district accountable.

What types of issues can you address in a TEA complaint?

  • Failure to implement the IEP.
  • Denial of FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education).
  • Procedural violations of IDEA.
  • Discrimination based on disability.

How to File a TEA Complaint:

  1. Obtain the TEA Complaint Form: You can download the complaint form from the TEA website or request it from TEA's special education division.
  2. Complete the Complaint Form: Carefully and completely fill out the complaint form. Be specific and factual in describing the violations. Include all relevant details, such as dates, names, and specific actions or omissions.
  3. Provide Supporting Documentation: Attach any supporting documentation to your complaint, such as IEP documents, evaluation reports, correspondence with the school, and disciplinary records.
  4. Submit the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA according to the instructions on the form.

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint. This may involve reviewing documents, interviewing witnesses, and contacting the school district. TEA will issue a written report with its findings.

Important Considerations:

  • Timelines: There are deadlines for filing a TEA complaint. Check the TEA website for the current deadlines.
  • Prioritize: TEA often prioritizes complaints involving serious violations of special education law.
  • Legal Counsel: Consider consulting with an attorney specializing in special education law before filing a TEA complaint.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal process under IDEA that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding their child's special education. It's a more adversarial process than mediation or grievance procedures, but it provides a formal hearing and the opportunity to present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer.

When to Consider Due Process:

  • When you disagree with the school's IEP, evaluation, or placement decisions.
  • When you believe the school has violated your child's rights under IDEA.
  • When mediation or other informal dispute resolution methods have failed to resolve the issue.

Steps in the Due Process Process:

  1. File a Due Process Complaint: You must file a written due process complaint with the school district and TEA. The complaint must contain specific information, including a description of the problem, the proposed resolution, and the facts supporting your position.
  2. Resolution Session: Within 15 days of receiving the complaint, the school district must convene a resolution session with you to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  3. Mediation: If the resolution session is unsuccessful, you and the school district may agree to participate in mediation.
  4. Due Process Hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful, a due process hearing will be scheduled. The hearing will be conducted by an impartial hearing officer. You and the school district will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine opposing witnesses.
  5. Hearing Officer Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision within a specified timeframe.
  6. Appeal: You have the right to appeal the hearing officer's decision to a state or federal court.

Important Considerations:

  • Legal Representation: Due process is a complex legal process. It is highly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law.
  • Documentation: Gather and organize all relevant documentation, such as IEP documents, evaluation reports, correspondence with the school, disciplinary records, and witness statements.
  • Timelines: There are strict timelines for filing due process complaints and appeals. Missing a deadline can result in your case being dismissed.

This is a stressful time. Remember to document everything, communicate in writing, and seek legal counsel if you feel overwhelmed or unsure of your rights. Your child's education is worth fighting for.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
