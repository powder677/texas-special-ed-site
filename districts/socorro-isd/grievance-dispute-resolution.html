Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Urgent Guide: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Socorro ISD

If your child is facing disciplinary action in Socorro ISD, especially if they have an IEP or 504 plan, you need to act NOW. This guide is designed to provide crucial information and steps you can take to protect your child's rights. This is not legal advice, but a guide to help you understand the process and advocate effectively. Time is of the essence!

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: A Critical Threshold

Socorro ISD, like all school districts in Texas, adheres to the 10-day suspension rule. This rule is vitally important, especially if your child receives special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or has a Section 504 plan. Here's what you NEED to know:

  • Cumulative Suspensions: The 10-day limit refers to cumulative days of suspension in a school year. This means that if your child has already been suspended for, say, 5 days and is now facing another suspension, the district is REQUIRED to consider whether the upcoming suspension will push them over that 10-day threshold.
  • Impact on Students with Disabilities: Once a student with an IEP or 504 plan accumulates more than 10 school days of suspension (either consecutively or cumulatively) in a school year, specific IDEA provisions are triggered. This is a pivotal moment! This can trigger a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).
  • Why 10 Days Matters: The IDEA recognizes that lengthy or repeated removals from the classroom can significantly disrupt the education of a student with disabilities. The 10-day rule is designed to protect those students and ensure their educational needs are being met.
  • Documentation is Key: Keep meticulous records of EVERY suspension your child receives. Note the dates, the reasons for the suspension, and any documentation provided by the school. This will be crucial if you need to challenge a disciplinary action.
  • Ask the Question: If your child is facing a suspension that could bring them close to or over 10 days, IMMEDIATELY ask the school administration: "Will this suspension result in my child exceeding 10 days of suspension this school year? If so, what steps are you taking to comply with IDEA regulations, including a Manifestation Determination Review and consideration of Change of Placement?" Get their response in writing if possible.

What is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a meeting that MUST be held when a student with an IEP or 504 plan faces a suspension that exceeds 10 cumulative school days in a year, or when a proposed disciplinary change of placement (explained below) is being considered. It's a crucial safeguard to determine if your child's behavior is related to their disability.

This is NOT just another meeting! It's a PROTECTIVE MECHANISM for your child!

  • Purpose of the MDR: The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's behavior that led to the disciplinary action was a manifestation of their disability. In other words, did the disability cause or significantly contribute to the behavior? Or, was the behavior a direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP or 504 plan?
  • Who Attends the MDR: The MDR team typically includes the parent(s)/guardian(s), relevant members of the IEP or 504 team (including special education teachers, general education teachers, administrators), and any other individuals who have relevant knowledge of the student. You have the right to bring an advocate or attorney to this meeting.
  • Key Questions Asked During the MDR:
    • Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to the child's disability?
    • Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP or 504 plan?
  • Outcomes of the MDR:
    • If the behavior was a manifestation of the disability: The student CANNOT be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA), if one hasn't been done already, and implement or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The student MUST be returned to their original placement (unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the BIP).
    • If the behavior was NOT a manifestation of the disability: The student can be disciplined in the same way as students without disabilities. However, the school district MUST continue to provide educational services to the student during the period of suspension, ensuring they can continue to participate in the general education curriculum.
  • Prepare for the MDR: Before the meeting, review your child's IEP or 504 plan thoroughly. Gather any relevant documentation that might support the argument that the behavior is related to their disability (e.g., doctor's notes, therapist reports, previous behavioral incidents, evidence that the IEP/504 plan wasn't being followed).
  • What if You Disagree?: If you disagree with the MDR outcome, you have the right to challenge the decision through a due process hearing (see "Due Process" section below). This is a serious step, but it's important to protect your child's rights.
  • Demand the MDR: If your child is nearing or has exceeded 10 days of suspension, and the school hasn't scheduled an MDR, DEMAND one IMMEDIATELY in writing. Send an email and a certified letter.

Change of Placement: When Does Suspension Become a Change of Placement and Why Does It Matter?

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days OR when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a change of placement. This is a critical concept because it triggers additional procedural safeguards under IDEA.

  • More than 10 Consecutive Days: If your child is suspended for more than 10 consecutive school days, this automatically constitutes a Change of Placement and requires an MDR.
  • Pattern of Removals: Even if no single suspension exceeds 10 days, a pattern of shorter suspensions can also be considered a Change of Placement. Factors considered include:
    • The length of each removal
    • The total amount of time the student is removed
    • The proximity of the removals to one another
    • The nature of the removals (e.g., are they all for similar behaviors?)
  • Why "Change of Placement" Matters: A Change of Placement triggers the right to a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and requires the IEP team to review and potentially revise the student's IEP to address the behaviors leading to the suspensions. It also raises questions about whether the current placement is appropriate.
  • Example: Imagine your child is suspended for 3 days every week for a month due to disruptive behavior. Even though no single suspension is longer than 10 days, this pattern of frequent removals could be considered a Change of Placement, triggering MDR requirements.
  • Don't Let Them Sidestep the Process: Some schools may try to avoid the "Change of Placement" designation to avoid the required MDR and IEP review. Be vigilant! If you believe your child's suspensions are creating a pattern that effectively removes them from their educational setting, demand an MDR and insist that the school address the issue.
  • Alternative Educational Setting: A change of placement could mean moving your child to a different classroom, a different school, or even a more restrictive environment (like a disciplinary alternative education program - DAEP). You have the right to be involved in this decision and to advocate for the least restrictive environment that meets your child's needs.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in Socorro ISD

If you believe your child's rights have been violated by Socorro ISD's disciplinary actions, you have the right to file a grievance. Socorro ISD, like all Texas districts, has a formal grievance process. Understand the steps. ACT QUICKLY! Grievances have strict deadlines.

  • Level 1: Campus Level: The first step is to file a Level 1 grievance with the principal or designated administrator at your child's school.
  • Time Limits: You typically have a very limited time (usually 10-15 school days) from the date you knew or should have known about the issue to file the grievance. Find out the specific deadline from the district's policy (usually available on their website) and MARK IT ON YOUR CALENDAR. Missing the deadline will likely result in your grievance being rejected.
  • Content of the Grievance: Your grievance should be clear, concise, and factual. Include:
    • Your child's name and grade level
    • The date(s) of the incident(s) you are grieving
    • A clear description of what happened and why you believe it violates your child's rights or district policy. Be specific!
    • Cite the specific policy or law that you believe has been violated (e.g., IDEA, Section 504, Socorro ISD policy).
    • The specific relief you are seeking (e.g., reinstatement of your child, removal of the suspension from their record, an MDR meeting, revision of the IEP).
    • Any supporting documentation (e.g., copies of suspension notices, IEP documents, emails).
  • Submitting the Grievance: Submit the grievance in writing (email and certified mail is best) to the principal or designated administrator. Keep a copy for your records.
  • Response Time: The principal or administrator typically has a limited time (again, usually 10-15 school days) to respond to your grievance in writing.
  • If You're Not Satisfied: If you are not satisfied with the Level 1 response, you can appeal to Level 2 (usually to a district-level administrator). Again, there will be strict deadlines.
  • Documentation is EVERYTHING: Keep copies of EVERYTHING: the grievance you filed, the school's response, any supporting documentation, emails, and certified mail receipts.

TEA (Texas Education Agency) State Complaint

A TEA State Complaint is a formal written complaint filed with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) alleging that a school district has violated a requirement of IDEA or other federal regulations related to special education. This is a powerful tool when the district is failing to follow the law.

  • When to File: You can file a TEA State Complaint if you believe Socorro ISD has violated IDEA or other related regulations. This is usually done after exhausting the local grievance process, or if the local process is clearly inadequate or unresponsive.
  • What to Include: Your complaint must be in writing and include:
    • A clear statement that Socorro ISD has violated a requirement of IDEA or federal regulations.
    • The facts upon which the complaint is based (be specific and provide dates, names, and details).
    • Information about the student (name, address, school, grade level, disability).
    • A proposed resolution of the problem.
    • A copy of any relevant documentation (IEP, 504 plan, suspension notices, grievance responses, etc.).
    • Your signature and contact information.
  • TEA Investigation: Once TEA receives your complaint, they will investigate the allegations. They may request additional information from you and the school district.
  • TEA Findings: TEA will issue a written decision regarding your complaint. If they find that the district violated IDEA, they will order corrective action.
  • Deadlines: TEA complaints generally must be filed within one year of the date of the alleged violation.
  • Where to File: Information on how to file a TEA State Complaint can be found on the TEA website. Search for "TEA Special Education Complaint"

Due Process: Your Ultimate Legal Recourse

Due Process is the most formal legal avenue available to parents who disagree with the school district regarding their child's special education. This is a serious step, and it's highly recommended to consult with an attorney experienced in special education law before filing a due process complaint.

  • When to File: You can file a due process complaint if you disagree with the school district regarding:
    • The identification, evaluation, or educational placement of your child.
    • The provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE) to your child.
  • Content of the Complaint: Your due process complaint must be very specific and include:
    • The name and address of the child.
    • The name of the school the child attends.
    • A description of the nature of the problem, including the facts relating to the problem.
    • A proposed resolution of the problem.
  • Resolution Meeting: After you file a due process complaint, the school district is required to hold a resolution meeting with you to try to resolve the dispute.
  • Due Process Hearing: If the resolution meeting is unsuccessful, you have the right to a due process hearing before an impartial hearing officer. This is similar to a court trial, where you can present evidence and witnesses to support your case.
  • Hearing Officer's Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision based on the evidence presented at the hearing. This decision is binding on both you and the school district, unless you appeal it to a court of law.
  • Legal Representation: Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming. It is highly recommended that you hire an attorney to represent you at the hearing.
  • Statute of Limitations: There is a statute of limitations for filing a due process complaint, so it is important to act quickly. Typically, you have two years from the date you knew or should have known about the issue to file the complaint.

Don't wait! Take action today to protect your child's rights. Contact the Socorro ISD special education department, disability rights organizations, and consider consulting with an attorney specializing in special education law. Your child's future depends on it!

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
