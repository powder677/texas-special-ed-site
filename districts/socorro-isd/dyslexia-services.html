Dyslexia Services in Socorro ISD

Dyslexia Services in Socorro ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students within the Socorro Independent School District (ISD) in Texas. It is intended for parents, educators, and anyone seeking to understand how Socorro ISD supports students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (referenced as the Handbook hereafter) is the primary resource guiding dyslexia identification, instruction, and support in Texas public schools. Socorro ISD adheres to the guidelines and recommendations outlined in this handbook. The 2024 update, and all subsequent versions, reflect current research-based best practices and legal requirements.

Key Components of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024):

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The Handbook provides a clear definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurobiological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Identification Procedures: The Handbook details the process for identifying students with dyslexia, including universal screening, progress monitoring, and comprehensive evaluations when warranted. Emphasis is placed on using Response to Intervention (RTI) data and other relevant information to inform decision-making.
  • Instructional Practices: The Handbook mandates the use of Structured Literacy approaches for students identified with dyslexia. It outlines the essential components of Structured Literacy, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and reading comprehension.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: The Handbook provides guidance on providing appropriate accommodations and modifications to support students with dyslexia in the classroom and during assessments.
  • Legal Requirements: The Handbook summarizes the legal requirements related to dyslexia services under state and federal law, including the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.
  • Parental Involvement: The Handbook emphasizes the importance of parental involvement in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. It provides resources and information for parents to support their children's learning.

Parents are strongly encouraged to familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. It is available for free download from the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website at the link provided above.

Mandatory Screening

Socorro ISD follows the Texas Education Agency's (TEA) guidelines regarding mandatory dyslexia screening. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and require further evaluation.

Universal Screening:

  • All kindergarten and first-grade students are screened for dyslexia risk factors. This screening typically occurs early in the school year.
  • Screening tools are selected based on TEA recommendations and are designed to assess foundational reading skills such as phonological awareness, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and letter-sound knowledge.
  • The specific screening tools used may vary slightly from year to year based on district decisions, but will always align with TEA guidelines.

Follow-Up Screening:

  • Students who demonstrate risk factors for dyslexia during the universal screening are provided with targeted interventions.
  • Progress monitoring is used to track the effectiveness of these interventions.
  • Students who do not make adequate progress despite targeted interventions may be referred for a comprehensive evaluation to determine if they have dyslexia.

Notification to Parents: Socorro ISD will notify parents if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia based on the screening results. This notification will include information about the screening results, recommended interventions, and the process for requesting a comprehensive evaluation if needed.

Structured Literacy Programs

Socorro ISD utilizes Structured Literacy programs to provide explicit, systematic, and sequential instruction to students identified with dyslexia. Structured Literacy is an evidence-based approach that addresses the underlying phonological weaknesses that contribute to reading difficulties.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonological Awareness: Instruction focuses on developing awareness of the sounds of language, including rhyming, blending, segmenting, and manipulating phonemes.
  • Phonics: Students learn the relationships between letters and sounds (graphemes and phonemes) and how to decode words.
  • Fluency: Instruction focuses on improving reading speed and accuracy.
  • Vocabulary: Students learn the meanings of words and how to use them in context.
  • Reading Comprehension: Students learn strategies for understanding and remembering what they read.
  • Multisensory Approach: Instruction often incorporates visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile modalities to enhance learning.

Examples of Structured Literacy Programs used in Socorro ISD (subject to change based on district adoption):

While specific program names might vary, the District often utilizes programs aligned with Orton-Gillingham principles. Contact your child's school for the most current information on specific programs being implemented.

Implementation:

  • Structured Literacy instruction is typically delivered in small groups or individually, depending on the student's needs.
  • Instruction is data-driven, with progress monitoring used to track student progress and adjust instruction as needed.
  • Highly trained and qualified teachers or dyslexia therapists provide Structured Literacy instruction.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the level of support required.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Section 504 is a civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in programs and activities that receive federal funding.
  • A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • For a student with dyslexia, a 504 plan may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, and the use of assistive technology.
  • To qualify for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading).
  • 504 Plans typically do not provide specialized instruction.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • IDEA is a federal law that provides special education and related services to eligible children with disabilities.
  • An IEP is a written document that outlines a student's educational needs, goals, and the specific services that will be provided to meet those needs.
  • For a student with dyslexia, an IEP may include specialized instruction in Structured Literacy, as well as accommodations and modifications.
  • To qualify for an IEP, a student must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. Dyslexia is a recognized disability under IDEA.
  • IEPs provide specialized instruction, often delivered in a resource setting.

Key Differences:

Feature Section 504 Plan IEP (IDEA)
Legal Basis Civil Rights Law (Section 504) Special Education Law (IDEA)
Eligibility Impairment substantially limiting a major life activity Disability adversely affecting educational performance AND requiring specialized instruction
Focus Equal access through accommodations Specialized instruction and related services
Instruction Generally, no specialized instruction Specialized instruction (e.g., Structured Literacy)
Evaluation Less formal evaluation process Comprehensive evaluation required

The decision of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and parents. The team will consider the student's individual needs, strengths, and weaknesses to determine the most appropriate level of support.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns or is assessed that do not change the content of what is being taught. The purpose of accommodations is to provide students with equal access to the curriculum and to demonstrate their knowledge and skills.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to textbooks and other materials in audio format.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that reads text aloud.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Using software that converts spoken words into written text.
  • Word Prediction Software: Using software that suggests words as the student types.
  • Graphic Organizers: Providing visual aids to help students organize their thoughts.
  • Modified Assignments: Reducing the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Alternative Assessments: Using alternative methods of assessment, such as oral presentations or projects.
  • Use of Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology devices, such as tablets or computers.
  • Copy of Notes: Providing a copy of teacher's notes.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing for short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Highlighted Text: Highlighting key words or phrases in text.
  • Multi-Sensory Learning Tools: Utilizing manipulatives, hands-on activities, and visual aids.
  • Dictation: Allowing the student to dictate answers to a scribe.
  • Calculator/Math Charts: Providing necessary tools for math computations.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a space with minimal distractions for testing.

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will vary depending on their individual needs. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, for students with IEPs, or the 504 committee determines appropriate accommodations.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from Socorro ISD.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact Your Child's School: The first step is to contact your child's teacher or the school principal. Express your concerns about your child's reading difficulties and your desire for an evaluation for dyslexia.
  2. Submit a Written Request: It is important to submit your request in writing. This creates a formal record of your request. You can address the letter to the school principal or the Special Education Director for Socorro ISD. Clearly state that you are requesting an evaluation for dyslexia.

    Sample Letter:

    [Your Name]
    [Your Address]
    [Your Phone Number]
    [Your Email Address]

    [Date]

    [Principal's Name]
    [School Name]
    [School Address]

    Subject: Request for Dyslexia Evaluation

    Dear [Principal's Name],

    I am writing to request a comprehensive evaluation for my child, [Child's Name], who is in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s reading difficulties, specifically [Describe specific difficulties, e.g., difficulty sounding out words, slow reading speed, poor spelling]. I believe that [Child's Name] may have dyslexia, and I would like to request an evaluation to determine if this is the case.

    I understand that Socorro ISD follows the guidelines outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, and I would appreciate it if the evaluation process could be conducted in accordance with those guidelines.

    Thank you for your time and consideration. I look forward to hearing from you soon.

    Sincerely,
    [Your Name]

  3. Review and Consent: The school will then provide you with information about the evaluation process, including the types of assessments that will be used. You will need to provide written consent before the evaluation can begin.
  4. Evaluation Process: The evaluation will be conducted by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians or dyslexia specialists. The evaluation will assess your child's reading skills, phonological awareness, and other relevant areas.
  5. Evaluation Report: You will receive a written report summarizing the evaluation findings. The report will indicate whether or not your child meets the criteria for dyslexia.
  6. ARD/504 Meeting: If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee (for IEP eligibility) or 504 committee will be convened to develop an appropriate plan to support your child's learning.

Timeline: Socorro ISD is required to complete the evaluation process within a reasonable timeframe, as outlined in state and federal law. This timeline typically begins once the school receives your written consent for the evaluation.

Contact Information: For further questions or concerns, you can contact the Special Education Department at Socorro ISD.

Disclaimer: This guide is intended to provide general information about dyslexia services in Socorro ISD. It is not a substitute for professional advice. Laws and procedures are subject to change, so always consult with school officials and review official documents for the most up-to-date information.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
