What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Channelview ISD! An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a cornerstone of the special education process. It's a very important meeting where crucial decisions are made about your child's education if they have been identified as having a disability that requires specialized support. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and, when appropriate, revise your child's Individualized Education Program, also known as an IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific services, supports, and accommodations your child will receive to help them access the general education curriculum and make progress. This process is mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and further defined by Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which ensures that students with disabilities receive a free appropriate public education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs.

The ARD meeting is a collaborative process, bringing together individuals who have valuable insights and expertise regarding your child. Typically, the ARD committee includes you, the parent or legal guardian; a representative from the school district who is knowledgeable about special education programs and resources (usually an administrator or special education director); your child's teacher(s); a special education teacher; an evaluator (such as a diagnostician or educational psychologist) who can interpret evaluation results; and, when appropriate, your child themselves. Other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited, such as therapists, counselors, or outside specialists. You have the right to invite anyone you believe can contribute to the discussion and help the team develop the best possible IEP for your child. Remember, you are a critical member of this team, and your input is essential to ensure the IEP meets your child's unique needs. The ARD committee is responsible for determining whether your child is eligible for special education services, developing the IEP, and deciding on the appropriate educational placement for your child. These decisions are made as a team, with the goal of providing your child with the best possible educational opportunities.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Channelview ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is not five school days, but rather any five days including weekends and holidays. The purpose of this notice is to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting, gather any relevant information, and arrange for any support you may want to bring with you. The notice must include specific information, such as the date, time, and location of the meeting; the purpose of the meeting; the names and titles of the individuals who will be attending; and a clear explanation of your rights as a parent in the special education process. The notice should also clearly state that you have the right to invite anyone you choose to the ARD meeting who has knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. It is very important to carefully review this notice to understand the agenda and the issues that will be discussed at the meeting.

However, there may be times when you would prefer to have the ARD meeting sooner than five days. In this case, you can waive your right to the five-day notice requirement. To do so, you must provide written consent, indicating that you understand your right to the notice and that you voluntarily agree to waive it. This waiver should be a separate document from the ARD meeting notice itself. It's crucial to understand that waiving your right to the five-day notice is entirely your choice, and you should only do so if you feel comfortable and prepared to proceed with the meeting. You should never feel pressured or coerced into waiving this right. If you have any questions or concerns about the five-day notice requirement or the waiver process, don't hesitate to contact the special education department at Channelview ISD for clarification.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but being well-prepared can make a significant difference in ensuring your child's needs are met. One of the most important things you can do is gather and organize all relevant documentation pertaining to your child's education and development. This includes any prior evaluations or assessments conducted by the school district, independent evaluations from outside professionals, doctor's letters or medical reports, and previous school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and any disciplinary records. Sharing these documents with the ARD committee can provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and areas where they may need additional support. If you are unsure about whether a document is pertinent, bring it anyway. It's better to have too much information than not enough.

In addition to documentation, it's helpful to create a written list of your specific concerns and questions regarding your child's education. This list can serve as a guide during the ARD meeting, ensuring that you address all of your concerns and that the team has a clear understanding of your priorities. Be specific and provide concrete examples whenever possible. For example, instead of saying "My child is struggling in reading," you could say "My child is having difficulty decoding words and understanding the main idea of passages, which is affecting their performance on reading comprehension tests." Having a written list will help you stay focused and organized during the meeting, especially if you are feeling nervous or emotional. Also, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or a professional advocate who is knowledgeable about special education law and procedures. An advocate can provide emotional support, help you articulate your concerns, and ensure that your rights are protected. In Texas, it is legal to audio record your ARD meeting provided that you give the school district advanced notice that you will be recording the meeting. You may find this helpful for accurately recalling details discussed during the meeting, but be sure to adhere to the notice requirement. If you wish to record the meeting, it is generally considered best practice to provide this notice in writing and to obtain written acknowledgment of the notice from the school district.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During the ARD meeting, you might feel overwhelmed or unsure about a particular decision or proposed service. The good news is that you don't have to make any decisions immediately. Under Texas law, any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess of the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days. This recess allows you time to gather more information, seek advice from an advocate or other professionals, or simply reflect on the information presented during the meeting before making a decision. The purpose of the recess is to ensure that you have ample opportunity to make informed decisions that are in the best interest of your child. The district cannot force you to make an immediate decision.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state during the ARD meeting that you would like to request a recess to gather more information or consult with an advocate. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation for your request, but it is helpful to be clear about what you hope to accomplish during the recess period. For example, you might say, "I would like to request a 10-day recess to review the proposed IEP with an advocate" or "I need some time to consider the evaluation results and how they align with my child's needs at home." Once you have requested the recess, the ARD committee must agree to postpone the meeting to a later date. The meeting must reconvene within 10 school days, unless you agree to a longer period. At the reconvened meeting, the ARD committee will continue its discussions and make decisions based on the new information gathered during the recess period. This is your right, and you should feel empowered to use it whenever you need more time to make a considered decision.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the central document that guides your child's special education services. It's a detailed plan that outlines your child's current academic and functional performance, sets measurable annual goals, specifies the services and supports they will receive, determines the appropriate educational placement, and lists any necessary accommodations or modifications. The IEP is developed collaboratively by the ARD committee, with your active participation and input. It is important to understand each section of the IEP and how it contributes to your child's overall educational success. The IEP document contains decisions made during the ARD process.

The "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses, based on evaluation data, classroom observations, and your input. The "Annual Goals" section outlines specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The "Services" section details the specific services and supports your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling), and assistive technology. The "Placement" section describes the educational setting where your child will receive their services, such as the general education classroom, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The "Accommodations and Modifications" section lists any adjustments to the curriculum, instruction, or assessment procedures that will help your child access the general education curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Remember, you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek advice from others before signing. Your signature indicates that you agree to the services and supports outlined in the IEP.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that you are an equal and essential member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. However, there may be times when you disagree with the team's decisions or recommendations. You have specific rights if you disagree with the ARD committee's recommendations. You have the right to refuse consent for initial placement of your child in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the team's decision that your child needs special education services, you can refuse to allow them to be placed in a special education program. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with any of its contents. This does not mean that your child will not receive any services, but it does mean that you do not agree with the plan as it is written.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the district believes their evaluation was appropriate, they must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to defend their evaluation. Finally, you always have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it. This indicates that you do not agree with some or all of the contents of the IEP, and it protects your right to pursue further action. This statement can be as brief or as detailed as you deem necessary. The school district is obligated to include your statement in the IEP document.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee and you still feel that your child's rights are being violated, you have several formal options for escalating your concerns. It's helpful to understand the order in which to proceed with these options. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. A TEA complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law. The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a trained, impartial mediator to attempt to resolve your disagreements. Mediation is also free through TEA and can be a helpful way to find common ground and reach a mutually agreeable solution. Participation in mediation is voluntary for both the parents and the school district.

The third option is to request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a more formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's special education needs. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is highly recommended that you seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option. The fourth option is to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. These rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and 19 Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89, which outline the specific procedures and timelines for each of these options. Understanding your rights and the available escalation options can empower you to advocate effectively for your child and ensure that they receive the free appropriate public education to which they are entitled.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
