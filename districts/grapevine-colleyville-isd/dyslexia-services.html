Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), is the primary resource guiding identification and intervention for students with dyslexia in Texas public schools. The 2024 update includes significant revisions and clarifications regarding screening, identification, and instruction. Parents and educators should familiarize themselves with this document. Key changes and ongoing guidance address: early identification and prevention, improved alignment with the science of reading, requirements for structured literacy programs, and updated procedures for evaluation and support. Understanding the Handbook is crucial for advocating for your child's needs and ensuring they receive appropriate dyslexia services within GCISD.

Mandatory Screening

GCISD adheres to the Texas Education Code and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook regarding mandatory dyslexia screening. Universal screening for dyslexia indicators begins in kindergarten and continues throughout elementary school. This screening typically occurs in the fall and spring of each year, or as required by TEA guidelines. Screening tools used are aligned with the requirements outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and are designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. Parents will be notified if their child's screening results indicate a need for further evaluation or intervention. It is important to note that screening is not diagnostic; it is a preliminary step to identify students who may benefit from more in-depth assessment.

Structured Literacy Programs

GCISD implements structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multisensory approach to reading instruction. It focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and reading comprehension. These programs are aligned with the principles of the science of reading and are designed to address the underlying language deficits associated with dyslexia. GCISD utilizes research-based programs that have been proven effective in supporting students with dyslexia. Specific programs used may vary by campus and grade level, but all adhere to the structured literacy principles outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Contact your child's school or the GCISD Special Education Department for information on the specific programs used at their campus.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or under an Individualized Education Program (IEP) through special education services. A 504 plan provides accommodations to level the playing field for students with disabilities, allowing them to access the general education curriculum. An IEP, on the other hand, is designed for students with more significant needs that require specialized instruction and related services. The determination of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and special education staff, based on the student's individual needs and evaluation data. The need for specially designed instruction to address the student's dyslexia will be a key factor in determining eligibility for an IEP.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional practices that help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia in GCISD may include, but are not limited to:

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Use of assistive technology (e.g., text-to-speech software, audiobooks)
  • Preferential seating
  • Reduced workload or modified assignments
  • Use of graphic organizers
  • Oral administration of tests
  • Access to notes or outlines
  • Frequent breaks
  • Multisensory teaching techniques
  • Dictation software

The specific accommodations provided will be tailored to the student's individual needs and documented in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, contact their teacher or the school principal to discuss your concerns. You can formally request a dyslexia evaluation by submitting a written request to the school principal or the school's special education coordinator. The request should include specific concerns and any relevant information, such as observations of reading difficulties or a family history of dyslexia. The school will then convene a team to review your request and determine if an evaluation is warranted. The evaluation process will follow the guidelines outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and may include assessments of reading, spelling, phonological awareness, and other language skills. Remember to keep documentation of all communication and requests. If you are not satisfied with the school's response, you have the right to contact the GCISD Special Education Department for assistance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
