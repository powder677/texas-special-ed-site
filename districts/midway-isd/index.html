Special Education in Midway ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

Navigating the special education system can feel overwhelming, but understanding the basics is the first step to ensuring your child receives the appropriate support. Special education, as defined by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law, refers to specially designed instruction and related services provided to children with disabilities who require such assistance to benefit from their education. This includes a wide range of supports, from academic interventions to speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling services. Your child's needs are at the heart of this process.

Midway ISD, like all public school districts in Texas, is overseen by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). The TEA sets the standards and provides guidance for special education programs across the state. Additionally, Midway ISD benefits from the support of the TEA Region 12 Education Service Center, which offers professional development, technical assistance, and resources to help educators effectively serve students with disabilities. Understanding these oversight structures can help you advocate effectively for your child within the Midway ISD system. Remember, you are not alone.

It is crucial to remember that as a parent, you have enforceable legal rights throughout the entire special education process. These rights are guaranteed under IDEA and Texas law, ensuring that you are an active participant in all decisions regarding your child's education. From the initial evaluation to the development and implementation of your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP), your voice matters. Midway ISD is obligated to uphold these rights and work collaboratively with you to create a supportive learning environment for your child. Remember, you have the right to disagree, question, and advocate for what you believe is best for your child.

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in Midway ISD

If you suspect that your child may have a disability that requires special education services, the first step is to request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) from Midway ISD. It is absolutely crucial that this request is submitted in writing. A verbal request does not officially start the timeline for evaluation. The written request should clearly state your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress and why you believe a special education evaluation is warranted. Provide as much specific detail as possible to support your request. You have the right to this evaluation if you suspect a disability, regardless of what school staff tell you.

Once Midway ISD receives your written request and you provide written consent for the evaluation, the district has 45 school days to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). The FIE will assess your child's strengths and weaknesses in various areas, such as academic skills, cognitive abilities, communication skills, and social-emotional functioning. It is important to note that Child Find laws require Midway ISD to proactively identify students who may need special education services, even if you have not specifically requested an evaluation. If you believe your child is struggling, don't hesitate to reach out and request an evaluation. The sooner your child's needs are identified, the sooner they can receive the support they need to succeed. This is your right as a parent in Texas.

Take action now: Write a letter to the Midway ISD special education director requesting a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for your child. Keep a copy of the letter for your records, and send it via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery.

ARD Meetings in Midway ISD: Your Rights at the Table

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is a crucial process in special education in Midway ISD, bringing together parents, educators, and other relevant professionals to determine whether a student is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to the student's specific needs, and to review the student's progress on a regular basis. These meetings are collaborative and designed to ensure that your child's unique needs are being met in the most effective way possible. Your voice is an integral part of the ARD committee.

Midway ISD is required to provide you with at least five calendar days' written notice before any ARD meeting. This gives you time to review any relevant documents, prepare your questions, and gather any information you want to share with the team. During the ARD meeting, you have the right to actively participate in all discussions, express your opinions, and advocate for your child's needs. If you feel overwhelmed or unprepared during the meeting, you can request a 10-day recess at any time to gather more information, seek outside consultation, or simply take time to process the information presented. Parents are often surprised to hear this; however, under Texas law, you have the right to request a recess.

It is also important to remember that you are not obligated to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and seek clarification or make revisions before providing your consent. You can also implement parts of the IEP you agree with while disputing other portions. Never feel pressured to agree to something you are not comfortable with. Your consent is required for initial placement and for any changes to your child's placement. Remember, you are your child's strongest advocate.

Dyslexia Services in Midway ISD

Texas law mandates that Midway ISD screen all students for dyslexia. This screening process is designed to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties and ensure they receive appropriate interventions. The guidelines for dyslexia screening and intervention are outlined in the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook, which sets the standards that Midway ISD must adhere to. Familiarize yourself with this handbook; it provides detailed information about dyslexia, screening procedures, evidence-based interventions, and your rights as a parent. It also describes the methodology that Texas public schools must follow.

If your child is identified as having dyslexia, they are entitled to receive structured literacy intervention, which is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, vocabulary, morphology, and syntax. Depending on the severity of your child's dyslexia and their individual needs, they may also qualify for a 504 Plan or an IEP. A 504 Plan provides accommodations and modifications to the general education environment to support students with disabilities, while an IEP provides specially designed instruction and related services tailored to the student's unique needs. Remember, early identification and intervention are key to helping students with dyslexia achieve their full potential.

If your child has not yet been screened for dyslexia and you have concerns about their reading development, request a screening in writing from Midway ISD immediately.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in Midway ISD

Discipline is a complex issue for all students, but it becomes especially important when dealing with students who receive special education services. Under IDEA, there are specific protections in place to ensure that students with disabilities are not unfairly disciplined due to behaviors related to their disability. If your child is suspended from Midway ISD for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year, this triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This is an ARD meeting to determine whether the behavior that led to the suspension was caused by or directly related to your child's disability or if it was a result of the school's failure to implement the IEP. The MDR must occur within a 10 day timeframe.

A suspension of more than 10 cumulative days also constitutes a Change of Placement under IDEA, which means that the ARD committee must meet to determine whether the current placement is appropriate for your child and whether any changes need to be made to the IEP or educational setting. This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face as a parent of a child with special needs. You must act within days to protect your child's rights. If your child has been suspended, contact the school immediately to schedule an ARD meeting and ensure that a Manifestation Determination Review is conducted promptly. Do not wait. You must act assertively.

Filing a Complaint Against Midway ISD

If you believe that Midway ISD is not fulfilling its obligations under IDEA or Texas special education law, you have several options for filing a complaint. The first option is to file a TEA State Complaint. This is a formal written complaint submitted to the TEA outlining your concerns. The TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. This process is free and can be a useful tool for addressing systemic issues or violations of law.

You also have the option of requesting Mediation through the TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process that brings you and Midway ISD representatives together with a trained mediator to try to resolve your dispute. This process is also free and can be a less adversarial way to reach a mutually agreeable solution. If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer, you can pursue a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will issue a decision. You can also file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that Midway ISD has discriminated against your child based on their disability. Regardless of what Midway ISD staff may tell you, these rights exist and you have the right to pursue them. Don't let the district intimidate you out of exercising your legal options.

Frequently Asked Questions About Midway ISD Special Education

The questions every Midway ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Midway ISD? +

Submit a written request to the Midway ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in Midway ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does Midway ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires Midway ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how Midway ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if Midway ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. Midway ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
