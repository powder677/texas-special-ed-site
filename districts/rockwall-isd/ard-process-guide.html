What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their educational performance, you will be invited to participate in an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, and the ARD committee is the team responsible for making decisions about your child's special education program. The purpose of an ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP). This IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's unique educational needs and the specific services and supports they will receive to address those needs. The ARD process is mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law that ensures students with disabilities have access to a free and appropriate public education (FAPE). Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, further details the requirements for special education services in Texas, including the ARD process.

The ARD committee is comprised of several individuals who come together to share their expertise and perspectives on your child's education. The required members of the ARD committee include you, the parent; at least one general education teacher (if your child is or may be participating in the general education environment); at least one special education teacher or provider; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education and is knowledgeable about the general education curriculum and the availability of resources of the district; an individual who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results (this may be one of the other members); and, whenever appropriate, the child. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited to attend, such as therapists, diagnosticians, or outside consultants. The goal is to create a collaborative environment where everyone works together to determine the best educational plan for your child.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Under both IDEA and Texas law, the school district is required to provide you with written notice of the ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the scheduled date. This notice is very important as it provides you with the information you need to prepare for the meeting. This notice must include several key details, including the purpose of the meeting, the date, time, and location of the meeting, who will be in attendance, and a clear explanation of your parental rights regarding the ARD process. It is crucial to review this notice carefully to ensure you understand the agenda and are prepared to actively participate in the discussion. If the district is proposing to conduct an initial evaluation of your child, the notice will also include a copy of the Procedural Safeguards, which outline all of your rights under IDEA.

While the district is required to provide you with at least five days' notice, you have the right to waive this requirement if you choose to meet sooner. This might be beneficial if there's an urgent need to address a particular issue or if you simply prefer to expedite the process. However, it is important to carefully consider whether waiving the notice period is in your best interest. Ensure you have enough time to adequately prepare for the meeting and gather any necessary information. If you choose to waive the five-day notice, you will typically be asked to sign a written waiver acknowledging that you understand your right to the notice and are voluntarily giving it up.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but being well-prepared can empower you to be an effective advocate for your child. Gathering and bringing relevant documents and information can significantly contribute to a productive and informed discussion. Here's a detailed list of items you should consider bringing to your ARD meeting:

Firstly, bring any prior evaluations of your child, including psychological evaluations, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, or any other assessments that provide insights into your child's strengths and needs. These evaluations can be conducted by the school district or by independent professionals. Having these reports readily available allows the ARD committee to have a comprehensive understanding of your child's current abilities and areas where they may require support. Secondly, if your child has received medical diagnoses or has been under the care of a doctor, bring any relevant letters or reports from their physician. These documents can provide valuable information about your child's medical condition and how it may impact their learning. It's particularly helpful if the doctor's letter specifically addresses the educational implications of your child's diagnosis.

Additionally, bring any school records that you think might be relevant. This could include report cards, progress reports, work samples, and any documentation of communication between you and the school staff. This information can help illustrate your child's academic performance over time and highlight any patterns or trends. Furthermore, it is beneficial to create a written list of your specific concerns regarding your child's education. This list should be detailed and specific, outlining any areas where you feel your child is struggling or where you believe their needs are not being adequately met. This ensures that your concerns are clearly articulated and addressed during the meeting. Don't hesitate to bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, a friend, or a professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you articulate your concerns, and ensure that your voice is heard. Having a second set of ears can also be beneficial for remembering key details and following the discussion.

Finally, Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. You are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting if you choose to do so, provided that you inform the ARD committee that you are recording. This can be a valuable tool for ensuring that you have an accurate record of the meeting and can refer back to it later. Be sure to announce at the beginning of the meeting that you are recording. It is highly recommended to bring a voice recorder to the meeting for your own personal records.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a critical component of the ARD process in Texas that empowers parents and other ARD committee members to ensure that decisions are made with careful consideration and access to all necessary information. This rule states that any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, can request a recess of up to 10 school days to gather additional information, seek outside consultation, or simply take time to consider the proposed IEP before making a final decision. This recess period provides an opportunity to ensure that all decisions are made in the best interest of the child.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request during the ARD meeting. There is no specific form or procedure required; you can simply say something like, "I would like to request a 10-day recess to consider the proposed IEP." The ARD committee is obligated to grant your request. During the recess period, you can use the time to consult with advocates, review relevant documents, or seek additional evaluations. It's important to note that the recess period is for school days only, meaning weekends and holidays are not counted. Once the recess period has ended, the ARD committee will reconvene to finalize the IEP. The 10-day recess rule can be a powerful tool for ensuring that your child's IEP is appropriate and addresses their unique needs. Don't hesitate to use it if you feel you need more time or information to make informed decisions.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the cornerstone of your child's special education services. It is a comprehensive plan that outlines your child's unique educational needs and the specific supports and services they will receive to address those needs. Understanding the different sections of the IEP document is crucial for ensuring that your child receives an appropriate education. Several key elements are decided upon during the ARD meeting and documented within the IEP. These include:

Firstly, the IEP will include a section on your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, and social-emotional skills. It is based on data from evaluations, classroom observations, and input from parents and teachers. Secondly, the IEP will outline annual goals for your child. These goals are measurable and specific, and they describe what your child is expected to achieve within a one-year timeframe. The goals should be aligned with your child's present levels and should be ambitious yet attainable. Thirdly, the IEP will specify the special education and related services that your child will receive. This may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or other supports that are necessary to help your child achieve their goals. The IEP will also detail the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

The IEP will address the appropriate educational placement for your child. This refers to the setting in which your child will receive their education, such as a general education classroom, a special education classroom, or a combination of both. The IEP team must consider the least restrictive environment (LRE) when determining placement, meaning that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Finally, the IEP will outline any accommodations or modifications that your child needs to be successful in the classroom. Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered or assessed, such as providing extended time on tests or allowing the use of assistive technology. Modifications are changes to the content of the curriculum, such as simplifying assignments or providing alternative assessments.

It is important to remember that you are not required to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek additional input before making a decision. If you are unsure about any aspect of the IEP, don't hesitate to ask questions or request clarification from the ARD committee.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee at some point in the special education process. Remember that you are an equal member of the team, and your voice is important. There are several steps you can take if you find yourself disagreeing with the ARD committee's recommendations.

Firstly, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you do not agree with the school district's determination that your child requires special education services, you can refuse to sign the consent form for initial placement. This will prevent the district from moving forward with special education services at that time. Secondly, even if you agree with the overall IEP, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with specific components. This does not mean that your child will not receive any services; it simply means that you do not agree with those particular aspects of the plan. It is important to communicate your concerns to the ARD committee and attempt to reach a resolution. If you are willing to compromise on certain aspects, it can be beneficial to state that, as well.

Thirdly, if you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide a valuable second opinion and can be used to inform the ARD committee's decisions. The district is required to either pay for the IEE or demonstrate that their own evaluation was appropriate. Finally, regardless of whether you sign the IEP or not, you have the right to write "Parent Disagrees" on the document. This signifies that you do not fully support the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action if necessary.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal means, there are several formal options available to you. These options are outlined in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and further detailed in Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, which is part of the Legal Framework for Texas Education. It's important to familiarize yourself with these rights. The first option is to file a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process that allows you to file a written complaint alleging that the school district has violated IDEA or state special education laws. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. This is often the quickest and least adversarial option.

The second option is Mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA offers free mediation services to parents of students with disabilities. A mediator can facilitate communication and help you explore potential solutions to your disagreements. Mediation is often a successful way to resolve disputes without resorting to more formal and adversarial processes. The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and issues a binding decision. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is generally recommended to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option. There are strict timelines for filing for a due process hearing, so it is critical that you consult with an attorney as soon as possible if you are considering this option. Finally, you also have the option to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates allegations of discrimination based on disability in educational programs that receive federal funding. If OCR finds that the school district has discriminated against your child, they can order the district to take corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
