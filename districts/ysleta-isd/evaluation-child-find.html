How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Ysleta ISD: A Comprehensive Guide

This guide provides information for parents and legal guardians of students in the Ysleta Independent School District (ISD) who suspect their child may have a disability requiring special education services. It outlines the process for requesting a special education evaluation, understanding the legal timelines, and navigating potential challenges. This guide is based on Texas Education Code and related regulations.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is to submit a written request to the school. While verbal requests might be acknowledged, a written request provides a clear record of your concern and formally triggers the district's legal obligations. Here's what your written request should include:

  • Date: Clearly date the request.
  • Child's Full Name: Include the student's full legal name.
  • Child's Date of Birth: Provide the student's date of birth for accurate identification.
  • Child's Grade Level: Specify the student's current grade level.
  • Child's School: Indicate the school the student currently attends within Ysleta ISD.
  • Your Contact Information: Include your full name, address, phone number, and email address. If multiple guardians are involved, provide contact information for all.
  • A Clear Statement Requesting an Evaluation: Explicitly state that you are requesting a Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) for special education services. For example, "I am writing to formally request a Full Individual and Initial Evaluation (FIIE) to determine my child's eligibility for special education services."
  • A Brief Description of Your Concerns: Outline your specific concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. Be as specific as possible, citing examples of difficulties your child is experiencing. This could include specific subjects they struggle with, behavioral issues in the classroom, social difficulties, or developmental delays.
  • Supporting Documentation (Optional): If you have any relevant documentation, such as medical records, previous evaluations, or teacher observations, you can include copies with your request. This is not mandatory but can be helpful.
  • Signature: Sign and date the request.

Where to Submit the Request: The written request should be submitted to the school principal or the school's special education director. It's advisable to send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery. Keep a copy of the request for your records.

Example Statement of Concern: "My son, [Child's Name], struggles significantly with reading comprehension. He often has difficulty understanding written instructions and performs poorly on reading comprehension assessments. He also exhibits frustration and anxiety during reading activities, sometimes refusing to participate."

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a specific timeline for the special education evaluation process. Once the school receives your written request, the district has 45 school days to complete the evaluation and determine eligibility for special education services. It is crucial to understand that this is 45 school days, not calendar days. This means weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted towards the 45-day timeline.

Key Events Within the 45 School Day Timeline:

  • Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, including your child's academic records, medical records (if provided), and teacher observations.
  • ARD Committee Meeting to Discuss Evaluation: An Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting must be held to determine the scope of the evaluation. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the ARD committee. This meeting should occur reasonably soon after the written request is received. The purpose of this meeting is to determine what areas need to be assessed to fully evaluate your child's needs.
  • Conducting the Evaluation: Once the scope of the evaluation is determined, qualified professionals will conduct the necessary assessments.
  • Preparation of the Full Individual Evaluation (FIIE) Report: The evaluation results will be compiled into a comprehensive report called the Full Individual Evaluation (FIIE).
  • ARD Committee Meeting to Discuss Evaluation Results and Eligibility: Another ARD committee meeting will be held to discuss the FIIE results and determine if your child meets the criteria for special education services under Texas Education Code and federal law (IDEA - Individuals with Disabilities Education Act). You will be provided with a copy of the FIIE report prior to this meeting.

Timeline Extensions: The 45-day timeline can only be extended under very specific circumstances and requires your written consent. Do not agree to an extension unless you fully understand the reason and believe it is in your child's best interest.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual Evaluation (FIIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires special education and related services. The FIIE must be conducted by qualified professionals and must adhere to specific guidelines outlined in Texas Education Code and related regulations. The specific areas covered in the FIIE will depend on the individual child's needs and the concerns raised in the initial request. However, a comprehensive FIIE typically includes assessments in the following areas:

  • Cognitive Abilities: This assesses the child's general intellectual functioning, including problem-solving, reasoning, and memory.
  • Academic Achievement: This evaluates the child's performance in academic areas such as reading, writing, math, and spelling. Standardized tests are often used to compare the child's performance to their peers.
  • Communication Skills: This assesses the child's receptive and expressive language skills, including vocabulary, grammar, articulation, and fluency.
  • Social and Emotional Development: This evaluates the child's social skills, emotional regulation, and behavior. This may include observations, interviews, and questionnaires.
  • Adaptive Behavior: This assesses the child's ability to function independently in daily life activities, such as self-care, social skills, and communication.
  • Motor Skills: This evaluates the child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Sensory Processing: This assesses how the child processes sensory information from their environment.
  • Medical Information: This includes a review of relevant medical records and information provided by the child's physician.

Specific Disability Areas: If a specific disability is suspected (e.g., Autism, Specific Learning Disability, Speech Impairment), the FIIE must include assessments specific to that disability. For example, if Autism is suspected, the evaluation must include observations of the child's social interactions and communication skills, as well as standardized assessments designed to identify characteristics of Autism Spectrum Disorder.

Parent Input: Your input as a parent is crucial to the FIIE process. The evaluators must consider your observations and concerns about your child's development and learning. Share specific examples of your child's strengths and weaknesses, as well as any strategies you have found helpful at home.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school's FIIE, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense. The school district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. You are entitled to review the district's criteria for IEE providers.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) place a "Child Find" obligation on school districts. This means that Ysleta ISD has a legal responsibility to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within its boundaries who may be in need of special education and related services, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or are homeschooled. This obligation extends to children who are suspected of having a disability but are not yet identified.

Referral Process: Anyone can make a referral to the school district if they suspect a child has a disability. This includes parents, teachers, doctors, and other professionals. The referral should be in writing and should include the child's name, date of birth, and a brief description of the concerns. The school district is then obligated to investigate the referral and determine if an evaluation is warranted.

Awareness Activities: Ysleta ISD should engage in public awareness activities to inform parents and the community about special education services and the availability of evaluations. This may include distributing information through schools, community organizations, and local media.

Duty to Refer: School personnel have a heightened duty to refer students for evaluation if they observe behaviors or learning difficulties that suggest a possible disability. Failure to refer a student who is suspected of having a disability can be a violation of the district's Child Find obligations.

What If They Refuse

If Ysleta ISD refuses to conduct a special education evaluation after you have submitted a written request, you have several options. It is important to act promptly and assert your rights.

  • Request a Written Explanation: Immediately request a written explanation from the school district outlining the reasons for the refusal. This explanation must be provided within a reasonable timeframe.
  • Request Mediation: You can request mediation with the school district through the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with TEA alleging that the school district has violated state or federal special education laws. The TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision. The complaint must typically be filed within one year of the alleged violation.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: You have the right to request a due process hearing, which is a formal legal proceeding where you can present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will issue a decision that is binding on both you and the school district. You may want to seek legal representation for a due process hearing.

Legal Representation: Consider consulting with an attorney specializing in special education law. An attorney can advise you on your rights, help you navigate the legal process, and represent you in negotiations or legal proceedings. Legal Aid organizations and disability rights groups may offer free or low-cost legal assistance.

Documentation is Key: Keep detailed records of all communication with the school district, including emails, letters, and meeting notes. This documentation will be crucial if you need to file a complaint or request a due process hearing.

After the Evaluation

Once the evaluation is complete, the ARD committee will meet to review the results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services. If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible, an Individualized Education Program (IEP) will be developed. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services that will be provided to meet those needs.

Components of an IEP: The IEP must include:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance: A description of your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic and functional areas.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the special education and related services that will be provided to your child, including the frequency, duration, and location of those services. Related services may include speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, and transportation.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to help your child access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, while modifications are changes to what a student learns.
  • Participation in State and District Assessments: A description of how your child will participate in state and district assessments. If your child requires accommodations for testing, those accommodations must be specified in the IEP.
  • Placement: The IEP must specify the least restrictive environment (LRE) in which your child will receive special education services. The LRE is the setting that allows your child to be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

Parent Rights: As a parent, you have the right to participate in all ARD committee meetings and to provide input into the development of your child's IEP. You also have the right to disagree with the IEP and to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing, if you cannot reach an agreement with the school district.

IEP Review: The IEP must be reviewed at least annually, or more frequently if needed. This allows the ARD committee to monitor your child's progress and make adjustments to the IEP as necessary.

Ongoing Communication: Maintain open and ongoing communication with your child's teachers and other school staff. This will help ensure that your child is receiving the support they need to succeed.

This guide provides general information and should not be considered legal advice. It is essential to consult with legal professionals or advocacy groups for personalized guidance related to your specific circumstances. The Ysleta ISD and the Texas Education Agency (TEA) websites offer additional resources and information about special education services.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
