Dyslexia Services in Ysleta ISD

Dyslexia Services in Ysleta ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Ysleta Independent School District (ISD). It is intended to help parents, educators, and students understand the district's procedures and resources related to dyslexia identification and intervention, in accordance with Texas state law and the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Ysleta ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024), published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This handbook serves as the primary resource for understanding dyslexia, its characteristics, screening procedures, evaluation methods, and evidence-based instructional approaches. The handbook is updated periodically to reflect current research and best practices.

Key changes and important elements emphasized in the 2024 Handbook that Ysleta ISD utilizes include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The Texas Dyslexia Handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Early Identification and Intervention: The importance of early identification through screening and intervention is emphasized. Ysleta ISD provides universal screening and targeted interventions to address literacy difficulties as early as possible.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook underscores the use of structured literacy approaches for instruction, which are evidence-based and research-proven. Ysleta ISD implements structured literacy programs as described below.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The handbook highlights the role of MTSS in providing tiered levels of support to students with reading difficulties, including those with dyslexia. Ysleta ISD uses an MTSS framework.
  • Parent Involvement: Active parent involvement is recognized as critical to student success. Ysleta ISD encourages open communication and collaboration with parents throughout the identification and intervention process.
  • Technology Integration: The handbook acknowledges the role of technology in supporting students with dyslexia. Ysleta ISD utilizes assistive technology to support instruction and access to curriculum.

Parents are strongly encouraged to review the Texas Dyslexia Handbook for a comprehensive understanding of dyslexia and the services available to students in Texas. The link provided above leads directly to the TEA website's publication.

Mandatory Screening

Ysleta ISD follows state mandates regarding dyslexia screening. According to Texas Education Code (TEC) §38.003, all students in kindergarten and first grade must be screened for dyslexia and related disorders. Additionally, students in grades 2-12 must be screened if they show signs of reading difficulties based on classroom performance, teacher observations, or parent concerns.

Screening Procedures:

  • Universal Screening (K-1): All students in kindergarten and first grade receive universal screening using TEA-approved instruments. These screenings typically assess phonological awareness, phonological memory, rapid naming, and letter-sound knowledge.
  • Targeted Screening (2-12): Students in grades 2-12 are screened if they exhibit characteristics associated with dyslexia, such as:
    • Difficulty with phonological awareness and phonological memory.
    • Struggles with decoding and encoding (spelling).
    • Slow and labored reading.
    • Difficulty with reading comprehension due to decoding challenges.
    • Family history of dyslexia.

Notification of Results: Parents/guardians will be notified in writing of the screening results. If a student is identified as being at risk for dyslexia, the school will provide targeted interventions and monitor progress.

Parent Right to Request Evaluation: Regardless of screening results, parents have the right to request a full and individual evaluation for dyslexia at any time. This process is described in more detail in the "How to Request Evaluation" section below.

Structured Literacy Programs

Ysleta ISD utilizes structured literacy approaches in its dyslexia intervention programs. Structured literacy is an evidence-based approach that emphasizes explicit, systematic, and cumulative instruction in the foundational skills of reading and spelling.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Instruction focuses on the sound structure of language, including phonological awareness (rhyming, segmenting, blending) and phoneme-grapheme correspondence (the relationship between sounds and letters).
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Students learn the systematic relationships between letters and sounds in both directions (encoding and decoding).
  • Syllable Instruction: Students learn about different types of syllables and how they influence pronunciation.
  • Morphology: Instruction focuses on the meaning-bearing units of words (prefixes, suffixes, and roots).
  • Syntax: Students learn about sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: Instruction focuses on the meaning of words and phrases.

Specific Programs Used in Ysleta ISD: Ysleta ISD may utilize various TEA-approved structured literacy programs. While specific program names may vary based on campus and student needs, they all share the core principles of structured literacy. Parents should contact their child's school to inquire about the specific program being used.

Implementation: Structured literacy interventions are typically delivered in small groups or individually by trained teachers or specialists. The frequency and duration of intervention sessions will vary depending on the student's needs. Progress is regularly monitored to ensure the effectiveness of the intervention.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for accommodations and/or specialized instruction through either a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the severity of the student's needs and the impact of the dyslexia on their ability to access the general education curriculum.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading, concentrating). Dyslexia can qualify as a disability under Section 504.
  • Focus: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to the learning environment to ensure that the student has equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Instruction: Typically, students with 504 plans remain in the general education classroom and receive their instruction from the general education teacher.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability listed under IDEA (which includes a Specific Learning Disability like Dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: An IEP provides specialized instruction and related services to address the student's unique learning needs and enable them to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services may include specialized reading instruction (e.g., structured literacy interventions), speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, and assistive technology.
  • Instruction: Students with IEPs may receive instruction in the general education classroom with modifications and supports, or in a special education setting, depending on their individual needs. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee makes this determination.

Key Differences:

  • Level of Support: IEPs generally provide a more intensive level of support than 504 plans, including specialized instruction.
  • Eligibility Criteria: IEP eligibility criteria are more stringent than 504 eligibility criteria.
  • Procedural Safeguards: IDEA provides greater procedural safeguards for parents and students than Section 504.

The ARD committee (for IEPs) or the 504 committee (for 504 plans) will determine which type of plan is most appropriate for the student based on a comprehensive assessment of their needs.

Accommodations List

The following is a list of common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia through a 504 plan or an IEP. The specific accommodations provided will be individualized based on the student's needs. It's crucial to remember accommodations are not "dumbing down" the material; they are tools to provide access.

  • Reading Accommodations:
    • Extended time on reading tasks and tests.
    • Text-to-speech software.
    • Audiobooks.
    • Preferential seating near the teacher or board.
    • Use of a reading guide or highlighter.
    • Modified reading assignments (e.g., shorter passages, simplified text).
    • Provision of notes or outlines.
  • Writing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on writing tasks and tests.
    • Use of assistive technology for writing (e.g., speech-to-text software, word prediction software).
    • Access to a computer for writing assignments.
    • Modified writing assignments (e.g., shorter essays, alternative formats).
    • Use of graphic organizers.
    • Spelling and grammar check software.
  • Spelling Accommodations:
    • Use of a spell checker.
    • Modified spelling lists.
    • Allowing phonetic spelling in early grades or informal writing.
    • Focusing on content rather than spelling accuracy in some assignments.
  • Math Accommodations:
    • Use of a calculator.
    • Extended time on math tests.
    • Provision of math manipulatives.
    • Modified math assignments (e.g., fewer problems, simplified instructions).
    • Use of graph paper to organize math problems.
  • Testing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on tests.
    • Testing in a quiet environment.
    • Read aloud of test questions (when appropriate).
    • Use of assistive technology on tests.
    • Breaks during testing.
  • Classroom Management Accommodations:
    • Preferential seating.
    • Frequent breaks.
    • Clear and concise instructions.
    • Use of visual aids.
    • Opportunities for movement.
    • Check-ins for understanding.

This is not an exhaustive list, and other accommodations may be appropriate depending on the individual student's needs. The 504 committee or ARD committee will determine the specific accommodations that will be included in the student's plan.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents have the right to request a full and individual evaluation for dyslexia at any time, regardless of whether the student has been screened or has received interventions. To request an evaluation, follow these steps:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's school principal, counselor, or special education teacher to express your concerns and request an evaluation for dyslexia. It is helpful to put your request in writing.
  2. Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth.
    • Your child's grade level and teacher.
    • A brief description of your concerns about your child's reading and spelling skills.
    • A statement that you are requesting a full and individual evaluation for dyslexia.
    • Your contact information (phone number and email address).
  3. Timeline: Ysleta ISD has specific timelines for responding to evaluation requests, conducting evaluations, and holding ARD or 504 committee meetings. The district must respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe as defined by state law.
  4. Evaluation Process: The evaluation will typically include a variety of assessments to evaluate your child's reading, spelling, phonological awareness, and other related skills. The evaluation team may include a diagnostician, educational psychologist, speech-language pathologist, and other qualified professionals.
  5. ARD or 504 Committee Meeting: After the evaluation is completed, an ARD committee (for special education) or a 504 committee will be convened to review the evaluation results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services or accommodations. You, as the parent, are a key member of this committee.
  6. Parent Rights: You have the right to participate fully in the evaluation and planning process. You also have the right to disagree with the evaluation results or the recommendations of the ARD or 504 committee. You have recourse through dispute resolution processes outlined by TEA.

Contact Information: If you have any questions about dyslexia services in Ysleta ISD, please contact your child's school or the Ysleta ISD Special Education Department. Contact information can typically be found on the Ysleta ISD website.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
