Dyslexia Services in Klein ISD

Dyslexia Services in Klein ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services and support available to students within Klein Independent School District (ISD) in Texas. It is essential for parents, educators, and students to understand the processes, procedures, and resources related to identifying and supporting students with dyslexia. This guide is based on the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, legal mandates, and Klein ISD's implementation practices.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (hereafter referred to as "the Handbook") is the primary guiding document for dyslexia identification, instruction, and support in Texas public schools. The most recent version is the 2024 edition. It's crucial to consult the Handbook directly for the most up-to-date and comprehensive information.

Key Updates and Considerations from the 2024 Handbook (Consult the Handbook directly for detailed information):

  • Comprehensive Definition of Dyslexia: The Handbook provides a clear and comprehensive definition of dyslexia, emphasizing its neurological origin, its impact on phonological processing, and its unexpectedness in relation to other cognitive abilities.
  • Updated Screening Procedures: The Handbook outlines recommended screening procedures for identifying students at risk for dyslexia, including specific assessments and timelines. Klein ISD adheres to these guidelines.
  • Evidence-Based Instruction: The Handbook emphasizes the use of structured literacy programs that are evidence-based and aligned with the science of reading.
  • Progress Monitoring: Regular progress monitoring is essential for tracking student growth and adjusting instruction as needed. The Handbook provides guidance on selecting and using appropriate progress monitoring tools.
  • Parent Involvement: Parental involvement is crucial for student success. The Handbook encourages schools to communicate effectively with parents and to provide them with resources and support.
  • Assistive Technology: The Handbook acknowledges the potential benefits of assistive technology for students with dyslexia. Klein ISD considers assistive technology on a case-by-case basis, based on individual student needs.
  • Training and Professional Development: The Handbook highlights the importance of ongoing training and professional development for teachers and administrators in dyslexia identification and intervention.

Accessing the Handbook: You can download the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 from the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. The link is provided above. Please familiarize yourself with this important resource.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates that all students in kindergarten and first grade be screened for dyslexia. Klein ISD adheres to these mandates and also conducts screenings at other grade levels when concerns are raised.

Kindergarten Screening:

  • All kindergarten students are screened to identify those at risk for developing dyslexia.
  • Screenings typically focus on foundational literacy skills such as phonological awareness (identifying and manipulating sounds in words), letter knowledge, and rapid naming.

First Grade Screening:

  • All first-grade students are screened for dyslexia.
  • The first-grade screening builds upon the kindergarten screening and may include additional measures of reading and spelling.

Screening at Other Grade Levels:

  • If a teacher, parent, or other school staff member has concerns about a student's reading or spelling skills at any grade level, the student may be screened for dyslexia.
  • These screenings are conducted by trained personnel within Klein ISD.

What Happens After Screening?

  • If the screening indicates that a student is at risk for dyslexia, further evaluation may be recommended.
  • Parents will be notified of the screening results and any recommendations for further action.
  • Early intervention is key to supporting students with dyslexia. Klein ISD provides a range of interventions to address the needs of students identified as being at risk.

Structured Literacy Programs

Klein ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide explicit, systematic, and multi-sensory instruction to students with dyslexia. These programs are based on the science of reading and address the core deficits associated with dyslexia.

Key Characteristics of Structured Literacy Programs:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions made about prior knowledge.
  • Systematic Instruction: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Cumulative Instruction: Previously learned skills are reviewed and integrated into new learning.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Instruction engages multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance learning and memory.
  • Phonological Awareness: Focus on identifying, manipulating, and blending sounds in words.
  • Phonics: Systematic instruction in the relationship between letters and sounds.
  • Fluency: Practice in reading text accurately and quickly.
  • Vocabulary: Explicit instruction in word meanings and vocabulary development.
  • Reading Comprehension: Strategies for understanding and remembering what is read.
  • Spelling: Instruction in spelling rules and patterns.

Examples of Structured Literacy Programs Used in Klein ISD (Subject to change based on district adoption):

Klein ISD uses a variety of state-approved structured literacy programs to address the diverse needs of students with dyslexia. The specific program used will depend on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee or the 504 committee. Consult with your child's school to learn more about the specific program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under special education. The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the severity of the student's dyslexia and the level of support needed.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: Students are eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: 504 plans focus on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Instruction: 504 plans typically do not provide specialized instruction, but may include accommodations to support the student's participation in general education classes.

IEP (Individualized Education Program) under Special Education:

  • Eligibility: Students are eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. Dyslexia can be a specific learning disability under the category of special education.
  • Focus: IEPs focus on providing specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: Services may include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling.
  • Instruction: IEPs include individualized goals and objectives that are designed to address the student's specific learning deficits.

Key Differences:

  • Severity of Disability: Students with more significant learning deficits typically require an IEP to receive the intensive, specialized instruction they need.
  • Level of Support: 504 plans provide accommodations to ensure access to the general education curriculum, while IEPs provide specialized instruction to address underlying learning deficits.
  • Evaluation Process: The evaluation process for an IEP is more comprehensive than the evaluation process for a 504 plan.

The ARD Committee: The Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee is responsible for developing and implementing IEPs for students with disabilities. The ARD committee includes parents, teachers, administrators, and other professionals who have knowledge of the student's needs.

Important Note: The determination of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the individual needs of the student.

Accommodations List

The following is a list of common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia in Klein ISD. The specific accommodations provided will depend on the individual needs of the student and the recommendations of the 504 committee or the ARD committee.

  • Extended Time on Tests and Assignments: Allows students additional time to complete tasks.
  • Preferential Seating: Provides students with a seat in the classroom that minimizes distractions and allows them to focus on instruction.
  • Assistive Technology: May include text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, audiobooks, and other tools.
  • Modified Assignments: May include reduced workload, simplified instructions, or alternative assessments.
  • Audiobooks: Allows students to listen to text while reading along.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Reads text aloud to students.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Allows students to dictate their thoughts and ideas instead of writing.
  • Visual Aids: May include graphic organizers, charts, and diagrams.
  • Multi-Sensory Activities: Engages multiple senses to enhance learning and memory.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allows students to take short breaks to reduce fatigue and maintain focus.
  • Copies of Notes: Provides students with copies of teacher notes to reduce the demands of note-taking.
  • Use of a Calculator: For students who struggle with math facts.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Reduces distractions during testing.
  • Reading Aloud: Allows students to read aloud to themselves or to a teacher during tests.
  • Highlighted Text: Highlights key information in textbooks or other materials.
  • Simplified Instructions: Provides clear and concise instructions.
  • Check for Understanding: Teachers regularly check for student understanding.
  • Frequent Feedback: Provides students with frequent feedback on their progress.

Important Note: This is not an exhaustive list, and other accommodations may be appropriate depending on the individual needs of the student. Accommodations should be reviewed and adjusted as needed to ensure that they are effective in supporting the student's learning.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading or spelling skills, you can request an evaluation for dyslexia. The following steps outline the process for requesting an evaluation in Klein ISD:

  1. Contact Your Child's Teacher: The first step is to contact your child's teacher to discuss your concerns. The teacher can provide valuable insights into your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or Principal: If you continue to have concerns after speaking with the teacher, contact the school counselor or principal. They can provide you with information about the school's policies and procedures for evaluating students for dyslexia.
  3. Submit a Written Request: To formally request an evaluation, you must submit a written request to the school principal or the school's special education department. The request should include the following information:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your child's grade level and school
    • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading or spelling skills
    • Any relevant information about your child's medical or educational history
    • Your contact information
  4. School's Response: Upon receiving your written request, the school is required to respond within a specified timeframe (typically within 15 school days). The school will either agree to evaluate your child or provide you with a written explanation of why they are not recommending an evaluation.
  5. Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to evaluate your child, they will conduct a comprehensive evaluation to determine if your child has dyslexia and what type of support is needed. The evaluation may include:
    • Review of your child's academic records
    • Observations of your child in the classroom
    • Assessments of your child's reading, spelling, and language skills
  6. Evaluation Results: Once the evaluation is complete, the school will provide you with a written report of the results. The report will include information about your child's strengths and weaknesses, as well as recommendations for intervention and support.
  7. ARD or 504 Meeting: If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia, the school will schedule an ARD (for special education eligibility) or 504 meeting to develop an appropriate plan for your child.

Important Contact Information: Contact your child's school directly to speak with the principal, counselor, or special education staff.

Legal Rights: Parents have specific rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. You have the right to participate in all meetings regarding your child's education and to receive copies of all relevant documents. If you have concerns about the school's evaluation or the services being provided to your child, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency.

Disclaimer: This guide is intended to provide general information about dyslexia services in Klein ISD. It is not a substitute for legal advice or professional guidance. Please consult with your child's school or the Klein ISD Special Education Department for specific information about your child's individual needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
