What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a very important meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code 29. It's designed to create or review an Individualized Education Program, often called an IEP, for your child. If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their ability to learn in a general education setting, the ARD committee will determine the appropriate special education services and supports needed to help them succeed. Think of it as a collaborative effort to tailor your child's education to their specific needs.

The ARD meeting is a team effort, and you, as the parent, are a critical member of that team. Other members typically include a school administrator or designee, one or more of your child's teachers (both general education and special education, if applicable), an assessment personnel (like an educational diagnostician or psychologist), and other relevant professionals depending on your child's specific needs. For example, if your child receives speech therapy, the speech therapist will likely attend. College Station ISD might also include other specialists or related service providers as necessary. Your child may also attend, especially as they get older, to participate in the process and advocate for their own needs. The purpose of the team is to share information, discuss your child's strengths and areas where they need support, and make decisions about their educational program. The ultimate goal is to develop an IEP that is individualized to your child's unique circumstances, providing them with the necessary tools and support to make progress in school.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is very important because it gives you time to prepare, gather any necessary documents, and arrange for support. This notice must include several key pieces of information. It must tell you the purpose of the meeting - for example, is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility for special education, an annual ARD to review the IEP, or a meeting to address a specific concern? The notice must also inform you who will be attending the meeting and explain your right to participate. Crucially, it will also explain the options the committee will be discussing. The notice should also include a phone number or contact information if you have any questions or need to reschedule the meeting.

It's important to carefully review the notice as soon as you receive it. If the proposed meeting time doesn't work for you, don't hesitate to contact the school to reschedule. Your participation is vital, and the district is obligated to make reasonable efforts to accommodate your schedule. If you feel like you need more time to prepare or gather information, reaching out to reschedule can provide that opportunity. It's also important to know that you can waive the five-day notice requirement if you wish to meet sooner. This is usually done in writing, and it might be beneficial if there's an urgent situation or if you prefer to address the issues promptly. However, it is never required, and you should only waive the notice if you are truly comfortable doing so.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Being prepared for your child's ARD meeting can make a significant difference in the outcome. You are the expert on your child, and your insights are invaluable to the ARD committee. Therefore, gathering and bringing relevant information can help ensure that your child's IEP reflects their unique needs and strengths. Start by collecting any prior evaluations or assessments your child has undergone, whether they were conducted by the school, private therapists, or medical professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy reports, and any other diagnostic information. These reports provide valuable data points that can inform the development of appropriate goals and services.

Doctor's letters and medical records are also essential, especially if your child has any medical conditions that impact their learning or behavior. This might include information about ADHD, sensory processing disorder, anxiety, or any other relevant medical diagnoses. Sharing these records helps the ARD committee understand the whole child and tailor the IEP accordingly. It's also helpful to bring any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, and examples of your child's work. These documents provide a clear picture of your child's academic performance and areas where they may be struggling. Perhaps most importantly, prepare a written list of your concerns and priorities for your child. This can help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your questions and concerns are addressed. This list is also useful in case emotions run high, and you feel like you may forget to mention key items. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can help you take notes, ask questions, and provide emotional support during what can be a stressful process.

Finally, you have the right to audio record the ARD meeting in Texas, provided that you give the school district notice that you will be doing so. This can be extremely helpful for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that all decisions are accurately documented. Many parents find that recording the meeting allows them to focus on the conversation without having to worry about taking detailed notes.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The "10-Day Recess Rule" is a powerful tool available to every member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent. It allows anyone on the team to request a temporary pause, or "recess," of the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days. This recess provides an opportunity to gather more information, consult with outside experts, or simply take time to process the information discussed and make more informed decisions. It's critical to remember that invoking this rule is your right, and you should not hesitate to do so if you feel overwhelmed or unsure about any aspect of the IEP being developed.

You might invoke the 10-day recess rule if, for example, you need time to obtain an independent evaluation of your child, consult with a specialist about a particular service, or simply review the proposed IEP more carefully. Perhaps a new concern arose during the meeting that requires further investigation. Maybe you just feel like you need more time to think about the proposed placement or services. Any of these reasons are valid. To invoke the rule, simply state during the ARD meeting that you would like to request a recess of up to 10 school days. There is no special form to fill out, and you do not need to provide a detailed explanation for your request. The team cannot deny your request for a recess, although they may ask you about the purpose to better understand how to use the time effectively. Once the recess is invoked, the ARD committee will reconvene within 10 school days to continue the meeting and finalize the IEP.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the cornerstone of your child's special education program. It outlines their present levels of academic and functional performance, sets measurable annual goals, specifies the services and supports they will receive, determines their placement in the least restrictive environment, and lists any necessary accommodations and modifications. At the ARD meeting, the team will collaboratively discuss and make decisions about each of these components. "Present levels" describes your child's current skills and abilities in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, and behavior. This section forms the foundation for setting realistic and achievable goals. The "annual goals" are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) targets that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be directly related to your child's needs and should be ambitious yet attainable. The "services" section outlines the special education and related services your child will receive, such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or specialized instruction. This section will specify the frequency, duration, and location of each service.

The IEP also determines your child's "placement," which refers to the educational setting where they will receive their special education services. The goal is to place your child in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the greatest extent appropriate. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, depending on their individual needs. Finally, the IEP will list any "accommodations and modifications" that your child needs to access the general education curriculum and demonstrate their learning. Accommodations are changes to how the material is presented or assessed, while modifications are changes to the content or expectations of the curriculum. Remember that you do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is important to understand that as a parent, you have certain rights, especially when you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that you are not obligated to accept special education services for your child if you do not believe it is in their best interest. If you refuse consent for initial placement, your child will not receive special education services. You also have the right to disagree with the IEP that is developed by the ARD committee. You are not required to sign the IEP if you do not agree with its contents. In this case, the district may implement the portions of the IEP with which you agree, but they cannot implement the portions with which you disagree.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the evaluation conducted by the school. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. This can provide a second opinion and help you better understand your child's needs. Finally, even if you sign the IEP, you can write "Parent Disagrees" on the document next to your signature. This indicates that you have concerns about the IEP and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all attempts to resolve disagreements with the ARD committee and you still feel that your child's rights are being violated, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and the Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89. First, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This option is best suited for issues related to procedural violations of IDEA, such as failure to provide required notice or failure to follow proper ARD procedures.

Second, you can request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. This is also a free service offered by TEA. Mediation can be a valuable tool for resolving disputes in a collaborative and non-adversarial manner. Third, you have the right to request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the issues in dispute. This is a more complex and time-consuming process than filing a complaint or requesting mediation, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue a due process hearing. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process is an excellent resource to access your rights under IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
