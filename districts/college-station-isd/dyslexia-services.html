Dyslexia Services in College Station ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services offered within College Station Independent School District (CSISD), aligning with Texas regulations and best practices. It is intended to be a resource for parents, students, and educators.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The foundation of dyslexia services in Texas, and therefore CSISD, is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. The 2024 edition (and any subsequent revisions) serves as the primary guide for identification, instruction, and support of students with dyslexia. CSISD adheres strictly to the guidelines outlined in this handbook. It's critical that parents and educators familiarize themselves with this document. You can access the latest version on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Key areas covered in the Handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia
  • Identification Procedures
  • Components of Effective Dyslexia Intervention
  • Progress Monitoring
  • Parent Involvement
  • Legal Requirements under state and federal law (e.g., IDEA, Section 504)

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. CSISD conducts universal screening using instruments approved by TEA and aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. This screening helps to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. Further, screening may be conducted at other grade levels if concerns arise from teachers, parents, or the student themselves. The specific screening tools used are selected based on best practices and district policy, often incorporating measures of phonological awareness, rapid naming, and letter-sound correspondence. Parents will be notified if their child is identified as potentially at-risk based on the screening results.

Structured Literacy Programs

CSISD employs structured literacy approaches in its dyslexia intervention programs. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and cumulative approach to teaching reading and spelling. This means instruction is:

  • Explicit: Concepts are taught directly and clearly.
  • Systematic: Instruction follows a logical sequence and builds upon previously learned skills.
  • Cumulative: Previously taught concepts are consistently reviewed and integrated into new learning.

These programs often focus on the following components:

  • Phonological Awareness
  • Phonics
  • Fluency
  • Vocabulary
  • Reading Comprehension

Specific program names and implementations may vary depending on the campus and student needs, but all programs will adhere to the principles of structured literacy outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Contact your child's school or the CSISD Special Education Department for specific program information.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). Understanding the differences is crucial:

  • 504 Plan: Provides accommodations and modifications to ensure a student with a disability has equal access to the general education curriculum. Eligibility is based on a broader definition of disability than IDEA. A student with dyslexia who does not require specialized instruction may be served under a 504 plan.
  • IEP (IDEA): Provides specialized instruction tailored to the student's individual needs in addition to accommodations. Eligibility requires the student to have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and necessitates specially designed instruction. If a student with dyslexia requires a comprehensive intervention program to address their reading difficulties, they may be eligible for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability (SLD).

The determination of whether a student requires a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team that includes parents, teachers, and other relevant professionals.

Accommodations List

Common accommodations for students with dyslexia may include (but are not limited to):

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Use of assistive technology (e.g., text-to-speech, speech-to-text)
  • Preferential seating
  • Modified assignments
  • Oral administration of tests
  • Use of graphic organizers
  • Access to notes or outlines
  • Multisensory teaching strategies

The specific accommodations provided will be individualized based on the student's needs and outlined in their 504 plan or IEP.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to discuss your concerns. You can formally request a full and individual evaluation for dyslexia. This request should be made in writing to the school principal or the special education department. The school will then initiate the evaluation process, which will include assessments to determine if the student meets the criteria for dyslexia as defined by the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. You will be an active participant in the evaluation process and the development of any subsequent 504 plan or IEP.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
