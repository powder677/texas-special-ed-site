Submit a written evaluation request today — verbal requests do not start the legal clock. College Station ISD has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the evaluation. Special Education in College Station ISD: What Every Parent Needs to Know

If you're a parent of a child with a disability in College Station ISD, you're likely navigating a complex and sometimes overwhelming system. Special education, as defined by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas law, is designed to provide individualized instruction and related services to children with disabilities who require it to access a free and appropriate public education (FAPE). This means that College Station ISD is legally obligated to identify, evaluate, and provide services to eligible students with disabilities.

College Station ISD operates under the oversight of the Texas Education Agency (TEA). TEA provides guidance and resources to school districts across the state. Additionally, College Station ISD benefits from the support of TEA Region 6 Education Service Center, which offers professional development, technical assistance, and other services to help ensure that students with disabilities receive the appropriate supports. It's important to remember that you, as a parent, are a crucial member of your child's educational team, and you have enforceable legal rights throughout the entire special education process. These rights are designed to ensure your voice is heard and your child's needs are met within College Station ISD.

Understanding your rights is paramount. The special education system in Texas, and specifically in College Station ISD, can be difficult to navigate. The law provides you with specific procedural safeguards designed to protect your child's right to a free and appropriate public education. Take the time to learn your rights and don't hesitate to seek assistance when you need it. Start by familiarizing yourself with the TEA's "Parent's Guide to the Admission, Review, and Dismissal Process."

How to Request a Special Education Evaluation in College Station ISD

The first step in accessing special education services for your child in College Station ISD is typically a referral for a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). This evaluation is designed to determine if your child has a disability that impacts their ability to learn and, if so, what specific supports and services they need. It's crucial to understand that a parent must submit a written request for an evaluation. A verbal request is not sufficient to trigger the legal timelines.

Once College Station ISD receives your written consent for the evaluation, the district has 45 school days to complete the FIE. It's imperative to keep a record of the date you provided consent. Also keep in mind that College Station ISD has a legal obligation under Child Find laws to proactively identify students who may need special education services, even if you haven't made a formal request. If you suspect your child has a disability and is not receiving the support they need, don't wait. Write a letter requesting a Full Individual Evaluation, clearly stating your concerns and the reasons you believe your child may have a disability. Keep a copy of the letter for your records, and send it to the school principal and special education director at College Station ISD via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides you with proof that your request was received.

ARD Meetings in College Station ISD: Your Rights at the Table

The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the cornerstone of the special education process in College Station ISD. It's where decisions are made about your child's eligibility for special education, the development of their Individualized Education Program (IEP), and the services they will receive. It is imperative to attend these meetings and be prepared to advocate for your child's needs.

College Station ISD is required to provide you with at least five calendar days' written notice before any ARD meeting, giving you time to review documents and prepare your input. During the ARD meeting, you have the right to request a 10-day recess, meaning a temporary pause in the meeting, to allow you time to consider the information presented and seek outside consultation if needed. This is a powerful tool to avoid being pressured into making decisions on the spot. Remember, you are a vital member of the ARD committee, and your input is essential. It's also crucial to know that you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and sign it later if you are in agreement. If you don't agree with certain aspects of the IEP, you can partially agree and provide written notice of your disagreements.

Being well-prepared for your ARD meetings is paramount. Your voice matters and you must be ready to advocate. Before your next ARD meeting in College Station ISD, download our free ARD preparation checklist to help you organize your thoughts, gather necessary information, and ensure you're ready to actively participate in the process.

Dyslexia Services in College Station ISD

Texas law mandates that College Station ISD screen all students for dyslexia, typically in kindergarten and first grade. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for reading difficulties. It's important to understand that if your child is identified as potentially dyslexic, they are entitled to specific interventions.

The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook outlines the standards that College Station ISD must follow in identifying and supporting students with dyslexia. These students are entitled to structured literacy intervention, which is a research-based approach to teaching reading that is systematic, explicit, and multi-sensory. Depending on the severity of their dyslexia and the impact on their learning, students may also qualify for a 504 Plan or an IEP, providing additional accommodations and support. If your child has not been screened for dyslexia and you have concerns about their reading skills, request a screening in writing from College Station ISD immediately. Document your request and keep a copy for your records.

Discipline, Suspensions, and Your Child's Rights in College Station ISD

Disciplinary actions, particularly suspensions, can be especially challenging for students with disabilities. It's critical to understand your child's rights if they are suspended from College Station ISD schools. If a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, it triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This review determines whether the student's behavior was a manifestation of their disability.

Suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative days also constitutes a Change of Placement under IDEA, requiring an ARD meeting to review the situation and determine the appropriate next steps. This is one of the most time-sensitive situations you will face in special education. You must act within days to protect your child's rights. If your child with disabilities has been suspended from College Station ISD, immediately use the Discipline Defense Kit to understand your rights and advocate effectively for your child's return to school and the appropriate support.

Filing a Complaint Against College Station ISD

Sometimes, despite your best efforts, disagreements with College Station ISD may not be resolved through ARD meetings and informal communication. In such situations, you have several avenues for escalating your concerns. These rights exist regardless of what district staff tell you. Understanding these options is crucial in advocating for your child's needs.

There are four primary escalation paths available to you. First, you can file a TEA State Complaint, which is a free process that the TEA aims to resolve within 60 days. Second, you can request Mediation through the TEA, which is also free and provides a neutral third party to help facilitate a resolution. Third, you have the right to request a Due Process Hearing, a more formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer makes a decision. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe College Station ISD has discriminated against your child based on their disability. If you feel College Station ISD is not meeting its legal obligations to your child, research these escalation paths and determine which is most appropriate for your situation. Taking action to enforce your child's rights is often necessary to ensure they receive the free and appropriate public education they deserve.

Frequently Asked Questions About College Station ISD Special Education

The questions every College Station ISD parent should know the answer to.

How do I request a special education evaluation in College Station ISD? +

Submit a written request to the College Station ISD Special Education Director. The district has 45 school days from your written consent to complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE). Always keep a dated copy of everything you send and follow up in writing.

What are my rights at an ARD meeting in College Station ISD? +

You have the right to 5 days written notice before any ARD meeting, the right to bring an advocate or attorney, the right to request a 10-day recess at any time before decisions are finalized, and the right to refuse to sign the IEP the same day. You can also record the meeting with advance notice under Texas law.

Does College Station ISD have to screen my child for dyslexia? +

Yes. Texas law requires College Station ISD to screen all students for dyslexia. If your child has not been screened, submit a written request to the campus principal immediately. The 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook governs how College Station ISD must identify and serve students with dyslexia.

What if College Station ISD suspends my special education child for more than 10 days? +

A suspension exceeding 10 cumulative school days triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) and is a Change of Placement under IDEA. College Station ISD must convene an ARD meeting. This is a legal emergency — contact a special education advocate immediately.
