Requesting an Evaluation in College Station ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Here's a guide on how to request a special education evaluation in College Station ISD, focusing on key aspects of Texas law.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first and most important step is to submit a written request for a full and individual initial evaluation (FIE). This request formally triggers the school district's obligations under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law. Your request should be addressed to the special education director, principal, or other appropriate school administrator at your child's school. You can typically find contact information on the school district website. Make sure your request is dated and that you keep a copy for your records.

Your written request should clearly state that you are requesting an evaluation for special education services due to concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. While not legally required, it's helpful to briefly describe your specific concerns. For example, you might mention difficulties with reading comprehension, struggles with math concepts, persistent behavioral issues in the classroom, or concerns about speech and language development. Providing specific examples can help the district understand your perspective and the reasons for your request.

Consider including any relevant documentation with your request, such as reports from doctors, therapists, or previous academic assessments. Although the district is legally obligated to consider all sources of information, providing these documents upfront can expedite the process and provide a more comprehensive picture of your child's needs. Clearly state in your letter that you are including these documents.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law establishes a specific timeline for the evaluation process. Once the school district receives your written request, they have 15 school days to decide whether or not to conduct an evaluation. During this time, the school's Response to Intervention (RTI) team (if applicable) may review existing data and implement interventions. They may also contact you to discuss your concerns.

If the district decides to evaluate, they must obtain your written consent to proceed with the evaluation. Once they receive your consent, the school has an additional 45 school days to complete the full and individual evaluation (FIE) and hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to determine eligibility for special education services. Remember that this is 45 school days, not calendar days, so holidays and school breaks are not included in the timeline.

It is crucial to track the timeline carefully. If the district fails to meet the deadlines, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Document all communication with the school district, including dates, times, and names of individuals you spoke with. This documentation will be invaluable if you need to pursue further action.

What the FIE Must Cover

The full and individual evaluation (FIE) must be comprehensive and address all areas of suspected disability. Texas law mandates that the FIE include assessments in all areas related to the student's suspected disability. This may include, but is not limited to: academic achievement, cognitive abilities, communication skills, adaptive behavior, social-emotional development, and motor skills. The evaluation must use technically sound instruments administered by qualified personnel.

The FIE should not rely solely on one test or assessment. Instead, it should incorporate multiple sources of information, including observations, interviews, and standardized assessments. The evaluators should also consider the student's cultural and linguistic background when interpreting the results. You have the right to request specific assessments if you believe they are necessary to accurately evaluate your child's needs.

Child Find Obligations

College Station ISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a "Child Find" obligation. This means they have a legal responsibility to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This applies to children ages 3 through 21. If you suspect your child has a disability but is not receiving the necessary support, the district is obligated to investigate and determine if an evaluation is warranted.

The Child Find mandate is particularly important for children who are homeschooled, attend private schools, or are experiencing difficulties even if they are passing their classes. Even if a student is achieving academically, they may still have a disability that requires special education services to ensure their continued success.

What If They Refuse

If College Station ISD refuses your written request for an evaluation, they must provide you with a written explanation of their decision, including the reasons for the refusal. This is called Prior Written Notice. They must also inform you of your right to disagree with their decision and to seek dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing.

If you disagree with the district's refusal to evaluate, it is highly recommended that you seek legal advice from a special education attorney or advocate. They can help you understand your rights and navigate the dispute resolution process. You also have the right to request an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation (or lack thereof). However, specific criteria and procedures apply to IEEs, so consulting with an attorney or advocate is crucial.

After the Evaluation

Once the FIE is complete, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting will be held. This committee includes you (the parent), school administrators, teachers, and other relevant professionals. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to review the evaluation results, determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law, and, if eligible, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services that will be provided to support their progress. It is crucial to actively participate in the ARD meeting and advocate for your child's needs. You have the right to request revisions to the IEP if you disagree with any of its components. Remember, you are a vital member of the ARD committee and your input is essential to ensuring your child receives an appropriate education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
