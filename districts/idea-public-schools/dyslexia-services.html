Dyslexia Services in IDEA Public Schools

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students attending IDEA Public Schools in Texas. It outlines key components of the dyslexia program, referencing the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and other relevant policies. Please note that this is a general overview and specific implementation may vary slightly between IDEA campuses. Contact your child's school directly for details regarding their individual dyslexia support.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024), published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the primary resource for understanding dyslexia and related disorders, as well as the legal requirements and best practices for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia in Texas public schools, including IDEA Public Schools. The handbook is updated periodically to reflect current research and legal changes. It is crucial for parents, educators, and administrators to familiarize themselves with the content of the handbook. Key aspects covered in the handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides the state's definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook outlines the procedures for screening, testing, and identifying students with dyslexia. It emphasizes a multi-tiered system of support (MTSS) approach, incorporating universal screening, targeted interventions, and comprehensive evaluations.
  • Instructional Strategies: The handbook details research-based, multisensory structured literacy instructional approaches that are effective for teaching students with dyslexia.
  • Accommodation and Modifications: It provides guidance on providing appropriate accommodations and modifications to the curriculum and instruction to support students with dyslexia.
  • Legal Requirements: The handbook outlines the legal requirements related to dyslexia services under state and federal law, including Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook underscores the importance of parental involvement in the identification, evaluation, and support of students with dyslexia.

IDEA Public Schools are required to adhere to the guidelines and procedures outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates that all students in kindergarten and first grade be screened for dyslexia. IDEA Public Schools comply with this requirement. The purpose of screening is to identify students who are at risk for dyslexia early in their academic careers, allowing for timely intervention.

  • Kindergarten Screening: Kindergarten students are typically screened during the latter half of the school year.
  • First Grade Screening: First grade students are typically screened at the beginning of the school year.
  • Screening Tools: IDEA Public Schools utilize TEA-approved screening tools that assess foundational reading skills such as phonological awareness, phonemic awareness, letter-sound knowledge, and rapid naming. Specific screening tools may vary between campuses, but they are all designed to identify potential reading difficulties.
  • Screening Results: Parents will be notified of the screening results. If a student is identified as being at risk for dyslexia, the school will initiate further evaluation and intervention.
  • Ongoing Monitoring: Even after the initial screening, students' reading progress is continuously monitored throughout the year to identify any emerging reading difficulties.

Structured Literacy Programs

Students identified with dyslexia in IDEA Public Schools receive instruction through structured literacy programs. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and sequential approach to teaching reading and spelling. It focuses on the essential components of reading, including:

  • Phonology: The study of speech sounds (phonemes) and their patterns. Instruction emphasizes phonemic awareness skills, such as blending, segmenting, and manipulating sounds.
  • Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics): The relationship between letters (graphemes) and sounds (phonemes). Instruction is systematic and explicit, teaching students how to decode and encode words.
  • Syllable Instruction: Understanding syllable types and patterns to improve reading and spelling skills.
  • Morphology: The study of word parts (morphemes) such as prefixes, suffixes, and root words. Instruction in morphology helps students understand the meaning of words and improve their vocabulary.
  • Syntax: The rules of sentence structure. Instruction in syntax helps students understand how words are combined to form sentences.
  • Semantics: The meaning of words and sentences. Instruction in semantics helps students develop their vocabulary and comprehension skills.

IDEA Public Schools typically implement specific structured literacy programs that are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook's recommendations. Examples of commonly used programs include (but are not limited to, and may vary by campus):

  • Orton-Gillingham based approaches
  • Take Flight: A Comprehensive Intervention for Students with Dyslexia
  • Neuhaus Education Center programs

The specific program used will depend on the individual needs of the student. Parents are encouraged to communicate with the school to understand the specific program being used and how it addresses their child's learning needs. Instruction is usually provided in a small group setting, allowing for individualized attention and support.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support services under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The appropriate plan depends on the severity of the student's dyslexia and the level of support needed.

  • Section 504 Plan: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to the general education environment to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to learning. Students with dyslexia who can access the general curriculum with accommodations may be eligible for a 504 plan. The focus is on removing barriers to learning within the general education classroom.
  • Individualized Education Program (IEP): An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific special education and related services that a student with a disability will receive. Students with dyslexia who require specialized instruction and significant support to access the curriculum may be eligible for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability. The IEP team, including parents, develops individualized goals and objectives for the student.

Key Differences:

  • Eligibility: Section 504 eligibility is broader, encompassing any student with a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. IDEA eligibility requires that the student has a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Services: Section 504 provides accommodations and modifications. IDEA provides specialized instruction and related services.
  • Funding: IDEA provides federal funding for special education services. Section 504 does not provide direct funding.
  • Evaluation: IDEA requires a comprehensive evaluation to determine eligibility. Section 504 requires an evaluation, but the scope may be less extensive.
  • IEP: IEP is only for students receiving services under IDEA.

The school's evaluation team will determine whether a student is eligible for a 504 plan or an IEP based on the evaluation results and the student's individual needs. Parents are integral members of the evaluation team.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way a student learns the material but do not change the content or expectations of the curriculum. They are designed to provide students with equal access to learning. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia in IDEA Public Schools include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Audio Books: Providing access to audio versions of textbooks and other reading materials.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that reads text aloud.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Using software that converts speech into text.
  • Preferential Seating: Providing seating that minimizes distractions and allows the student to focus on instruction.
  • Copies of Notes: Providing copies of notes from the teacher or a peer.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or format of assignments.
  • Reduced Workload: Reducing the amount of work assigned.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing for frequent breaks to prevent fatigue.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Providing graphic organizers to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Highlighting Key Information: Allowing the student to highlight key information in texts.
  • Dictation: Allowing the student to dictate answers to tests or assignments.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology devices such as word processors and spell checkers.
  • Multisensory Teaching Strategies: Using multisensory techniques to reinforce learning.
  • Use of Manipulatives: Using manipulatives to help students understand concepts.
  • Chunking of Information: Breaking down complex information into smaller, more manageable chunks.
  • Clear and Concise Instructions: Providing clear and concise instructions.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids to support learning.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet testing environment.

The specific accommodations provided will be tailored to the individual needs of the student and outlined in their 504 plan or IEP. Parent input is vital in determining effective accommodations.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from IDEA Public Schools. The request should be made in writing to the school principal or special education director. Your written request should include:

  • Your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your child's grade level and current school.
  • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading skills. Provide specific examples of challenges your child is facing.
  • Any relevant information, such as previous testing or interventions.
  • Your contact information (phone number and email address).

Upon receiving your written request, the school is required to respond within a reasonable timeframe, as outlined in state and federal law. The school will likely:

  • Contact you to discuss your concerns.
  • Review your child's academic records and classroom performance.
  • Conduct informal assessments to gather additional information.
  • Convene an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee (for students suspected of needing special education) or a 504 committee to determine if a formal evaluation is warranted.

If the committee determines that an evaluation is necessary, the school will obtain your written consent before proceeding. The evaluation will assess your child's reading skills, including phonological awareness, decoding, reading fluency, and reading comprehension. Following the evaluation, the committee will meet to review the results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services under IDEA or accommodations under Section 504. You have the right to receive a copy of the evaluation report.

Important Considerations:

  • Keep detailed records of your communication with the school, including dates, names, and a summary of conversations.
  • Actively participate in all meetings and share your insights about your child's strengths and challenges.
  • Familiarize yourself with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and your rights as a parent.
  • Don't hesitate to ask questions and seek clarification if you are unsure about anything.
  • If you are not satisfied with the school's response, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

This guide is intended to provide general information about dyslexia services in IDEA Public Schools. Please consult with your child's school and the Texas Education Agency for more specific information and guidance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
