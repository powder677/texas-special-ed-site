Dispute & Discipline Rights in IDEA Public Schools

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Discipline Rights & Grievances in IDEA Public Schools: An Urgent Guide

Discipline Rights & Grievances in IDEA Public Schools: An Urgent Guide

If your child with an IEP is facing suspension or other disciplinary action in IDEA Public Schools, ACT IMMEDIATELY! Time is of the essence. This guide is designed to give you a basic overview of your rights and what steps you can take. It is not a substitute for legal advice. Consult with a special education attorney or advocate as soon as possible.

This guide is for parents facing the nightmare of disciplinary action against their child with an IEP in IDEA Public Schools. Navigating this system can be overwhelming and intimidating. This guide will provide a framework for understanding your child's rights and the steps you can take to protect them. Remember, your child has rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

IDEA provides specific protections for students with disabilities facing disciplinary action. One of the most critical aspects of these protections is the "10-day rule." Here's what you need to know:

  • Total Days Matter: The 10-day rule applies not just to a single suspension, but to cumulative suspensions within a school year. If your child has been suspended for a total of 10 school days or more in a school year, IDEA's protections are triggered. It doesn't matter if those suspensions were for different reasons or on different dates.
  • Counting Days: Include every day your child is removed from their regular educational setting due to a suspension. In-school suspensions count towards the 10-day limit if your child is not able to appropriately participate in the general curriculum and receive the services outlined in the IEP.
  • What Happens After 10 Days?: Once your child has been suspended for more than 10 days in a school year, IDEA requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This is a critical process to determine if your child's behavior was related to their disability.
  • Special Circumstances: Even if your child hasn't reached 10 days, there are specific circumstances that can trigger IDEA protections and MDR requirements. These include suspensions or removals that constitute a "Change of Placement" (discussed below).
  • School's Responsibility: The school is legally obligated to initiate the MDR process if the 10-day threshold is reached or a Change of Placement is deemed to occur. Don't wait for them. Track your child's suspension days and be proactive in demanding an MDR.
  • Documentation is Key: Keep meticulous records of every suspension, including dates, reasons, and the number of days. This is essential for protecting your child's rights.

Example: Your child was suspended for 3 days in October for disruptive behavior, 2 days in November for being late to class, and 6 days in January for fighting. This totals 11 days of suspension. The school is legally required to conduct a Manifestation Determination Review.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

The Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is a crucial meeting to determine if your child's misbehavior was a "manifestation" of their disability. This means the IEP team must decide if the behavior was caused by or directly related to your child's disability, or if it was the direct result of the school's failure to implement your child's IEP.

  • Who Attends?: The IEP team, including you (the parent), relevant school personnel (teachers, administrators, specialists), and potentially your child (depending on their age and abilities).
  • What Happens at the MDR?: The team will review all relevant information, including your child's IEP, teacher observations, disciplinary records, and any information you provide. You MUST bring your own evidence to support your claim that the behavior is linked to the disability or a failure of IEP implementation!
  • Two Critical Questions: The team must answer these two questions:
    1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
    2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP?
  • If the Behavior is a Manifestation: If the team answers "yes" to either of these questions, the behavior is considered a manifestation of the disability. In this case:
    • Your child cannot be suspended for more than 10 days (unless it involves weapons, drugs, or serious bodily injury, see below).
    • The IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) if one hasn't been done already, and create or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior.
    • The school must return your child to their original placement (unless you and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the BIP).
  • If the Behavior is NOT a Manifestation: If the team answers "no" to both questions, the behavior is NOT considered a manifestation of the disability. In this case:
    • The school can discipline your child in the same way as they would a non-disabled student.
    • However, the school MUST continue to provide your child with a Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) during the disciplinary period. This means they must continue to receive the services outlined in their IEP, even if they are suspended or placed in an alternative setting.
  • Your Role is Crucial: You are the most important advocate for your child at the MDR! Be prepared to explain how your child's disability affects their behavior. Provide specific examples and documentation. Don't be afraid to challenge the school's conclusions if you disagree. If you feel the IEP is not being properly implemented, document every instance where the school is not following the IEP.
  • Special Circumstances: IDEA allows school personnel to remove a student with a disability to an interim alternative educational setting for up to 45 school days (regardless of whether the behavior is a manifestation) if the student:
    • Carries a weapon to school or possesses a weapon at school.
    • Knowingly possesses or uses illegal drugs, or sells or solicits the sale of a controlled substance, at school.
    • Has inflicted serious bodily injury upon another person while at school, on school premises, or at a school function.

Change of Placement

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a disciplinary action results in your child being removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or for a pattern of removals that constitute a change in placement. Even if the individual removals are for less than 10 days, a pattern can still trigger a Change of Placement and require an MDR.

  • What Constitutes a Pattern?: Factors that determine if a pattern exists include:
    • The length of each removal.
    • The total amount of time the student is removed.
    • The proximity of the removals to one another.
    • The nature of the student's behavior.
  • Why It Matters: A Change of Placement triggers the same protections as exceeding 10 cumulative suspension days. This includes the requirement for an MDR.
  • Example: Your child is repeatedly suspended for 2-3 days every other week for disruptive behavior. Even though no single suspension exceeds 10 days, the pattern of removals could be considered a Change of Placement, requiring an MDR.
  • Fighting Back: The school might try to argue that a series of short suspensions doesn't constitute a Change of Placement. Be prepared to argue that the cumulative effect of these suspensions disrupts your child's education and constitutes a change in placement, thereby triggering an MDR.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe IDEA Public Schools has violated your child's rights under IDEA, you have the right to file a grievance. Often, IDEA schools will have a specific grievance process outlined in their policy manuals. Usually this process begins with a Level 1 Grievance filed at the campus level.

  • Review School Policy: Obtain a copy of IDEA Public Schools' grievance policy. This document will outline the specific procedures and timelines for filing a grievance. Usually, this can be found on their website or by requesting a copy from the school administration.
  • Document Everything: Before filing a grievance, gather all relevant documentation, including suspension notices, IEP documents, communication with school staff, and any other evidence supporting your claim.
  • Formal Written Grievance: Your grievance must be in writing. Clearly state the specific violation of IDEA that you believe has occurred. Be specific about the dates, incidents, and individuals involved. Explain how the violation has harmed your child.
  • Deadlines: Pay close attention to the deadlines for filing a Level 1 grievance. Missing the deadline could jeopardize your ability to pursue further action. Usually these deadlines are very short and must be carefully followed.
  • Submission: Submit your written grievance to the designated school official, as outlined in the school's policy. Usually this is the principal of the campus. Keep a copy of your grievance for your records, as well as any proof of submission you may be able to obtain.
  • Response: The school is required to respond to your grievance within a specific timeframe (usually 10-15 business days). The response will outline the school's position and any actions they intend to take.
  • Unresolved Grievance: If you are not satisfied with the school's response to your Level 1 grievance, you typically have the right to appeal to the next level (e.g., district level). Refer to the school's grievance policy for the specific procedures for appealing.

TEA State Complaint

You have the right to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe IDEA Public Schools has violated IDEA. This is a formal process that can lead to a TEA investigation and potential corrective action.

  • When to File: A TEA complaint can be filed concurrently with or after a Level 1 or 2 Grievance. It's often used when the grievance process is unsuccessful or when the violation is particularly serious.
  • Complaint Requirements: Your complaint to TEA must:
    • Be in writing.
    • State the specific violation of IDEA that you believe has occurred.
    • Include the facts on which your complaint is based (dates, incidents, individuals involved).
    • Be signed and dated.
    • Allege a violation that occurred not more than one year prior to the date the complaint is received.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision. This process can take several months.
  • Corrective Action: If TEA finds that IDEA Public Schools has violated IDEA, they may order the school to take corrective action, such as providing compensatory services to your child, revising school policies, or providing staff training.
  • TEA Website: Visit the TEA website for detailed information on the complaint process and the required complaint form. Search "TEA Special Education Complaints".

Due Process

Due process is the most formal and adversarial method of resolving disputes under IDEA. It involves a formal hearing before an impartial hearing officer who will make a legally binding decision.

  • When to Request Due Process: Due process is typically used when other methods of dispute resolution (grievances, TEA complaints) have failed, or when the issue is particularly serious (e.g., a refusal to provide FAPE, a change of placement without parental consent).
  • Legal Representation: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. It is highly recommended that you obtain legal representation from a special education attorney.
  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: To initiate due process, you must file a formal written complaint with IDEA Public Schools and the TEA. The complaint must meet specific requirements under IDEA.
  • Resolution Meeting: IDEA requires a resolution meeting between you and the school district within 30 days of receiving the due process complaint. The purpose of this meeting is to attempt to resolve the dispute without a formal hearing.
  • Due Process Hearing: If the resolution meeting is unsuccessful, a due process hearing will be scheduled. The hearing is similar to a court trial, with both sides presenting evidence and witnesses.
  • Hearing Officer's Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision within a specified timeframe. This decision is legally binding on both parties.
  • Appeals: If you disagree with the hearing officer's decision, you may have the right to appeal to a court of law.
  • Time is CRITICAL: There are strict timelines associated with due process. Missing a deadline could jeopardize your right to pursue legal action.
This guide provides general information only and does not constitute legal advice. You should consult with a special education attorney or advocate as soon as possible if your child is facing disciplinary action. Protect your child's rights!

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
