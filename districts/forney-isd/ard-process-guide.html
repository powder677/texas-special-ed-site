What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

1. What Is an ARD Meeting

If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their ability to learn in the general education setting in Forney ISD, you will be invited to participate in what is called an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is the cornerstone of special education, and it's legally mandated under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which deals with special education services. The ARD committee makes important decisions about your child's education, including determining whether your child is eligible for special education services, developing an Individualized Education Program (IEP) that is tailored to meet your child's unique needs, and deciding where your child will receive those services.

The purpose of the ARD meeting is to bring together a team of individuals who have knowledge and expertise about your child, including you, the parent. You are a vital member of this team and your input is extremely important. The team also typically includes school administrators, general education teachers, special education teachers, and other professionals who can provide valuable insight into your child's academic, social, and emotional development. A school administrator or their designee, who has the authority to commit district resources, will also be present. The team will review your child's strengths and areas of need, discuss appropriate goals for your child to work towards, and determine the specific special education and related services that your child requires to make progress in their education. These services may include things like specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. The ARD committee will also decide the appropriate placement for your child, meaning the setting where they will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom with support, a resource room, or, in more rare situations, a self-contained classroom.

2. Your 5-Day Notice Right

Forney ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is important because it gives you time to prepare, gather any relevant information, and arrange for someone to attend the meeting with you if you would like support. The notice must include the purpose of the meeting (for example, is it to develop an initial IEP, conduct an annual review, or address a specific concern?), the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of the individuals who are expected to attend. This notice must also inform you of your right to participate in the ARD meeting and to bring anyone you choose to the meeting, such as a friend, family member, or advocate. If the meeting will address transition planning for when your child turns 16, the notice must also indicate that transition services will be discussed.

Keep a record of the date you receive the notice. While the district must provide at least five calendar days' notice, you do have the right to waive this requirement in writing. This might be helpful if there's an urgent situation, or you are available to meet sooner. To waive the five-day notice, you would typically need to sign a form provided by the school district indicating your agreement to proceed with the meeting earlier. Carefully consider whether you need the full five days to prepare before agreeing to waive your right to the notice. If you feel pressured to waive the notice, it is perfectly acceptable to decline. The five-day notice is there to protect your rights as a parent and give you adequate time to prepare for this crucial meeting.

3. What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is one of the most important ways you can advocate for their needs. Think of yourself as the foremost expert on your child. It is important for the team to understand your perspective. First, gather any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by outside professionals. This can include psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, or any other relevant reports. If your child has seen a doctor or specialist who has provided a diagnosis or recommendations, bring copies of those letters or reports as well. Collect your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, and any examples of their work that you feel are relevant to the discussion.

Next, prepare a written list of your specific concerns about your child's education and development. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. Be as specific as possible when describing your concerns. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or a professional advocate who is familiar with special education law and procedures. Having someone there to take notes, offer support, and help you articulate your concerns can be invaluable. Finally, remember that it is legal to record the ARD meeting in Texas as long as you provide notice to the other attendees. A voice recorder can be a helpful tool for documenting what was discussed and agreed upon during the meeting. It can also be useful if you need to review the meeting later or share it with someone who was unable to attend.

4. The 10-Day Recess Rule

During the ARD meeting, there may be times when you or another member of the ARD committee feels that more information is needed before making a decision about your child's IEP. In these situations, any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess of up to ten school days. This "10-day recess rule" allows time to gather additional information, such as further evaluations, teacher observations, or expert opinions. It also gives you the opportunity to consult with an advocate or other professionals before making a decision you feel unsure about. The recess provides time to carefully consider the proposed IEP and its implications for your child's education. It also gives the district an opportunity to collect more data and better understand your child's needs.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state during the ARD meeting that you would like to recess the meeting for up to ten school days to gather more information or seek outside consultation. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation for your request, but it is helpful to specify what type of information you are seeking or who you plan to consult with. The other members of the ARD committee cannot deny your request for a recess. The meeting will then be reconvened within ten school days to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP. It is important to use this time wisely to gather the information you need and feel confident in the decisions being made about your child's education.

5. Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the written plan that outlines your child's special education and related services. It's created during the ARD meeting and serves as a blueprint for how your child's educational needs will be met. Several key areas get decided at the ARD meeting and are documented in the IEP. These include present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, which describes your child's current strengths and areas of need. This section should be based on data and observations from a variety of sources, including evaluations, teacher reports, and parent input. The ARD committee will also develop measurable annual goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). Services are then determined, outlining the specific special education and related services that your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling. The IEP will also specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

Placement determines the educational setting where your child will receive their services, such as a general education classroom with support, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The IEP must also include any accommodations or modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum and participate in school activities. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material, while modifications are changes to what a student is expected to learn. Remember that you do not have to sign the IEP document at the end of the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home to review it carefully and consider whether you agree with the recommendations. You can schedule another ARD meeting to discuss any concerns or proposed changes.

6. When You Disagree With the Team

It is crucial to understand that you, as a parent, have certain rights when you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions. One of the most important rights is the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's decision that your child needs special education services, you can decline to give your consent, and the district cannot proceed with providing those services. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you do not agree with its contents. Signing the IEP indicates that you attended the meeting, not that you agree with everything in the document. If you disagree with the IEP, you can write "Parent Disagrees" next to your signature.

Another important right is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with an evaluation conducted by the district. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. Finally, it is always acceptable to write "Parent Disagrees" anywhere in the IEP document where you feel it does not accurately reflect your perspective. Documenting your disagreement is crucial for preserving your rights.

7. Escalation Options

If you have tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions and meetings, and you are still not satisfied with the outcome, there are several formal options available to you. These options are governed by federal law (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code (19 TAC Chapter 89) which you can access via the Legal Framework website. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. The second option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution to your dispute. This is also a free process and can be a less adversarial way to resolve disagreements.

The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer, who will then issue a binding decision. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a Due Process Hearing. Due Process Hearings can be costly and time-consuming, so it is important to consider this option carefully. The fourth option is to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability in programs and activities that receive federal funding. If you believe that your child has been discriminated against because of their disability, you can file a complaint with OCR.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
