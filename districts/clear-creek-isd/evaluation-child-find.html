Requesting an Evaluation in Clear Creek ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Clear Creek ISD

This guide provides information about requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Clear Creek Independent School District (CCISD) in Texas. Understanding the process and your rights is crucial to advocating for your child's educational needs. This information is for informational purposes only and should not be considered legal advice. Consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law for personalized guidance.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is submitting a written request to the school. This request triggers specific legal obligations for the district under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law, specifically the Texas Education Code (TEC) and the Texas Administrative Code (TAC). A verbal request is *not* sufficient to initiate the formal timeline.

Your written request should be clear, concise, and include the following information:

  • Date: The date you are submitting the request.
  • Your Name and Contact Information: Include your full name, address, phone number, and email address.
  • Your Child's Name and Date of Birth: Include your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your Child's School and Grade Level: Specify the school your child attends and their current grade level.
  • Specific Concerns: Clearly describe your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. Be as specific as possible and provide examples. For example, instead of saying "My child is struggling in reading," say "My child has difficulty sounding out words, often guesses at words instead of decoding them, and avoids reading aloud. This has resulted in failing grades on reading comprehension tests."
  • Reason for Requesting Evaluation: State that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation (FIIE) to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education and related services.
  • Documentation (Optional): If you have any relevant documentation, such as medical reports, previous evaluations, or teacher observations, you can include copies with your request. Keep the originals for your records.
  • Request for Communication: Express your preference for how you would like to be contacted regarding the evaluation process (e.g., phone, email).
  • Signature: Sign and date the letter.

Send the written request to the appropriate school personnel. The most appropriate recipient is usually the school principal, the special education director, or the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) Committee chairperson at your child's school. You can confirm who to send the request to by contacting the school's front office. Keep a copy of the request for your records, noting the date you sent it and to whom.

You can send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure you have proof that the school received it. Alternatively, you can hand-deliver the request and ask the recipient to sign and date a copy acknowledging receipt. This documentation will be important if there are any disputes regarding the timeline.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Upon receiving your written request, CCISD has a strict timeline to adhere to under Texas law. This timeline is measured in *school days*, not calendar days. School days exclude weekends, holidays, and school breaks.

The 45-school-day timeline encompasses the following steps:

  • Review of Existing Data (Immediately): The school must immediately review existing data about your child, including academic records, classroom observations, teacher input, and any information you provide.
  • Decision to Evaluate (Within a Reasonable Time): Based on the review of existing data, the school must decide whether to conduct an FIIE. While there's no specifically defined "reasonable time" within the 45-day window for this initial decision, delays in making this decision are scrutinized.
  • Notice of Decision (Without Delay): The school must provide you with written notice of their decision, either to proceed with the evaluation or to refuse the evaluation. This notice must be provided in your native language, if feasible.
  • Consent for Evaluation (If Proceeding): If the school decides to evaluate, they must obtain your written consent before proceeding. The consent form must clearly explain the purpose of the evaluation, the types of assessments that will be conducted, and how the information will be used. You have the right to revoke your consent at any time before the evaluation is completed.
  • Conducting the Evaluation: Once you provide consent, the school has the remainder of the 45-school-day timeline to complete the FIIE. This includes administering various assessments, gathering information from teachers and other professionals, and compiling the evaluation report.
  • ARD Meeting to Discuss Evaluation Results: After the evaluation is completed, the school must schedule an ARD meeting to discuss the results with you. You must receive sufficient notice of the ARD meeting to allow you to prepare and attend. The ARD committee, including you, will review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services.

It is critical to track the 45-school-day timeline. If the school fails to meet these deadlines, it may be considered a denial of a free and appropriate public education (FAPE), which is a violation of IDEA and Texas law.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIIE) must be comprehensive and address all areas of suspected disability. Texas law requires the evaluation to be tailored to your child's individual needs and consider all relevant information. A "cookie cutter" evaluation is not sufficient.

The FIE must include, at a minimum, the following:

  • Assessment in All Areas of Suspected Disability: The evaluation must assess your child's functioning in all areas where a disability is suspected. This could include academic achievement, cognitive abilities, communication skills, motor skills, social-emotional functioning, and adaptive behavior. If you suspect a learning disability, the FIE must include appropriate assessments to rule out or identify a learning disability in specific academic areas.
  • Observations: The evaluators must observe your child in their natural learning environment (e.g., classroom, playground). These observations provide valuable information about your child's behavior and learning style in a real-world setting.
  • Parent Input: The evaluation must consider your input and perspectives on your child's strengths, weaknesses, and educational needs. Your voice is critical to the evaluation process.
  • Teacher Input: The evaluation must include input from your child's teachers regarding their academic performance, classroom behavior, and learning style.
  • Review of Records: The evaluators must review your child's academic records, medical records (with your consent), and any other relevant documentation.
  • Use of Valid and Reliable Assessments: The evaluators must use assessment tools that are valid and reliable for your child's age, cultural background, and language. The assessments must be administered by qualified professionals who are trained in their use and interpretation.
  • Assessment of Adaptive Behavior: The FIE must assess your child's adaptive behavior skills, which are the skills needed to function independently in daily life. This includes skills such as self-care, communication, social skills, and community use.
  • Clear and Comprehensive Report: The FIE report must be clear, comprehensive, and written in language that you can understand. It should describe the assessments that were conducted, the results of the assessments, the evaluators' observations, and the evaluators' recommendations. The report must also explain how the evaluation results relate to your child's educational needs.

If you believe that the FIE is incomplete or does not adequately address your child's needs, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense, under certain circumstances. CCISD may have specific criteria for granting an IEE at public expense, which they must provide to you upon request. If the district believes their evaluation is appropriate they may initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate this. However, you always have the right to obtain a private evaluation at your own expense.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law, in accordance with IDEA, places a "Child Find" obligation on all school districts, including CCISD. This means that CCISD has a legal responsibility to actively seek out and identify children with disabilities who may be in need of special education and related services, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public schools, private schools, or are homeschooled.

Child Find activities include:

  • Public Awareness Campaigns: CCISD must conduct public awareness campaigns to inform parents and the community about the availability of special education services and how to access them.
  • Screening and Referral Programs: CCISD may conduct screening programs to identify children who may be at risk for disabilities. These screenings are typically brief and are not intended to be a substitute for a comprehensive evaluation. Referrals can be made by parents, teachers, doctors, or other professionals who suspect that a child may have a disability.
  • Collaboration with Community Agencies: CCISD must collaborate with community agencies, such as hospitals, clinics, and social service organizations, to identify children with disabilities who may not be receiving services.

If you suspect that your child has a disability, you have the right to contact CCISD and request an evaluation, regardless of whether your child is currently enrolled in a CCISD school. The district cannot deny your request simply because your child is not enrolled or because they have not observed any problems. The Child Find obligation requires them to consider your concerns and initiate an evaluation if there is reason to suspect a disability.

What If They Refuse

If CCISD refuses to conduct an FIIE after receiving your written request, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for the refusal. This notice must be provided in your native language, if feasible. The reasons must be based on a review of existing data and must be supported by evidence.

If you disagree with the district's decision, you have several options:

  • Request a Meeting: Request a meeting with school officials to discuss their decision and to provide additional information about your child's needs. You can bring an advocate or attorney to this meeting.
  • Request Mediation: You can request mediation through the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the district reach a mutually agreeable resolution.
  • File a Complaint with the TEA: You can file a formal complaint with the TEA alleging that CCISD has violated IDEA or Texas special education law. The TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: You can request a due process hearing, which is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's eligibility for special education services. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. Due process hearings are often complex and require significant preparation.

Choosing the right course of action will depend on the specific circumstances of your case. Consulting with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law is highly recommended to understand your rights and options.

After the Evaluation

Following the completion of the FIE, the ARD committee, which includes you, will meet to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law and IDEA. To be eligible, your child must have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires special education and related services.

If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education, the committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports that will be provided to help them achieve those goals. You are a critical member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate in all decisions regarding your child's IEP.

The IEP must include:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): A description of your child's current strengths and weaknesses in all areas of concern.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child to achieve within a year.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the special education and related services that will be provided to your child, including the frequency, duration, and location of those services. Related services may include speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, and transportation.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A description of the accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child to help them access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material, while modifications are changes to what a student is expected to learn.
  • Placement: A description of your child's educational placement, which is the setting where they will receive their special education services. Placement decisions must be based on your child's individual needs and must be made in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

You have the right to disagree with any part of the IEP. If you disagree, you can request another ARD meeting to discuss your concerns. If you still disagree after the second meeting, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing. The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. Throughout the special education process, remember that you are your child's strongest advocate. Stay informed, ask questions, and actively participate in all decisions regarding your child's education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
