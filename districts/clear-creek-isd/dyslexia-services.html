Dyslexia Services in Clear Creek ISD

Dyslexia Services in Clear Creek ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available within Clear Creek Independent School District (CCISD) in accordance with Texas state law and best practices. It covers key aspects of dyslexia identification, intervention, and support.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The cornerstone of dyslexia services in Texas is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024), published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This document serves as the primary resource for educators, parents, and administrators regarding dyslexia identification, instruction, and support. It is crucial to familiarize yourself with this handbook.

Key updates and features of the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook include:

  • Revised Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a precise and updated definition of dyslexia, aligning with current research and best practices. Dyslexia is defined as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Enhanced Screening Procedures: The handbook outlines specific, evidence-based screening procedures that must be implemented to identify students at risk for dyslexia. This includes guidance on universal screening in early grades and targeted screening for students who exhibit warning signs.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook emphasizes the importance of Structured Literacy as the most effective approach to teaching students with dyslexia. It details the essential components of Structured Literacy, including phonological awareness, phonics, morphology, syntax, semantics, and handwriting.
  • Data-Driven Decision Making: The handbook stresses the importance of using data from screening and progress monitoring to inform instructional decisions and ensure that interventions are effective. Regular progress monitoring is essential to track student growth and adjust instruction as needed.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the critical role of parents in supporting their child's literacy development. It provides information and resources for parents to understand dyslexia and advocate for their child's needs.
  • Emphasis on Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The handbook underscores the importance of providing support through a tiered system, with increasing levels of intensity based on student need. This ensures that all students receive the appropriate level of support to succeed.

Accessing the Texas Dyslexia Handbook:

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is available for free download on the TEA website: https://tea.texas.gov/academics/early-childhood-education/early-learning-programs/reading-and-dyslexia/texas-dyslexia-handbook

Parents and educators are strongly encouraged to review this document thoroughly.

Mandatory Screening

Clear Creek ISD adheres to the state's mandatory dyslexia screening requirements. This means that all students in kindergarten and first grade, and any student in grades 2-12 exhibiting characteristics of dyslexia, will be screened to identify potential risk. The screenings are designed to assess key areas related to reading development, including:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds in spoken language.
  • Phonological Memory: The ability to hold phonological information in working memory.
  • Rapid Naming: The ability to quickly name familiar objects, colors, and letters.
  • Letter-Sound Knowledge: The understanding of the relationship between letters and their corresponding sounds.
  • Decoding Skills: The ability to sound out and read words.

Screening Process in CCISD:

  1. Universal Screening (K-1): All students in kindergarten and first grade are screened using standardized assessments administered by trained school personnel.
  2. Targeted Screening (2-12): Students in grades 2-12 who demonstrate characteristics of dyslexia, as identified by teachers, parents, or through academic performance, may undergo further screening. This might include reviewing academic records, observing the student in the classroom, and administering additional assessments.
  3. Parent Notification: Parents are notified of the screening results and any recommendations for further evaluation or intervention.

Understanding Screening Results:

It's important to understand that screening is not a diagnosis. A screening indicates whether a student is at risk for dyslexia and warrants further evaluation. A full diagnostic evaluation is needed to confirm a diagnosis of dyslexia.

Structured Literacy Programs

Clear Creek ISD utilizes Structured Literacy programs as the primary intervention for students identified with dyslexia or at risk for dyslexia. Structured Literacy is an evidence-based approach that explicitly and systematically teaches the foundational skills necessary for reading and spelling. It is characterized by its direct, sequential, cumulative, and multisensory approach.

Key components of Structured Literacy programs used in CCISD typically include:

  • Phonological Awareness: Explicit instruction in identifying, blending, segmenting, and manipulating phonemes (individual sounds) in words.
  • Phonics: Systematic and explicit instruction in the relationships between letters and sounds.
  • Morphology: Instruction in the meaning and structure of words, including prefixes, suffixes, and root words.
  • Syntax: Instruction in sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: Instruction in vocabulary and word meaning.
  • Handwriting: Explicit instruction in letter formation and handwriting skills.

Specific programs used in CCISD may vary by campus, but generally align with the principles of Structured Literacy and are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Contact your child's school to inquire about the specific program being used.

Progress Monitoring: Students receiving Structured Literacy intervention are regularly monitored to track their progress and ensure that the intervention is effective. Data from progress monitoring is used to make adjustments to the intervention as needed.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which type of support is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the impact of dyslexia on their access to the general education curriculum.

Section 504:

  • Eligibility: Students with a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (including learning).
  • Focus: Provides accommodations to ensure equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Process: 504 plans are developed by a team that includes the student, parents, teachers, and administrators.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: Students with one of the 13 specific disabilities listed in IDEA, including a specific learning disability (SLD) such as dyslexia, who require specialized instruction.
  • Focus: Provides specialized instruction and related services to address the student's unique needs and enable them to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Services: May include specialized reading instruction using a Structured Literacy approach, speech-language therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling, in addition to accommodations.
  • Process: IEPs are developed by an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee that includes the student, parents, teachers, administrators, and specialists.

Key Differences:

  • Need for Specialized Instruction: The primary distinction lies in the need for specialized instruction. If a student requires intensive, individualized instruction to address their reading deficits, an IEP may be necessary. If the student can access the general education curriculum with accommodations alone, a 504 plan may be sufficient.
  • Funding: IEPs are federally funded under IDEA, while 504 plans are not.
  • Legal Protections: IEPs provide stronger legal protections and procedural safeguards for students and their families.

The ARD committee (for IEPs) or the 504 committee will determine the most appropriate type of support based on a comprehensive evaluation of the student's needs.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the way instruction is delivered or assessments are administered that allow students with dyslexia to demonstrate their knowledge and skills without being unfairly penalized for their reading difficulties. These accommodations are typically documented in a 504 plan or IEP.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location that minimizes distractions and maximizes access to instruction.
  • Audiobooks and Text-to-Speech Software: Providing access to audio versions of textbooks and other reading materials.
  • Use of Assistive Technology: Allowing the use of tools such as calculators, spell checkers, and speech-to-text software.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments to match the student's reading level.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Scribe: Allowing the student to dictate their answers to a scribe who writes them down.
  • Graphic Organizers: Providing visual aids to help the student organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Incorporating visual, auditory, and kinesthetic activities into instruction.
  • Chunking and Highlighting Text: Breaking down large amounts of text into smaller, more manageable chunks and highlighting key information.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take short breaks during instruction and testing.
  • Dictation Software: Allowing students to use dictation to compose written assignments.

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations provided will vary based on the individual needs of the student. The 504 or ARD committee will determine the most appropriate accommodations based on a comprehensive assessment of the student's strengths and weaknesses.

How to Request an Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, it is important to contact your child's school to request an evaluation. The process for requesting an evaluation in CCISD is as follows:

  1. Contact the School: Begin by contacting your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal. Express your concerns about your child's reading difficulties and request an evaluation for dyslexia. It is helpful to document your concerns in writing.
  2. Provide Documentation: Gather any relevant documentation, such as report cards, previous testing results, and observations of your child's reading difficulties. This information will help the school understand your concerns and make informed decisions.
  3. Written Request: It is recommended to submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. This provides a clear record of your request and helps ensure that it is addressed in a timely manner.
  4. School's Response: The school is required to respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe. They will typically schedule a meeting to discuss your concerns and determine whether an evaluation is warranted.
  5. Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to conduct an evaluation, they will gather information about your child's academic performance, cognitive abilities, and reading skills. This may involve administering standardized tests, conducting classroom observations, and interviewing your child and teachers.
  6. Evaluation Report: Once the evaluation is complete, the school will provide you with a written report summarizing the findings and recommendations. The report will indicate whether or not your child meets the criteria for dyslexia and whether they are eligible for special education services or a 504 plan.
  7. ARD or 504 Meeting: If your child is found to have dyslexia and is eligible for special education services or a 504 plan, the school will schedule an ARD meeting (for IEPs) or a 504 meeting to develop an individualized plan to address your child's needs.

Key Contacts in CCISD:

Contact your child's school directly to get specific contact information for special education personnel. You can also contact the CCISD Special Education Department for general inquiries.

Parent Rights:

Parents have the right to be actively involved in all aspects of their child's education, including the evaluation and development of an IEP or 504 plan. You have the right to request information, ask questions, and advocate for your child's needs. The TEA provides resources on parent rights related to special education.

This guide is intended to provide general information about dyslexia services in Clear Creek ISD. It is not a substitute for professional advice. Please consult with your child's school and relevant professionals to determine the best course of action for your child.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
