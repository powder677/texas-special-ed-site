Dyslexia Services in Tyler ISD

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Tyler ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, specifically the most current version (2024). This handbook provides comprehensive information regarding dyslexia identification, assessment, and intervention strategies. Parents and educators are encouraged to familiarize themselves with this resource. Key areas covered in the handbook include: the definition of dyslexia according to Texas law, characteristics of dyslexia, screening and evaluation procedures, evidence-based interventions, and parent rights and responsibilities. The handbook serves as the foundation for all dyslexia-related services provided within Tyler ISD.

Mandatory Screening

Tyler ISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. The screening typically involves assessments of phonological awareness, rapid automatized naming, and letter-sound knowledge. The specific screening tools used by Tyler ISD are consistent with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) guidelines and are administered by trained personnel. Students who demonstrate indicators of dyslexia on the initial screening may be recommended for further evaluation.

Structured Literacy Programs

Tyler ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide evidence-based intervention for students identified with dyslexia. These programs are research-based and address the core components of reading instruction, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. Common features of structured literacy programs include: systematic and explicit instruction, multisensory teaching techniques, cumulative practice, and ongoing progress monitoring. The specific program chosen for a student will depend on their individual needs and learning style. Examples of structured literacy programs that might be used include, but are not limited to, those listed as meeting the requirements in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Contact your child's school for specific details about programs used at that campus.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support through a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to the learning environment to ensure students with disabilities have equal access to education. To qualify for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. An IEP, on the other hand, is for students with a disability that requires specialized instruction. To qualify for an IEP, a student must meet specific criteria under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). Students with dyslexia may qualify for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability (SLD). The decision to pursue a 504 plan or an IEP will depend on the student's individual needs and the severity of their reading difficulties. An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee makes the determination for IEP eligibility, while a 504 committee makes the determination for 504 plan eligibility.

Accommodations List

The following are examples of common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia through a 504 plan or IEP:

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Preferential seating
  • Audiobooks and text-to-speech software
  • Use of assistive technology
  • Modified assignments
  • Frequent breaks
  • Oral reading of tests
  • Visual aids
  • Multi-sensory learning activities
  • Copies of notes

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations provided will be tailored to meet the individual needs of each student.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia should contact their child's teacher or school counselor to discuss their concerns. A written request for a dyslexia evaluation should be submitted to the school principal. This request should include specific observations and concerns regarding the student's reading abilities. The school will then initiate the evaluation process, which may involve assessments of reading, spelling, and other related skills. Parents will be involved throughout the evaluation process and will receive a copy of the evaluation report. Based on the evaluation results, the school will determine whether the student is eligible for dyslexia services and, if so, will develop a plan to address their individual needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
