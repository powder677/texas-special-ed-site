Dyslexia Services in Irving ISD

Dyslexia Services in Irving ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services offered within Irving Independent School District (ISD) in accordance with Texas state law and guidelines. It outlines key aspects of identification, intervention, and support for students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook serves as the primary resource for educators, parents, and other stakeholders regarding dyslexia in Texas public schools. The 2024 update (or the most current version available at the time of implementation) provides comprehensive guidelines for identification, instruction, and accommodation for students with dyslexia and related disorders. Irving ISD adheres to the principles and practices outlined in this handbook.

Key elements of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a clear and consistent definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Early Identification Procedures: The handbook emphasizes the importance of early screening and identification of students at risk for dyslexia. This includes universal screening in kindergarten and first grade, as well as ongoing progress monitoring to identify students who may be struggling with reading.
  • Structured Literacy Intervention: The handbook mandates the use of structured literacy approaches for students identified with dyslexia. Structured literacy is a research-based approach that emphasizes systematic and explicit instruction in phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension.
  • Progress Monitoring: Regular progress monitoring is crucial to track student progress and adjust instruction as needed. The handbook provides guidance on selecting appropriate assessment tools and using data to inform instructional decisions.
  • Accommodations: The handbook outlines various accommodations that can be provided to students with dyslexia to support their learning in the classroom. These accommodations may include extended time on tests, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the importance of parent involvement in the identification, instruction, and support of students with dyslexia. Parents are valuable partners in the educational process and should be kept informed of their child's progress.
  • Legal Requirements: The handbook details the legal requirements related to dyslexia services in Texas, including the requirements of House Bill 3, which mandates universal screening and structured literacy instruction.

Parents and educators can access the most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. It is crucial to consult this document directly for the most accurate and up-to-date information.

Mandatory Screening

Irving ISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening as mandated by Texas law, specifically House Bill 3. This screening process aims to identify students at risk for dyslexia as early as possible, allowing for timely intervention and support.

The screening process typically involves the following:

  • Universal Screening: All students in kindergarten and first grade are screened for risk factors associated with dyslexia. This usually involves assessments of phonological awareness, letter knowledge, and rapid automatized naming (RAN). Specific screening tools used by Irving ISD are determined at the district level and may vary slightly from year to year. Contact your child's school for information about the specific tools being used.
  • Screening for Older Students: Students in grades 2 and above who are demonstrating reading difficulties or are suspected of having dyslexia may also be screened. This screening is often triggered by teacher observations, parent concerns, or low performance on reading assessments.
  • Screening Results: Parents are notified of the results of the dyslexia screening. If a student is identified as being at risk for dyslexia, the school will develop a plan for intervention and support.
  • Follow-up Assessments: Students who are identified as at-risk during the initial screening may undergo more comprehensive assessments to determine if they have dyslexia and to inform instructional planning.

The goal of the screening process is to identify students who may benefit from early intervention and to prevent reading difficulties from becoming more severe. Early identification and intervention are critical for improving reading outcomes for students with dyslexia.

Structured Literacy Programs

Irving ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide evidence-based instruction for students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is an approach to reading instruction that is systematic, explicit, cumulative, and diagnostic. It focuses on teaching the foundational skills necessary for reading success, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension.

Key characteristics of structured literacy programs include:

  • Systematic and Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught in a logical sequence, and skills are explicitly taught rather than assumed.
  • Phonological Awareness: Instruction focuses on the ability to hear and manipulate the individual sounds (phonemes) in words.
  • Phonics: Instruction focuses on the relationship between letters and sounds and how to use this knowledge to decode words.
  • Fluency: Instruction helps students develop the ability to read accurately, quickly, and with expression.
  • Vocabulary: Instruction focuses on building students' vocabulary knowledge.
  • Comprehension: Instruction focuses on teaching students strategies for understanding what they read.
  • Cumulative: New skills are built upon previously learned skills.
  • Diagnostic: Instruction is tailored to meet the individual needs of each student. Teachers regularly assess student progress and adjust instruction accordingly.

Examples of structured literacy programs that may be used in Irving ISD (the specific programs used may vary by campus and grade level):

  • Take Flight: A Comprehensive Intervention for Students with Dyslexia
  • Neuhaus Basic Language Skills
  • Other TEA approved programs. Parents should check with their child's school for the specific program being used.

These programs are designed to provide students with the intensive, individualized instruction they need to overcome their reading difficulties. The district ensures that teachers implementing these programs receive appropriate training and ongoing support.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or through an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the severity of the student's needs and the type of services required.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (such as learning). Dyslexia can qualify as a disability under Section 504.
  • Focus: 504 plans focus on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: 504 plans typically include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, and assistive technology.
  • Process: The 504 process is less formal than the IEP process. A 504 team, which includes the student's parents, teachers, and other relevant school personnel, develops the 504 plan.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have one of the 13 disabilities listed in IDEA (including a specific learning disability such as dyslexia) and, as a result, need specialized instruction and related services to make progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Focus: IEPs focus on providing specialized instruction and related services to meet the unique needs of students with disabilities.
  • Services: IEPs may include accommodations, modifications, and specialized instruction in reading, writing, and other areas. They can also include related services such as speech therapy or occupational therapy.
  • Process: The IEP process is more formal than the 504 process. An IEP team, which includes the student's parents, teachers, special education staff, and other relevant school personnel, develops the IEP. The IEP must include measurable goals and objectives, as well as a description of the services that will be provided.

Key Differences:

  • Severity of Need: Students with more significant needs who require specialized instruction are typically served under an IEP. Students with less significant needs who primarily require accommodations may be served under a 504 plan.
  • Specialized Instruction: IEPs provide for specialized instruction, while 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by IDEA, while 504 plans are governed by Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. IDEA provides more extensive procedural safeguards for parents and students.

The decision of whether to pursue a 504 plan or an IEP should be made in consultation with school personnel and based on the individual needs of the student.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered or the way students demonstrate their knowledge. They are designed to provide students with dyslexia equal access to the curriculum without changing the content or expectations.

Common accommodations for students with dyslexia may include:

  • Extended Time: Providing additional time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Allowing the student to sit in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or format of assignments to reduce reading and writing demands.
  • Visual Aids: Providing visual supports such as graphic organizers, charts, and diagrams.
  • Auditory Aids: Providing access to audio recordings of lectures and readings.
  • Multi-sensory Instruction: Using visual, auditory, and kinesthetic activities to reinforce learning.
  • Chunking: Breaking down large tasks into smaller, more manageable chunks.
  • Note-taking Assistance: Providing a copy of notes or allowing the student to record lectures.
  • Oral Testing: Allowing the student to answer test questions orally.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the student to use a calculator for math problems.
  • Access to Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing the student to use a computer with spell check for writing assignments.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet location for testing.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take frequent breaks during instruction and testing.
  • Color-Coded Materials: Using colored overlays or colored paper to reduce visual stress.

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will depend on their individual needs. The 504 team or IEP team will determine the appropriate accommodations for each student.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect that their child may have dyslexia have the right to request an evaluation from Irving ISD. The evaluation process is designed to determine if the student has dyslexia and to identify the student's specific learning needs.

The steps for requesting an evaluation are as follows:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, counselor, or principal to express your concerns about your child's reading difficulties.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education department. The request should include your child's name, grade level, and a description of your concerns. While a formal letter is preferred, an email is usually acceptable.
  3. Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, such as classroom performance, standardized test scores, and screening results, to determine if an evaluation is warranted.
  4. Evaluation Process: If an evaluation is warranted, the school will conduct a comprehensive evaluation to assess your child's reading skills, cognitive abilities, and other relevant areas. This evaluation may include standardized tests, informal assessments, and observations.
  5. Evaluation Report: After the evaluation is complete, the school will provide you with a written evaluation report that summarizes the findings and provides recommendations for intervention and support.
  6. Eligibility Meeting: If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia, an eligibility meeting will be held to determine if your child is eligible for special education services under IDEA or accommodations under Section 504.

Parents have the right to participate in all aspects of the evaluation process. You should be informed of your child's progress and provided with opportunities to provide input and ask questions. Irving ISD is committed to working collaboratively with parents to ensure that students with dyslexia receive the support they need to succeed.

Important Note: This guide provides general information about dyslexia services in Irving ISD. Specific policies and procedures may vary. Always consult with your child's school and the district's special education department for the most accurate and up-to-date information. You can also find information on the Irving ISD website.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
