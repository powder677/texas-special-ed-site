Requesting an Evaluation in Irving ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Irving ISD

This guide provides information for parents and guardians who suspect their child may have a disability and require special education services in Irving Independent School District (ISD). It outlines the process for requesting a special education evaluation, focusing on Texas-specific regulations and procedures. Understanding your rights and responsibilities is crucial to ensuring your child receives the appropriate support and services.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating the special education evaluation process is to submit a written request to Irving ISD. This request triggers the district's legal obligation to consider your child's needs. The request should be clear, concise, and delivered to the appropriate school official. Here's a breakdown of what should be included and how to submit it:

  • Content of the Request: The letter should explicitly request a full and individual initial evaluation (FIIE) for special education services. Avoid vague language; be direct and state your concern that your child may have a disability that impacts their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Child's Identifying Information: Include your child's full name, date of birth, student ID number (if applicable), grade level, and the name of the school they attend within Irving ISD.
  • Specific Concerns and Observations: Detail your specific concerns regarding your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental difficulties. Provide concrete examples. For instance, if your child struggles with reading, describe the specific reading challenges you've observed (e.g., difficulty decoding words, slow reading pace, poor comprehension). If your child has behavioral issues, document the specific behaviors, frequency, and the situations in which they occur. Mention any interventions or supports you have already tried at home or at school.
  • Supporting Documentation: Include any relevant supporting documentation, such as medical records, previous evaluations (even if not from Irving ISD), report cards, work samples, or teacher observations. This documentation provides valuable context for the evaluation team.
  • Contact Information: Provide your current contact information, including your phone number and email address, so the school can easily reach you to discuss the request and schedule meetings.
  • Recipient of the Request: The request should be addressed to the appropriate school official. Ideally, this would be the school principal or the special education director/coordinator for your child's campus. Contact the school office to confirm the correct recipient if you are unsure. Sending a copy to both the principal and the special education contact is advisable.
  • Delivery Method: Send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the school received your request and the date of receipt, which is crucial for tracking the 45-school-day timeline. Keep a copy of the letter and the certified mail receipt for your records. Alternatively, you can hand-deliver the letter to the school and request a signed and dated receipt acknowledging their receipt of the letter.

Sample Language: "I am writing to formally request a full and individual initial evaluation (FIIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. I am concerned that [Child's Name] may have a disability that is impacting their educational performance and requires special education services. I have observed [specific concerns and examples]. I have attached [list of documents]. Please contact me at [phone number] or [email address] to discuss this further."

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a specific timeline for Irving ISD to respond to your written request for a special education evaluation. This timeline is critical, and you should carefully monitor its progress. The timeline is based on "school days," which are days when students are in attendance for instruction. Weekends, holidays, and school closures do not count as school days.

  • Upon Receipt of the Request: Once Irving ISD receives your written request, they have 15 school days to make a decision about whether or not to conduct an evaluation.
  • Decision to Evaluate: If the district decides to evaluate, they must obtain your consent for the evaluation. They will send you a Prior Written Notice (PWN) and a consent form. Review the PWN carefully to understand the proposed evaluation plan. Provide your consent promptly to avoid delaying the evaluation process.
  • Evaluation Completion: Once the district receives your signed consent, they have 45 school days to complete the full and individual initial evaluation (FIIE) and hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the results.
  • Decision Not to Evaluate: If the district decides not to evaluate your child, they must provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) explaining the reasons for their decision. This PWN must include a description of other options considered and the reasons why those options were rejected. You have the right to challenge this decision (see the section "What If They Refuse").

Important Considerations: The 45-school-day timeline can be extended only under very specific circumstances, such as when the parent repeatedly fails or refuses to produce the child for the evaluation. The district must document the reasons for any delays. It's crucial to maintain open communication with the school and document all interactions (e.g., dates of phone calls, emails). If the district fails to adhere to the timeline without a valid reason, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if your child has a disability that qualifies them for special education services and to identify their specific educational needs. The FIE must be tailored to your child's individual circumstances and address all areas of suspected disability. Texas Education Agency (TEA) guidelines provide specific requirements for the FIE.

  • Areas of Suspected Disability: The FIE must assess all areas related to the suspected disability. For example, if you are concerned about your child's reading skills, the evaluation should include assessments of phonological awareness, decoding skills, reading fluency, and reading comprehension. If you are concerned about their behavior, the evaluation should include observations in various settings, interviews with teachers and parents, and possibly standardized behavioral assessments.
  • Comprehensive Assessment: The FIE should include a variety of assessment methods, such as standardized tests, observations, interviews, and a review of your child's records. Relying solely on one test is generally insufficient.
  • Qualified Professionals: The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in assessing children with disabilities. This may include educational diagnosticians, psychologists, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, and other specialists.
  • Parent Input: The evaluation team must consider your input as a parent. Your perspectives on your child's strengths, weaknesses, and developmental history are invaluable to the evaluation process.
  • Specific Areas to Consider: While not exhaustive, the FIE might cover the following areas depending on the suspected disability:
    • Cognitive Abilities: Assessing intellectual functioning and learning potential.
    • Academic Achievement: Evaluating reading, writing, math, and other academic skills.
    • Communication Skills: Assessing speech, language, and communication abilities.
    • Motor Skills: Evaluating fine motor and gross motor skills.
    • Social-Emotional Functioning: Assessing emotional regulation, social skills, and behavior.
    • Adaptive Behavior: Evaluating the child's ability to function independently in daily life.
    • Health and Medical History: Reviewing relevant medical information.
  • Cultural and Linguistic Appropriateness: The evaluation must be conducted in your child's native language or mode of communication, unless it is clearly not feasible to do so. The assessment tools and procedures must be culturally and linguistically appropriate.

Review the Evaluation Plan: Before you provide consent for the evaluation, carefully review the proposed evaluation plan outlined in the Prior Written Notice. If you have any questions or concerns about the proposed assessments, discuss them with the school. You have the right to request additional assessments if you believe they are necessary to fully evaluate your child's needs.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law, in accordance with the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), places a "Child Find" obligation on all school districts, including Irving ISD. This means that the district has a proactive responsibility to identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or homeschooled. This obligation extends to children from birth through age 21.

  • Proactive Identification: Irving ISD must have procedures in place to actively search for children who may have disabilities. This includes screening programs, awareness campaigns, and collaboration with community agencies.
  • Referral Process: The district must have a clear and accessible referral process for parents, teachers, and other individuals who suspect that a child may have a disability. Your written request for an evaluation fulfills this referral requirement.
  • Public Awareness: Irving ISD is required to inform the public about the availability of special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation.
  • Timely Evaluation: Once a child is identified or referred, the district must conduct an evaluation in a timely manner, following the 45-school-day timeline outlined earlier.
  • Confidentiality: The district must protect the confidentiality of student records and information related to the evaluation process.

Knowing Your Rights: If you believe that Irving ISD is not fulfilling its Child Find obligations, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This could occur if the district fails to respond to your concerns, delays the evaluation process, or refuses to evaluate your child without a valid reason.

What If They Refuse

If Irving ISD refuses to conduct a special education evaluation for your child, they must provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) explaining the reasons for their decision. This PWN is critical because it outlines your rights and options for challenging the district's decision. Do not accept a verbal denial; insist on a written explanation.

  • Review the Prior Written Notice: Carefully review the PWN to understand the district's rationale for refusing the evaluation. Pay attention to the specific reasons cited and any supporting documentation.
  • Request More Information: If the PWN is unclear or lacks sufficient detail, request additional information from the district. Ask them to clarify their reasoning and provide supporting evidence.
  • Your Options for Challenging the Decision: You have several options for challenging the district's decision:
    • Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the district meet with a neutral third party (a mediator) to try to resolve the dispute. The mediator facilitates communication and helps you and the district reach a mutually agreeable solution.
    • Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about whether the district violated your child's rights. This is a more adversarial process than mediation. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing.
    • State Complaint: You can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) alleging that the district violated state or federal special education laws. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation (or lack thereof). An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you request an IEE at public expense, the district must either pay for the IEE or request a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate.
  • Documentation is Key: Keep meticulous records of all communications with the school district, including dates, times, and summaries of conversations. Save all emails, letters, and other documents related to your child's special education needs.

Seek Advocacy and Legal Support: Navigating the special education system can be complex and challenging. Consider seeking assistance from a special education advocate or attorney who can provide guidance, support, and representation. Several organizations in Texas offer free or low-cost legal services to families of children with disabilities.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) is complete, Irving ISD must convene an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services. The ARD committee is a group of individuals responsible for developing, reviewing, and revising your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP), if they are found eligible.

  • ARD Committee Composition: The ARD committee must include:
    • You, the parent(s) or guardian(s)
    • At least one regular education teacher (if your child is, or may be, participating in the regular education environment)
    • At least one special education teacher or provider
    • A representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services
    • An individual who can interpret the instructional implications of the evaluation results (this may be one of the above individuals)
    • Your child (when appropriate)
    • Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child (at your or the district's discretion)
  • ARD Committee Meeting: The purpose of the ARD committee meeting is to:
    • Review the evaluation results
    • Determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under Texas law and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
    • If eligible, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's unique needs
  • The Individualized Education Program (IEP): The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's special education and related services. It includes:
    • Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance
    • Measurable annual goals
    • Special education and related services to be provided
    • Accommodations and modifications to be provided in the classroom
    • The extent to which your child will participate in the regular education environment
    • How your child's progress will be measured and reported
  • Your Rights at the ARD Meeting: You are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in the decision-making process. You have the right to:
    • Ask questions and seek clarification
    • Express your opinions and concerns
    • Review and provide input on the IEP
    • Request additional assessments or services
    • Disagree with the committee's recommendations
    • Request a recess of the meeting if you need more time to consider the information
  • Prior Written Notice (PWN): The district must provide you with a Prior Written Notice (PWN) whenever they propose to initiate or change, or refuse to initiate or change, your child's identification, evaluation, educational placement, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE). This PWN must explain the reasons for the proposed action and your rights as a parent.

Ongoing Monitoring and Review: Your child's IEP should be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to request an ARD committee meeting at any time if you have concerns about your child's progress or the effectiveness of their IEP. Remember that you are your child's strongest advocate. Stay informed, participate actively in the special education process, and work collaboratively with the school district to ensure your child receives the support and services they need to succeed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
