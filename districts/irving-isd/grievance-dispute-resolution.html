Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Discipline Rights & Grievances in Irving ISD: An Urgent Guide for Parents

If your child is facing suspension, MDR, or a Change of Placement, ACT IMMEDIATELY. Time is of the essence. This guide is designed to provide initial information and empower you to protect your child's rights. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education or student rights as soon as possible.

This guide is intended to help parents navigate the complex discipline policies within Irving ISD. It's crucial to understand your child's rights, especially if they have an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or a 504 plan. Ignoring these procedures can have serious consequences for your child's education and future.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Irving ISD, like most school districts, has rules regarding short-term suspensions. Understand this: cumulative suspensions of more than 10 school days in a school year trigger specific rights, especially for students with disabilities.

  • What constitutes a "suspension"? Any removal from the regular educational setting for disciplinary reasons counts as a suspension. This includes in-school suspension (ISS) if your child is not receiving their regular instruction.
  • Tracking Suspensions: Keep meticulous records of every suspension your child receives, noting the date, length, and reason. Irving ISD should provide this information, but double-check it for accuracy. Mistakes happen, and accurate records are vital.
  • The 10-Day Threshold: Once your child reaches 10 cumulative days of suspension in a school year, certain protections under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) kick in. This is where the "Change of Placement" rules and Manifestation Determination Reviews (MDRs) become incredibly important.
  • Before the 10th Day: Even before the 10-day mark, you have the right to be informed of the reasons for the suspension and to discuss the situation with school officials. Don't be afraid to ask questions and voice your concerns. Document everything!
  • Fighting a Suspension: You have the right to challenge a suspension if you believe it's unwarranted or violates your child's rights. Start by communicating with the school principal and following Irving ISD's grievance procedures (detailed below). Gather any evidence that supports your case.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a crucial process to determine if a student's misconduct is related to their disability. This is a key protection under IDEA for students with IEPs or 504 plans.

  • Triggering an MDR: An MDR must be conducted whenever a student with a disability faces a suspension that would constitute a Change of Placement (more on that below). Generally, this is triggered by more than 10 cumulative days of suspension.
  • The MDR Team: The MDR team typically includes the parent(s), relevant school personnel (special education teacher, general education teacher, administrator), and potentially other professionals familiar with the child's disability. You are a vital member of this team!
  • The Key Questions: The MDR team must answer two critical questions:
    1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
    2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the child's IEP?
  • What to Expect at the MDR Meeting: The meeting will involve a review of the student's IEP, relevant evaluations, teacher observations, and information provided by the parents. Be prepared to advocate for your child and explain how their disability may have contributed to the behavior. Bring any supporting documentation.
  • If the Conduct is a Manifestation: If the MDR team determines that the conduct was a manifestation of the child's disability, the school district cannot suspend the child for more than 10 days (unless it involves drugs, weapons, or serious bodily injury - see below). The IEP team must then conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and develop or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the underlying causes of the behavior. The focus must shift from punishment to addressing the disability-related needs that are driving the behavior.
  • If the Conduct is NOT a Manifestation: If the MDR team determines that the conduct was NOT a manifestation of the child's disability, the student can be disciplined in the same way as students without disabilities. However, the school district must still provide educational services to the student during the period of suspension. This is a critical point often overlooked! Demand to know what services your child will receive.
  • Special Circumstances (Drugs, Weapons, Serious Bodily Injury): Even if the conduct is a manifestation of the disability, if the incident involves illegal drugs, weapons, or serious bodily injury, the school district can remove the student to an Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES) for up to 45 school days. However, an MDR is still required, and the IAES must provide services to allow the student to continue to participate in the general education curriculum and to progress toward meeting their IEP goals.
  • What if you Disagree? If you disagree with the MDR team's determination, you have the right to challenge it through due process (detailed below). Do not hesitate to exercise this right if you believe the decision is incorrect.

Change of Placement

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or when there is a pattern of removals that constitutes a significant change in the student's educational experience. This is a critical concept because it triggers the MDR requirements and other procedural safeguards.

  • Examples of Change of Placement:
    • Suspension exceeding 10 consecutive school days.
    • A series of short-term suspensions that total more than 10 days in a school year and create a pattern (e.g., repeated suspensions right before tests or major school events).
    • Placement in an alternative educational setting that differs significantly from the student's regular classroom (e.g., reduced instruction time, different curriculum).
  • Your Rights When a Change of Placement is Proposed: You have the right to be notified in writing of the proposed Change of Placement and the reasons for it. You have the right to participate in the MDR meeting (if the student has an IEP or 504 plan). You have the right to challenge the Change of Placement through due process.
  • Fighting an Inappropriate Change of Placement: If you believe the proposed Change of Placement is not appropriate for your child, document your concerns and present them to the school district. Request data supporting their decision. Be prepared to argue that the Change of Placement is not necessary, that less restrictive alternatives are available, or that the school district has failed to adequately address your child's needs.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

Irving ISD has a formal grievance procedure for resolving disputes. Understanding this process is crucial if you feel your child's rights have been violated.

  • What is a Grievance? A grievance is a formal complaint alleging a violation of school district policy or procedure. This could include issues related to discipline, special education services, or other matters.
  • Level 1: The Principal Level: The first step in the grievance process is typically to file a written grievance with the school principal. Be specific and detailed in your grievance. Clearly state the facts of the situation, the specific policy or procedure that you believe has been violated, and the remedy you are seeking.
  • Time is of the Essence: Irving ISD likely has strict deadlines for filing a grievance. Obtain a copy of the district's grievance policy and adhere to the deadlines. Missing a deadline can result in your grievance being dismissed. Typically, grievances must be filed within a very short timeframe (e.g., 10-15 days) of the incident.
  • What to Include in Your Grievance:
    • Your child's name and grade level.
    • The date of the incident.
    • A detailed description of the incident.
    • The specific school district policy or procedure that you believe has been violated.
    • The names of any witnesses.
    • Any supporting documentation (e.g., emails, letters, medical records).
    • The remedy you are seeking (e.g., reinstatement of services, removal of a suspension from your child's record).
  • The Principal's Response: The principal is required to investigate your grievance and provide a written response within a specified timeframe (again, check the district's policy).
  • If You're Not Satisfied: If you are not satisfied with the principal's response, you can appeal to the next level of the grievance process (typically the superintendent or a designated administrator).
  • Document Everything: Keep copies of all documents related to your grievance, including the grievance itself, the principal's response, and any supporting evidence.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Irving ISD has violated a state or federal law related to special education, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than the district's grievance procedure.

  • What Types of Violations? TEA complaints can address a wide range of special education issues, such as failure to provide appropriate services, failure to follow IEP procedures, or discrimination based on disability.
  • How to File a Complaint: You can typically find the complaint form and instructions on the TEA website. The complaint must be in writing and must include specific information about the alleged violation, including the dates, facts, and the specific laws or regulations that you believe have been violated.
  • TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and determine whether a violation has occurred. If TEA finds a violation, it can order the school district to take corrective action.
  • Time Limits: There are strict deadlines for filing a TEA complaint. Generally, you must file the complaint within one year of the date of the alleged violation.
  • Complementary to Grievances: Filing a TEA complaint does *not* preclude you from also pursuing a grievance within the district. They can be pursued simultaneously.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal process that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE). It is the most formal (and often most expensive) avenue for resolving disagreements.

  • When to Consider Due Process: Due process is typically considered when all other attempts to resolve the dispute have failed, such as through IEP meetings, mediation, or the district's grievance procedure. It's appropriate when disagreements are significant and concern fundamental issues related to your child's education.
  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: To initiate due process, you must file a written complaint with the TEA and provide a copy to the school district. The complaint must include specific information about the dispute, including the facts, the issues, and the proposed resolution.
  • Mediation: TEA will offer mediation as a way to attempt to resolve the dispute before proceeding to a due process hearing. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps the parties reach an agreement.
  • Due Process Hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful, the case will proceed to a due process hearing. The hearing is similar to a court trial, with both sides presenting evidence and witnesses. An impartial hearing officer will make a decision based on the evidence presented.
  • Legal Representation: Due process hearings can be complex and require legal expertise. It is highly recommended that you hire an attorney specializing in special education law to represent you at the hearing.
  • Cost: Due process hearings can be expensive, as you will be responsible for paying your attorney's fees and other costs.
  • Remember: Due process is a powerful tool, but it should be used as a last resort. Explore all other options for resolving the dispute before initiating due process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
