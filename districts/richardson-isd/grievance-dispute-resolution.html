The 10-Day Suspension Rule: What You NEED to Know

Texas Education Code allows schools to suspend students for disciplinary reasons. However, there are limits. Generally, a school can suspend a student for up to three school days without needing to provide a formal hearing. Suspensions beyond this typically require more formal procedures. The key is the cumulative effect of short-term suspensions.

Crucially, if a student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single academic year, even if those suspensions are in separate incidents, special protections under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) may be triggered, particularly if the student has an IEP or 504 plan. This is where the concept of a "Change of Placement" becomes critical.

What to do:

  • Track ALL suspensions. Keep a detailed record of every suspension your child receives, including dates, reasons, and duration.
  • Demand Documentation: Insist on receiving official documentation for each suspension. This should clearly state the reason for the suspension and the number of days.
  • If approaching 10 days: If your child is nearing the 10-day cumulative suspension threshold, contact the school's special education department IMMEDIATELY and formally request a meeting to discuss whether the disciplinary actions constitute a Change of Placement, particularly if you believe the behavior is related to your child's disability. Document this request.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)? Why It's VITAL

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a critical process required under IDEA when a student with an IEP or 504 plan faces a Change of Placement due to disciplinary action. A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a significant alteration of their educational program, even if the individual removals are for fewer than 10 days each.

The purpose of the MDR is to determine whether the student's misconduct was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability. It also aims to determine if the misconduct was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

What to expect at an MDR:

  • Team Meeting: The MDR is conducted by a team that includes you (the parent), relevant school personnel (teachers, administrators, special education staff), and potentially other individuals knowledgeable about your child.
  • IEP Review: The team will review your child's IEP, evaluation data, and any relevant observations related to your child's behavior.
  • Causation Determination: The team will discuss whether the behavior leading to the disciplinary action was caused by or directly and substantially related to your child's disability, OR if it was the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

MDR Outcomes and What They Mean:

  • If the behavior IS a manifestation of the disability or due to IEP implementation failures: Your child CANNOT be disciplined in the same way as a non-disabled student. The school MUST take steps to address the underlying causes of the behavior, including reviewing and revising the IEP, implementing positive behavior interventions, and potentially conducting a Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) and developing a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP). Your child MUST be returned to their previous placement, unless you and the school agree to a different placement as part of the IEP.
  • If the behavior is NOT a manifestation of the disability: Your child can be subject to the same disciplinary measures as non-disabled students. However, the school MUST still provide Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE), even during a suspension or expulsion.

If the school attempts to hold a disciplinary hearing or suspend your child for more than 10 days WITHOUT conducting an MDR when one is required, this is a serious violation of IDEA. Demand an MDR IN WRITING immediately.

Change of Placement: Protecting Your Child's Educational Setting

As mentioned above, a Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a significant alteration of their educational program. This is a critical trigger for IDEA protections.

Examples of a Change of Placement:

  • Suspension exceeding 10 consecutive school days.
  • A series of short-term suspensions that create a pattern of exclusion from the educational environment.
  • Placement in an alternative educational setting (e.g., DAEP) for more than 45 days for weapons or drugs (with specific exceptions and procedures under IDEA).

Your Rights During a Change of Placement:

  • Right to an MDR (if the student has an IEP or 504 plan): As discussed above, this is crucial.
  • Right to Continued FAPE: Even if your child is suspended or placed in an alternative setting, the school must continue to provide FAPE, meaning they must have access to the curriculum and services outlined in their IEP or 504 plan.
  • Right to Participate in Placement Decisions: You have the right to be actively involved in any decisions regarding your child's placement.

Do NOT agree to any placement change without fully understanding its implications and your rights. If you disagree with the proposed placement, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, including mediation and due process.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance: The First Step in Addressing Wrongful Actions

Richardson ISD, like all Texas school districts, has a formal grievance policy. This policy provides a process for addressing complaints and concerns related to school policies and procedures, including disciplinary actions. The first step in this process is typically filing a Level 1 grievance.

When to File a Level 1 Grievance:

  • When you believe the school has violated your child's rights or acted unfairly in a disciplinary matter.
  • When you disagree with a disciplinary decision and have exhausted informal attempts to resolve the issue with the teacher or administrator involved.
  • When you believe the school has failed to follow its own policies and procedures in a disciplinary matter.

How to File a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Form: Contact the principal's office or the district's central office to obtain a copy of the official grievance form. Many districts also post these online. Search "Richardson ISD grievance policy" on the RISD website.
  2. Complete the Form: Fill out the form completely and accurately. Be specific about the actions you are grieving, the dates of the incidents, and the individuals involved. Clearly state the relief you are seeking (e.g., reversal of a suspension, a change in placement, implementation of the IEP).
  3. Attach Supporting Documentation: Include any documents that support your grievance, such as copies of suspension notices, emails, IEP documents, medical records, or witness statements.
  4. Submit the Grievance: Submit the completed grievance form to the designated individual at the school (usually the principal or a designated grievance officer). Keep a copy of the submitted grievance for your records.
  5. Timeline: Pay close attention to the timelines outlined in the grievance policy. There are strict deadlines for filing and responding to grievances. Missed deadlines can jeopardize your ability to pursue your complaint.

Your Level 1 grievance should clearly articulate the violation of your child's rights and/or the specific school policies that were not followed. If the grievance involves a special education matter, specifically reference IDEA and the specific provisions that were violated.

TEA State Complaint: An Option for Serious IDEA Violations

If you believe Richardson ISD has violated IDEA and you have exhausted other dispute resolution options (or believe they would be futile), you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

When to File a TEA Complaint:

  • When you believe the school district is not complying with IDEA requirements regarding FAPE, evaluation, IEPs, placement, or discipline.
  • When you have evidence that the school district has systemic issues related to special education compliance.
  • When you believe the school district has failed to properly investigate or resolve a grievance.

How to File a TEA Complaint:

  1. Obtain the Complaint Form: Download the official TEA complaint form from the TEA website. Search for "TEA Special Education Complaint" on the TEA website.
  2. Complete the Form: Fill out the form completely and accurately. Be specific about the IDEA violations you are alleging, the dates of the incidents, and the individuals involved. Provide detailed information and supporting documentation.
  3. Attach Supporting Documentation: Include any documents that support your complaint, such as IEP documents, evaluation reports, correspondence with the school district, and grievance filings.
  4. Submit the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA. Keep a copy for your records.

TEA has specific timelines for investigating and resolving complaints. TEA will typically require the school district to respond to your complaint and provide documentation. TEA may conduct an investigation, interview witnesses, and review records. TEA will issue a written decision regarding your complaint.

Due Process: The Ultimate Protection

Due process is a formal legal process available under IDEA to resolve disputes between parents and school districts regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability, or the provision of FAPE. It is a more formal and adversarial process than mediation or a TEA complaint.

When to Consider Due Process:

  • When you have exhausted other dispute resolution options and are unable to reach an agreement with the school district.
  • When you believe the school district has committed serious violations of IDEA that have significantly impacted your child's education.
  • When you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, their IEP, or their placement.

Due process is a complex legal process. It is HIGHLY recommended that you consult with an attorney specializing in education law BEFORE filing a due process complaint. An attorney can advise you on the merits of your case, help you gather evidence, and represent you during the due process hearing.

Filing Due Process:

  • Obtain a Due Process Complaint Form Contact the TEA to obtain one.
  • Notice Requirements: There are very specific requirements for what to include in a due process complaint. You will have to follow these exactly in order to properly give notice to the school district.
  • Hearing: if you and the district cannot resolve your issues through mediation, you will proceed to a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge.

Time is of the essence in all of these processes. Contact an attorney specializing in education law immediately to protect your child's rights. Document everything! Good luck.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
