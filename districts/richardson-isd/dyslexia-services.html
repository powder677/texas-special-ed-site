Dyslexia Services in Richardson ISD

Dyslexia Services in Richardson ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services and support available within the Richardson Independent School District (RISD) in Texas. It is intended to help parents, educators, and students understand the identification, evaluation, and intervention processes for dyslexia as outlined by Texas state law and RISD policy.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), is the primary resource for understanding dyslexia and related disorders in Texas schools. The 2024 version (or most current version available) outlines the legal requirements, best practices, and recommended procedures for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia. It's crucial to consult the official handbook for the most up-to-date information.

Key Aspects Covered in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: A clear and consistent definition of dyslexia and related disorders as outlined in the Texas Education Code.
  • Screening: Guidance on universal screening for reading difficulties in early grades (Kindergarten through 2nd grade).
  • Evaluation: Procedures for comprehensive evaluations to determine if a student has dyslexia. This involves analyzing various reading and language skills.
  • Instruction: Emphasis on evidence-based, structured literacy interventions delivered by trained professionals.
  • Accommodations: Information about appropriate accommodations for students with dyslexia in the classroom.
  • Parent Involvement: The importance of parental involvement and collaboration in the dyslexia intervention process.
  • Legal Requirements: Summary of relevant Texas laws and regulations related to dyslexia.

Always consult the official TEA document.

Where to Find the Handbook: The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is available for free download on the Texas Education Agency website. Use the provided link above. Always refer to the official TEA website for the latest version.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates dyslexia screening for all students in Kindergarten, 1st grade, and 2nd grade. Richardson ISD adheres to these requirements and has implemented procedures to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may need further evaluation and intervention.

Screening Procedures in RISD:

  • Universal Screening: All students in K-2 are screened using TEA-approved or recognized screening instruments. These instruments typically assess foundational reading skills such as phonological awareness, phonemic awareness, letter-sound knowledge, rapid naming, and reading fluency.
  • Screening Timeline: Screenings are typically conducted at the beginning of the school year and may be repeated at mid-year to monitor progress and identify students who may not have been initially identified.
  • Parent Notification: Parents will be notified if their child exhibits characteristics of dyslexia based on the screening results. This notification will include information about the screening results and recommendations for further action.
  • Follow-Up: Students who exhibit characteristics of dyslexia based on the screening results will receive targeted interventions to address their specific needs. They may also be recommended for a comprehensive evaluation to determine if they have dyslexia.

Importance of Early Screening: Early identification and intervention are critical for students with dyslexia. Research shows that students who receive timely and appropriate interventions are more likely to develop strong reading skills and achieve academic success.

Structured Literacy Programs

Structured Literacy is an explicit, systematic, and multi-sensory approach to teaching reading and spelling. It is considered the gold standard for teaching students with dyslexia and other reading difficulties. Richardson ISD utilizes structured literacy programs that align with the principles outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Focuses on the sound structure of language, including phonemic awareness (the ability to hear and manipulate individual sounds in words).
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Teaches the relationship between sounds (phonemes) and letters (graphemes).
  • Syllable Instruction: Explores different syllable types and how they impact pronunciation and spelling.
  • Morphology: Focuses on the meaning-bearing parts of words (e.g., prefixes, suffixes, root words).
  • Syntax: Addresses sentence structure and grammar.
  • Semantics: Explores the meaning of words and phrases.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support services under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Purpose: To provide accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: Accommodations in the regular classroom to help the student access the curriculum (e.g., extended time on tests, preferential seating).
  • Dyslexia Support: 504 plans can provide accommodations for students with dyslexia, such as assistive technology, modified assignments, and extra time for reading and writing tasks.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Purpose: To provide specialized instruction and related services to students with disabilities who require more intensive support than can be provided through general education.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction. Dyslexia can be a qualifying disability under the category of Specific Learning Disability.
  • Focus: Specialized instruction delivered by qualified professionals, as well as related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy).
  • Dyslexia Support: IEPs for students with dyslexia may include specialized reading instruction (Structured Literacy), accommodations, and modifications to the curriculum.

Key Differences:

Legal Basis Rehabilitation Act of 1973 Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)

Which Plan is Right? The decision about whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and parents. Factors considered include the severity of the student's reading difficulties, the impact on their academic performance, and the level of support required to make meaningful progress.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material, but they do not change what the student is expected to learn. Accommodations level the playing field for students with dyslexia, allowing them to demonstrate their knowledge and skills without being hindered by their reading difficulties. The following list provides examples of accommodations that may be included in a 504 plan or IEP for students with dyslexia. This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations will vary based on the student's individual needs.

Reading Accommodations:

  • Audiobooks: Listening to text read aloud.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that converts written text into spoken words.
  • Highlighted Text: Using highlighters to emphasize key information in text.
  • Reduced Reading Load: Providing shorter reading assignments or excerpts.
  • Chunking Text: Breaking down large blocks of text into smaller, more manageable chunks.
  • Visual Supports: Using pictures, diagrams, and graphic organizers to support comprehension.
  • Modified Font and Spacing: Using a dyslexia-friendly font and increasing line spacing.
  • Oral Reading of Tests: Having tests read aloud to the student.

Writing Accommodations:

  • Speech-to-Text Software: Using software that converts spoken words into written text.
  • Word Prediction Software: Using software that predicts words as the student types.
  • Graphic Organizers: Using graphic organizers to plan and organize writing.
  • Scribe: Having someone write down the student's responses.
  • Extended Time for Writing Assignments: Providing additional time to complete writing tasks.
  • Alternative Assessments: Allowing students to demonstrate their knowledge through alternative assessments, such as oral presentations or projects.
  • Reduced Writing Load: Providing shorter writing assignments.

Testing Accommodations:

  • Extended Time: Providing additional time to complete tests.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Allowing the student to take tests in a quiet and distraction-free environment.
  • Read Aloud: Having the test read aloud to the student (as appropriate).
  • Use of Assistive Technology: Allowing the student to use assistive technology, such as a calculator or word processor, during testing.
  • Breaks: Allowing the student to take breaks during testing.

Classroom Accommodations:

  • Preferential Seating: Providing preferential seating near the teacher or away from distractions.
  • Copies of Notes: Providing copies of notes to the student.
  • Pre-teaching Vocabulary: Introducing new vocabulary words before the lesson.
  • Multi-sensory Instruction: Using multiple senses (e.g., visual, auditory, kinesthetic) to teach concepts.
  • Frequent Checks for Understanding: Regularly checking for understanding to ensure the student is following along.
  • Positive Reinforcement: Providing positive reinforcement and encouragement.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from Richardson ISD. It is best to start by communicating your concerns with your child's teacher. They can share classroom observations and discuss any interventions already being implemented.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the Teacher: Schedule a meeting with your child's teacher to discuss your concerns about their reading.
  2. Contact the School Principal or Counselor: If you are not satisfied with the initial meeting with the teacher, contact the school principal or counselor to discuss your concerns further.
  3. Submit a Written Request: Submit a formal written request for a full and individual evaluation for dyslexia to the school principal or the Special Education Department. This request should clearly state your concerns and the reasons why you believe your child may have dyslexia. It's helpful to include any supporting documentation, such as previous test scores or teacher observations.
  4. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, they will convene a team to determine if an evaluation is warranted. If the team decides to conduct an evaluation, they will obtain your consent and begin the process. The evaluation will typically involve a variety of assessments to evaluate your child's reading, spelling, and language skills.
  5. ARD/504 Meeting: After the evaluation is completed, the school will schedule an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting (for potential IEP eligibility) or a 504 meeting (for potential 504 eligibility) to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services or 504 accommodations. You are a crucial member of this team.

Timeline: The school district is required to follow specific timelines for evaluating students for dyslexia. Consult the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and RISD policies for specific timelines. It's important to document all communication with the school in writing.

Parent Rights: You have the right to participate in all stages of the evaluation and intervention process. You also have the right to obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) if you disagree with the school's evaluation. Information on your rights as a parent is available from the Texas Education Agency and RISD's Special Education Department.

RISD Resources: Contact the RISD Special Education Department for additional information and support regarding dyslexia services. Their website and contact information will provide further details about district-specific procedures.

Disclaimer: This guide is intended for informational purposes only and should not be considered legal advice. Always consult with qualified professionals and refer to the official Texas Dyslexia Handbook and RISD policies for the most up-to-date information.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
