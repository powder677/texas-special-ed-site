What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal meeting, is a very important process in your child's special education journey. It's essentially a meeting where a team of people, including you as the parent, come together to make decisions about your child's education. The main purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs and the services they will receive to meet those needs. This could include things like specialized instruction, therapy, accommodations, and modifications to the regular classroom setting.

The ARD committee is comprised of several key individuals. By law, this team must include you, the parent or guardian. You are a vital member of the team, and your input is extremely important. You know your child best, and your insights are crucial to developing an effective IEP. Other members typically include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of special education, one of your child's teachers, and someone who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results. Depending on your child's needs, other professionals may also attend, such as a school psychologist, therapist (speech, occupational, physical), or diagnostician. If your child is old enough (generally around age 16, but sometimes earlier), they will also be invited to attend the ARD meeting.

These meetings are mandated by two very important laws. First is the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA. This is a federal law that ensures children with disabilities have access to a free appropriate public education, or FAPE. Second, is the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which implements IDEA in Texas. The ARD process is designed to make sure your child receives the support and services they need to succeed in school, as guaranteed by these laws. Understanding the ARD process and actively participating in the meetings is essential to advocating for your child's educational rights.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notification of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is a crucial safeguard to ensure you have adequate time to prepare and gather any information or support you need to effectively participate. This written notice isn't just a courtesy; it's a legal requirement designed to empower you as an active member of the ARD team.

The notice must include specific information about the meeting. It must clearly state the purpose of the meeting - for example, is it an annual review, a meeting to address specific concerns, or a meeting to determine initial eligibility for special education services? The notice must also include the date, time, and location of the meeting. It should list the names and titles of the individuals who will be attending, if that information is available. Importantly, the notice must also inform you of your right to invite other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child to attend the meeting. This could include therapists, doctors, or advocates.

While the five-day notice is your right, you can choose to waive it. If you and the school district agree that it's in your child's best interest to meet sooner, you can sign a written waiver relinquishing your right to the five-day notice. This might be appropriate if there's an urgent issue that needs to be addressed quickly. However, it's important to carefully consider the implications before waiving your rights. Make sure you feel fully prepared and comfortable proceeding with the meeting before you agree to waive the five-day notice.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting is essential for effectively advocating for your child. There are several items you should consider bringing to ensure you are well-equipped to participate in the discussion and contribute to the development of a comprehensive IEP.

First, bring any prior evaluations of your child. This includes evaluations conducted by the school district as well as any private evaluations you have obtained. These evaluations provide valuable insights into your child's strengths and weaknesses, and they can inform the development of appropriate goals and services. Next, gather any relevant letters or reports from your child's doctors or therapists. Medical documentation can provide important context for your child's educational needs and help the ARD team understand any underlying health conditions that may be impacting their learning.

It's also helpful to bring copies of your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, and any work samples that demonstrate their academic performance. This information can provide a clear picture of your child's progress over time and highlight areas where they may be struggling. Consider preparing a written list of your concerns and observations regarding your child's education. This can help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. Be specific about what you've noticed at home and how it relates to their school performance.

Another valuable resource is bringing a trusted advocate or support person to the meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you remember important details, and ensure that your voice is heard. Finally, you are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting in Texas, as long as you provide reasonable notice to the school district. A voice recorder can be a helpful tool for documenting the discussion and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides an important safeguard called the "10-day recess rule" designed to ensure thoughtful decision-making during the ARD process. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a recess, or temporary break, in the meeting to gather additional information, consult with an advocate, or simply have more time to consider the information being presented. The recess can be up to ten school days.

This recess is invaluable when you feel overwhelmed, unsure, or that the team is moving too quickly. Perhaps you need time to research a particular service or accommodation, or maybe you want to consult with a professional who has expertise in your child's specific needs. The 10-day recess provides you with the time to do so without feeling pressured to make immediate decisions.

Invoking the 10-day recess is simple. During the ARD meeting, if you feel you need more time, clearly state that you are requesting a recess of up to 10 school days under the 10-day recess rule. It is a good idea to state your reasoning for needing the recess. The ARD committee must honor your request. The meeting will then be paused and rescheduled to reconvene within those ten school days. The school district is responsible for setting the next meeting date within the 10-day window. Don't hesitate to use this rule if you feel it will help you make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the heart of the ARD process. It's a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs and the services they will receive to meet those needs. Several key components are discussed and decided upon during the ARD meeting that get documented in the IEP.

One of the first things addressed is your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current skills and abilities in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, and social skills. It also includes information about how their disability affects their involvement and progress in the general education curriculum. Based on the present levels, the ARD committee will develop annual goals for your child. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals).

The IEP will also specify the special education and related services that your child will receive. This could include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, or other support services. The IEP will also outline any accommodations or modifications that will be provided to help your child succeed in the classroom. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material, such as extended time on tests or preferential seating. Modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn, such as reducing the number of assignments or providing alternative assessments.

Finally, the IEP will determine your child's placement. This refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their services. Placement options range from the general education classroom with support services to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or a self-contained classroom. The IEP should document these decisions made at the ARD meeting. You do not have to sign the IEP the same day as the ARD meeting. You can take it home and review it.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that you are an equal member of the ARD team, and your voice matters. There may be times when you disagree with the recommendations or decisions of the other team members. You have specific rights when such disagreements arise.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's recommendation to place your child in special education, you can refuse to sign the IEP and your child will not be placed in special education. Second, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP even after your child has been initially placed in special education. This means that you do not agree with everything that is written in the document. If you disagree with specific aspects of the IEP, you can write "parent disagrees" next to those sections before signing the document. This indicates that you do not endorse those particular recommendations and preserves your right to pursue further action.

You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the results of the school district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If the district agrees to pay for the IEE, you will receive the results. The ARD committee must consider the results of the IEE when making decisions about your child's IEP. If the district believes their evaluation was appropriate, they can pursue a due process hearing to defend their evaluation, but they must prove it was appropriate.

Escalation Options

If you have tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD team and are still unable to reach a satisfactory resolution, you have several formal options for escalating your concerns.

First, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of state or federal special education laws. Second, you can request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable solution. This is also a free service. Third, you can request a due process hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's educational needs. Due process hearings can be complex and often require legal representation. You can also file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe your child has been discriminated against on the basis of their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order corrective action if discrimination is found.

It is important to understand that these rights are governed by both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and the Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89. Familiarizing yourself with these laws can empower you to effectively advocate for your child's educational needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
