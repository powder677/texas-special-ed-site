What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Burleson ISD. The ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is the cornerstone of how students with disabilities receive support and services in our schools. This meeting is required by both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. It's a structured process designed to ensure that your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs. In essence, the ARD committee acts as a team that develops and implements an Individualized Education Program (IEP) for your child.

The purpose of the ARD meeting is multifaceted. First, it's where the team determines if a student is eligible for special education services based on evaluation data. If eligible, the team then reviews the student's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance; these describe where your child currently stands in their learning and daily functioning. From there, the team develops specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) annual goals for your child to work toward during the school year. The meeting also determines the specific special education and related services that will be provided to help your child reach those goals, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or assistive technology. Finally, the team determines the appropriate educational placement for your child, meaning the setting where they will receive their instruction and services. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting.

The ARD committee is comprised of several key members who each play a vital role in the process. You, as the parent, are a critical member of the team. Your input, insights, and knowledge of your child are invaluable. Other members include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services; usually, this is a special education administrator or designee. Your child's general education teacher is also a required member, if your child is or may be participating in the general education environment. A special education teacher or specialist who is knowledgeable about your child's specific disability and the special education services that are available will also be present. An educational diagnostician or other professional who can interpret evaluation results is also crucial. And finally, your child is invited to attend the ARD meeting, especially as they get older and can actively participate in decisions about their education. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited to participate, such as therapists, counselors, or advocates.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Burleson ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notification is not just a formality; it's a legal right designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting and gather any information you want to share with the ARD committee. This is in place so you can adequately prepare to engage as an informed and empowered member of the team. It's important to understand what this notice should include and what options you have regarding the timing of the meeting.

The 5-day notice must include specific information about the upcoming ARD meeting. It must clearly state the purpose of the meeting, whether it's to determine eligibility, review the IEP, consider a change in placement, or address any other relevant issue. The notice must also include the date, time, and location of the meeting. Furthermore, the notice must list the individuals who will be attending the meeting, including their roles. And perhaps most importantly, the notice must inform you of your rights as a parent in the special education process, including your right to participate in the development of your child's IEP and your right to disagree with the team's decisions.

While the 5-day notice is in place to protect your right to adequate preparation time, you do have the option to waive this right and agree to meet sooner. This might be necessary if there is an urgent issue that needs to be addressed or if you have a scheduling conflict that makes it difficult to attend a meeting scheduled more than five days out. If you choose to waive your right to the 5-day notice, you will need to do so in writing. Be sure you understand the implications of waiving this right before you agree to it. You are under no obligation to waive your right, and you should only do so if you feel comfortable and prepared to proceed with the ARD meeting on a shorter timeline.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but taking the time to gather relevant information and materials can make a significant difference in ensuring that your child's needs are fully addressed. Think of it as building a strong case for your child's educational well-being. Here's a detailed checklist of items you might consider bringing to the meeting:

First and foremost, gather any prior evaluations or assessments that have been conducted on your child, both from the school district and from outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other reports that provide insights into your child's strengths and areas of need. Sharing these reports with the ARD committee can help them gain a more comprehensive understanding of your child's learning profile.

If your child has seen any doctors or specialists related to their disability, bring any relevant letters or reports from these professionals. This could include letters from pediatricians, neurologists, psychiatrists, or other medical providers who can offer valuable information about your child's medical condition and its impact on their education. These letters can help the ARD committee understand the medical context surrounding your child's educational needs.

Bring any school records that you feel are relevant to the discussion. This might include report cards, progress reports, work samples, or any other documentation that showcases your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom. These records can provide concrete examples of your child's strengths and challenges in the school setting.

One of the most important things you can bring to an ARD meeting is a written list of your concerns and goals for your child's education. Take the time to carefully think about what you want your child to achieve during the school year and what supports they need to be successful. Writing down your concerns and goals beforehand will help you stay organized and ensure that all of your important points are addressed during the meeting. Be as specific as possible when outlining your concerns and goals.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can help you understand the process, ask questions, and ensure that your voice is heard. Having a second person present can provide emotional support and help you stay focused during what can sometimes be a stressful meeting.

Finally, remember that you are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting in Texas, as long as you provide reasonable notice to the other members of the committee. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for documenting the discussion and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made. This can be particularly helpful if you need to refer back to the meeting later or if you disagree with something that was decided. Be sure to inform the ARD committee in advance that you plan to record the meeting.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is an important safeguard designed to ensure that all members of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, have adequate time to consider the information presented and make informed decisions about your child's IEP. This rule allows any member of the ARD committee to request a recess of up to 10 school days to gather more information, consult with experts, or simply take time to process the decisions being made.

There are many situations where invoking the 10-day recess rule might be beneficial. Perhaps you're presented with new evaluation data that you need time to review. Or maybe you have questions about a particular service or accommodation that you want to research further. It's also possible that you simply feel overwhelmed by the amount of information being discussed and need time to consult with an advocate or trusted advisor before making a decision.

Invoking the 10-day recess rule is straightforward. Simply state that you are requesting a recess of the ARD meeting to gather more information or consult with an advocate. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation or justification for your request. The ARD committee is obligated to grant your request. The recess will then last for up to 10 school days, during which time you can gather the information you need and prepare to reconvene the meeting.

It's important to note that the 10-day recess rule is not intended to be used as a delaying tactic. It's meant to be a tool for ensuring that all members of the ARD committee have the information and support they need to make informed decisions in the best interests of the child. Use this right wisely and strategically to ensure that your child's IEP is truly tailored to their individual needs.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) document is the written record of the decisions made at the ARD meeting. It serves as a blueprint for your child's special education services and outlines the specific supports and accommodations they will receive to help them succeed in school. It is crucial to understand the different sections of the IEP and the decisions that are made regarding each one.

One of the most important sections of the IEP is the present levels of academic achievement and functional performance (PLAAFP). This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic areas such as reading, writing, and math, as well as in functional areas such as communication, social skills, and self-help skills. The PLAAFP is based on a variety of data sources, including evaluations, assessments, classroom observations, and input from you as the parent. It provides a baseline for measuring your child's progress and for developing appropriate annual goals.

The annual goals section of the IEP outlines the specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child will work toward during the school year. These goals are based on your child's PLAAFP and are designed to address their identified areas of need. Each goal should be accompanied by short-term objectives or benchmarks that will be used to monitor your child's progress throughout the year. It is important to ensure that the annual goals are ambitious but realistic and that they are aligned with your child's overall educational needs.

The services section of the IEP describes the specific special education and related services that will be provided to your child to help them reach their annual goals. This might include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, assistive technology, or any other supports that are deemed necessary. The IEP should specify the frequency, duration, and location of each service.

The placement section of the IEP determines the educational setting where your child will receive their instruction and services. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or a self-contained classroom. The placement decision should be based on your child's individual needs and should ensure that they have the opportunity to learn and interact with their peers to the greatest extent possible.

The accommodations section of the IEP outlines the specific accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child to help them access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material (e.g., extended time on tests, preferential seating), while modifications are changes to what a student is expected to learn (e.g., simplified assignments, reduced workload). The IEP should specify which accommodations and modifications will be provided in each subject area.

Remember, you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before making a decision. Don't feel pressured to sign anything until you are completely comfortable with the contents of the document.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD committee. You know your child best, and your perspective is invaluable. The law recognizes your right to disagree and provides several avenues for resolving disputes.

You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you disagree with the ARD committee's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can refuse to sign the consent form. This means your child will not receive special education services at this time. This differs from withdrawing services once they are in place.

Even after initial placement, you always have the right to refuse to sign the IEP. Signing the IEP indicates that you attended the ARD meeting and received a copy of the document; it does not necessarily mean that you agree with everything in it. If you disagree with certain aspects of the IEP, you can still sign it but indicate that you disagree with specific sections. This allows you to document your concerns and preserve your right to pursue further action.

If you disagree with the school district's evaluations of your child, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide a different perspective on your child's needs and can be used to inform the ARD committee's decisions. Be aware that the district can challenge your request for an IEE, but they must do so through a due process hearing.

Regardless of whether you sign the IEP or not, you always have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document. This is a simple but effective way to document your concerns and ensure that they are included in the official record. Be sure to specify which sections of the IEP you disagree with and the reasons for your disagreement.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal means, there are several formal escalation options available to you. These options are designed to provide you with a fair and impartial process for resolving disputes and ensuring that your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). These rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and 19 Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89, also known as the Legal Framework.

The first option is to file a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free and relatively straightforward process that involves submitting a written complaint to TEA outlining your concerns. TEA will then investigate the complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The State Complaint process is best suited for addressing systemic issues or violations of procedural safeguards.

The second option is to request Mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and the school district meet with a neutral third-party mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. The mediator does not make decisions or impose solutions; their role is to facilitate communication and help the parties find common ground. Mediation is a confidential and non-adversarial process that can be a valuable tool for resolving disputes in a collaborative manner.

The third and most formal option is to request a Due Process Hearing. A Due Process Hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you and the school district present evidence and arguments before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer then issues a written decision that is binding on both parties. Due Process Hearings are typically more complex and time-consuming than State Complaints or Mediation, and it is often advisable to seek legal representation if you are considering this option. This is similar to a trial, but in an educational setting.

Finally, you also have the option of filing a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability, race, color, national origin, sex, age, or religion. If OCR finds that the school district has violated your child's civil rights, it can order the district to take corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
