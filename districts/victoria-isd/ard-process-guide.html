What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of ARD meetings! If you're reading this, you likely have a child in Victoria ISD who is receiving, or may be eligible to receive, special education services. The ARD meeting is the cornerstone of the special education process. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. It's the formal meeting where decisions are made about your child's educational program. The meeting is required by both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code 29. These laws ensure that children with disabilities receive a Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs.

The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, sets measurable annual goals, and details the specific services and supports the school will provide to help your child achieve those goals. It's essentially a roadmap for your child's special education journey.

So, who attends these meetings? The ARD committee is a team, and you, as the parent, are a critical member of that team. Other required members typically include: a representative of the school district (usually an administrator who is knowledgeable about special education programs), your child's special education teacher, a general education teacher (if your child is participating in general education classes), an evaluation specialist (someone who can interpret your child's testing results), and, when appropriate, your child themselves. Other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child may also be invited, such as therapists, counselors, or other specialists who work with your child. Remember, you have the right to invite anyone you believe can contribute valuable information or support to the team.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Before an ARD meeting takes place, Victoria ISD is required to provide you with written notice at least five calendar days prior to the scheduled date. This is your legal right, designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting and gather any information you want to share. Don't count the day you receive the notice. Weekends and holidays count toward the five days.

This written notice is more than just a date and time. It must include several crucial pieces of information, including the purpose of the meeting (e.g., initial ARD, annual review, amendment), a list of the individuals who will be attending (to the extent that they are known), and a description of any evaluation results or reports the school plans to present. It also needs to inform you of your right to invite other individuals to the meeting who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. The notice should also clearly state your procedural safeguards as a parent in the special education process. These safeguards are legal protections afforded to you and your child under IDEA.

While you have a right to five days' notice, there may be situations where you prefer to meet sooner. Perhaps a pressing issue needs to be addressed, or you want to expedite the process. In such cases, you can waive your right to the five-day notice in writing. This is entirely your choice, and the school cannot pressure you to waive your rights. Only do so if you feel fully prepared and comfortable proceeding without the full five-day period.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your ARD meeting is crucial to ensuring your child's needs are fully considered. Think of yourself as the expert on your child, and bring information that supports your expertise.

Firstly, bring any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy evaluations, or any other reports that provide insight into your child's strengths and areas of need. Doctor's letters can also be incredibly valuable. If your child has a medical diagnosis that impacts their learning, such as ADHD, autism, or dyslexia, bring documentation from their doctor outlining the diagnosis and any recommended accommodations or interventions.

Gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, work samples, or communication logs between you and your child's teachers. These documents can provide valuable context and demonstrate your child's progress (or lack thereof) in the classroom. Prepare a written list of your concerns. Before the meeting, take some time to write down all of your concerns about your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your questions and concerns are addressed. This list can include academic concerns, social-emotional concerns, behavioral concerns, or any other issues that are impacting your child's learning.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you. ARD meetings can be overwhelming, and having someone there to listen, take notes, and offer support can be incredibly helpful. This could be a family member, a friend, or a professional advocate who is familiar with special education laws and procedures.

Finally, remember that in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided that you inform the other members of the team that you are doing so. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for capturing the details of the meeting and ensuring that you have an accurate record of what was discussed and agreed upon. Announce at the start of the meeting that you are recording for your personal records and understanding. No one can stop you.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The "10-Day Recess Rule" is a powerful tool in Texas that allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a temporary pause in the ARD process. This "recess" can be up to ten school days long, and it's designed to provide time to gather additional information, consult with experts, or simply reflect on the decisions being made.

You might invoke the 10-day recess if, for example, new information is presented at the meeting that you need time to consider. Perhaps the school psychologist shares the results of a new assessment, or a teacher raises concerns about a specific behavior. You might also request a recess if you feel pressured to make a decision before you are ready, or if you want to consult with an advocate or outside expert before agreeing to the IEP.

Invoking the 10-day recess is simple. You just need to verbally state during the ARD meeting that you are requesting a recess. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation, although you may choose to do so. It's a good idea to say something like, "I would like to invoke the 10-day recess to have time to consider this information and consult with [name of person or type of expert]." The team must then schedule a date for the ARD meeting to reconvene within ten school days.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the tangible outcome of the ARD meeting, and it's essential to understand its key components. The IEP is driven by what the ARD committee decides during the meeting: It contains, and documents, decisions about your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, annual goals, the specific services and supports, placement and accommodations.

First, the IEP will detail your child's present levels. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in various academic and functional areas. It should be based on a variety of data sources, including assessments, observations, and parent input.

Next, the IEP will outline annual goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be ambitious but realistic, and they should be aligned with your child's present levels and areas of need.

The IEP will also specify the services that the school will provide to help your child achieve their goals. These services might include special education instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or assistive technology. The IEP will detail the frequency, duration, and location of each service.

Placement refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom, a resource room, a self-contained classroom, or a combination of settings. The IEP must explain why the chosen placement is the least restrictive environment (LRE) that meets your child's needs.

Finally, the IEP will list any accommodations that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material. Common accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and the use of assistive technology.

Crucially, understand that you do NOT have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. Take the IEP home, review it carefully, and consult with others if needed. Only sign it when you are fully comfortable with its contents.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's perfectly normal to disagree with the ARD team at times. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your voice matters. If you disagree with the team's recommendations, there are several steps you can take.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you do not consent to the initial provision of services, your child will not receive those services. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with its contents. Simply signing the document indicates that you attended the meeting, not that you agree with everything in it. You can write "I attended, but I do not agree" next to your signature.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you request an IEE, the school district must either pay for it or demonstrate at a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate.

Always make sure to write "Parent Disagrees" on the IEP document next to any section that you are in disagreement with. This clearly documents your concerns and preserves your rights.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all informal attempts to resolve your disagreements with Victoria ISD regarding your child's special education program, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are governed by IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89, which together constitute the Legal Framework.

Your first option is to file a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process where TEA will investigate your concerns and issue a written decision. The complaint must allege violations of IDEA or state special education law. TEA typically has 60 days to resolve the complaint. You can find the complaint form on the TEA website.

Your second option is to request Mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA provides mediation services at no cost to parents. It's a great way to try and work things out collaboratively.

Your third, and most formal, option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is an administrative hearing before an impartial hearing officer who will hear evidence and issue a binding decision. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it's highly recommended that you seek legal representation if you pursue this option.

Finally, you can also file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on race, color, national origin, sex, disability, and age.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
