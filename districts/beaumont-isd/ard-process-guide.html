What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as potentially needing special education services in Beaumont ISD, you will be invited to an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is the cornerstone of ensuring that students with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education, often referred to as FAPE. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to their unique needs, to review the effectiveness of the current IEP (if one exists), and to discuss whether special education services are still necessary. It's a collaborative process that brings together various individuals who can contribute to a comprehensive understanding of your child's strengths and areas where they need support. This process is mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and by Chapter 29 of the Texas Education Code, ensuring your child's rights are protected. Remember that the ARD meeting is not just a formality; it is a critical opportunity for you to actively participate in shaping your child's educational journey.

The ARD committee consists of several key members, each with specific roles and responsibilities. As a parent, you are a vital member of this team. Other required members include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of specially designed instruction, your child's special education teacher (or if your child is not yet receiving special education, a special education teacher qualified to serve a child of your child's age), a general education teacher familiar with your child's progress if the child is participating in the general education environment, and someone who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results. The district may also invite other individuals with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, such as related service providers (speech therapists, occupational therapists, counselors, etc.) or diagnosticians. You, as the parent, also have the right to invite anyone you believe can contribute valuable insight to the meeting, such as a trusted friend, family member, or advocate. The ARD committee works together to make decisions regarding your child's eligibility for special education, their educational needs, appropriate goals, and the services necessary to help them achieve those goals.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Beaumont ISD is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is extremely important because it allows you adequate time to prepare and gather any information you want to share with the ARD committee. The notice must include specific information about the purpose of the meeting, including whether it's for an initial evaluation, annual review, or to address a specific concern. It must also include the date, time, and location of the meeting, as well as a list of the individuals who will be attending, if known. The notice should clearly explain your rights as a parent within the ARD process, including your right to participate and to bring others to the meeting. If you are unsure why the meeting is being called or have any questions about the information provided, do not hesitate to contact the school district to request clarification. It is crucial that you understand the purpose of the meeting and feel adequately prepared to participate effectively. If the district fails to give the 5-day notice, you are entitled to postpone the meeting until it is given. While you are entitled to the 5-day notice, it is possible to waive this right, but only if you do so in writing. This may be beneficial if you are eager to move forward with the process and believe that delaying the meeting would not be in your child's best interest.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is essential for advocating for their needs. Assemble all relevant documents to ensure the ARD committee has a complete picture of your child's strengths, challenges, and progress. If your child has undergone any prior evaluations, whether from the school district or independent professionals, bring copies of those reports. These evaluations can provide valuable insights into your child's academic, cognitive, or behavioral functioning. If your child has seen any doctors or specialists, bring letters or reports from them outlining their diagnoses, treatment plans, and any recommendations they have for supporting your child's educational needs. Gather any school records, such as report cards, progress reports, or work samples, that demonstrate your child's academic performance and areas where they may be struggling. It's also helpful to prepare a written list of your specific concerns about your child's education. This list can include academic challenges, social-emotional difficulties, or any other areas where you believe your child needs additional support. Bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can help you understand the information presented, voice your concerns, and ensure your child's rights are protected. Texas is a one-party consent state for recording audio. You are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting as long as you notify the other members of the ARD team that you are recording the session. Using a voice recorder can be incredibly helpful, allowing you to focus on the discussion without worrying about taking detailed notes. You can refer back to the recording later to ensure that the IEP accurately reflects the decisions made during the meeting.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a critical safeguard to ensure that ARD committee decisions are made thoughtfully and with adequate information. Any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess of up to ten school days before decisions are finalized. This is known as the "10-day recess rule." This recess allows you to gather more information, consult with experts, or simply take time to process the information discussed during the meeting. For instance, if you are presented with a new evaluation report or a proposed IEP that you need more time to review, you can invoke the 10-day recess rule. To invoke the rule, simply state during the ARD meeting that you are requesting a recess of up to ten school days to gather additional information or seek further consultation. It is best to make this request explicitly and clearly, so there is no confusion. The team cannot finalize the IEP until after this recess period, unless you agree to reconvene sooner. This tool empowers you to ensure that you have all the information you need to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the central document created during the ARD meeting. It outlines your child's unique educational needs and the specific services and supports they will receive to address those needs. Several key areas are decided upon during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP. First, the team will determine your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in various areas, based on evaluations, classroom observations, and input from parents and teachers. Next, the ARD committee will develop annual goals that are measurable and designed to help your child make progress in their areas of need. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The IEP also outlines the specific services your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. The frequency, duration, and location of these services are also specified. Placement refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom with accommodations and support, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. The IEP also includes a list of accommodations, which are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional practices that help your child access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Examples include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. It is important to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home to review it carefully and consult with others before providing your consent.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with some or all of the decisions made by the ARD committee. You, as a parent, have specific rights when disagreements arise. First, you have the right to refuse consent for the initial placement of your child in special education. This means that even if the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education services, you can decline to allow them to be placed in a special education program. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you do not agree with its contents. By not signing, you are indicating that you do not consent to the implementation of the IEP as it is currently written. If you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. This means that you can have your child evaluated by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district, and the district must pay for the evaluation if it meets certain criteria. Even if you sign the IEP, you can write "Parent Disagrees" on the document next to your signature. This indicates that you do not agree with certain aspects of the IEP, and it preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you have tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions and have been unsuccessful, there are several formal options available to you. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a resolution within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law. The second option is mediation, which is also free through TEA. Mediation involves a neutral third party who facilitates communication between you and the school district to help you reach a mutually agreeable resolution. The mediator does not make decisions but helps the parties explore options and find common ground. If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you prefer to pursue a more formal legal process, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal administrative hearing where you and the school district present evidence and arguments before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will make a decision based on the evidence presented. This decision can be appealed to state or federal court. Finally, you also have the option to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. Your rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Chapter 89 of Title 19 of the Texas Administrative Code (19 TAC Chapter 89). Understanding these laws will help you advocate effectively for your child's educational needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
