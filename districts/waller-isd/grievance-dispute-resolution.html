Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Know Your Limits!

Waller ISD, like all Texas school districts, adheres to state laws regarding suspensions. Texas Education Code permits schools to suspend students for various disciplinary infractions. Crucially, a student cannot be suspended for more than three school days for minor infractions. However, certain offenses trigger longer suspensions, including potential placement in a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP).

If your child is suspended, IMMEDIATELY request a written explanation of the reasons for the suspension. Scrutinize this document. Was the alleged offense described accurately? Does the punishment fit the crime? Is there any indication of bias or discrimination? A pattern of short-term suspensions adding up to more than 10 cumulative days within a school year may trigger additional protections, especially for students with IEPs (Individualized Education Programs) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

What Is an MDR? (Manifestation Determination Review)

An MDR, or Manifestation Determination Review, is a critical safeguard for students with IEPs facing long-term suspension (more than 10 consecutive school days) or a change in placement. If your child has an IEP and is facing this level of discipline, INSIST on an MDR immediately.

The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's behavior leading to the disciplinary action was caused by or directly and substantially related to the student's disability, or if it was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP. If the answer to either of these questions is YES, then the disciplinary action may be deemed inappropriate and the student may need to return to their regular placement, or adjustments to the IEP need to be made.

Prepare for the MDR meeting. Gather all relevant documentation, including IEPs, behavior intervention plans (BIPs), psychological evaluations, and any communication with the school regarding your child's behavior. Bring an advocate or attorney if possible. Demand that the school show clear evidence that the behavior was NOT related to the disability.

Change of Placement: Fighting for Your Child's Education

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days for disciplinary reasons. This includes expulsion, placement in a DAEP, or suspension that effectively removes the student from their educational program. For students with IEPs, a Change of Placement triggers specific rights under IDEA.

Waller ISD cannot unilaterally change the placement of a student with an IEP for disciplinary reasons without holding an MDR (if applicable) and providing due process protections. If the school attempts to do so, you must object IMMEDIATELY and assert your child's rights. Demand a clear explanation of the proposed new placement and how it will meet your child's educational needs. Is the new placement appropriate? Does it provide the necessary supports and services outlined in the IEP?

Filing a Level 1 Grievance: Your First Line of Defense

Waller ISD has a formal grievance process. If you believe your child's rights have been violated, file a Level 1 Grievance IMMEDIATELY. Obtain the grievance form from the school or district website. Complete it thoroughly, outlining the specific incident(s), the policy violation(s), and the desired resolution. Include all supporting documentation.

Be specific and factual. Avoid emotional language. Focus on the concrete evidence of the school's wrongdoing. Submit the grievance to the appropriate administrator (usually the principal or assistant principal). You have a limited timeframe to file a Level 1 grievance - typically 10-15 days from the incident. Act fast.

TEA State Complaint: Escalating Your Fight

If the Level 1 Grievance is unsuccessful, or if you believe the district is violating IDEA or other federal or state laws, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). TEA investigates allegations of noncompliance and can order the district to take corrective action.

TEA complaints have strict deadlines. Research the TEA complaint process thoroughly. Gather all relevant documentation, including the Level 1 Grievance, the school's response, and any other evidence of wrongdoing. A well-documented TEA complaint can be a powerful tool for holding the district accountable.

Due Process: Your Right to a Fair Hearing

Due process is your fundamental right to a fair hearing and a chance to challenge disciplinary actions taken against your child. This is especially critical for students with disabilities under IDEA. You have the right to notice, the right to present evidence, the right to question witnesses, and the right to legal representation.

Never waive your right to due process. If the school proposes a disciplinary action that you believe is unjust or violates your child's rights, demand a formal hearing. Consult with an attorney or advocate to understand your rights and prepare your case. The timeline for requesting a hearing is often short, so act quickly.

Remember: You are your child's best advocate. Arm yourself with knowledge, assert your rights, and never give up fighting for their education.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
