What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting is a very important meeting about your child's special education services. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. It is a meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and by Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, that makes sure children with disabilities receive a free appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to bring together a team of people who know your child well to make decisions about their educational program. This includes deciding if your child is eligible for special education, creating an Individualized Education Program (IEP) if they are eligible, reviewing the IEP to make sure it is working, and, if appropriate, dismissing your child from special education services when they no longer need them.

The ARD meeting is not just a formality; it's a crucial opportunity for you, as the parent, to be an active and equal participant in shaping your child's education. You have valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, needs, and learning style. Your input is essential for developing an IEP that is truly tailored to your child. The ARD team usually includes you, the parent; one or more teachers; a school administrator; an evaluation specialist (someone who can interpret evaluation results); and sometimes other professionals, such as therapists or counselors, depending on your child's needs. The district must also invite the student if they are 14 years of age or older, or younger if appropriate, to participate in their ARD meeting. The student is a valuable source of information and should be included in the process as much as possible.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Waller ISD, like all school districts, is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This five-day notice is designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting, gather any relevant information, and arrange your schedule so that you can attend without feeling rushed. It's important to understand that these are calendar days, so weekends and holidays count towards the five-day requirement.

The written notice must include several important details. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting. For example, is it an initial ARD to determine eligibility for special education, a review of an existing IEP, or a meeting to discuss a change in placement? Second, the notice must list the date, time, and location of the meeting. This allows you to make arrangements to be present. Third, the notice must list the names and titles of the individuals who will be attending the meeting. This allows you to anticipate who will be there and potentially request the attendance of other individuals who may have valuable input regarding your child. Finally, the notice must inform you of your right to participate in the ARD meeting and to bring anyone you choose to support you. If you are unable to attend the meeting at the scheduled time, it is crucial that you contact the school as soon as possible to reschedule. If you wish to meet sooner than the required 5-day notice, you can waive this right in writing. However, never feel pressured to waive your right if you need more time to prepare.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparation is key to a successful ARD meeting. Coming prepared will help you advocate effectively for your child's needs. First, bring any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other relevant reports. These documents provide valuable information about your child's strengths and weaknesses. Also, bring any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other professionals who have worked with your child. These documents can provide medical or therapeutic perspectives that the school may not be aware of. In addition, gather any relevant school records, such as report cards, progress reports, work samples, and attendance records. These documents can provide insight into your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom.

More importantly, prepare a written list of your concerns, questions, and priorities for your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. It is easy to get overwhelmed in a meeting and forget important issues, so writing them down beforehand is a smart strategy. Bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, educational advocate, or special education attorney. Having someone with you can provide emotional support, help you remember important points, and offer a different perspective on the discussion. Finally, Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations, so you are legally permitted to record the ARD meeting, provided you notify the ARD team that you are doing so. Recording the meeting can be a helpful way to review the discussion later and ensure that you accurately remember what was said and agreed upon. However, be sure to announce at the beginning of the meeting that you are recording.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The 10-day recess rule is a powerful tool that you, as a parent, can use during an ARD meeting if you feel you need more time to consider the decisions being made. Any member of the ARD committee, including you, has the right to request a recess of up to ten school days to gather more information or consult with an advocate before finalizing any decisions about your child's IEP. This recess is designed to ensure that all members of the ARD committee have adequate time to review information, seek expert advice, and make informed decisions in the best interests of the child. This is ten school days, meaning weekends and holidays do not count.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state that you would like to recess the meeting. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation, although you may choose to briefly explain why you need more time. For example, you might say, "I would like to recess the meeting for ten days to review the proposed IEP with an advocate." The ARD committee must then grant your request and schedule a date for the reconvened meeting. The recess allows you to step back from the often-pressured environment of the ARD meeting and carefully consider the proposed IEP, placement, and services. Use this time to review documents, conduct independent research, speak with other professionals, or simply reflect on your child's needs and goals. Do not hesitate to invoke the 10-day recess rule if you feel uncertain, overwhelmed, or unprepared to make decisions about your child's education. It is your right, and it can be a valuable tool for ensuring that your child receives the best possible education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP (Individualized Education Program) document is the written record of the decisions made at the ARD meeting. It is a legally binding document that outlines your child's unique educational needs, goals, and services. Understanding the different sections of the IEP is essential for ensuring that your child receives the appropriate support and services.

Several key components are decided during the ARD meeting and then memorialized in the IEP document. First, "present levels of academic achievement and functional performance" describe your child's current strengths and areas where they need support. This section is based on assessments, observations, and input from parents and teachers. Second, "annual goals" are measurable statements that describe what your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). Third, "special education and related services" are the specific supports and services that your child will receive to help them achieve their annual goals. This may include specialized instruction, therapy, counseling, transportation, and assistive technology. Fourth, "placement" refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom with support, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. Fifth, "accommodations and modifications" are adjustments to the curriculum, instruction, or assessment methods that help your child access the general education curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Accommodations change *how* a student learns the material, while modifications change *what* a student is expected to learn.

It is crucial to understand that you do not have to sign the IEP document the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and seek advice from others before signing. Signing the IEP indicates that you agree with the decisions made by the ARD committee. If you do not agree with the IEP, you have the right to refuse to sign it and pursue other options.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD team at some point during their child's special education journey. Remember, you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. You have specific rights if you disagree with the team's decisions.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD team's recommendation that your child receive special education services, you can refuse to give your consent, and the school cannot proceed with providing those services. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with its contents. By refusing to sign, you are indicating that you do not agree with the decisions made by the ARD committee. You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate. Even if you sign the IEP, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document next to the specific areas where you disagree. This creates a written record of your disagreement and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD team through informal discussions, you have several formal escalation options available to you. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89, also known as the Legal Framework.

First, you can file a formal State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. Second, you can request Mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also free and can be a less adversarial way to resolve disagreements. Third, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's special education needs. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing, and the hearing officer's decision is binding on both you and the school district. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action. Remember that these are your rights, and you have the power to advocate for your child's educational needs. Don't hesitate to explore these options if you feel that your child's rights are not being respected.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
