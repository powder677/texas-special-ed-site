Dyslexia Services in Alvin ISD

Dyslexia Services in Alvin ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in Alvin Independent School District (ISD) in Texas. It outlines the district's approach to identification, evaluation, and support for students with dyslexia, adhering to Texas state law and guidelines.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Alvin ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the most current version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. This handbook, published by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), provides comprehensive information for educators and parents regarding dyslexia, including:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook details the procedures for universal screening, progress monitoring, and comprehensive evaluations to identify students at risk for or with dyslexia.
  • Instructional Practices: It outlines evidence-based, structured literacy approaches for teaching students with dyslexia.
  • Legal Framework: The handbook clarifies the legal requirements for serving students with dyslexia under state and federal laws, including Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the importance of collaboration between schools and parents in supporting students with dyslexia.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: Guidance on implementing appropriate accommodations and modifications in the classroom to support student learning.

Parents are strongly encouraged to familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. A digital copy is available on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website.

Mandatory Screening

Alvin ISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. This screening is designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. The screening process typically involves assessing:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to recognize and manipulate the sounds of spoken language (e.g., rhyming, segmenting words into sounds).
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly name a series of familiar objects, colors, letters, or numbers.
  • Letter Knowledge: The ability to identify and name letters of the alphabet.
  • Phoneme Segmentation: The ability to break words down into individual sounds.
  • Nonsense Word Decoding: The ability to apply phonics knowledge to read unfamiliar words.

Students who demonstrate characteristics of dyslexia on the initial screening will receive further evaluation and support. The results of these screenings are shared with parents. It is important to note that a screening is not a diagnosis. A comprehensive evaluation is needed to determine if a student has dyslexia.

Students in higher grades can be referred for screening or evaluation at any time if concerns arise regarding their reading or spelling skills.

Structured Literacy Programs

Alvin ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide explicit, systematic, and multisensory instruction to students with dyslexia. These programs are based on the science of reading and address the key components of reading:

  • Phonology: The sound system of language.
  • Orthography: The writing system of language (spelling).
  • Morphology: The study of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, root words).
  • Syntax: The rules governing sentence structure.
  • Semantics: The meaning of words and sentences.

While specific programs may vary across campuses, all programs used within Alvin ISD are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and meet the following criteria:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly.
  • Systematic Instruction: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Multisensory Instruction: Students learn through visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile modalities.
  • Sequential Instruction: Skills are taught in a specific order.
  • Cumulative Instruction: Each lesson builds upon previous knowledge.

Commonly used structured literacy programs in Texas include (but are not limited to):

  • Take Flight: Developed by the Luke Waites Center for Dyslexia at Texas Scottish Rite Hospital for Children.
  • Neuhaus Education Center Programs: Several programs offered, including Basic Language Skills and Reading Readiness.
  • Orton-Gillingham Approach: A general instructional approach; specific programs must be Orton-Gillingham-based.

Parents can contact their child's school to inquire about the specific structured literacy program being used.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the impact of dyslexia on their academic performance.

  • Section 504 Plan: A Section 504 plan provides accommodations to students with disabilities to ensure they have equal access to education. To qualify for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. Dyslexia can qualify as a disability under Section 504. Students with a 504 plan typically receive accommodations within the general education classroom.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program): An IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the special education and related services a student with a disability will receive. To qualify for an IEP, a student must have one of the 13 disabilities listed in IDEA, and the disability must adversely affect their educational performance to the extent that they need specialized instruction. Dyslexia can be a specific learning disability that qualifies a student for an IEP. An IEP may include specialized instruction, related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy), and accommodations.

Key Differences:

  • Eligibility Criteria: IEP has stricter eligibility requirements than Section 504.
  • Services Provided: IEPs provide specialized instruction and related services, while 504 plans primarily offer accommodations.
  • Funding: IEPs are funded through federal and state special education funds, while 504 plans are typically funded through general education funds.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs have more stringent legal requirements than 504 plans.

The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee or the 504 committee, which includes parents, educators, and other relevant professionals, will determine whether a student qualifies for a 504 plan or an IEP based on the student's individual needs and assessment data.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, but they do not change what the student is expected to learn. These are implemented within general education to allow students to fully access the curriculum. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia include, but are not limited to:

  • Extended Time: Allowing students extra time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing students in a location where they can easily see and hear the teacher and avoid distractions.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to audio recordings of textbooks and other reading materials.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that reads text aloud.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Using software that converts spoken words into written text.
  • Word Prediction Software: Using software that suggests words as the student types.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing students with notes or allowing them to record lectures.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or format of assignments.
  • Alternative Assessments: Offering alternative ways for students to demonstrate their knowledge, such as oral presentations or projects.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Providing templates to organize thoughts and written output.
  • Access to a Quiet Testing Environment: Reducing distractions during assessments.
  • Spelling Support: Using spell check, providing a word bank, or allowing phonetic spelling.
  • Chunking Assignments: Breaking down large assignments into smaller, more manageable parts.

The specific accommodations provided to a student will be determined by the ARD committee or 504 committee based on the student's individual needs. The Texas Dyslexia Handbook provides a more comprehensive list of accommodations.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading or spelling skills, you can request an evaluation for dyslexia. The process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Contact Your Child's Teacher: The first step is to communicate your concerns with your child's teacher. They can provide insights into your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom.
  2. Contact the School Principal or Counselor: If concerns persist, contact the school principal or counselor to discuss your concerns and request an evaluation.
  3. Submit a Written Request: While a verbal request is helpful, it is highly recommended to submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The written request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth.
    • A description of your concerns about your child's reading or spelling skills.
    • Any relevant information about your child's developmental history or medical conditions.
    • Your contact information.
  4. Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, such as classroom performance, grades, and previous assessments, to determine if an evaluation is warranted.
  5. Evaluation Process: If an evaluation is deemed necessary, the school will conduct a comprehensive evaluation to assess your child's reading, spelling, and other relevant skills. The evaluation may include standardized tests, informal assessments, and classroom observations.
  6. Evaluation Report and ARD/504 Meeting: Following the evaluation, the school will provide you with a written report outlining the results and recommendations. An ARD (for special education) or 504 committee meeting will be held to discuss the evaluation findings and determine if your child is eligible for special education services or accommodations under Section 504.

Alvin ISD is committed to providing timely and appropriate support to students with dyslexia. If you have any questions or concerns, please contact your child's school or the district's Special Education Department.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
