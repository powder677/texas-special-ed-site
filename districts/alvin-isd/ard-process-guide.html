What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Alvin ISD! If you're reading this, you are likely a parent or guardian of a student who is either being considered for special education services or is already receiving them. Understanding the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) process is crucial to advocating for your child's educational needs. An ARD meeting is essentially a meeting of experts, including you, who come together to develop and review an Individualized Education Program, or IEP, for your child. This IEP is a written document that outlines your child's unique needs, sets specific, measurable goals, and describes the services and supports the school will provide to help your child achieve those goals.

The ARD meeting is required by both federal law, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), and Texas state law, specifically Texas Education Code Section 29. These laws ensure that students with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their individual needs. The ARD committee is responsible for making decisions about all aspects of your child's special education program. This includes determining whether your child is eligible for special education, developing the IEP, deciding on the appropriate placement for your child (where they will receive services), and reviewing your child's progress at least annually. The ARD committee must consider your child's strengths and areas of need as well as consider evaluations, if any, provided by outside sources.

Who attends the ARD meeting? The ARD committee is a team, and you, as the parent, are a vital member. Other members typically include: a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education services (often an administrator or special education director), your child's general education teacher (if your child is participating in the general education curriculum), your child's special education teacher or service provider, an educational diagnostician (who can interpret evaluation results), and any other individuals who have knowledge or expertise about your child, such as related service providers like speech therapists, occupational therapists, or counselors. You can also invite anyone you feel would be helpful to the process, such as a family member, friend, or advocate. Your child may also attend the ARD meeting, especially as they get older, to participate in the discussion about their education and goals.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

In Texas, you have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This notice is not just a formality; it's designed to give you adequate time to prepare for the meeting and gather any information you need to effectively participate. The notice must include very specific details, such as the purpose of the meeting (e.g., initial eligibility determination, annual review, change of placement), the date, time, and location of the meeting, who will be in attendance (names and titles), and a clear statement of your parental rights under IDEA and state law. The notice should also inform you that you have the right to invite other individuals to the meeting who have knowledge or expertise about your child.

It is very important to pay close attention to the proposed agenda included with the notice. If you feel something is missing from the agenda or that additional topics should be addressed, contact the school as soon as possible to request that the agenda be amended. This ensures that all relevant issues are discussed during the meeting. For example, if you are concerned about your child's behavior at school, you should ensure that behavior is specifically listed on the agenda.

While you are entitled to at least five days' notice, you can waive this right in writing if you choose to meet sooner. This might be necessary if there is an urgent situation or if you and the school agree that it is in your child's best interest to hold the meeting as soon as possible. However, never feel pressured to waive your right to the five-day notice. Take the time you need to prepare and gather your thoughts.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but being well-prepared can make a significant difference in the outcome. One of the most important things you can do is gather relevant documents and information to share with the ARD committee. Start by compiling any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other relevant reports.

Bring any letters or reports from doctors, therapists, or other professionals who have worked with your child. These documents can provide valuable insights into your child's medical, emotional, or behavioral needs. If you have any school records that you feel are relevant, such as report cards, progress reports, or disciplinary records, bring those along as well. It is also helpful to create a written list of your concerns and priorities for your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. Writing it down in advance can help you remember important points you want to make, especially when you are feeling stressed or emotional.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, help you understand the information being presented, and ensure that your voice is heard. Having someone with you can make you feel more confident and empowered during the meeting. In Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided you give the school district notice that you intend to do so. A recording can be a helpful tool for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that all decisions are accurately documented.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a valuable tool that every parent should know about: the 10-day recess rule. Any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess of the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days. This recess allows time to gather additional information, consult with outside experts, or simply to take a break and process the information that has been presented. This is not 10 calendar days, but 10 instructional days for the school district.

There are many situations where invoking the 10-day recess rule might be beneficial. For example, if the ARD committee is considering a significant change to your child's placement, such as moving them to a more restrictive environment, you might want to recess the meeting to consult with an advocate or attorney to understand the implications of that change. If the ARD committee is proposing goals or services that you don't understand or disagree with, you might want to recess the meeting to gather more information or seek a second opinion. The recess is designed to empower you to make informed decisions about your child's education.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state your request to the ARD committee during the meeting. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation for your request, although it can be helpful to briefly explain why you need the recess. The ARD committee is required to grant your request. The meeting will then be reconvened within 10 school days to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the heart of your child's special education program. It is a legally binding document that outlines your child's unique needs, sets specific, measurable goals, and describes the services and supports the school will provide to help your child achieve those goals. Several key elements are decided upon during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP.

One of the most important sections of the IEP is the "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" (often shortened to Present Levels). This section describes your child's current academic and functional skills, including their strengths, areas of need, and how their disability affects their involvement and progress in the general education curriculum. This section should be very detailed and specific, providing a clear picture of your child's current abilities. The Present Levels section serves as the foundation for developing appropriate goals for your child. Next, the IEP outlines annual goals, which are measurable statements that describe what your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be based on your child's Present Levels and should be ambitious but realistic. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The IEP also specifies the services and supports your child will receive, such as special education instruction, related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling), accommodations (changes to how your child learns), and modifications (changes to what your child learns). The IEP will also specify the location (placement) where your child will receive these services. This could include the general education classroom, a resource room, or a self-contained classroom. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material. A common accommodation is extra time on tests.

Remember, you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and consider whether you agree with the decisions that were made. Take as much time as you need to make an informed decision. If you have any questions or concerns, contact the school to discuss them before signing the IEP.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD committee. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your voice matters. If you disagree with the ARD committee's decisions, there are several steps you can take. First, it is important to document your disagreement. If you disagree with the initial eligibility determination for special education services, you have the right to refuse consent for your child to receive those services. You can also refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with the goals, services, or placement outlined in the document. If you choose not to sign the IEP, the school district cannot implement the IEP without your consent. You can document your disagreement by writing "Parent Disagrees" on the IEP document before signing and returning it.

You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The IEE can provide a second opinion about your child's needs and can be valuable in resolving disagreements with the ARD committee. You should make this request in writing and keep a copy for your records.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal means, there are several formal options available to you under IDEA and Texas law. It is important to remember that these options are designed to protect your child's rights and ensure that they receive a free and appropriate public education.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). A TEA state complaint is a formal written complaint that alleges the school district has violated IDEA or state special education laws. TEA must investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This is a free process. Secondly, Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party (a mediator) helps you and the school district reach an agreement. Mediation is also free and is offered through TEA. This is often a good first step because it helps open communication. Thirdly, you can request a Due Process Hearing, which is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence from both sides and makes a decision about the dispute. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a Due Process Hearing. Due Process is often a very lengthy and costly process. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child on the basis of their disability. These rights are governed by the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process which references IDEA 2004 and Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
