How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Alvin ISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Alvin Independent School District (AISD), based on Texas law. This includes understanding your rights, the evaluation process, and what to do if you encounter obstacles. Navigating the special education system can be challenging, but this guide aims to provide clarity and empower you as your child's advocate.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first and most crucial step in initiating the special education process is submitting a formal, written request for a full individual and initial evaluation (FIE). This request should be directed to the appropriate school official. Ideally, this would be the school principal, the special education director, or the school's 504 coordinator. Sending the request via certified mail with a return receipt requested is highly recommended to ensure proof of delivery and receipt by the district.

Your written request should include the following information:

  • Your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your child's current grade level and school.
  • Your contact information, including your phone number and email address.
  • A clear and concise statement requesting a full individual and initial evaluation to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education and related services.
  • A detailed explanation of your specific concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. Be as specific as possible, providing concrete examples. For instance, instead of saying "My child is struggling in reading," you might say "My child is struggling to decode single-syllable words, cannot comprehend grade-level reading passages, and consistently scores below grade level on reading assessments."
  • Any supporting documentation that highlights your concerns. This may include report cards, test scores, teacher communications, medical records, or observations you have made at home.
  • A statement that you are requesting an evaluation under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.

Here's a sample sentence you can use: "I am writing to formally request a full individual and initial evaluation to determine if my child, [Child's Name], born on [Date of Birth], has a disability and needs special education and related services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law."

Keep a copy of your written request for your records.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law establishes a strict timeline for the special education evaluation process. Once the school district receives your written request for an evaluation, they have a maximum of 45 school days to complete the evaluation and hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. It is critical to understand that this is 45 *school* days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks do not count towards the 45-day timeline.

During this 45-day period, the school district will likely take the following steps:

  • Review your request and any supporting documentation.
  • Contact you to gather additional information about your child's needs.
  • Conduct the necessary evaluations in all areas of suspected disability. This may include academic testing, psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy evaluations, and other specialized assessments.
  • Compile the evaluation results into a comprehensive written report called the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE).
  • Schedule an ARD committee meeting to discuss the FIE results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services.

It is important to actively participate in this process. Communicate with the school district, attend scheduled meetings, and ask questions to ensure that your child's needs are being adequately addressed. Document all communication with the school district, including dates, times, and the content of conversations.

If the school district fails to complete the evaluation within the 45-school day timeline, you have the right to pursue legal remedies, such as filing a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) or requesting mediation.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is the cornerstone of the special education process. It is a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and weaknesses in all areas related to the suspected disability. The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals and must include a variety of assessment tools and techniques.

Texas law requires the FIE to cover the following areas, as appropriate:

  • Academic Achievement: Assessments of reading, writing, math, and other academic skills.
  • Cognitive Abilities: Assessments of intellectual functioning, such as IQ tests.
  • Adaptive Behavior: Assessments of daily living skills, such as self-care, communication, and social skills.
  • Communication Skills: Assessments of speech, language, and communication abilities.
  • Motor Skills: Assessments of gross motor and fine motor skills.
  • Social and Emotional Development: Assessments of social skills, emotional regulation, and mental health.
  • Vision and Hearing: Screening of vision and hearing. If concerns are identified, a more comprehensive evaluation by a qualified professional is required.
  • Health: Review of relevant medical records and information from your child's physician.

The FIE must also include:

  • A review of your child's educational history, including report cards, previous evaluations, and teacher observations.
  • Information from parents and other relevant sources. Your input is crucial and should be carefully considered by the evaluation team.
  • Observations of your child in the classroom and other school settings.
  • A determination of whether your child has a disability as defined by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.
  • A description of your child's educational needs, including specific areas of concern and recommendations for appropriate special education and related services.

After the evaluation, you have the right to receive a copy of the FIE report. Review it carefully and ask questions if anything is unclear.

Child Find Obligations

The "Child Find" mandate is a critical component of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law. It requires all school districts, including Alvin ISD, to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within their boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This includes children who are suspected of having a disability but are not yet receiving special education services.

AISD has a responsibility to proactively seek out children who may be eligible for special education. This includes:

  • Screening children upon enrollment in school.
  • Monitoring student progress in the general education curriculum.
  • Responding to referrals from parents, teachers, and other professionals who suspect that a child may have a disability.
  • Conducting public awareness campaigns to inform the community about special education services.

If you believe that your child may have a disability, you do not need to wait for the school district to initiate an evaluation. You have the right to request an evaluation at any time. The Child Find mandate strengthens your position as a parent because it highlights the district's proactive duty, not just reactive one, to identify children who may need help.

What If They Refuse

The school district may refuse to conduct a special education evaluation if they believe that there is no reasonable suspicion that your child has a disability. However, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for refusing. This written explanation is called Prior Written Notice (PWN).

If you disagree with the school district's refusal, you have several options:

  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe that the school district has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Texas state law.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which you can present evidence and argue your case before an impartial hearing officer. This is a more adversarial process than mediation and may require the assistance of an attorney.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) of your child at public expense if you disagree with the school district's evaluation. The school district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that their evaluation was appropriate.

It is highly recommended that you consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law if the school district refuses to evaluate your child. They can help you understand your rights and navigate the legal process.

After the Evaluation

After the evaluation is complete, the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee will meet to discuss the results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services. The ARD committee includes you, the parent, as an equal member, along with school administrators, teachers, and evaluation personnel.

If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education, they will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written plan that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific special education and related services that will be provided to meet those needs. The IEP should be tailored to your child's individual strengths and weaknesses and should be reviewed and updated at least annually.

If the ARD committee determines that your child is not eligible for special education, you have the right to appeal this decision. You can also explore other options, such as a 504 plan, which provides accommodations and supports for students with disabilities who do not require special education services. A 504 plan is covered under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.

Remember, you are your child's most important advocate. Stay informed, participate actively in the special education process, and don't hesitate to seek help from attorneys, advocates, or parent support groups.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
