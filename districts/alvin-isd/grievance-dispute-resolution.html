Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Alvin ISD, like all Texas school districts, has rules regarding suspensions. Understanding the 10-day suspension rule is crucial. This rule is particularly important for students with disabilities, but it impacts all students.

The General Rule: A school can suspend a student for up to, but not exceeding, 10 school days for a disciplinary infraction. This can be done in incremental blocks (e.g., 2 days here, 3 days there) or all at once. Keep meticulous records of every suspension day your child receives.

The Trigger Point for Students with Disabilities: The crucial point is when the *cumulative* suspension days reach 10 in a single academic year. Once a student with a disability reaches 10 days of suspension, a Change of Placement becomes a potential issue, triggering specific rights and procedures under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). This includes the right to a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).

Tracking Suspension Days: It is YOUR responsibility to track your child's suspension days. Don't rely solely on the school. Keep a detailed log with dates, reasons for suspension, and the number of days assigned. If you believe the school is not accurately counting suspension days, document your concerns in writing to the principal and special education director immediately.

What to Do if Near 10 Days: If your child is nearing 10 cumulative suspension days, contact the school's special education department (if applicable) and the principal immediately. Request a meeting to discuss the situation and proactively address any underlying issues contributing to the disciplinary actions. Put your request in writing, with a dated copy for your records.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a critical meeting designed to determine whether a student's behavior leading to disciplinary action is a manifestation of their disability. This means the behavior was either caused by the disability or was a direct result of the school's failure to properly implement the student's Individualized Education Program (IEP).

When Does an MDR Occur? An MDR is required when a student with a disability is facing a Change of Placement due to a disciplinary violation (typically after exceeding 10 days of suspension or if the school is considering expulsion or removal to an Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES) for more than 10 days). The MDR must occur no later than 10 school days after the decision to change the student's placement.

Who Attends the MDR? The IEP team, including the parent(s)/guardian(s), relevant school personnel (e.g., teachers, administrators, special education staff), and anyone else with relevant knowledge of the child. You have the right to invite outside experts, such as therapists or advocates, to the MDR. Inform the school in advance if you plan to bring someone.

What Happens at the MDR? The team will review all relevant information, including:

  • The student's IEP
  • Evaluation data
  • Observations of the student
  • Disciplinary records
  • Information provided by the parents

Two Key Questions: The MDR team will address two crucial questions:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP?

Possible Outcomes of the MDR:

  • If the Answer is YES to either question: The behavior IS considered a manifestation of the disability. The student returns to their original placement (unless the parents and school agree otherwise), or the IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and implement a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP), or revise the existing BIP if one is already in place. The goal is to prevent the behavior from recurring. The school must also address any failures in IEP implementation.
  • If the Answer is NO to both questions: The behavior is NOT considered a manifestation of the disability. The student can be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. However, the school must still provide special education services as outlined in the IEP during the period of suspension or alternative placement.

Important Considerations:

  • Parental Input is Crucial: Your voice is essential during the MDR. Prepare thoroughly by gathering any relevant documentation, observations, and information about your child's behavior and disability. Don't be afraid to advocate for your child's needs.
  • Documentation is Key: Document everything discussed and agreed upon during the MDR. Request a copy of the MDR outcome in writing.
  • If You Disagree: If you disagree with the MDR outcome, you have the right to challenge the decision through the grievance process, mediation, or a due process hearing. See the sections below.

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a significant change in the student's educational program. This is a trigger for IDEA protections for students with disabilities.

Examples of Change of Placement:

  • Expulsion
  • Removal to an Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES) for more than 10 days
  • Suspensions that accumulate to more than 10 days and create a pattern of removal

Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES): An IAES is a temporary placement designed to provide a student with disabilities with continued educational services while addressing disciplinary concerns. The IAES must allow the student to continue to participate in the general education curriculum (to the extent possible), receive the services outlined in their IEP, and address the behavior that led to the removal.

"Unique Circumstances" and the 45-Day IAES: IDEA allows school personnel to remove a student to an IAES for up to 45 school days, regardless of whether the behavior is a manifestation of their disability, if the student:

  • Carries a weapon to school or possesses a weapon at school.
  • Knowingly possesses or uses illegal drugs, or sells or solicits the sale of a controlled substance, while at school or a school function.
  • Has inflicted serious bodily injury upon another person while at school or a school function.

Even in these "unique circumstances," you still have rights and can challenge the placement. Consult with an attorney immediately.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

If you believe Alvin ISD has violated your child's rights or has acted unfairly, you can file a grievance. The first step is typically a Level 1 grievance. Understand that Alvin ISD policy determines the grievance process. Consult the Alvin ISD website for the most up-to-date policy (search "Alvin ISD grievance policy"). Here's a general overview:

Who to File With: A Level 1 grievance is typically filed with the person closest to the issue - often the teacher, coach, or principal. Check the Alvin ISD policy for specific instructions.

Timing: There are strict deadlines for filing a grievance. Typically, you must file the grievance within a specific timeframe (e.g., 10-15 days) of the event that prompted the grievance. Don't delay! Missing the deadline can invalidate your grievance.

Content of the Grievance: Your written grievance should be clear, concise, and factual. Include the following:

  • Your child's name and student ID number
  • The date(s) of the incident(s)
  • A detailed description of the issue, including who was involved and what happened
  • The specific policy or procedure that you believe was violated
  • The remedy you are seeking (e.g., reversal of a suspension, correction of records, change in policy)
  • Any supporting documentation (e.g., emails, witness statements)
  • Your contact information

Keep a Copy: Always keep a dated copy of your grievance and any supporting documents for your records.

Response: The school official is required to respond to your grievance within a specific timeframe (as outlined in the Alvin ISD policy). The response will typically be in writing.

Appeal to Level 2 (and Beyond): If you are not satisfied with the response at Level 1, you may be able to appeal to the next level (e.g., the superintendent or a designated administrator). Again, there are strict deadlines for appealing. Follow the procedures outlined in the Alvin ISD grievance policy carefully.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Alvin ISD has violated state or federal special education laws (IDEA), you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a local grievance.

When to File: You can file a TEA complaint if you believe the district has failed to comply with IDEA or other special education regulations.

Requirements: The TEA requires specific information in your complaint, including:

  • A description of the alleged violation
  • The facts upon which the complaint is based
  • The names and contact information of the individuals involved
  • A proposed resolution to the problem
  • A signed statement confirming that the information is true and accurate

Timeline: The TEA has specific timelines for investigating and resolving complaints. Be prepared to provide documentation and cooperate with the TEA's investigation.

TEA Website: Visit the TEA website (tea.texas.gov) for detailed information on filing a state complaint, including the required forms and procedures.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal process available to parents of students with disabilities who disagree with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, educational placement, or provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE). It's a serious step and typically involves legal representation.

When to Request Due Process: You can request a due process hearing if you have exhausted other avenues (e.g., grievances, mediation) and still disagree with the school district's decisions. Common reasons for requesting due process include disagreements about IEP content, eligibility for special education services, and disciplinary actions.

The Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is similar to a court trial. Both sides present evidence, call witnesses, and make legal arguments before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will issue a written decision.

Legal Representation: Due process hearings are complex legal proceedings. It is highly recommended to seek legal representation from an attorney specializing in special education law. An attorney can help you prepare your case, present evidence effectively, and understand your legal rights.

Statute of Limitations: There are strict statutes of limitations for filing a due process request. Don't delay in seeking legal advice if you are considering this option.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
