What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their ability to learn in a general education setting, you'll be invited to attend an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. Think of it as the most important meeting you'll attend regarding your child's education. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine whether your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program, or IEP, that meets your child's unique needs, to review your child's progress, and if appropriate, to determine if your child no longer needs special education services.

These meetings are required by a federal law called the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA, and by Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. These laws ensure that children with disabilities have access to a free and appropriate public education designed to meet their individual needs. Because your child is at the center of this process, you, as the parent, are a vital member of the ARD committee. Other members of the ARD committee typically include a school administrator, a special education teacher, a general education teacher, and a school psychologist or diagnostician. Depending on your child's needs, other professionals, such as a speech therapist, occupational therapist, or physical therapist, may also be present. And importantly, your child is also encouraged to attend the meeting, especially as they get older, to advocate for themselves. Each person present brings unique knowledge and perspectives to the table, all with the common goal of creating the best possible educational plan for your child.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Harlingen CISD, like all school districts in Texas, is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the scheduled meeting. This means that weekends and holidays count towards those five days. The purpose of this notice is to give you sufficient time to prepare for the meeting, gather your thoughts, collect any relevant documentation, and arrange for childcare or other support you may need. The notice must include several important details. First, it will clearly state the purpose of the meeting: for example, to determine eligibility for special education, to develop an IEP, to review progress, or to consider a change in placement. The notice will also include the date, time, and location of the meeting, as well as a list of the individuals who are expected to attend. It is critical that the notice also clearly explains your rights as a parent under IDEA and Texas Education Code. Finally, the notice should provide contact information for someone at the school district who you can reach out to if you have any questions or concerns before the meeting.

It's important to remember that you have the right to this five-day notice. However, there may be situations where you want to meet sooner. For example, perhaps a pressing issue has arisen that you want to address with the team as quickly as possible. In these cases, you can waive your right to the five-day notice by providing written consent to meet earlier. This is entirely your choice, and the school cannot pressure you to waive this right. Make sure you understand why meeting sooner is being suggested and that you feel comfortable doing so before you agree to waive the notice period.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your ARD meeting can make a significant difference in ensuring that your child's needs are fully considered. You are your child's best advocate, and bringing relevant information to the meeting empowers you to participate effectively. One of the most helpful things you can bring is any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district, private therapists, or doctors. These evaluations can provide valuable insights into your child's strengths and weaknesses, as well as any areas where they may need support. This can include psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy reports, or medical diagnoses. Any documentation that supports your observations and concerns is helpful.

In addition to evaluations, bring any relevant letters or reports from doctors or other medical professionals who have treated your child. These documents can provide important medical context and can help the ARD committee understand any underlying medical conditions that may be impacting your child's learning. Don't hesitate to bring copies of your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, and any notes or communications from teachers or other school staff. These records can help illustrate your child's academic progress and any challenges they may be facing in the classroom. A written list of your concerns is also extremely helpful. It's easy to get overwhelmed during the meeting, so having a prepared list of your questions and concerns ensures that you don't forget anything important. This list can include your observations about your child's academic performance, social-emotional well-being, or any other areas of concern.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person to the meeting with you. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support and help you navigate the meeting. Sometimes, having another person present can help you stay calm and focused, and they can also help you remember important details or ask clarifying questions. Finally, remember that Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. This means you can legally record the ARD meeting, provided you inform the committee that you are doing so. A recording can be a valuable tool for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made. It's always a good idea to provide this notice in writing before the meeting begins.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

Texas law provides a valuable tool for parents (and other ARD committee members) who need more time to consider information presented during an ARD meeting or to gather additional information. This is known as the "10-Day Recess Rule." If at any point during the ARD meeting you feel overwhelmed, unsure about the information being presented, or that you need more time to consult with an advocate or gather additional documentation, you have the right to request a recess of the meeting for up to 10 school days. This means that the meeting will be paused, and the ARD committee will reconvene at a later date, giving you time to address your concerns.

Any member of the ARD committee can invoke the 10-day recess rule, including you, the parent. To invoke this rule, simply state clearly during the meeting that you are requesting a recess under the 10-day rule to [briefly state your reason, such as "review the proposed IEP goals" or "consult with an advocate"]. The school district is then obligated to grant your request. They cannot deny you this right. The recess lasts for a maximum of 10 school days, but you can agree to reconvene the meeting sooner if you are ready. The purpose of the recess is to give you time to make an informed decision about your child's education, so don't hesitate to use this right if you need it.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the heart of the ARD process. It's a written plan that outlines your child's educational needs and the specific services and supports they will receive to meet those needs. Several key areas are addressed in the IEP. First, the IEP will describe your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section summarizes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in different areas, such as reading, writing, math, and social skills. This information is gathered from various sources, including evaluations, teacher observations, and parent input. Based on the present levels, the ARD committee will develop annual goals for your child. These goals are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They outline what your child is expected to achieve in each area within one year. The IEP will also detail the specific special education and related services that your child will receive to help them achieve their annual goals. This might include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or other supports.

The IEP will also specify your child's placement, which refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their services. This could be a general education classroom with supports, a resource room, or a self-contained special education classroom. The IEP will also list any accommodations or modifications that your child needs to be successful in the classroom. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns the material, while modifications are changes to what your child is expected to learn. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. It is critical to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP document at the end of the ARD meeting. Take the time you need to carefully review the document, discuss it with your family and advocate, and ensure that you are comfortable with all the provisions before signing. Your signature indicates that you agree with the IEP and consent to its implementation. If you're not ready to sign, that's perfectly acceptable.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee at some point during the process. Remember, you are an equal member of the team, and your voice matters. You have several rights if you disagree with the team's decisions. First, you have the right to refuse consent for the initial provision of special education services to your child. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's decision that your child needs special education services, you can refuse to consent, and the school district cannot provide those services. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with any of its provisions. As mentioned earlier, your signature indicates your agreement with the IEP. If you disagree, you should not sign the document. Instead, you can write "Parent Disagrees" on the signature line. This indicates that you do not agree with the IEP and preserves your right to pursue other options. If you disagree with an evaluation conducted by the school district, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can be used to inform the ARD committee's decisions.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, you have several formal options available to you. These options are designed to ensure that your child's rights are protected. The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or Texas Education Code. Another option is to request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a trained mediator to try to resolve your disagreements. The mediator is a neutral third party who helps facilitate communication and explore potential solutions. This is also a free service provided by TEA.

If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you choose not to pursue mediation, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal process where you and the school district present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision. Due Process Hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it is highly recommended that you seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. This is a separate process from the TEA complaint and Due Process Hearing. All of these rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code, specifically Title 19, Chapter 89 (19 TAC Chapter 89). Understanding these options empowers you to advocate effectively for your child's education and ensure that their rights are protected.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
