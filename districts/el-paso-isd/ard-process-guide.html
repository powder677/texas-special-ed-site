ARD Meetings in El Paso ISD: A Parent's Guide to Advocating for Your Child

Welcome! This guide is designed to empower you, the parent, to effectively participate in your child's Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meetings within El Paso Independent School District (EPISD). ARD meetings are a critical part of the special education process, where decisions about your child's individualized education program (IEP) are made. Navigating this system can be challenging, but with knowledge and preparation, you can be a powerful advocate for your child's needs. This guide will walk you through key aspects of ARD meetings, equipping you with the information you need to ensure your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE).

What Is an ARD Meeting?

An ARD meeting is a legally mandated meeting under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code (TEC) Chapter 29. It's the central forum where a team, including you as the parent, determines whether your child is eligible for special education services and, if so, develops, reviews, and revises their IEP. The IEP is a written document outlining your child's unique educational needs and the supports and services they require to make progress in the general education curriculum. The ARD committee is composed of several members: * **Parents (You!):** You are an integral part of the team and your input is essential. IDEA and TEC emphasize the importance of parental participation. * **A Representative of the School District:** Usually an administrator or special education supervisor. They must be knowledgeable about the general curriculum and the resources available to the district. * **A Special Education Teacher:** This teacher has specialized knowledge in educating students with disabilities. * **A General Education Teacher:** If your child participates in the general education curriculum, a general education teacher must be present. They provide insight into the grade-level expectations and how your child is performing compared to their peers. * **An Evaluation Specialist:** This person interprets evaluation results, such as psychological or educational assessments. * **The Student (When Appropriate):** If your child is old enough and able to participate meaningfully, they should be included in the meeting. * **Other Individuals:** You, the district, or any other ARD member can invite individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. This could include therapists, doctors, or advocates. Remember, you have the right to bring anyone you feel would be beneficial to the meeting. During the ARD meeting, the team will discuss: * **Eligibility:** Determining if your child meets the criteria for a specific disability category under IDEA. * **Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP):** This describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses, both academically and functionally. It forms the foundation for setting IEP goals. * **Measurable Annual Goals:** These are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within a year. * **Special Education and Related Services:** These are the specific supports and services your child needs to achieve their goals, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and assistive technology. * **Placement:** Determining the least restrictive environment (LRE) where your child can receive their education. This means educating your child with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. * **Accommodations and Modifications:** Adjustments to the curriculum, instruction, or assessment methods that help your child access the general education curriculum.

5-Day Notice Rule

You have the right to receive written notice of the ARD meeting at least five school days before the meeting date. This is mandated by both IDEA and TEC. This notice must include: * **The Purpose of the Meeting:** Clearly state what the ARD meeting will address (e.g., annual review, initial evaluation, revision of IEP). * **The Date, Time, and Location of the Meeting:** Ensure the meeting is scheduled at a time and place convenient for you. * **A List of Participants:** Identify who will be attending the meeting. * **Your Rights as a Parent:** A summary of your procedural safeguards under IDEA, which includes your right to participate, access records, and dispute decisions. **Why is this important?** The 5-day notice rule gives you adequate time to prepare for the meeting, gather information, consult with professionals, and arrange for childcare or other support. If you do not receive proper notice, you have the right to request that the meeting be rescheduled. Document the date you received the notice and compare it to the meeting date to ensure compliance. If the meeting proceeds without proper notice, any decisions made can be challenged.

What to Bring to the ARD Meeting

Preparation is key to a successful ARD meeting. Bringing the right materials can help you advocate effectively for your child. Here's a checklist of items to consider: * **Your Child's IEP (if applicable):** Review the current IEP and identify areas of concern or progress. Highlight goals that have been met or need revision. * **Evaluation Reports:** Bring copies of any recent evaluations, including psychological, educational, speech, occupational, or physical therapy reports. Ensure you understand the findings and how they relate to your child's needs. * **Progress Reports:** Gather progress reports from teachers, therapists, and other service providers. These reports provide valuable data on your child's performance. * **Work Samples:** Collect examples of your child's work, both good and areas needing improvement. This can help illustrate their strengths and weaknesses. * **Medical Records:** If your child has medical conditions that affect their education, bring relevant medical records and doctor's notes. * **A List of Concerns and Questions:** Prepare a list of specific concerns you have about your child's education. Write down any questions you want to ask the ARD committee. * **A Written Statement:** Consider writing a brief statement summarizing your concerns, goals, and expectations for your child's education. This can help you stay focused during the meeting. * **An Advocate or Support Person:** Bring a friend, family member, or advocate to provide support and help you remember important details. * **A Notebook and Pen:** Take notes during the meeting to document the discussion, decisions, and any agreements made. * **Any Relevant Documentation:** This could include private evaluation reports, documentation of communication with school staff, or anything else that supports your child's needs. * **A Positive Attitude (Even When It's Hard!):** While it's important to be assertive, try to maintain a collaborative and respectful tone. Remember, you and the school district share the same goal: to help your child succeed.

The 10-Day Recess

Under Texas Education Code (TEC §29.005), you have the right to request a 10-day recess if you disagree with the ARD committee's proposed IEP. This recess gives you time to consider the IEP, gather additional information, and consult with professionals. **How to Request a Recess:** * **During the ARD Meeting:** You must verbally request the recess *during* the ARD meeting. Clearly state that you disagree with the proposed IEP and are requesting a 10-day recess. * **In Writing:** It's also a good idea to follow up with a written request to the ARD chairperson, documenting your request and the date it was made. * **The Recess Period:** The 10-day recess does not include weekends or holidays. It provides you with actual working days to consider the IEP. **What to Do During the Recess:** * **Review the IEP Thoroughly:** Carefully examine the goals, services, and accommodations outlined in the IEP. * **Consult with Professionals:** Seek advice from therapists, doctors, or educational consultants. * **Gather Additional Information:** Collect any additional data or documentation that supports your position. * **Prepare Your Response:** Develop a written response outlining your specific concerns and proposed changes to the IEP. **After the Recess:** * **Reconvene the ARD Committee:** The ARD committee must reconvene within a reasonable timeframe after the 10-day recess. * **Present Your Concerns:** Clearly articulate your concerns and proposed changes to the IEP. * **Negotiate in Good Faith:** Work collaboratively with the ARD committee to reach an agreement on the IEP. The 10-day recess is a powerful tool that allows you to pause the process and ensure you have the time and information you need to make informed decisions. Don't hesitate to use it if you feel overwhelmed or uncertain about the proposed IEP.

Disagreeing With the Team

Even with thorough preparation, you may find yourself disagreeing with the ARD committee. It's crucial to understand your rights and options in this situation. Remember, you are a vital member of the team, and your voice matters. **Document Your Disagreement:** * **Clearly State Your Position:** During the ARD meeting, clearly state that you disagree with the proposed IEP and explain the reasons for your disagreement. * **Request Documentation:** Ask that your dissenting opinion be documented in the ARD meeting minutes. * **Refuse to Sign:** You are not required to sign the IEP if you disagree with it. Signing the IEP indicates that you attended the meeting, not necessarily that you agree with the contents. **Available Options When You Disagree:** * **10-Day Recess (as discussed above):** Utilize the 10-day recess to gather information and prepare your response. * **Mediation:** Request mediation through the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. * **Due Process Hearing:** File a formal complaint with TEA requesting a due process hearing. This is a more formal and adversarial process where an independent hearing officer will make a decision about the dispute. * **State Complaint:** File a complaint with TEA alleging that the school district has violated IDEA or state special education laws. **Choosing the Right Option:** The best option for resolving your disagreement depends on the specific circumstances of your case. Mediation is a good option if you believe you can work collaboratively with the school district to reach a resolution. A due process hearing may be necessary if you have significant disagreements and believe the school district is not providing your child with FAPE. A state complaint can address systemic issues within the school district.

Filing a State Complaint

A state complaint is a formal written complaint filed with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) alleging that El Paso ISD has violated a requirement of IDEA or state special education laws and regulations. This is different from a due process hearing, which addresses specific issues related to your child's IEP. A state complaint addresses broader issues of compliance. **Grounds for Filing a State Complaint:** You can file a state complaint if you believe EPISD has: * Failed to properly evaluate your child. * Failed to develop an appropriate IEP. * Failed to implement the IEP. * Failed to provide necessary related services. * Failed to follow procedural safeguards. * Discriminated against your child based on their disability. **How to File a State Complaint:** * **In Writing:** The complaint must be submitted in writing to TEA. * **Include Specific Information:** The complaint must include: * Your child's name and address. * The name and address of the school your child attends. * A description of the problem or violation, including specific facts and dates. * A proposed resolution to the problem. * **Timeline:** You must file the complaint within one year of the date of the alleged violation. **TEA's Investigation:** TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days, unless an extension is granted. The investigation may involve: * Reviewing documents. * Conducting interviews with school staff and parents. * Visiting the school. **Outcomes of the Investigation:** If TEA finds that EPISD has violated IDEA or state special education laws, TEA will order the district to take corrective action, which may include: * Developing or revising policies and procedures. * Providing training to staff. * Compensating your child for any harm caused by the violation. **Important Considerations:** * **Documentation is Key:** Maintain thorough records of all communication with the school district, including emails, letters, and meeting minutes. * **Consult with an Advocate:** Consider consulting with a special education advocate or attorney for assistance in preparing and filing the complaint. This guide is a starting point. Remember to stay informed, advocate assertively, and always keep your child's best interests at the forefront. You are your child's best advocate! Good luck.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
