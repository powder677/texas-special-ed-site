Dyslexia Services in El Paso ISD

Dyslexia Services in El Paso ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available within the El Paso Independent School District (EPISD), based on current Texas guidelines and best practices. It is intended for parents, educators, and students to understand the identification, evaluation, and support processes for students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

EPISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the most recent version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. This handbook, updated in 2024, serves as the primary resource for understanding dyslexia, identifying students at risk, and providing appropriate interventions. It is crucial for all stakeholders to familiarize themselves with this document.

Key Updates and Considerations from the 2024 Handbook:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides the updated definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Emphasis on Early Identification: The handbook stresses the importance of early identification and intervention. Universal screening measures and progress monitoring are key to identifying students at risk as early as kindergarten and first grade.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook reinforces the use of Structured Literacy principles as the foundation for effective reading intervention for students with dyslexia. This includes explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multisensory instruction.
  • Collaboration and Communication: The handbook emphasizes the importance of collaboration between parents, teachers, administrators, and specialists in the development and implementation of individualized intervention plans.
  • Technology Integration: The handbook recognizes the role of assistive technology in supporting students with dyslexia.

You can access the full Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website: https://tea.texas.gov/ (Search for "Texas Dyslexia Handbook"). Please note the actual direct link may change, so searching on the TEA website is the most reliable method.

Mandatory Screening

EPISD follows Texas Education Code §38.003, which mandates dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. The purpose of screening is to identify students who are at risk for dyslexia. This process includes assessing foundational reading skills such as phonological awareness, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and letter-sound knowledge.

Screening Process in EPISD:

  • Kindergarten: All kindergarten students are screened using a TEA-approved screening instrument. This typically happens in the fall and spring of the school year.
  • First Grade: All first-grade students are screened using a TEA-approved screening instrument. This also typically happens in the fall and spring of the school year.
  • Students Transferring to EPISD: Students transferring into EPISD, especially those in grades 2-12, may be screened if concerns about their reading skills are raised by teachers or parents.
  • Notification: Parents are notified in writing if their child is identified as being at risk for dyslexia based on the screening results. The notification will include information about the screening results, recommended interventions, and available resources.
  • Further Evaluation: Students who demonstrate significant risk factors during screening will be recommended for further evaluation to determine if they have dyslexia.

Important Note: Screening is not a diagnosis of dyslexia. It is a process to identify students who may benefit from targeted intervention.

Structured Literacy Programs

EPISD utilizes Structured Literacy programs to provide effective reading intervention for students with dyslexia. Structured Literacy is an approach to reading instruction that is explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multisensory. It focuses on teaching the foundational skills necessary for reading success, including phonological awareness, phonics, morphology, syntax, and semantics.

Characteristics of Structured Literacy Programs Used in EPISD:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions made about prior knowledge.
  • Systematic Instruction: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Cumulative Instruction: New skills are integrated with previously learned skills, reinforcing learning and promoting retention.
  • Multisensory Instruction: Instruction incorporates visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile modalities to engage different learning styles.
  • Phonological Awareness: Instruction includes activities to develop awareness of the sounds in language, including rhyming, segmenting, blending, and manipulating phonemes.
  • Phonics: Instruction focuses on the relationship between letters and sounds, teaching students how to decode and encode words.
  • Morphology: Instruction includes the study of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, roots) to enhance vocabulary and comprehension.
  • Syntax: Instruction addresses sentence structure and grammar to improve reading and writing skills.
  • Semantics: Instruction focuses on the meaning of words and phrases, enhancing vocabulary development and reading comprehension.

Examples of potential Structured Literacy programs implemented in EPISD (This is for illustrative purposes and may not be a current list):

  • Take Flight: A Comprehensive Intervention for Students with Dyslexia
  • Reading by Design
  • Neuhaus Education Center Programs

Contact your child's school or the EPISD Special Education Department for specific information about the Structured Literacy programs used at your child's school.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support through a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's specific needs and the impact of dyslexia on their educational performance.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning, reading, or writing. Dyslexia can qualify as such an impairment.
  • Focus: The 504 plan focuses on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services may include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Evaluation: The 504 evaluation process is less formal than the IEP evaluation process. It typically involves a review of existing data, including screening results, grades, and teacher observations.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability (including dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: The IEP focuses on providing specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: Services may include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling.
  • Evaluation: The IEP evaluation process is more comprehensive than the 504 evaluation process. It involves a full and individual evaluation to determine the student's strengths and weaknesses, and the impact of their disability on their educational performance.
  • ARD Committee: The IEP is developed by an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee, which includes parents, teachers, administrators, and specialists.

Key Differences Summarized:

Feature 504 Plan IEP
Eligibility Substantial limitation to a major life activity Disability that adversely affects educational performance and requires specialized instruction
Focus Accommodations for equal access Specialized instruction and related services
Evaluation Less formal, review of existing data Comprehensive, full and individual evaluation

The decision of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the school team.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered, or the way students demonstrate their learning, that do not change the content of what is being taught. These accommodations can help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing students additional time to complete assignments and tests.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing students in a location in the classroom that minimizes distractions and promotes focus.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and graphic organizers.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments to meet the student's needs.
  • Oral Reading of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the student to use a calculator for math problems.
  • Highlighting and Underlining: Encouraging students to highlight and underline important information in texts.
  • Chunking of Information: Breaking down large amounts of information into smaller, more manageable chunks.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Providing graphic organizers to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Access to Notes: Providing access to teacher notes or allowing students to use their own notes during tests.
  • Reduced Distractions: Minimizing distractions in the classroom, such as noise and visual clutter.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing students to take frequent breaks to avoid fatigue.
  • Alternative Assessment Methods: Using alternative assessment methods, such as oral presentations or projects, to assess student learning.

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will depend on their individual needs. The ARD or 504 committee will determine the most appropriate accommodations for each student.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading skills and suspect they may have dyslexia, you can request an evaluation from EPISD.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact Your Child's Teacher: The first step is to discuss your concerns with your child's teacher. The teacher can provide valuable insights into your child's reading performance in the classroom.
  2. Contact the School Principal or Counselor: If you continue to have concerns after speaking with the teacher, contact the school principal or counselor. They can provide you with information about the evaluation process and the resources available at the school.
  3. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include the following information:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your child's grade level and school
    • A clear statement that you are requesting an evaluation for dyslexia
    • A description of your concerns about your child's reading skills
    • Any relevant information about your child's academic history
    • Your contact information
  4. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, they will initiate the evaluation process. This may involve a review of existing data, such as screening results, grades, and teacher observations. It may also involve additional assessments to evaluate your child's reading skills, phonological awareness, and other related areas.
  5. ARD or 504 Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, the school will schedule an ARD (for special education) or 504 meeting to discuss the results and determine if your child is eligible for services. You have the right to participate in these meetings and to receive copies of all evaluation reports.

Parental Rights:

Parents have important rights throughout the evaluation and intervention process. These rights include:

  • The right to request an evaluation
  • The right to participate in all meetings regarding your child's education
  • The right to review and receive copies of all evaluation reports
  • The right to disagree with the school's recommendations and to request an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you meet certain criteria.

For more information about your rights as a parent, please refer to the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 and the TEA website.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
