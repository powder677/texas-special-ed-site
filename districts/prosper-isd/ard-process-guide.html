What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as having a disability that impacts their learning, you will be invited to participate in an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, and it's a very important process in ensuring your child receives the appropriate support and services within the school system. The ARD meeting is the cornerstone of special education, where a team of individuals, including you as the parent, comes together to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, often called an IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document outlining your child's specific needs, goals, and the services the school will provide to help them succeed.

This meeting isn't just a suggestion or a formality; it is required by federal law under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and by the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. These laws ensure that children with disabilities have access to a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) that is tailored to their individual needs. Because you, as the parent, know your child best, your voice is critical. The ARD committee uses information to make decisions about your child's educational program. The decisions must consider the least restrictive environment (LRE), which means your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

So, who attends an ARD meeting? The law outlines specific roles, but it's important to remember that the exact composition of the team can be tailored to your child's specific needs. Required members generally include you, the parent, as the most important member of the team; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of special education; one of your child's general education teachers (if your child is, or may be, participating in the regular education environment); one of your child's special education teachers or provider; an individual who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results, who may be one of the other members of the team; and, whenever appropriate, your child. Other individuals may be invited to the ARD meeting as well, such as therapists, counselors, diagnosticians, or any other person who has knowledge or special expertise regarding your child.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Prosper ISD, like all school districts in Texas, is legally required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting is scheduled to take place. This is not five school days, but five calendar days, meaning weekends and holidays are included in that count. This notice is intended to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting, gather your thoughts and materials, and arrange for any support you might want to bring with you. The purpose of the notice is to allow you the time needed to make informed decisions about your child's education.

The written notice isn't just about the date and time; it must also include specific information about the purpose of the meeting. It must clearly state the reason the ARD meeting is being held - is it for an annual review of the IEP, an initial eligibility determination, a change of placement, or some other reason? The notice should also list the individuals who will be attending the meeting, including their roles. If a particular evaluation or report will be discussed at the meeting, the notice should include that information as well. Furthermore, the notice should inform you of your rights as a parent under IDEA and Texas law, including your right to participate in the development of your child's IEP and your right to disagree with the team's decisions.

While the five-day notice is there to protect your rights, there may be instances where you want to meet sooner than that. For example, perhaps a pressing issue has arisen, and you want to address it with the ARD committee as soon as possible. In such cases, you can waive your right to the five-day notice and agree to meet on an earlier date. This waiver must be in writing, and it's important to only waive this right if you feel completely comfortable and prepared to proceed with the meeting.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting is crucial to ensuring your child's needs are met. Think of yourself as your child's advocate, and bringing the right information and resources can make a significant difference in the outcome of the meeting. Gather any documents related to your child's development, health, and education, because these documents can provide valuable insight into their strengths and challenges.

Prior evaluations are essential. Bring copies of any evaluations your child has received, whether from the school district or from private professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other assessments that provide information about your child's learning needs. Doctor's letters or reports from medical professionals are also valuable, especially if your child has a medical condition that impacts their learning. These reports can provide documentation of diagnoses, medications, and any necessary accommodations or modifications.

Don't forget school records. Bring copies of your child's report cards, progress reports, and any work samples that demonstrate their academic performance. If your child has had behavior issues at school, bring any documentation related to those issues, such as incident reports or behavior plans. It's also a good idea to create a written list of your concerns. Before the meeting, take some time to write down all of your concerns about your child's education, including their academic progress, social-emotional development, and any other issues that are important to you. Having a written list will help you stay organized and ensure that you don't forget to address any of your concerns during the meeting.

Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person. ARD meetings can be overwhelming, and it can be helpful to have someone there to support you. This could be a friend, family member, or a professional advocate who is familiar with special education law. This person can help you take notes, ask questions, and ensure that your voice is heard. Finally, remember that Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations. This means you can legally record the ARD meeting, provided you inform the committee that you are doing so. A recording can be a valuable tool for reviewing the meeting later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The ARD process is designed to be collaborative, with decisions being made by a team that includes you, the parent. However, there may be times when you feel that you need more time to consider the information presented, gather additional data, or consult with an advocate before making a final decision. In these situations, Texas law provides you with the right to request a recess of the ARD meeting. This is often referred to as the "10-day recess rule."

Any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, can request a recess of the meeting. The purpose of the recess is to allow time to gather additional information, conduct further evaluations, consult with experts, or simply to have more time to process the information and make informed decisions. This is a powerful tool that you can use to ensure that you are fully comfortable with the decisions being made about your child's education. The recess can last up to ten school days. This means that weekends and holidays are not included in the ten-day count. During this time, the ARD committee cannot make any final decisions about your child's IEP.

Invoking the 10-day recess rule is straightforward. Simply state to the ARD committee that you are requesting a recess of the meeting. It's helpful to explain the reason for your request, such as the need to gather additional information or consult with an advocate. Once you've made the request, the ARD committee must grant it. The meeting will then be adjourned, and a new date will be scheduled to reconvene after the recess period. It's important to remember that you don't need to justify your request extensively. If you simply feel that you need more time to consider the information, that is a valid reason to request a recess.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the central document that guides your child's special education. It outlines everything the ARD committee has determined your child needs to make progress in school. Understanding the different sections of the IEP is critical to ensuring your child receives the appropriate support and services. Several key components are decided upon during the ARD meeting and then documented in the IEP.

One of the most important sections is the Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance, often shortened to "Present Levels." This section describes your child's current academic and functional skills, including their strengths and areas where they need support. It should be based on data from evaluations, observations, and input from you and your child's teachers. The Present Levels section provides a baseline for measuring your child's progress over time. Based on the present levels, the ARD committee will develop Annual Goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They should address your child's identified needs in areas such as reading, writing, math, communication, and social-emotional development.

The IEP also outlines the specific Services that your child will receive to help them achieve their annual goals. This may include special education instruction, related services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, physical therapy, counseling, or transportation. The IEP will specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services. The Placement section of the IEP describes the educational setting where your child will receive their services. The law requires that children with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), which means that they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP should explain the rationale for the chosen placement and how it meets your child's individual needs.

Finally, the IEP will list any Accommodations and Modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as simplified assignments or a reduced workload. It's important to remember that you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and consult with others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

While the ARD process is intended to be collaborative, there may be times when you disagree with the decisions being made by the ARD committee. It's important to know your rights and the steps you can take if you find yourself in this situation. One of your most fundamental rights is the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's recommendation for special education services, you can refuse to give your consent. Without your consent, the school district cannot place your child in special education.

Even if you initially consent to special education services, you have the right to disagree with specific aspects of the IEP. You are not required to sign the IEP if you do not agree with its contents. If you choose not to sign the IEP, it is important to document your reasons for disagreeing. One way to do this is to write "Parent Disagrees" on the signature page of the IEP. You can also attach a separate written statement explaining your concerns. This will ensure that your disagreement is documented and that the school district is aware of your concerns.

Another important right you have is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. You have the right to request an IEE if you disagree with the results of the school district's evaluation. The school district must either grant your request or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that its evaluation was appropriate. If the school district grants your request for an IEE, they must pay for it. The results of the IEE must be considered by the ARD committee when making decisions about your child's IEP.

Escalation Options

If, after attempting to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee, you are still not satisfied, there are several formal escalation options available to you. These options are outlined in IDEA 2004 and further detailed in the Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, and can be found through the Legal Framework managed by the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

The first option is to file a TEA State Complaint. This is a formal written complaint submitted to the TEA outlining your concerns. The TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This is a free option, and it can be a relatively quick way to resolve some issues. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. It is important to be specific and provide as much detail as possible in your complaint.

The next step is Mediation, which is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a trained, impartial mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also free and is offered through the TEA. The mediator does not take sides or make decisions; instead, they facilitate communication and help the parties explore potential solutions. Mediation can be a helpful way to resolve disputes without resorting to more adversarial processes. The agreement reached in mediation is legally binding.

If mediation is unsuccessful, or if you choose to bypass mediation altogether, you can request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal process where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's IEP. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing. The school district will also be represented by an attorney. The hearing officer's decision is legally binding, although it can be appealed to a state or federal court. Due process hearings can be time-consuming and expensive, but they are an important option for resolving serious disputes.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). This option is available if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. The OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action. Filing a complaint with the OCR is a free option, but it can take a long time for the OCR to investigate and resolve the complaint. It's important to remember that these escalation options are in place to protect your child's rights. You are not alone in this process. There are many resources available to help you navigate the special education system and advocate for your child's needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
