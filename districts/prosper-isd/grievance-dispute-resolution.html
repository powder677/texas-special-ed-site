Dispute & Discipline Rights in Prosper ISD

🚨 Know This Now

Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Discipline Rights & Grievances in Prosper ISD: URGENT GUIDE

This guide is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. If your child is facing disciplinary action in Prosper ISD, it is CRUCIAL to seek legal counsel from an education attorney immediately. Time is of the essence!

This guide is designed to help parents navigate the complex and often confusing world of student discipline in Prosper ISD, particularly when dealing with special education students or those suspected of having disabilities. Understanding your child's rights is paramount to ensuring they receive a fair and appropriate education. This is a crisis guide - act quickly and don't delay!

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Texas law allows schools to suspend students for various disciplinary infractions. However, for students with disabilities (or those who the school knows or should know have a disability) the rules change significantly after 10 cumulative days of suspension in a single school year.

Key Takeaways:

  • Cumulative Suspensions: The 10 days are cumulative. A 2-day suspension followed by an 8-day suspension triggers the special education protections.
  • School Year: This rule applies within a single school year.
  • "Should Have Known" Rule: This is CRITICAL. Even if your child *doesn't* have a formal IEP or 504 plan, if the school had reason to suspect a disability (e.g., documented academic struggles, behavioral issues, prior referrals for special education services), they are often obligated to treat the student as if they have a disability for disciplinary purposes. Document everything! Keep copies of all emails, report cards, and any communication with the school.

What Happens After 10 Days? After a student with a disability has been removed from school for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, the school is required to determine if the removal constitutes a "Change of Placement" (more on this later). They also have to hold a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a meeting held by the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee (or a similar group) to determine whether a student's behavior that led to a disciplinary action was a manifestation of their disability. In other words, did the disability CAUSE or CONTRIBUTE to the behavior?

Crucial Participants:

  • Parents: Your active participation is essential. Bring any documentation you have that supports your child's disability and how it might relate to the behavior.
  • School Staff: This should include special education teachers, general education teachers, administrators, and other relevant personnel who can provide insight into your child's behavior and academic performance.
  • Student (Ideally): Depending on the student's age and maturity, their presence and input can be invaluable.

The MDR Process:

  1. Review All Relevant Information: The committee will review the student's IEP, teacher observations, disciplinary records, and any other relevant information, including parent input.
  2. Determine Manifestation: The committee must answer two critical questions:
    • Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
    • Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP?
  3. Two Possible Outcomes:
    • Manifestation: If the answer to either of the above questions is "yes," the behavior is deemed a manifestation of the disability. The student MUST return to their placement (unless the parents and school agree otherwise). The school MUST also conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) if one hasn't been done already, and implement (or revise) a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP).
    • Not a Manifestation: If the answer to both questions is "no," the behavior is not deemed a manifestation. The student can be disciplined in the same way as a non-disabled student, EXCEPT the school MUST continue to provide educational services so the student can continue to participate in the general education curriculum, although in another setting, and to progress toward meeting the goals set out in the student's IEP.

If you disagree with the MDR decision, you have the right to challenge it through due process (see below). Don't hesitate! The timeline is tight.

Change of Placement

A Change of Placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when a series of removals constitute a pattern of exclusion. A Change of Placement triggers specific procedural safeguards under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

What Constitutes a Change of Placement? This is complex, but consider these factors:

  • Total Days of Removal: More than 10 consecutive days is automatically a Change of Placement.
  • Pattern of Removals: Even if no single suspension exceeds 10 days, a pattern of short-term suspensions that, taken together, result in a significant exclusion from the student's educational program can be considered a Change of Placement. Look for removals totaling more than 10 days in a school year, or if the behavior is the same or similar to a prior incident.
  • Other Removals: In-school suspension or shortened days might still be considered removals. Document everything!

What Happens When There's a Change of Placement? The school MUST:

  • Hold an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review): As described above.
  • Provide Educational Services: Even during suspension, the school must ensure the student continues to receive educational services to enable them to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals. This can take many forms, and you need to be vigilant to ensure the services are adequate.
  • Re-evaluate the IEP: The ARD committee should convene to review the student's IEP and determine if any modifications are needed to address the behavior.

If you believe a Change of Placement has occurred and the school is not acknowledging it, you need to take immediate action. Consider filing a grievance and/or a TEA State Complaint (see below).

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

Prosper ISD has a grievance policy that allows parents to formally complain about district actions or decisions. While the policy is usually geared towards employment issues, it can be used to challenge disciplinary decisions.

Before Filing:

  • Review the Prosper ISD Grievance Policy: Find it on the Prosper ISD website. Understand the specific procedures, timelines, and requirements. Find the one that addresses student complaints, not employment.
  • Gather Documentation: Collect all relevant documents, including suspension notices, emails, meeting minutes, IEPs, and any other evidence that supports your grievance.
  • Clearly Define the Issue: What specific action or decision are you grieving? Be precise and avoid vague statements.
  • State Your Desired Outcome: What do you want the district to do to resolve the issue? Be realistic and specific.

The Level 1 Grievance:

  • File with the Appropriate Person: The grievance policy will specify who to file the Level 1 grievance with (usually the principal or other immediate supervisor).
  • Meet the Deadline: Grievance policies have strict deadlines. Missing the deadline can invalidate your grievance.
  • Keep a Copy: Always keep a copy of your grievance and any related documents.

The Grievance Process: The district will investigate your grievance and provide a response. If you are not satisfied with the response, you can appeal to the next level (Level 2). Be prepared to meet again, and keep detailed notes of all communications.

A grievance does not automatically stop the disciplinary process. You need to pursue other legal avenues simultaneously.

TEA State Complaint

You can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe Prosper ISD has violated state or federal special education laws. This is a more formal and potentially more effective process than a Level 1 Grievance, but it also requires careful preparation and documentation.

When to File a TEA Complaint:

  • Suspected Violations of IDEA: Examples include failure to conduct an MDR, failure to implement the IEP, denial of due process rights, and discrimination based on disability.
  • Exhausted Other Remedies (Optional, but Recommended): While not always required, it's often beneficial to attempt to resolve the issue with the district first through a Level 1 Grievance. This shows you've made an effort to work with the school.

Filing the Complaint:

  • TEA Complaint Form: Download the complaint form from the TEA website.
  • Detailed Information: Provide specific information about the alleged violation, including dates, names, and relevant facts.
  • Supporting Documentation: Attach all relevant documents to your complaint.
  • Timeliness: TEA has a specific timeframe for filing complaints (usually within one year of the alleged violation).

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and may conduct interviews, review documents, and request information from the district. TEA will issue a written decision. If TEA finds that the district violated special education laws, it will order corrective action. The TEA has authority to mandate corrective action but doesn't typically offer compensation to parents.

Filing a TEA complaint is a powerful tool, but it can be a complex process. Seek legal counsel to ensure your complaint is properly prepared and filed.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal procedure that allows parents to challenge the school's decisions regarding their child's special education services. This is the most serious step, and it involves a formal hearing with an administrative law judge. You absolutely need an attorney.

When to Request Due Process:

  • Disagreement with the MDR Decision: If you disagree with the MDR committee's determination that your child's behavior was not a manifestation of their disability.
  • Disagreement with the IEP: If you disagree with the content or implementation of your child's IEP.
  • Failure to Provide FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education): If you believe the school is not providing your child with a FAPE.

The Due Process Hearing:

  • Request a Hearing: File a written request for a due process hearing with the TEA.
  • Mediation (Optional): TEA offers mediation services to help parents and schools resolve disputes before a formal hearing.
  • Hearing Procedures: The due process hearing is similar to a court trial. You will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine the school's witnesses.
  • Administrative Law Judge: An administrative law judge will preside over the hearing and issue a written decision.

Legal Representation: Due process hearings are complex and require specialized legal knowledge. It is essential to have an experienced education attorney represent you.

Remember, time is of the essence when dealing with student discipline issues. Do not delay in seeking legal advice. Your child's education and future may depend on it. Contact an education attorney specializing in special education law immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
