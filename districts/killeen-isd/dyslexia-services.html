Dyslexia Services in Killeen ISD

Dyslexia Services in Killeen ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services offered in Killeen Independent School District (KISD), adhering to the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 and relevant Texas Education Agency (TEA) guidelines. It outlines screening procedures, intervention programs, the differences between 504 plans and IEPs for dyslexia, common accommodations, and the process for requesting an evaluation.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024) serves as the primary resource for identifying and supporting students with dyslexia in Texas public schools. It is developed and updated by the TEA to ensure consistent, evidence-based practices across the state. KISD adheres to the guidelines and recommendations outlined in this handbook.

Key aspects of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024 include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: Dyslexia is defined as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Importance of Early Identification and Intervention: The handbook emphasizes the critical need for early identification and intervention to mitigate the impact of dyslexia on a student's academic progress. The earlier a student receives appropriate support, the better the outcomes.
  • Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS): The handbook promotes the use of a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) framework, which involves providing increasing levels of support based on student needs. This includes high-quality classroom instruction (Tier 1), targeted interventions for students who are struggling (Tier 2), and intensive, individualized interventions for students with significant needs (Tier 3).
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook highlights the importance of structured literacy instruction as the most effective approach for teaching students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading and spelling that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, vocabulary, morphology, and syntax.
  • Parental Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the importance of parental involvement in the identification, assessment, and intervention processes. Parents are essential partners in supporting their child's reading development.
  • Professional Development: The handbook stresses the need for ongoing professional development for teachers and other educators on dyslexia and effective instructional practices.

Mandatory Screening

KISD follows the state's requirements for dyslexia screening. These requirements are outlined in the Texas Education Code and further detailed in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Universal Screening in Kindergarten and First Grade: KISD conducts universal screenings for dyslexia indicators for all students in kindergarten and first grade. These screenings typically occur in the fall and spring of each school year. The purpose of universal screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and require further evaluation or intervention.

Screening Procedures: The screenings typically include assessments of:

  • Phonological Awareness: The ability to hear, identify, and manipulate the individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words. Examples include rhyming, segmenting, and blending.
  • Rapid Automatized Naming (RAN): The ability to quickly and accurately name a series of familiar objects, colors, or letters.
  • Letter-Sound Knowledge: The ability to identify the sounds that correspond to letters.
  • Decoding Skills: The ability to sound out and read words.

Screening Results: Parents will be notified of the results of the dyslexia screening. If a student demonstrates indicators of dyslexia during the screening process, KISD will take appropriate action, which may include:

  • Further Evaluation: The student may be referred for a comprehensive dyslexia evaluation to determine if they meet the criteria for a diagnosis of dyslexia.
  • Targeted Intervention: The student may be provided with targeted intervention services to address their specific reading difficulties.

Referral for Screening at Other Grades: Parents or teachers can request a dyslexia screening for a student in any grade if they have concerns about the student's reading development. The process for requesting an evaluation is described in a later section.

Structured Literacy Programs

KISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide effective instruction to students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is a research-based approach to reading instruction that explicitly and systematically teaches the essential components of reading. These programs are aligned with the recommendations of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Key Features of Structured Literacy Programs:

  • Systematic and Explicit Instruction: Structured literacy programs provide systematic and explicit instruction in all areas of reading, including phonological awareness, phonics, vocabulary, morphology, and syntax.
  • Multisensory Approach: Structured literacy programs often incorporate multisensory techniques, which involve engaging multiple senses (e.g., visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance learning.
  • Diagnostic and Prescriptive Teaching: Structured literacy programs utilize diagnostic assessments to identify students' strengths and weaknesses and tailor instruction to meet their individual needs.
  • Cumulative Review: Structured literacy programs provide ongoing opportunities for students to review and practice previously learned skills.

Examples of Structured Literacy Programs Used in KISD: (Note: Specific program names may vary; contact KISD for current program information) While specific programs change, the emphasis is on using programs that adhere to Structured Literacy principles.

  • Programs focusing on phonological awareness and phonics: These programs help students develop the foundational skills necessary for decoding and spelling.
  • Multisensory language programs: These programs incorporate visual, auditory, and kinesthetic activities to help students learn and remember new concepts.

Implementation of Structured Literacy Programs: KISD teachers who provide dyslexia intervention receive specialized training in structured literacy principles and the implementation of specific structured literacy programs. The programs are typically delivered in small group settings to provide individualized attention and support.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP), depending on their specific needs and eligibility criteria. It's crucial to understand the key differences between these two options.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Legal Basis: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 is a civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in programs and activities that receive federal funding.
  • Eligibility: To be eligible for a 504 plan, a student must have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: A 504 plan focuses on providing accommodations and modifications to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Services typically involve accommodations and modifications to the classroom environment, instruction, or assessments. Examples include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and assistive technology.
  • Assessment: Assessment for a 504 plan is typically less comprehensive than an IEP evaluation.
  • Dyslexia and 504: A student with dyslexia may be eligible for a 504 plan if their dyslexia substantially limits their ability to learn. The plan will outline accommodations to help them access the curriculum.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Legal Basis: The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is a federal law that ensures that children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) in the least restrictive environment (LRE).
  • Eligibility: To be eligible for an IEP, a student must have a disability that is listed in IDEA (e.g., specific learning disability, such as dyslexia) and must require specialized instruction to benefit from education.
  • Focus: An IEP focuses on providing specialized instruction and related services to address the student's individual learning needs.
  • Services: Services may include specialized instruction in reading, writing, or math; speech therapy; occupational therapy; counseling; and assistive technology.
  • Assessment: Assessment for an IEP is a comprehensive evaluation that includes a variety of assessments to determine the student's strengths and weaknesses and identify their specific learning needs.
  • Dyslexia and IEP: A student with dyslexia may be eligible for an IEP if their dyslexia significantly impacts their academic performance and requires specialized instruction that cannot be adequately addressed through general education interventions alone.

Key Differences Summarized:

Legal Basis Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
Eligibility Disability that substantially limits a major life activity Disability listed in IDEA that requires specialized instruction

Which is Right for My Child? The decision of whether a 504 plan or an IEP is more appropriate for a student with dyslexia is made on an individual basis, considering the student's specific needs and the severity of their reading difficulties. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee (for IEP) or the 504 committee will make this determination.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the classroom environment, instruction, or assessments that help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning. Accommodations do not change the content of the curriculum or the expectations for learning. These can be part of a 504 plan or an IEP.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia in KISD:

  • Extended Time: Providing additional time on tests and assignments to allow students to process information and complete tasks.
  • Preferential Seating: Seating the student near the teacher or away from distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Using tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks to support reading and writing.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments to meet the student's individual needs.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the student to use a calculator for math problems.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing the student with notes or allowing them to record lectures.
  • Graphic Organizers: Using visual aids such as graphic organizers to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Multi-sensory Teaching Strategies: Incorporating visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile methods.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual cues like color-coding or highlighting.

Important Considerations: The specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student with dyslexia will vary depending on their individual needs. The ARD committee or 504 committee will determine the most effective accommodations for each student.

How to Request Evaluation

If you have concerns about your child's reading development and suspect they may have dyslexia, you can request a dyslexia evaluation from KISD.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to express your concerns and request a dyslexia evaluation. Explain the specific difficulties your child is experiencing with reading, spelling, and writing.
  2. Submit a Written Request: It is recommended to submit your request for an evaluation in writing. This provides a clear record of your request and ensures that it is properly documented. Your written request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your contact information (phone number and email address)
    • A detailed description of your concerns about your child's reading development
    • Any relevant information about your child's academic history or previous interventions
  3. School Response: Upon receiving your written request, the school will review the information and determine whether an evaluation is warranted. The school is required to respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe, as defined by Texas Education Agency guidelines.
  4. Evaluation Process: If the school determines that an evaluation is appropriate, they will initiate the evaluation process. This typically involves a team of professionals, including a diagnostician, special education teacher, and other relevant staff members, who will conduct a variety of assessments to evaluate your child's reading, spelling, and writing skills. Parental consent is required before the evaluation can begin.
  5. Evaluation Results: Once the evaluation is complete, the evaluation team will meet to review the results and determine whether your child meets the criteria for a diagnosis of dyslexia. You will be invited to attend this meeting and will receive a copy of the evaluation report.
  6. IEP or 504 Plan Development: If your child is diagnosed with dyslexia and is eligible for special education services or 504 accommodations, the ARD committee (for IEP) or 504 committee will develop an individualized plan to address your child's specific needs.

Parent Rights: Parents have the right to participate in all aspects of the evaluation and IEP/504 plan development process. You have the right to review all relevant records, ask questions, and provide input. If you disagree with the school's decisions, you have the right to file a complaint or request mediation.

KISD Contact Information: For specific information about dyslexia services in Killeen ISD, please contact your child's school or the KISD Special Education Department. You can find contact information on the KISD website.

Disclaimer: This guide is intended to provide general information about dyslexia services in Killeen ISD. It is not intended to be a substitute for professional advice. Please consult with your child's school or the KISD Special Education Department for personalized guidance and support.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
