Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Discipline Rights & Grievances in Killeen ISD: URGENT GUIDE

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Killeen ISD

If your child is facing disciplinary action in Killeen ISD, TIME IS OF THE ESSENCE. This guide is designed to provide immediate, critical information about your rights and how to protect your child. Do not delay seeking help. Document everything. Time is NOT on your side.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: What You MUST Know

Texas Education Code allows for suspensions, but there are limits. Pay very close attention to the number of days your child is being suspended, as cumulative suspensions exceeding 10 school days in a single school year trigger significant rights, especially if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

KEY POINT: Keep a meticulous record of every suspension, including the dates, the reasons, and the number of days. Even if you disagree with a suspension, documenting it is crucial. Consider a small notebook or spreadsheet specifically for discipline events.

What happens after 10 days of suspension? Once a student with an IEP has been suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, a Change of Placement is considered. This necessitates specific procedures to ensure your child's rights are protected. This triggers IDEA's requirements.

IF your child does NOT have an IEP but has exhibited behaviors that suggest a possible disability, you should immediately request an evaluation for special education services in writing. This request can trigger protections under IDEA while the evaluation is pending.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR (Manifestation Determination Review) is a critical meeting that MUST be held if a student with an IEP is facing a suspension that would constitute a Change of Placement (generally more than 10 cumulative days). The purpose of the MDR is to determine if the student's misconduct was caused by, or directly and substantially related to, the student's disability. It also determines if the misconduct was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

Think of it this way: Was your child acting out because of their disability? Did the school fail to provide the support outlined in their IEP that could have prevented the behavior?

Who attends the MDR? The MDR team typically includes the parent(s)/guardian(s), relevant members of the IEP team (including the teacher and a district representative), and other qualified personnel who have knowledge of the child and the disability. You have the right to bring an advocate or attorney to this meeting. Do not hesitate to do so!

What happens during the MDR? The team reviews all relevant information, including the student's IEP, teacher observations, disciplinary records, and any other relevant evaluations. The team will then answer two crucial questions:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP?

If the answer to EITHER of these questions is YES, the conduct is considered a manifestation of the disability. This means the student cannot be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team must then conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA), implement a behavior intervention plan (BIP), or revise the existing BIP if one is already in place.

If the answer to BOTH questions is NO, the conduct is NOT a manifestation of the disability. The student can be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. However, the school district is still obligated to provide services to the student, even during the period of suspension, to ensure they can participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals.

CRITICAL: If you disagree with the outcome of the MDR, you have the right to appeal. DO NOT delay.

Change of Placement: What it Means & How to Fight it

A Change of Placement occurs when a student with an IEP is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that total more than 10 school days in a school year and that constitute a significant change in the student's educational program. A change of placement also happens if the conduct is the same or virtually the same misconduct. A change of placement triggers certain rights under IDEA that must be followed.

What constitutes a "significant change"? This is determined on a case-by-case basis, but factors include the length of each removal, the total amount of time the student is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another. A series of short suspensions close together can be considered a Change of Placement even if no single suspension exceeds 10 days.

Your rights during a Change of Placement:

  • Notice: You must receive written notice of the proposed Change of Placement.
  • MDR (Manifestation Determination Review): As discussed above, this is crucial.
  • Stay Put: During any dispute regarding a Change of Placement, your child generally has the right to remain in their current educational placement ("stay put") pending the outcome of the dispute resolution process. This is incredibly important to prevent your child from being removed from their familiar setting while the issue is being resolved. There are exceptions to this rule (eg: weapons violations).
  • Appeal: If you disagree with the Change of Placement, you have the right to appeal the decision.

If the MDR determines that the behavior was NOT a manifestation of the disability, the school can proceed with the disciplinary action, but MUST still provide services to your child. They cannot simply kick your child out and ignore their IEP.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in Killeen ISD

Killeen ISD has a grievance policy for addressing complaints. Understanding this process is vital if you believe your child's rights have been violated. This is often the first step in formally challenging a disciplinary decision.

What is a grievance? A grievance is a formal written complaint alleging a violation of policy, rule, or regulation. In the context of discipline, a grievance could be filed if you believe the school district violated your child's rights under IDEA, state law, or district policy during the disciplinary process.

Level 1: This is the initial step in the grievance process. You must file the grievance with the appropriate administrator (typically the principal) within a specified timeframe - usually very short, often within 15 days of the event! Do not delay! Check the Killeen ISD policy manual for the exact timeline. This timeframe is crucial. A missed deadline can invalidate your grievance.

What to include in your Level 1 grievance:

  • Your child's name and school.
  • A clear and concise statement of the problem or issue. Be specific about what happened, when it happened, and who was involved.
  • The specific policy, rule, or regulation that you believe was violated. Refer to the Killeen ISD policy manual and IDEA regulations.
  • The specific relief or remedy you are seeking. What do you want the school district to do to resolve the issue? Examples: reinstatement of services, removal of the suspension from the record, a formal apology, training for staff.
  • Any supporting documentation (e.g., copies of suspension notices, IEP documents, emails).
  • Your signature and contact information.
  • Date the grievance.

After you file the grievance: The principal is required to investigate the grievance and provide a written response within a specified timeframe (again, check the Killeen ISD policy manual). If you are not satisfied with the principal's response, you can appeal to the next level (Level 2).

Documentation is EVERYTHING. Keep copies of EVERYTHING you submit and receive.

TEA (Texas Education Agency) State Complaint

Filing a State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) is another avenue for addressing violations of IDEA. This is a more formal process than a district-level grievance.

When to file a TEA complaint: You can file a TEA complaint if you believe Killeen ISD has violated IDEA requirements regarding your child's special education services or disciplinary procedures. This is especially relevant if you believe the district is not properly implementing your child's IEP or is violating their rights during a suspension or Change of Placement.

TEA Complaint Requirements: A TEA complaint must:

  • Be in writing.
  • Be signed.
  • State the specific violation of IDEA that is alleged.
  • Describe the facts on which the complaint is based. Be detailed and provide specific dates, times, and names.
  • Include a proposed resolution of the problem to the extent known and available to the party at the time the complaint is submitted.
  • Be filed within one year of the date of the alleged violation.

TEA Investigation: TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision. If TEA finds that the district violated IDEA, it will order the district to take corrective action.

The TEA complaint process can be complex. Consider seeking legal assistance from a special education attorney or advocate.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Weapon

Due Process is the most powerful tool you have to protect your child's rights under IDEA. It is a formal legal process that allows you to challenge the school district's actions regarding your child's special education services or disciplinary procedures.

When to File for Due Process: You can file for due process if you disagree with the school district's identification, evaluation, educational placement, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE) to your child. In the context of discipline, you might file for due process if you believe the district violated IDEA requirements during a suspension or Change of Placement, particularly if the MDR was not conducted properly or if you disagree with the MDR determination.

Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is like a mini-trial before an impartial hearing officer. You will have the opportunity to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine the school district's witnesses. The hearing officer will then issue a written decision.

Filing for due process is a serious step. It is highly recommended that you seek legal representation from a special education attorney. The school district will likely have legal counsel, and you need someone on your side who understands the law and can effectively advocate for your child.

IMMEDIATE ACTION ITEMS:

  • Document EVERYTHING. Keep detailed records of all communication with the school district, including emails, phone calls, and meetings.
  • Request copies of all relevant documents. This includes your child's IEP, disciplinary records, and any other evaluations or assessments.
  • Contact Disability Rights Texas or another legal aid organization.
  • Consult with a special education attorney or advocate IMMEDIATELY. Do not wait until it is too late.

Remember, you are your child's best advocate. Be informed, be persistent, and don't be afraid to fight for their rights. This guide is intended to provide general information and should not be considered legal advice. You must consult with an attorney to get advice specific to your situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
