What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome! As a parent in Grand Prairie ISD with a child who needs special education services, you will be involved in a very important process called the Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) process. An ARD meeting is a meeting held to make decisions about your child's special education program. It's required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and also by the Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. These laws ensure that children with disabilities receive a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) that is tailored to their individual needs. The purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and revise your child's Individualized Education Program, also known as the IEP. This IEP is a legally binding document that outlines the specific supports and services your child will receive to help them succeed in school.

The ARD meeting brings together a team of people who are all invested in your child's education. The most important member of the team is you, the parent. You know your child best, and your input is essential. Other members of the ARD committee typically include a school administrator or designee who can commit resources, your child's special education teacher, a general education teacher if your child is participating in the general education classroom, and an educational diagnostician or other professional who can interpret evaluation results. Depending on your child's needs, other specialists may also attend, such as a speech therapist, occupational therapist, physical therapist, counselor, or behavior specialist. Your child may also be invited to attend the meeting, particularly as they get older, to share their own perspectives and goals.

The ARD committee works collaboratively to make important decisions about your child's education. These decisions include determining your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, setting measurable annual goals, identifying the specific special education and related services your child needs, determining the appropriate educational placement, and outlining any necessary accommodations or modifications to help your child succeed. The ARD process is designed to be a collaborative one, where all members of the team work together to create an IEP that meets your child's unique needs. Remember, you are a vital member of this team, and your voice matters.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This notice is extremely important because it gives you time to prepare, gather your thoughts, and collect any relevant information that you want to share with the ARD committee. The school district must provide this notice to you in a language you understand. It should be clear and easy to read.

The notice must include the purpose of the meeting. For instance, is it to develop an initial IEP, review an existing IEP, or address a specific concern? It must also list the date, time, and location of the meeting. Additionally, the notice should include a list of the individuals who are expected to attend the meeting. This allows you to see who will be participating and to understand the different perspectives that will be represented. The notice should also inform you of your right to invite other individuals to the meeting who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, including a trusted friend or advocate.

It is important to understand that you have the right to waive the five-day notice requirement. This means that you can agree to hold the ARD meeting sooner than five days after receiving the notice. You might choose to do this if there is an urgent issue that needs to be addressed or if it is simply more convenient for your schedule. However, it is essential to consider carefully whether you have enough time to prepare adequately before waiving this right. The waiver must be in writing, indicating that you understand your right to the five-day notice and are voluntarily choosing to forgo it. Never feel pressured to waive your rights if you need more time to prepare.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to an ARD meeting can make a significant difference in ensuring your child's needs are fully addressed. Think of it as gathering evidence to support your child's case. The most valuable thing you can bring is your personal knowledge of your child - their strengths, weaknesses, interests, and the challenges they face both inside and outside of school.

Gather any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, whether they were conducted by the school district or by independent professionals. These evaluations can provide valuable data about your child's academic, cognitive, and functional abilities. If your child has received any medical diagnoses or treatments, bring letters from their doctors or therapists. These letters should clearly explain the diagnosis, the impact on your child's functioning, and any recommendations for accommodations or support in the school setting. Any records from previous schools or educational programs can be helpful as well.

It's incredibly useful to create a written list of your specific concerns and goals for your child's education. Having it written down ensures you don't forget anything important during the meeting and provides a clear focus for the discussion. Be specific about what you want to see your child achieve and the supports you believe they need to get there. Consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you to the meeting. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can help you understand the process, ask questions, and ensure your voice is heard. They can provide emotional support and act as another set of ears during the meeting. Finally, Texas is a "one-party consent" state for recording conversations. This means that you can legally record the ARD meeting, as long as you inform the ARD committee at the beginning of the meeting that you are recording. This can be extremely helpful for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring accurate documentation of the decisions made.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

It's critical to know that at any point during the ARD meeting, any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, has the right to request a recess. This recess can be for up to ten school days. This is a powerful tool that allows you to pause the decision-making process if you feel overwhelmed, need more information, or want to consult with an advocate or other expert before finalizing the IEP.

You might want to invoke the 10-day recess rule if you are presented with information that you don't understand or if you feel that the ARD committee is not adequately considering your concerns. Perhaps you need more time to review evaluation reports, research potential services, or discuss the proposed IEP with your spouse or a special education advocate. Maybe the team is pushing for a placement that you are not comfortable with, and you want time to seek a second opinion. Whatever the reason, you have the right to request a recess. To do so, simply state clearly that you are requesting a recess of the ARD meeting for up to ten school days. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation, although it can be helpful to briefly explain why you need the time.

The school district is required to grant your request for a recess. The ARD committee will then reconvene after the recess period to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP. Use this time wisely to gather the information and support you need to make informed decisions about your child's education. Don't hesitate to use this tool if you feel it is necessary to ensure that your child's needs are being met.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the central document that is developed and agreed upon during the ARD meeting. It's a comprehensive plan that outlines your child's educational needs and the specific services and supports they will receive to address those needs. Understanding the different sections of the IEP is essential for ensuring that it accurately reflects your child's needs and goals.

One of the most important sections of the IEP is the Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP). This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in various areas, such as reading, writing, math, social skills, and behavior. It should be based on a variety of data sources, including evaluations, classroom observations, and your input as a parent. The PLAAFP serves as the foundation for setting annual goals. Annual goals are measurable statements that describe what your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The IEP will also outline the specific special education and related services your child will receive. These services may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and other supports necessary to help your child achieve their goals. The IEP will specify the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

The IEP also includes a statement of your child's educational placement, which refers to the setting where they will receive their special education services. This could be a general education classroom, a resource room, a self-contained classroom, or a combination of settings. The IEP will also outline any accommodations or modifications that your child needs to access the general education curriculum and participate in classroom activities. Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, such as extended time on tests or preferential seating. Modifications are changes to what a student is expected to learn, such as simplifying assignments or reducing the number of questions. It is extremely important to remember that you are not required to sign the IEP on the same day as the ARD meeting. Take the document home, review it carefully, and consider whether it truly reflects the agreements made during the meeting and whether you believe it is in your child's best interest.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's not uncommon for parents to disagree with the ARD committee about certain aspects of their child's IEP. Remember that you are an equal member of the team, and your voice matters. If you disagree with the proposed IEP, it's important to understand your rights and options.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD committee's recommendation for placement, you can refuse to enroll your child in special education services. However, it is important to note that once your child is receiving special education services, you do not have the right to unilaterally refuse all services. Instead, you must work with the ARD committee to develop an IEP that you can support. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP document if you disagree with its contents. By not signing, you are not consenting to the IEP, but your child will still receive the services outlined in the last agreed-upon IEP. In Texas, school districts often implement the IEP even if a parent does not sign.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with an evaluation conducted by the school. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. You also have the right to include a written statement of disagreement in your child's IEP. This statement will be attached to the IEP and will become part of your child's educational record. This is a way to document your concerns and ensure that they are considered in future ARD meetings.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve your disagreement with the ARD committee through informal discussions and you still feel that your child's rights are being violated, you have several formal options available to you. These options are designed to provide a process for resolving disputes and ensuring that your child receives a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE).

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege a violation of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or state special education laws. The complaint must be filed within one year of the alleged violation. The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third-party mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. TEA offers free mediation services to parents of children with disabilities. The mediator facilitates the discussion and helps both parties explore possible solutions. Mediation is a non-adversarial process and can be a helpful way to resolve disputes without resorting to more formal legal proceedings.

The third option is to request a due process hearing. This is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about your child's special education needs. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at the due process hearing. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming. The hearing officer's decision is binding on both you and the school district, unless it is appealed to a court of law. The fourth option is to file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). This option is available if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action.

Remember, these rights are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA 2004) and Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89. It is crucial to understand your rights and to advocate effectively for your child's needs. Don't hesitate to seek assistance from advocacy organizations or legal professionals if you need help navigating the special education system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
