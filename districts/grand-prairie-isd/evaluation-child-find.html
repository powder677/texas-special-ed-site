Requesting an Evaluation in Grand Prairie ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Grand Prairie ISD

If you believe your child, who attends a Grand Prairie ISD school, may have a disability that affects their ability to learn and benefit from their education, you have the right to request a special education evaluation. This evaluation is conducted by the school district to determine if your child qualifies for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in the special education process is to submit a written request for an evaluation to Grand Prairie ISD. This request should be clear, concise, and delivered to the appropriate individuals. Here's a breakdown of how to make your request:

1. Format: Your request must be in writing. While an email is acceptable, a formal letter provides a stronger record. Consider sending both an email and a hard copy letter via certified mail with return receipt requested.

2. Content: The letter should include the following information:

  • Your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your child's current grade level and school.
  • Your child's teacher's name.
  • Your full name, address, phone number, and email address.
  • A clear statement that you are requesting a full and individual evaluation for special education services. For example, "I am writing to formally request a full and individual evaluation to determine if my child, [Child's Name], is eligible for special education services."
  • A brief description of your concerns regarding your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental performance. Be specific and provide examples. For instance: "My child struggles with reading comprehension, often failing to understand the main idea of a passage. He also has difficulty focusing in class and frequently gets out of his seat without permission."
  • Any relevant medical or educational history, including previous evaluations or diagnoses. If your child has been evaluated by a private professional (e.g., psychologist, speech therapist), include copies of those reports with your request.
  • Mention any specific areas of concern you have (e.g., reading, writing, math, speech, behavior, social skills).

3. Who to Send It To: The best recipient is typically the school principal or the Special Education Director for Grand Prairie ISD. You can also send it to your child's teacher, who will then forward it to the appropriate personnel. To find the Special Education Director, check the Grand Prairie ISD website.

4. Keeping a Record: Keep a copy of your request letter for your records. If sending via certified mail, retain the receipt as proof of delivery.

Example Letter:

[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[Your Phone Number]
[Your Email Address]

[Date]

[Principal's Name] or [Special Education Director's Name]
[School Name] or [Grand Prairie ISD Office Address]
[School Address] or [Grand Prairie ISD Office Address]

Subject: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Name]

Dear [Principal's Name] or [Special Education Director's Name],

I am writing to formally request a full and individual evaluation for my child, [Child's Name], born on [Child's Date of Birth]. [Child's Name] is currently in [Child's Grade Level] grade at [School Name], and his/her teacher is [Teacher's Name].

I am concerned about [Child's Name]'s progress in [Specific Area of Concern, e.g., reading]. He/She [Describe specific examples of difficulties, e.g., "struggles to decode words and often misreads simple sentences. He/She is also falling behind his/her peers in reading comprehension."]. In addition, [Mention any other relevant concerns, e.g., "he/she has difficulty staying focused in class and often fidgets and talks out of turn."].

[Optional: Mention any prior evaluations or diagnoses, e.g., "My child was previously evaluated by [Name of Professional] at [Organization] on [Date] and was found to have [Brief summary of findings]. A copy of the report is attached."]

I believe a comprehensive evaluation is necessary to determine if [Child's Name] has a disability that is impacting his/her ability to learn and to determine if he/she is eligible for special education services.

Thank you for your time and consideration. I look forward to hearing from you soon to discuss this matter further.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once Grand Prairie ISD receives your written request for an evaluation, they have a specific timeline to follow under Texas law and IDEA. This timeline is typically 45 school days.

Day 1: Receipt of Request: The 45-school day timeline begins the day the school district receives your written request.

Within 15 School Days: The school district must decide whether or not to conduct an evaluation. If they choose not to evaluate, they must provide you with written notice of their refusal, along with the reasons for the refusal and information about your rights to appeal their decision.

If They Agree to Evaluate: If the school district agrees to conduct an evaluation, they must obtain your written consent before starting the evaluation process. They will send you an "ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) Committee Meeting Notice" to schedule a meeting to discuss the evaluation process and obtain your consent. This ARD meeting is called to determine the scope of the evaluation; meaning, what areas will be assessed.

Evaluation Completion and ARD Meeting: From the date of your written consent, the school district has the remainder of the 45 school days to complete the evaluation and hold an ARD committee meeting to discuss the results and determine eligibility for special education services. At the ARD meeting, the evaluation results will be reviewed, and the ARD committee (which includes you) will determine if your child meets the criteria for a specific disability and requires special education and related services.

Important Considerations:

  • Weekends, holidays, and school breaks (e.g., summer vacation) are not counted as school days.
  • The 45-school day timeline can be extended if both you and the school district agree in writing to an extension.
  • It is crucial to communicate with the school district throughout the evaluation process to ensure that the timeline is being followed and that you are kept informed of the progress.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment of your child's strengths and weaknesses in all areas related to the suspected disability. The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals, such as educational diagnosticians, psychologists, speech therapists, and occupational therapists, depending on the areas of concern.

Under Texas law and IDEA, the FIE must:

  • Assess All Areas of Suspected Disability: The evaluation must address all areas related to the suspected disability, including academic achievement, cognitive abilities, communication skills, motor skills, social-emotional functioning, and adaptive behavior.
  • Use a Variety of Assessment Tools and Strategies: The evaluation should not rely on a single assessment tool or strategy. It should include a variety of standardized tests, observations, interviews, and review of records to obtain a comprehensive picture of your child's abilities and needs.
  • Be Non-Discriminatory: The evaluation must be conducted in a non-discriminatory manner, meaning that it must be appropriate for your child's age, cultural background, and language proficiency. If your child's primary language is not English, the evaluation must be conducted in their native language or through a qualified interpreter.
  • Provide Information About Present Levels of Performance: The FIE must provide detailed information about your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This information will be used to develop an individualized education program (IEP) if your child is found eligible for special education services.
  • Identify Educational Needs: The evaluation must identify your child's specific educational needs and make recommendations for appropriate interventions and supports.

Specific Areas to Consider: Depending on the suspected disability, the FIE may include assessments in the following areas:

  • Cognitive Functioning: Intelligence tests (e.g., Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children - WISC) to assess intellectual abilities.
  • Academic Achievement: Standardized tests to assess reading, writing, and math skills.
  • Language and Communication: Assessments of receptive and expressive language skills, articulation, fluency, and social communication.
  • Motor Skills: Assessments of fine motor and gross motor skills.
  • Social-Emotional Functioning: Assessments of behavior, emotional regulation, and social skills.
  • Adaptive Behavior: Assessments of daily living skills, such as self-care, communication, and social interaction.
  • Assistive Technology: An assessment of whether the student requires assistive technology to access the curriculum and participate in educational activities.

It is important to review the evaluation results carefully and ask questions if you do not understand any of the information. You have the right to an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense if you disagree with the school district's evaluation, but there are specific procedures you must follow to request an IEE.

Child Find Obligations

Grand Prairie ISD has a legal obligation under IDEA and Texas state law to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities within its boundaries who may be in need of special education and related services. This is known as "Child Find."

What Does Child Find Mean? Child Find means that the school district must have procedures in place to:

  • Raise awareness about special education services in the community.
  • Screen and identify children who may have disabilities.
  • Evaluate children suspected of having disabilities.

How Does Child Find Work? Child Find activities can include:

  • Public service announcements about special education services.
  • Screening programs for preschool children.
  • Referrals from teachers, parents, doctors, and other professionals.
  • Review of student records.

Parent's Role in Child Find: As a parent, you play a crucial role in Child Find. If you have concerns about your child's development or learning, you have the right to contact Grand Prairie ISD and request an evaluation, even if your child is not yet enrolled in school. The district cannot ignore your concerns. The written request starts the process as described earlier. The district's responsibility to Child Find means that they should also be proactive in identifying children who might need help, rather than solely relying on parents to initiate the process.

What If They Refuse

If Grand Prairie ISD refuses to conduct an evaluation after you have submitted a written request, they are required to provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for the refusal. This notice must also inform you of your rights to challenge their decision.

Reasons for Refusal: The school district may refuse to evaluate if they believe there is insufficient evidence to suggest that your child has a disability that affects their ability to learn. However, they cannot simply dismiss your concerns without considering all available information.

Your Options: If the school district refuses to evaluate your child, you have several options:

  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a neutral third party to try to resolve the dispute.
  • Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you can present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about whether or not the school district should evaluate your child. You have the right to be represented by an attorney at a due process hearing.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a complaint with TEA if you believe the school district has violated state or federal special education laws.
  • Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at Public Expense: As mentioned earlier, if you disagree with the district's decision not to evaluate, this may be a path to take. However, this is typically considered when you disagree with the *results* of an evaluation, rather than a complete refusal to evaluate. Consult with a special education advocate or attorney.

Important Considerations:

  • There are deadlines for filing complaints and requesting due process hearings, so it is important to act quickly if you disagree with the school district's decision.
  • Consider seeking legal advice from an attorney specializing in special education law to understand your rights and options.

After the Evaluation

Following the completion of the FIE, the ARD committee (including you) will meet to review the evaluation results and determine if your child meets the criteria for a disability under IDEA and Texas state law. If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education services, they will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). This meeting must take place within 30 calendar days of the initial eligibility determination.

The IEP: The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services and supports that will be provided to help them achieve those goals. The IEP must include:

  • Present Levels of Performance: A description of your child's current academic achievement and functional performance.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child's progress in academic and functional areas.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the special education and related services that will be provided to your child, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, and transportation.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom and during testing.
  • Placement: A description of where your child will receive their special education services (e.g., general education classroom, resource room, self-contained classroom).
  • Dates and Duration of Services: The dates when services will begin and end, and the frequency and duration of services.
  • Progress Monitoring: How your child's progress towards their goals will be measured and reported.

Your Role in the IEP Process: You are a vital member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate in all decisions regarding your child's education. You have the right to:

  • Review the IEP and provide input.
  • Request changes to the IEP if you disagree with any of its provisions.
  • Bring an advocate or attorney to the ARD meeting.
  • Reject the IEP if you do not believe it meets your child's needs.

Ongoing Review and Revision: The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to request an ARD meeting at any time if you have concerns about your child's progress or the effectiveness of their IEP.

If you disagree with the IEP: If you disagree with the IEP, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing, as described previously.

This guide provides a general overview of the special education evaluation process in Grand Prairie ISD. It is important to consult with legal counsel or a special education advocate to ensure that your child's rights are protected and that they receive the appropriate services and supports to succeed in school.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
