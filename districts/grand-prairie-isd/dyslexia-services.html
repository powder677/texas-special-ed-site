Dyslexia Services in Grand Prairie ISD

Dyslexia Services in Grand Prairie ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available in Grand Prairie Independent School District (GPISD), in accordance with Texas state law and the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024, is a comprehensive resource for educators, parents, and students regarding dyslexia identification, intervention, and support in Texas public schools. GPISD adheres to the guidelines and recommendations outlined in this handbook.

Key Updates and Information from the 2024 Handbook:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook reaffirms the definition of dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurobiological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction. Secondary consequences may include problems in reading comprehension and reduced reading experience that can impede growth of vocabulary and background knowledge.
  • Emphasis on Early Identification and Intervention: The handbook stresses the importance of identifying students at risk for dyslexia as early as possible, ideally in kindergarten or first grade. Early intervention is critical for improving reading outcomes.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook strongly promotes the use of structured literacy approaches in reading instruction for all students, but particularly for those with dyslexia. Structured literacy explicitly and systematically teaches foundational reading skills, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension.
  • Screening Procedures: Specific screening procedures are outlined to identify students at risk for dyslexia. These screenings assess key skills such as phonological awareness, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and letter-sound knowledge.
  • Progress Monitoring: Regular progress monitoring is essential to track student progress and adjust interventions as needed. Data-driven decision-making is crucial.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the importance of collaboration between schools and parents. Parents should be informed about their child's progress and involved in the development of intervention plans.
  • Professional Development: Teachers need ongoing professional development in dyslexia and structured literacy. GPISD provides training opportunities for staff to enhance their knowledge and skills in this area.
  • Assistive Technology: The handbook recognizes the role of assistive technology in supporting students with dyslexia. Schools should consider providing assistive technology based on individual student needs.
  • Legal Mandates: The handbook details the legal requirements related to dyslexia services in Texas public schools, including the requirements under state law and federal special education laws.

Parents and educators are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook, which is available on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website.

Mandatory Screening

GPISD implements mandatory dyslexia screening for all kindergarten and first-grade students, as required by Texas law. Screening aims to identify students who are at risk for dyslexia and who may benefit from early intervention.

Screening Process:

  • Kindergarten Screening: Kindergarten students are screened using age-appropriate assessments that focus on pre-reading skills such as phonological awareness (e.g., rhyming, segmenting sounds), letter-sound knowledge, and rapid automatized naming (RAN).
  • First Grade Screening: First-grade students are screened using assessments that measure phonological awareness, phonics skills, reading fluency, and spelling.
  • Individualized Screening: If a student is suspected of having dyslexia at any grade level, or if a parent requests an evaluation, the student may receive additional individualized screening measures.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified of the screening results. If the screening indicates a risk for dyslexia, the school will contact parents to discuss intervention options and next steps.

It's important to note that screening is not a diagnosis of dyslexia. Screening identifies students who may need further evaluation and support. A comprehensive evaluation is required to determine if a student has dyslexia.

Structured Literacy Programs

GPISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide targeted intervention for students identified with dyslexia or at risk for dyslexia. Structured literacy is a research-based approach that explicitly and systematically teaches foundational reading skills.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonological Awareness: Understanding and manipulating the sounds of spoken language (e.g., blending, segmenting, rhyming).
  • Phonics: The relationship between letters and sounds. Students learn to decode (sound out) words.
  • Fluency: Reading accurately, at a good pace, and with expression.
  • Vocabulary: Understanding the meaning of words.
  • Comprehension: Understanding the meaning of text.
  • Morphology: Study of meaningful parts of words (prefixes, suffixes, root words)
  • Syntax: Rules governing how words are combined to form sentences.

Implementation:

  • Intervention is delivered by trained educators who have expertise in structured literacy.
  • Instruction is individualized to meet the specific needs of each student.
  • Progress is monitored regularly to track student growth and adjust instruction as needed.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), which results in an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the level of support required.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning. Dyslexia can qualify as a disability under Section 504.
  • Focus: A 504 plan provides accommodations and modifications to ensure that the student has equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Examples of Accommodations: Extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, modified assignments.
  • Service Delivery: Usually implemented within the general education classroom.

IEP (Individualized Education Program):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability (including a specific learning disability like dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: An IEP provides specialized instruction and related services to address the student's unique learning needs.
  • Examples of Services: Intensive reading intervention (structured literacy), speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling.
  • Service Delivery: May include instruction in the general education classroom, small group instruction, or specialized settings.

Key Differences:

  • Level of Support: IEPs provide a more intensive level of support than 504 plans, including specialized instruction.
  • Eligibility Criteria: IEP eligibility criteria are more stringent than 504 eligibility criteria.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by IDEA, which has more detailed legal requirements than Section 504.

The decision about whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and parents. The team will consider the student's individual needs and the level of support required to ensure their success.

Accommodations List

The following is a list of common accommodations that may be provided to students with dyslexia. The specific accommodations provided will be based on the student's individual needs and documented in their 504 plan or IEP.

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Seating the student in a location that minimizes distractions and maximizes learning.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to assistive technology tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, or audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting assignments to reduce the reading load or complexity.
  • Oral Reading of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the student to use a calculator for math problems.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing the student with notes or allowing them to record lectures.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids such as graphic organizers, charts, and diagrams.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take short breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Highlighters: Using highlighters to emphasize key information in text.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Learning material using a multi-sensory approach
  • Explicit and Systematic Instruction: Receiving explicit and systematic instruction in foundational reading skills.
  • Untimed Tests: Allowing the student to complete tests without a time limit.
  • Access to Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing the student to use a word processor with spell check for writing assignments.

This is not an exhaustive list, and other accommodations may be appropriate based on the student's individual needs.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from GPISD. The evaluation process is designed to determine if your child meets the criteria for dyslexia and requires special education services.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact Your Child's School: Begin by contacting your child's teacher, counselor, or principal. Discuss your concerns and provide any relevant information, such as observations of your child's reading difficulties.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education director. The request should include your child's name, date of birth, grade level, and a clear statement that you are requesting an evaluation for dyslexia. You should also include a brief explanation of your concerns.
  3. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, they have a specified timeframe (usually within 15 school days) to respond and begin the evaluation process. The evaluation will typically involve a variety of assessments, including measures of reading, spelling, phonological awareness, and other relevant skills.
  4. ARD Meeting (if applicable): If the evaluation indicates that your child has dyslexia and requires special education services, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting will be held to develop an IEP. The ARD committee will include you, your child's teachers, an administrator, and other relevant professionals.
  5. 504 Committee Meeting (if applicable): If the evaluation indicates that the student is eligible for accommodations under Section 504, a 504 committee will meet to develop a 504 plan.

Important Information:

  • GPISD is required to provide a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) to all students with disabilities, including dyslexia.
  • You have the right to participate in all stages of the evaluation and IEP/504 plan development process.
  • You have the right to access your child's educational records.
  • You can request a copy of the evaluation report.
  • If you disagree with the school's evaluation or IEP/504 plan, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or due process.

Contact Information:

For further information about dyslexia services in GPISD, please contact your child's school or the GPISD Special Education Department.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
