Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Urgent Guide: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Grand Prairie ISD

This guide is designed to provide parents and guardians of students in Grand Prairie ISD with critical information regarding discipline, suspension, and grievance procedures. This is a crisis-mode guide for protective parents. Know your rights and act quickly!

The 10-Day Suspension Rule: What You NEED to Know

Grand Prairie ISD, like all Texas school districts, operates under rules regarding student suspensions. Pay close attention because missing deadlines or misunderstandings can have severe consequences for your child's education.

The critical rule to remember is the 10-day suspension rule. What it means is this: a student can be suspended for up to 10 school days for certain disciplinary infractions. However, if a student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days in a single school year, certain rights are triggered, especially if the student has an IEP (Individualized Education Program) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

Important Considerations Regarding the 10-Day Rule:

  • Cumulative Suspensions Matter: The 10 days don't have to be consecutive. Keep a meticulous record of every suspension your child receives.
  • Suspensions Can Be for Seemingly Minor Offenses: Be vigilant, even for what appear to be minor infractions. The school can stack these suspensions, leading to the 10-day threshold being crossed without your immediate awareness.
  • Focus on Documentation: Always request written documentation of any suspension, no matter how short. This documentation is crucial for tracking cumulative days and initiating grievance procedures.
  • Demand Justification: Ask for a clear and concise explanation of why the suspension was issued. Don't accept vague reasons.
  • IEP Students Have Added Protections: If your child has an IEP, crossing the 10-day suspension threshold triggers a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). See the section below for details.

What to do if your child is facing suspension:

  1. Immediately Request a Meeting: Schedule a meeting with the school principal and, if applicable, your child's special education teacher or case manager.
  2. Bring Documentation: Bring all records of previous suspensions, IEP documents (if applicable), and any relevant medical or psychological evaluations.
  3. Advocate for Your Child: Listen carefully to the school's explanation, but also present your child's perspective. Highlight any mitigating circumstances.
  4. Know Your Rights: Remind the school officials of your child's rights under the Texas Education Code and, if applicable, IDEA.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

If your child has an IEP and has been suspended for more than 10 cumulative days in a school year, a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) MUST be conducted. This is a critical safeguard under IDEA to protect students with disabilities from being unfairly punished for behaviors that are a manifestation of their disability.

What Happens in an MDR:

The MDR is a meeting where a team, including you (the parent/guardian), relevant school personnel (special education teacher, administrator, etc.), and potentially other experts, must determine if the student's behavior that led to the disciplinary action was:

  1. Caused by or directly and substantially related to the student's disability; or
  2. The direct result of the school's failure to implement the student's IEP.

Key Points About the MDR Process:

  • School Must Initiate: It is the school's responsibility to initiate the MDR process when the 10-day threshold is reached. Don't wait for them; if they fail to act, demand an MDR immediately.
  • Parent Participation is Crucial: Your presence and input are essential at the MDR meeting. Prepare to explain how your child's disability may have contributed to the behavior in question.
  • IEP Implementation is Key: Scrutinize the implementation of your child's IEP. Was the school following the IEP's goals, accommodations, and modifications? If not, this is a strong argument that the behavior was a result of the school's failure.
  • What Happens If the Behavior IS a Manifestation? If the MDR team determines that the behavior was a manifestation of the student's disability or due to IEP implementation failures, the student cannot be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team must then develop or revise the IEP to address the behavior. The student may return to their original placement, or the team may agree on a temporary alternative placement.
  • What Happens If the Behavior is NOT a Manifestation? If the team determines that the behavior was NOT a manifestation of the student's disability, the student can be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. However, the school must still provide services to the student during the suspension to ensure they can continue to participate in the general education curriculum.
  • Demand Documentation: Request a written summary of the MDR team's findings and conclusions.

If you disagree with the MDR's findings, you have the right to appeal. Consult TEA (Texas Education Agency) resources immediately to understand the appeals process and deadlines.

Change of Placement: Understanding Your Rights

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a disciplinary action results in a student being removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or when a series of removals constitutes a pattern.

What Constitutes a Pattern of Removals?

A pattern of removals exists if:

  • The student is removed for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, AND
  • The behavior is substantially similar to the student's behavior in previous incidents that resulted in removals, AND
  • Additional factors, such as the length of each removal, the total amount of time the student is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another, exist.

Change of Placement Triggers More Protections:

If a change of placement occurs, the school must provide the parent with notice of the disciplinary action and procedural safeguards. For students with IEPs, a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is required.

Fighting an Unjust Change of Placement:

  • Challenge the "Pattern": If the school claims a pattern exists, scrutinize the incidents they are citing. Argue that the behaviors are not substantially similar or that the other factors (length, proximity) do not support a finding of a pattern.
  • Focus on IEP Implementation: As with any disciplinary action involving a student with an IEP, emphasize any failures in the implementation of the IEP.
  • Explore Alternative Placements: If a change of placement is unavoidable, work with the school to identify a less restrictive alternative placement that still meets your child's educational needs.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance in Grand Prairie ISD

If you believe that the school has violated your child's rights or that a disciplinary action was unfair or inappropriate, you have the right to file a grievance. Grand Prairie ISD has a multi-level grievance process. It's crucial to start at Level 1 and follow the process diligently.

Level 1 Grievance: School Principal

  1. Obtain the Grievance Form: Request a grievance form from the school principal's office or the district's website.
  2. Complete the Form Thoroughly: Clearly and concisely state the nature of your grievance. Provide specific dates, times, and names of individuals involved. Include copies of any relevant documentation (suspension notices, IEP documents, etc.).
  3. Be Specific: Avoid vague or general complaints. Focus on the specific actions or inactions that you believe violated your child's rights.
  4. Propose a Resolution: State clearly what outcome you are seeking (e.g., removal of the suspension from the student's record, modification of the IEP, etc.).
  5. Meet the Deadline: Pay VERY close attention to the deadline for filing a Level 1 grievance. Missing the deadline can invalidate your claim. This is usually within a very short timeframe (e.g., 10-15 school days) of the incident. Confirm the exact timeframe with the school or district.
  6. Submit the Grievance: Submit the completed grievance form to the school principal. Keep a copy for your records.
  7. Attend the Meeting: The principal will typically schedule a meeting with you to discuss the grievance. Be prepared to present your case clearly and calmly.
  8. Receive the Decision: The principal will provide a written decision within a specified timeframe.

What if you disagree with the Level 1 decision? You have the right to appeal to the next level (Level 2), which is typically the superintendent or their designee. Again, be aware of deadlines for appeal.

TEA (Texas Education Agency) State Complaint

If you believe that Grand Prairie ISD has violated state or federal laws related to special education (IDEA), you have the right to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

When to File a TEA Complaint:

  • When you believe the district has failed to properly implement your child's IEP.
  • When you believe the district has violated your child's rights under IDEA.
  • After exhausting district-level grievance procedures, if you are still unsatisfied with the outcome.

How to File a TEA Complaint:

  1. Obtain the Complaint Form: Download the official complaint form from the TEA website.
  2. Follow Instructions Carefully: The TEA complaint form is detailed and requires specific information. Read the instructions carefully and provide all required documentation.
  3. Meet the Deadline: TEA complaints must be filed within a specific timeframe (usually one year) of the alleged violation.
  4. Mail the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form to the TEA address provided on the form.

TEA Investigation:

TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision. If TEA finds that the district violated the law, it will order the district to take corrective action.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Protection

Due Process is a formal legal process available to parents of students with disabilities when they disagree with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of their child, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE).

When to Request Due Process:

  • When you have exhausted other dispute resolution options (e.g., mediation, state complaint) and are still unable to resolve your disagreement with the district.
  • When you believe the district has fundamentally violated your child's rights under IDEA.

Due Process Hearing:

A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding conducted before an impartial hearing officer. You have the right to present evidence, call witnesses, and be represented by an attorney.

Important Considerations:

  • Seek Legal Counsel: Due process is a complex legal process. It is highly recommended to consult with an attorney experienced in special education law.
  • Notice Requirements: There are specific notice requirements that must be followed when requesting a due process hearing.
  • Strict Deadlines: Due process proceedings are subject to strict deadlines.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
