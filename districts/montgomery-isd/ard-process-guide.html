What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Montgomery ISD! You're likely reading this because your child may need some extra support in school, and that's perfectly okay. The Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting is a cornerstone of special education, and it's designed to bring together everyone who cares about your child's education to create a plan tailored specifically for them. ARD is an acronym that stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal.

The purpose of an ARD meeting is to determine if your child is eligible for special education services, to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) if they are eligible, to review and update the IEP as needed, and ultimately, to determine if and when special education services are no longer required. Think of it as a collaborative problem-solving session where everyone works together to ensure your child receives the support they need to succeed in school.

Who attends an ARD meeting? The core members include you, the parent; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education (usually an administrator); a special education teacher; a general education teacher; an evaluation specialist (someone who can interpret your child's evaluation results); and, when appropriate, your child themselves. Other people may be invited, such as therapists, counselors, or anyone else who can contribute valuable information about your child's needs. It's important to remember that you, as the parent, are a vital member of this team, and your input is highly valued. Your unique insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and learning style are essential for developing an effective IEP.

The ARD process is not just a suggestion; it's a requirement under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code 29, a state law. These laws ensure that all children with disabilities have access to a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) designed to meet their unique needs.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Before an ARD meeting takes place, Montgomery ISD is required to provide you with written notice at least five calendar days in advance. This is to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting and gather any information you think might be helpful. This notice isn't just a formality; it's your right to be informed and involved in your child's education.

What should this notice include? It must state the purpose of the meeting (whether it's an initial eligibility determination, an annual review, or something else), the date, time, and location of the meeting, a list of the individuals who will be attending (if known), and a description of your rights as a parent under IDEA. The notice should also explain what topics will be discussed at the meeting.

Sometimes, it might be beneficial to hold an ARD meeting sooner than five days. Perhaps a situation has arisen that needs immediate attention. In these cases, you can waive your right to the five-day notice by providing written consent. This is entirely your choice, and you should only do so if you feel comfortable and prepared to participate in the meeting. There is absolutely no pressure to waive your rights.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting can feel overwhelming, but it doesn't have to be. Think of it as gathering information to help the team understand your child better. Here's a list of things you might want to bring:

First, bring any prior evaluations or assessments of your child. This could include evaluations from private therapists, psychologists, or medical professionals. These reports can provide valuable insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and learning style.

Second, any letters or reports from doctors or other healthcare providers can be helpful. This is especially true if your child has a medical condition that impacts their learning or behavior. These documents can help the ARD team understand the medical context of your child's needs.

Third, gather any relevant school records. This might include report cards, progress reports, work samples, or notes from teachers. These documents can provide a picture of your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom.

Fourth, create a written list of your concerns and questions. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all your concerns are addressed. It's easy to get caught up in the discussion and forget to ask important questions, so having a written list can be very helpful.

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can help you understand the information being presented and advocate for your child's needs. Having someone else present can provide emotional support and help you remember important details.

Sixth, remember that Texas is a "one-party consent" state for recording conversations. Therefore, you have the right to record the ARD meeting, provided you inform the team that you are doing so before the meeting begins. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for reviewing the meeting later and ensuring that you accurately remember the decisions that were made. Be upfront about it; transparency is key. This is not meant to be sneaky, it is meant to help you ensure you remember what was discussed.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The ARD meeting is a process, not a one-time event. If at any point during the meeting you feel overwhelmed, confused, or that you need more time to consider the information being presented, you have the right to request a recess. Any member of the ARD committee, including you, can request a recess. This recess can last for up to 10 school days, giving you time to gather more information, consult with an advocate, or simply process what has been discussed.

How do you invoke the 10-day recess rule? Simply state that you would like to recess the meeting for up to 10 school days to further consider the IEP (or other issue under discussion) or obtain additional information. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation. The team must honor your request. This is your right, and it's important to use it if you feel you need more time to make informed decisions about your child's education.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the heart of special education. It's a written document that outlines your child's unique needs, goals, and the services and supports they will receive to achieve those goals. The IEP is developed during the ARD meeting and is based on your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This means the IEP must describe where your child is currently functioning in different academic areas and in terms of their behavior and daily living skills.

The IEP also includes annual goals, which are measurable objectives that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). The ARD committee will also determine the services your child needs to reach these goals. These services can include specialized instruction, therapy (speech, occupational, physical), counseling, transportation, and assistive technology.

Another key element of the IEP is placement. This refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. The law requires that children with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), which means they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Placement decisions are made based on your child's individual needs and the services they require.

Finally, the IEP outlines any accommodations your child needs to access the general education curriculum. Accommodations are changes to the way instruction is delivered or assessed, but they don't change the content of what is being taught. Examples of accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, or the use of assistive technology.

It's important to remember that you don't have to sign the IEP on the same day it's developed. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before making a decision. You can reconvene the ARD committee at a later date to discuss any concerns you have.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's perfectly normal to have disagreements with the ARD team. You know your child best, and your perspective is valuable. If you disagree with the team's recommendations, it's important to voice your concerns.

You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you don't agree with the team's decision that your child is eligible for special education services, you can refuse to allow them to be placed in a special education program. If you refuse consent for initial placement, your child will not receive special education services.

Even if you agree with your child's eligibility, you can still disagree with specific elements of the IEP. You don't have to sign the IEP if you don't agree with it. Signing the IEP indicates that you participated in the meeting, not necessarily that you agree with everything in the document. If you don't sign the IEP, the school district can implement the IEP as written. However, you still retain your rights to pursue other options, such as mediation or a due process hearing.

One option you have is to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child, you have the right to request an IEE. The district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that its evaluation was appropriate.

Another way to express your disagreement is to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it. This indicates that you don't agree with all the decisions that were made at the meeting, even if you're willing to allow the IEP to be implemented. Writing "parent disagrees" preserves your right to challenge the IEP later if needed.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve your disagreements with the ARD team and you're still not satisfied, you have several formal options for escalating your concerns. These rights are governed by IDEA 2004 and 19 Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Chapter 89, also known as the Legal Framework.

First, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education law. This is a good option if you believe the district is not following proper procedures.

Second, you can request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution. Mediation is also free. It's a good way to try to find common ground and avoid the more adversarial process of a due process hearing.

Third, you can request a due process hearing. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer, who will then make a decision about your child's educational needs. This is a more complex and time-consuming process than filing a complaint or requesting mediation, and it's often advisable to seek legal representation if you pursue a due process hearing.

Fourth, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. This is an option if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child because of their disability.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
