Dyslexia Services in Amarillo ISD

Dyslexia Services in Amarillo ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services offered within Amarillo Independent School District (AISD) in accordance with Texas state law and guidelines. This information is intended to assist parents, educators, and community members in understanding the identification, evaluation, and support provided to students with dyslexia within AISD.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

Amarillo ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. The Texas Dyslexia Handbook serves as the definitive resource for understanding dyslexia and related disorders in Texas schools. The most recent update, the 2024 version, incorporates recent legislative changes and refined best practices in dyslexia identification, instruction, and accommodation.

Key Updates in the 2024 Handbook:

  • Early Identification: Emphasizes the importance of early screening and intervention for students at risk for dyslexia.
  • Structured Literacy: Reinforces the requirement for using structured literacy approaches in dyslexia intervention.
  • Progress Monitoring: Highlights the need for ongoing monitoring of student progress to inform instructional decisions.
  • Technology Integration: Encourages the use of assistive technology to support students with dyslexia.
  • Family Involvement: Stresses the importance of collaboration between schools and families in supporting students with dyslexia.

Parents and educators are strongly encouraged to familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024. It provides detailed information on all aspects of dyslexia services in Texas schools.

Mandatory Screening

Amarillo ISD conducts mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade, as mandated by Texas law. This screening aims to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. These screenings are typically conducted using measures that assess phonological awareness, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and letter-sound knowledge, all key indicators of potential reading difficulties.

Screening Process:

  • Universal Screening: All kindergarten and first-grade students participate in the initial screening.
  • Screening Tools: AISD uses approved screening instruments aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook recommendations. These instruments are designed to identify potential risk factors for dyslexia, such as difficulties with phonological awareness (ability to manipulate sounds in words), rapid naming (quickly naming letters or objects), and letter-sound correspondence.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified of their child's screening results.
  • Tiered Intervention: Students identified as at-risk receive targeted interventions to address their specific needs.

What Happens After Screening?

If a student demonstrates characteristics of dyslexia based on the initial screening, AISD will implement a multi-tiered system of support (MTSS) to provide targeted interventions. These interventions are designed to address specific reading difficulties and prevent further academic challenges.

  • Tier 1: High-quality core instruction provided to all students.
  • Tier 2: Targeted interventions for students who are not making sufficient progress in Tier 1. These interventions are typically provided in small groups and focus on foundational reading skills.
  • Tier 3: Intensive, individualized interventions for students who are not making sufficient progress in Tier 2. These interventions are often delivered by a certified dyslexia therapist or specialist.

Structured Literacy Programs

Amarillo ISD utilizes structured literacy programs for students identified with dyslexia or at risk for dyslexia. Structured literacy is an evidence-based approach to reading instruction that is explicitly and systematically taught and focuses on the following components:

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: The study of speech sounds and how they are organized in language. Instruction includes phonemic awareness (recognizing and manipulating individual sounds) and phonics (connecting sounds to letters).
  • Sound-Symbol Association: Explicit teaching of the relationships between letters and sounds (graphemes and phonemes).
  • Syllable Instruction: Understanding the different types of syllables and how they are combined to form words.
  • Morphology: The study of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, roots) and how they contribute to the meaning of words.
  • Syntax: Understanding the rules that govern sentence structure.
  • Semantics: Understanding the meaning of words and sentences.

AISD-Approved Structured Literacy Programs:

AISD utilizes programs that are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook guidelines. While specific programs may vary by campus based on student needs and staff training, they all adhere to the core principles of structured literacy. Common programs used include (but are not limited to):

  • Take Flight: A comprehensive intervention program developed by the Luke Waites Center for Dyslexia at Texas Scottish Rite Hospital for Children.
  • Neuhaus Basic Language Skills: A structured literacy program that focuses on phonological awareness, phonics, and reading comprehension.
  • Reading by Design Another structured literacy program.

These programs are implemented by trained educators who have received specialized training in dyslexia intervention. They provide explicit, systematic, and multisensory instruction to help students develop essential reading skills.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support through either a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's specific needs and the impact of dyslexia on their academic performance.

504 Plan:

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Focus: Provides accommodations and modifications to ensure equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Services: Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, access to assistive technology, and modified assignments.
  • Less Intensive Support: Generally provides less intensive and specialized support compared to an IEP.

IEP (Individualized Education Program):

  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability (such as dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: Provides specialized instruction and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: May include specialized reading instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and counseling, in addition to accommodations.
  • More Intensive Support: Offers more intensive and individualized support compared to a 504 plan. Requires formal evaluations and eligibility determination by an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee.

Key Differences:

  • Level of Support: IEPs provide more intensive and specialized support than 504 plans.
  • Eligibility Criteria: IEP eligibility requires a more significant impact on educational performance than 504 eligibility.
  • Services Provided: IEPs include specialized instruction, while 504 plans primarily focus on accommodations.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), while 504 plans are governed by Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act.

The decision of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the school's evaluation team. Parents are an integral part of this process.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments made to the way a student learns that do not change the content of what is being taught. These accommodations help students with dyslexia access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. Amarillo ISD provides a range of accommodations to students with dyslexia, based on their individual needs. These accommodations may be implemented through a 504 plan or an IEP.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Allowing additional time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location in the classroom that minimizes distractions and maximizes their ability to focus.
  • Read Aloud: Providing access to text read aloud, either by a human reader or assistive technology.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that converts written text into spoken words.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to books in audio format.
  • Multi-Sensory Instruction: Incorporating visual, auditory, and kinesthetic learning strategies.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Using visual aids to organize information and ideas.
  • Word Prediction Software: Using software that suggests words as the student types.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Using software that converts spoken words into written text.
  • Reduced Workload: Modifying assignments to reduce the amount of work required.
  • Chunking: Breaking down large assignments into smaller, more manageable tasks.
  • Modified Testing Format: Providing alternative testing formats, such as oral exams or multiple-choice instead of essay questions.
  • Access to Assistive Technology: Providing access to tools that can help students with reading and writing, such as tablets, computers, and specialized software.
  • Copy of Notes: Providing a copy of teacher's notes.
  • Clearly Defined Expectations: Making assignment expectations explicit and clear.

This list is not exhaustive, and the specific accommodations provided to a student will depend on their individual needs and the recommendations of the school's evaluation team.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia have the right to request an evaluation from Amarillo ISD. The evaluation process is designed to determine whether a student meets the criteria for dyslexia and to identify the student's specific learning needs.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the School: The first step is to contact your child's teacher, counselor, or principal at their school. Discuss your concerns and request information about the evaluation process.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for an evaluation to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth.
    • Your contact information.
    • A description of your concerns about your child's reading skills.
    • Any relevant information about your child's academic history.
  3. Evaluation Process: Once the school receives your written request, they will begin the evaluation process. This process may include:
    • Review of your child's academic records.
    • Observations of your child in the classroom.
    • Administration of standardized assessments to measure reading skills, phonological awareness, and other relevant areas.
  4. ARD Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting will be held to discuss the results. The ARD committee includes parents, teachers, administrators, and other professionals who have knowledge of the student.
  5. Eligibility Determination: The ARD committee will determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for dyslexia and whether they require special education services.
  6. IEP Development (if eligible): If your child is found eligible for special education services, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) that outlines the specific supports and services your child will receive.

AISD is committed to providing timely and thorough evaluations to students who may have dyslexia. Parents have the right to be involved in every step of the evaluation process.

Important Note: This guide provides general information about dyslexia services in Amarillo ISD. Specific procedures and programs may vary by campus. Contact your child's school for more detailed information.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
