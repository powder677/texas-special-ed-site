What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

Welcome to the world of special education in Amarillo ISD! If your child has been identified as potentially needing special education services, you will be invited to attend what is called an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is essentially the cornerstone of your child's special education journey. It is a legally mandated meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), a federal law, and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, which ensures that children with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) tailored to their individual needs.

The main purpose of the ARD meeting is to develop, review, and, when appropriate, revise your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance, measurable annual goals, the specific special education and related services they will receive, the accommodations and modifications they need to access the general education curriculum, and the setting where these services will be provided. The ARD committee is responsible for making these critical decisions.

So, who attends an ARD meeting? The core members of the ARD committee include you, the parent or legal guardian; a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of specially designed instruction (this is often a special education administrator or principal); one or more of your child's teachers, including a general education teacher if your child participates in general education; and a special education teacher. Depending on your child's needs, other individuals may also attend, such as related service providers (speech therapists, occupational therapists, counselors, etc.), diagnosticians who conducted assessments, and even your child, if appropriate. You, as the parent, are a crucial and equal member of this team. Your insights into your child's strengths, weaknesses, interests, and needs are invaluable to the ARD process.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

Before an ARD meeting takes place, Amarillo ISD is required to provide you with written notice at least five calendar days in advance. This notice is not just a friendly reminder; it's a legal right designed to give you ample time to prepare for the meeting. This notice must include several important pieces of information. First, it must clearly state the purpose of the meeting - whether it's an initial ARD to determine eligibility for special education, an annual review to update the IEP, or a meeting to address a specific concern. Second, the notice must list the date, time, and location of the meeting. Third, it must inform you who will be attending the meeting, including their roles. This allows you to anticipate the perspectives and expertise that will be represented at the table. Finally, the notice must explain your rights as a parent in the ARD process. This ensures you are aware of your ability to participate fully and advocate for your child's needs.

It's important to understand that the five-day notice is a minimum requirement. The school cannot hold an ARD meeting without providing you with this advance notice, unless you agree to waive this right. Sometimes, it may be beneficial to meet sooner, for example, if a pressing issue needs to be addressed quickly or if you have limited availability. In such cases, you can provide written consent to waive the five-day notice requirement and proceed with the meeting at an earlier date. However, never feel pressured to waive your right to the notice. Use the time to gather your thoughts, collect relevant information, and prepare any questions or concerns you may have.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your ARD meeting is key to ensuring your child's needs are fully addressed. Think of yourself as your child's advocate and gather all relevant information that will help the ARD committee make informed decisions. Here's a detailed list of items you might consider bringing:

First, bring copies of any prior evaluations or assessments conducted on your child, whether they were performed by the school district or by outside professionals. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language evaluations, occupational therapy evaluations, and any other relevant reports. These documents provide valuable data about your child's strengths and areas where they need support.

Second, gather any letters or reports from your child's doctors, therapists, or other medical professionals. These reports can provide important medical information that may impact your child's educational needs. For example, if your child has a medical condition that affects their attention or behavior, a doctor's letter explaining the condition and its impact can be very helpful.

Third, bring copies of your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, attendance records, and any samples of your child's work. These records provide a picture of your child's academic performance and behavior in the school setting.

Fourth, and perhaps most importantly, bring a written list of your specific concerns and questions. This list will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all of your concerns are addressed. Think about what you want your child to achieve in the coming year and what supports they need to reach those goals. Write down specific examples of challenges your child faces and any strategies you have found helpful at home.

Fifth, consider bringing a trusted advocate or support person with you. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support and help you navigate the ARD process. Having someone else present can help you stay calm and focused, remember important details, and ask clarifying questions.

Finally, in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided you give the school district reasonable notice that you will be doing so. A voice recorder can be a valuable tool for documenting the meeting and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the discussions and decisions made. This can be particularly helpful if you need to refer back to the meeting later or if there are any disagreements about what was said.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

The ARD process is designed to be collaborative, and sometimes, making the best decisions for your child requires more time and information. That's where the 10-day recess rule comes in. This rule states that any member of the ARD committee, including you, the parent, has the right to request a recess of up to 10 school days to gather additional information or consult with an advocate before making final decisions about the IEP.

Why might you invoke the 10-day recess rule? Perhaps you feel that the ARD committee has not adequately considered your concerns, or maybe you need more time to review the proposed IEP and discuss it with your family or an advocate. You might also need to gather additional information, such as an independent evaluation or input from a specialist. Whatever the reason, the 10-day recess rule is there to protect your right to participate meaningfully in the ARD process.

How do you invoke the 10-day recess rule? Simply state that you would like to recess the ARD meeting for up to 10 school days to gather more information or consult with an advocate. It is best to state this clearly and assertively. You do not need to provide a detailed explanation for your request. Once you make the request, the ARD committee must grant it. The meeting will be paused, and you will have up to 10 school days to gather the information you need. When the recess is over, the ARD committee will reconvene to continue the discussion and make final decisions about the IEP.

Understanding the IEP Document

The Individualized Education Program (IEP) is the heart of your child's special education experience. It's a legally binding document that outlines the specific supports and services your child will receive to make progress in school. Understanding the different components of the IEP is crucial to advocating for your child's needs.

The IEP begins with a section called "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance." This section describes your child's current academic skills, such as reading, writing, and math, as well as their functional skills, such as communication, social skills, and self-help skills. It should include information from a variety of sources, including assessments, observations, and your input as a parent.

Next, the IEP outlines measurable annual goals. These goals describe what your child is expected to achieve in each area of need within a year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). They should also be aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS), the state's curriculum standards.

The IEP also specifies the special education and related services your child will receive. Special education services are specially designed instruction tailored to meet your child's individual needs. Related services are additional supports that help your child benefit from special education, such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and assistive technology.

The IEP also addresses placement, which refers to the setting where your child will receive their special education services. The law requires that students with disabilities be educated in the least restrictive environment (LRE), meaning that they should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. The IEP will specify the percentage of time your child will spend in general education and in special education settings.

Finally, the IEP includes a list of accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as providing extra time on tests or allowing them to use a calculator. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as reducing the number of assignments or modifying the curriculum. It is important to remember that you, as a parent, do not have to sign the IEP on the day of the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the IEP home, review it carefully, and seek input from others before making a decision.

When You Disagree With the Team

It's important to remember that you are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. Sometimes, you may disagree with the team's recommendations or decisions. Understanding your rights in these situations is crucial.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. If you do not agree with the ARD committee's decision that your child needs special education services, you can refuse to sign the consent form. In this case, the school district cannot provide special education services to your child.

Even if you agree with the initial placement, you may still disagree with specific elements of the IEP. You have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you do not agree with the goals, services, placement, or any other aspect of the document. However, it's important to understand that if you refuse to sign the IEP, the school district may not be able to implement the plan as written. This can lead to disagreements about what services your child will receive.

Another important right is the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you disagree with the school district's evaluation of your child, you can request an IEE. The school district must either pay for the IEE or demonstrate at a due process hearing that its evaluation was appropriate.

Finally, even if you sign the IEP, you have the right to write "Parent Disagrees" next to your signature. This indicates that you do not agree with some or all of the IEP. Writing "Parent Disagrees" does not prevent the school district from implementing the IEP, but it does preserve your right to pursue further action if you are not satisfied with the outcome.

Escalation Options

If you have exhausted all efforts to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee and you are still not satisfied with your child's special education program, you have several formal options for escalating your concerns. These options are governed by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) of 2004 and the Texas Administrative Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89, which is known as the Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process.

The first option is to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free and relatively informal process. You can submit a written complaint to TEA alleging that the school district has violated IDEA or state special education laws. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days. This option is often the quickest way to resolve disputes.

The second option is mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is also free through TEA. A trained mediator will facilitate a discussion between you and the school district representatives to help you understand each other's perspectives and find common ground.

The third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal and adversarial process. A Due Process Hearing is like a court trial, where you and the school district present evidence and witnesses to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will issue a written decision that is binding on both parties. Due Process Hearings can be time-consuming and expensive, and it is advisable to seek legal counsel before pursuing this option.

Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR is a federal agency that investigates complaints of discrimination based on disability. If you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child because of their disability, you can file a complaint with OCR. OCR will investigate your complaint and may order the school district to take corrective action if it finds evidence of discrimination.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
