The 10-Day Suspension Rule: Know Your Rights!

Amarillo ISD, like all Texas school districts, has policies regarding student suspension. A key element to understand is the impact of the 10-day suspension rule, particularly for students with disabilities (covered under IDEA - the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act).

What is it? This rule states that a student cannot be suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a single school year without triggering specific protections and procedures, especially if the student has an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or a 504 plan.

Why is it important? Once a student with an IEP or 504 plan reaches this 10-day threshold of suspension, the school district MUST conduct a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). This is a crucial safeguard designed to protect students with disabilities from being unfairly punished for behaviors that are related to their disability.

What to do:

  • Keep meticulous records: Track every day your child is suspended, in-school suspension (ISS), or otherwise removed from their regular educational setting. Don't rely on the school to do this accurately - errors can happen.
  • Demand written documentation: Obtain written notification for every suspension or removal, specifying the reason for the action and the number of days of suspension.
  • If your child is nearing 10 days: Be proactive. Contact the school principal, special education director, and your child's case manager (for IEP students) or 504 coordinator (for 504 students). Express your concern and insist that they understand the implications of reaching the 10-day threshold.
  • If the 10-day limit is reached: IMMEDIATELY demand a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). Don't wait for the school to initiate it; they might not.

What is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)? Why is it Critical?

The Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is a critical process mandated by IDEA to determine if a student's behavior that led to disciplinary action was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability. It also examines whether the behavior was a direct result of the school district's failure to implement the student's IEP.

Who participates? The MDR team typically includes:

  • The parent(s) or guardian(s)
  • Relevant school personnel (e.g., special education teacher, general education teacher, principal, school psychologist)
  • The student (when appropriate)

What happens during an MDR? The team will review:

  • The student's IEP or 504 plan
  • All relevant information in the student's file (e.g., teacher observations, disciplinary records, medical records, evaluations)
  • Any information provided by the parent(s)

The Outcome: The MDR team must determine whether the behavior was a manifestation of the student's disability. There are two possible outcomes:

  • Manifestation: If the team determines that the behavior was a manifestation of the disability, the student cannot be suspended for more than 10 days (total) for that behavior. The IEP team MUST conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA), if one hasn't been done already, and create or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The student must be returned to their original placement, unless the parents and the school agree to a change of placement as part of the BIP.
  • Not a Manifestation: If the team determines that the behavior was not a manifestation of the disability, the student can be disciplined in the same manner as students without disabilities. However, the school MUST continue to provide special education services during the period of suspension, so the student can continue to progress in the general education curriculum and advance toward their IEP goals.

CRITICAL: Parents have the right to challenge the MDR decision through due process. If you disagree with the outcome, file a written complaint immediately. Don't delay. Seek legal counsel.

What to do:

  • Prepare thoroughly: Before the MDR meeting, gather all relevant information about your child's disability and how it impacts their behavior. Write down specific examples of how the disability manifests and how it might have contributed to the behavior in question.
  • Bring documentation: Bring any relevant medical records, evaluations, or other documents that support your case.
  • Advocate fiercely: Don't be intimidated by school personnel. Be prepared to advocate for your child's rights and explain why you believe the behavior is related to their disability.
  • Document everything: Keep detailed notes of the meeting, including who was present, what was discussed, and what decisions were made. Request a copy of the MDR report.
  • If you disagree: If you disagree with the MDR outcome, put your objections in writing immediately and state that you intend to pursue due process.

Change of Placement: A Red Flag!

A "Change of Placement" occurs when a student with a disability is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when there is a pattern of removals that constitute a change of placement. This can include suspension, expulsion, or transfer to a different school or program.

Why is it important? A Change of Placement triggers additional procedural safeguards under IDEA. The school district must provide you with written notice of the proposed change and explain your rights. You also have the right to challenge the change of placement through due process.

Examples of what may constitute a Change of Placement, even if not explicitly called one:

  • Requiring a student to attend a Disciplinary Alternative Education Program (DAEP)
  • Removing a student from their general education classroom and placing them in a separate special education classroom for the majority of the day.
  • Transferring a student to a different school that is not their neighborhood school.

What to do:

  • Be vigilant: Pay close attention to any changes in your child's educational setting, even seemingly minor ones.
  • Demand written notice: If the school proposes a change of placement, demand written notice that clearly explains the reasons for the change and your rights.
  • Request an IEP meeting: Request an IEP meeting to discuss the proposed change of placement and explore alternative options.
  • Consider an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child's needs, you have the right to request an IEE at public expense (under certain circumstances).
  • Seek legal counsel: Changes of placement can have a significant impact on a student's education. Seek legal counsel to understand your rights and options.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance: Your First Line of Defense

Amarillo ISD has a grievance policy that allows parents to formally complain about school-related issues, including disciplinary actions. Filing a Level 1 grievance is often the first step in resolving a dispute.

What is it? A Level 1 grievance is typically filed with the school principal or other designated administrator. It should be a written complaint that clearly states the issue, the specific actions or decisions you are grieving, and the outcome you are seeking.

Why is it important? Filing a grievance creates a formal record of your concerns and requires the school district to respond. It can also be a necessary prerequisite for pursuing further legal action.

How to file a Level 1 Grievance:

  • Obtain the grievance policy: Request a copy of Amarillo ISD's grievance policy from the school or district office. This policy will outline the specific procedures and timelines for filing a grievance.
  • Write a clear and concise complaint: Your grievance should include the following information:
    • Your child's name and student ID number
    • The date of the incident or decision you are grieving
    • A detailed description of the issue
    • The specific policy or procedure that you believe was violated
    • The outcome you are seeking (e.g., reversal of the suspension, reinstatement of services)
  • Gather supporting documentation: Include any relevant documents that support your complaint, such as suspension notices, emails, or witness statements.
  • Submit the grievance: Submit the grievance to the appropriate administrator, as specified in the grievance policy. Keep a copy for your records.
  • Track the timeline: The grievance policy will specify the timeline for the school district to respond to your grievance. Make sure to track the timeline and follow up if you do not receive a response within the allotted time.

TEA State Complaint: Escalating the Issue

If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the grievance process, or if you believe the school district has violated IDEA, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What is it? A TEA complaint is a formal written complaint that alleges a violation of federal or state special education laws. It must be filed within one year of the alleged violation.

Why is it important? TEA has the authority to investigate complaints and order school districts to take corrective action if they are found to be in violation of the law.

How to file a TEA State Complaint:

  • Obtain the complaint form: You can download the complaint form from the TEA website.
  • Provide detailed information: The complaint form requires you to provide detailed information about the alleged violation, including:
    • Your child's name and student ID number
    • The name of the school district
    • A description of the alleged violation
    • The specific laws or regulations that you believe were violated
    • Supporting documentation
  • Submit the complaint: Submit the completed complaint form to TEA, along with any supporting documentation.
  • Cooperate with the investigation: TEA will investigate your complaint and may contact you for additional information. Cooperate fully with the investigation.

Due Process: Your Ultimate Legal Recourse

Due process is the legal right to challenge the school district's decisions regarding your child's special education services. It is a formal legal procedure that can be costly and time-consuming, but it is often necessary to protect your child's rights.

When to consider due process: You may consider due process if you disagree with the school district's decisions regarding:

  • Your child's eligibility for special education
  • Your child's IEP
  • Your child's placement
  • Disciplinary actions, including suspensions and expulsions

How to initiate due process:

  • File a due process complaint: To initiate due process, you must file a written complaint with TEA. The complaint must contain specific information about the issues you are disputing and the relief you are seeking.
  • Mediation: TEA will offer mediation to attempt to resolve the dispute informally. Mediation is a voluntary process, but it can be a helpful way to reach a settlement.
  • Due process hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful, a due process hearing will be held before an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will hear evidence from both sides and issue a written decision.
  • Appeals: You have the right to appeal the hearing officer's decision to state or federal court.

CRITICAL: Due process is a complex legal process. It is essential to seek legal counsel from an attorney specializing in special education law. Deadlines are strict. Act immediately if you believe your child's rights are being violated. Time is NOT on your side.
