How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Amarillo ISD

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for a child suspected of having a disability within Amarillo Independent School District (AISD). Understanding your rights under Texas law is crucial to advocating for your child's needs. This guide outlines the process, legal timelines, required evaluation components, district obligations, and potential next steps.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is to submit a written request to AISD. This request should be clear, concise, and contain specific information about your concerns regarding your child's educational performance.

Who to Send the Request To: Ideally, the request should be sent to the principal of your child's school. Sending a copy to the school's Special Education Director and your child's teacher is also recommended. This ensures that multiple parties are aware of your request and helps avoid delays.

Content of the Request: Your written request should include the following elements:

  • Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: Ensure accurate identification of your child.
  • School and Grade Level: Clearly state the school your child attends and their current grade.
  • Your Contact Information: Provide your full name, address, phone number, and email address.
  • Specific Concerns: This is the most important part of the request. Clearly articulate your specific concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental performance. Avoid vague statements like "My child is struggling." Instead, provide specific examples. For example:
    • "My child is significantly behind in reading comprehension and is unable to answer questions about stories read aloud, despite being in the third grade."
    • "My child has difficulty focusing in class, frequently gets out of their seat, and has received numerous warnings for disruptive behavior."
    • "My child struggles with social interactions and has difficulty understanding social cues, leading to isolation and anxiety."
    • "My child has significant difficulty with written expression and struggles to organize their thoughts on paper, even with modifications."
  • Supporting Documentation (Optional but Recommended): Include any relevant documentation that supports your concerns. This may include:
    • Report cards
    • Work samples
    • Medical records (if applicable)
    • Notes from teachers or other professionals
    • Results from private evaluations (if applicable)
  • Statement of Suspected Disability: While not required, explicitly stating that you suspect your child has a disability that may require special education services can strengthen your request. For example: "Based on these concerns, I suspect my child may have a learning disability and requires a full and individual initial evaluation to determine eligibility for special education services."
  • Request for Evaluation: Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation to determine if your child is eligible for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.
  • Date and Signature: Date and sign the request.

Sample Language:

"[Date]
Principal [Principal's Name]
[School Name]
[School Address]
Re: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Full Name], DOB: [Child's Date of Birth]
Dear Principal [Principal's Name],
I am writing to request a full and individual initial evaluation for my child, [Child's Full Name], who is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. I have significant concerns regarding [Child's Name]'s [reading/math/behavior/etc.] performance. Specifically, [Detailed explanation of your concerns with specific examples].
[Optional: "I am attaching [Report cards/Work samples/Medical records/etc.] to further illustrate my concerns."]
Based on these concerns, I suspect that [Child's Name] may have a disability that requires special education services. I request that AISD conduct a comprehensive evaluation to determine [Child's Name]'s eligibility for special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law.
Thank you for your time and attention to this important matter. I look forward to hearing from you soon.
Sincerely,
[Your Full Name]
[Your Address]
[Your Phone Number]
[Your Email Address]"

Keep a Copy: Always keep a copy of your written request for your records. Consider sending the request via certified mail with return receipt requested to provide proof of delivery.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates specific timelines for the special education evaluation process. Once AISD receives your written request, they have a strict 45 school day timeline to complete the evaluation and determine your child's eligibility for special education services.

School Days vs. Calendar Days: It's crucial to understand that the 45-day timeline refers to school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks (such as summer break or winter break) are not counted as part of the 45-day timeline. If your request is submitted close to or during a school break, the timeline will be extended accordingly.

Initial Actions: Within a reasonable timeframe after receiving your request (ideally within a week or two), the school should convene an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss your request and determine whether to proceed with an evaluation. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of this ARD committee.

Decision to Evaluate: The ARD committee will review the information you provided in your request and other relevant data (such as classroom observations, teacher input, and previous interventions) to decide whether there is reason to suspect that your child has a disability and needs special education services. If the ARD committee decides not to evaluate, they must provide you with a written explanation of their decision, including the reasons for the refusal and the data they considered. You have the right to disagree with this decision and pursue further action (see the "What If They Refuse" section below).

Parent Consent: If the ARD committee decides to proceed with an evaluation, they must obtain your written consent before conducting any testing. The school should provide you with a written notice explaining the proposed evaluation plan, including the specific assessments that will be administered. You have the right to review this plan and ask questions before providing your consent. You can consent to some parts of the evaluation and refuse others. Make sure your written consent clearly specifies which assessments you are approving.

Evaluation Completion: Once you provide your written consent, the school has the remainder of the 45 school days to complete the evaluation, analyze the data, and prepare a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) report.

ARD Meeting to Discuss Results: After the evaluation is completed, the ARD committee must meet again to discuss the results of the FIE and determine whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services. You, as the parent, are a critical member of this ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in the discussion and decision-making process.

Documentation is Key: Keep detailed records of all communication with the school, including dates of meetings, phone calls, and emails. This documentation will be essential if you need to advocate for your child's rights later in the process.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine whether a child has a disability and, if so, what special education and related services are needed. Texas law requires the FIE to cover all areas of suspected disability.

Areas of Assessment: The specific areas that must be assessed will vary depending on your child's individual needs and the concerns you raised in your initial request. However, common areas of assessment include:

  • Cognitive Abilities: This assesses your child's intellectual functioning and learning potential using standardized tests like the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC).
  • Academic Achievement: This evaluates your child's performance in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas using standardized achievement tests.
  • Communication Skills: This assesses your child's receptive and expressive language skills, including articulation, fluency, and vocabulary.
  • Adaptive Behavior: This evaluates your child's ability to function independently in everyday life, including self-care skills, social skills, and community living skills.
  • Motor Skills: This assesses your child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, using scissors).
  • Social and Emotional Functioning: This evaluates your child's social skills, emotional regulation, and mental health. This may include observations, interviews, and standardized assessments of anxiety, depression, or other emotional or behavioral concerns.
  • Vision and Hearing: Screening for vision and hearing impairments is typically included to rule out these factors as contributing to learning difficulties.
  • Assistive Technology Needs: The evaluation should consider whether your child requires assistive technology to access the curriculum and participate in educational activities.
  • Other Areas as Needed: Depending on the specific concerns, the evaluation may also include assessments in other areas, such as sensory processing, attention, or behavior.

Evaluation Personnel: The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in administering and interpreting the specific assessments used. This may include:

  • Educational Diagnostician: A certified professional who specializes in assessing learning disabilities and other educational needs.
  • School Psychologist: A licensed professional who specializes in assessing cognitive, emotional, and behavioral functioning.
  • Speech-Language Pathologist: A licensed professional who specializes in assessing and treating communication disorders.
  • Occupational Therapist: A licensed professional who specializes in assessing and treating motor skills and sensory processing difficulties.
  • Physical Therapist: A licensed professional who specializes in assessing and treating gross motor skills.
  • Other Specialists: Depending on the child's needs, other specialists, such as medical doctors or psychiatrists, may be involved in the evaluation process.

Parent Input: The FIE must consider information provided by the parent, including their observations, concerns, and perspectives on their child's strengths and weaknesses. The school should actively solicit your input throughout the evaluation process.

FIE Report: The FIE report must be comprehensive and clearly describe the assessments used, the results obtained, and the evaluator's conclusions and recommendations. You have the right to receive a copy of the FIE report in a timely manner.

Child Find Obligations

Amarillo ISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and state law to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within its boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This obligation is known as "Child Find."

Who is Covered: Child Find applies to all children from birth to age 21 who are suspected of having a disability, including those who are:

  • Attending public schools
  • Attending private schools
  • Homeschooled
  • Living in shelters or other temporary housing
  • Migrant children

How Child Find Works: AISD employs various methods to fulfill its Child Find obligations, including:

  • Public Awareness Campaigns: Disseminating information about special education services through brochures, websites, and community events.
  • Screening Programs: Conducting screenings of preschool children to identify potential developmental delays.
  • Referral Systems: Establishing procedures for teachers, parents, and other professionals to refer children for evaluation.
  • Collaboration with Community Agencies: Working with healthcare providers, social service agencies, and other community organizations to identify children with disabilities.

Your Role in Child Find: If you suspect that your child has a disability, you have the right to contact AISD and request an evaluation, regardless of whether your child is enrolled in public school. The district cannot deny your request simply because your child is not attending a public school or because they have not previously been identified as having a disability.

Private School Students: If your child attends a private school within AISD boundaries, they are still entitled to a Child Find evaluation. If the evaluation determines that your child has a disability, AISD is required to offer a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). However, the specific services provided to private school students may differ from those provided to public school students. AISD is only required to provide a "fair share" of its federal special education funds to private school students. This often means that the services offered to private school students are limited.

What If They Refuse

AISD may refuse your request for a special education evaluation. Understanding your rights and options if this occurs is crucial.

Written Explanation Required: If AISD refuses to evaluate your child, they are legally required to provide you with a written explanation of their decision. This explanation must include:

  • The reasons for the refusal
  • The data considered in making the decision
  • A description of any other options considered
  • Information about your procedural safeguards, including your right to challenge the decision

Challenging the Refusal: You have several options for challenging AISD's refusal to evaluate your child:

  • Request an Informal Meeting: First, consider requesting an informal meeting with the school's special education staff to discuss your concerns and try to resolve the issue. This may involve presenting additional information or clarifying your request.
  • Request a Formal ARD Meeting: If an informal meeting is unsuccessful, request a formal ARD committee meeting to discuss the refusal to evaluate. Again, clearly articulate your concerns and present any supporting documentation.
  • Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and AISD reach an agreement. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) offers mediation services free of charge.
  • Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding in which an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision about the dispute. This is a more adversarial process and may require legal representation. You have the right to legal representation at your own expense.
  • Filing a Complaint with TEA: You can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe that AISD has violated state or federal special education laws. TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a decision.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) of your child at public expense if you disagree with the evaluation conducted by AISD. However, AISD can request a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. If the hearing officer determines that AISD's evaluation was appropriate, you may still obtain an IEE, but you will have to pay for it yourself. The results of an IEE must be considered by the ARD committee.

Document Everything: As always, it is essential to document all communication with the school and keep copies of all relevant documents. This documentation will be crucial if you need to pursue any of the above options.

After the Evaluation

Following the completion of the FIE, the ARD committee will meet to discuss the results and determine your child's eligibility for special education services.

Eligibility Determination: To be eligible for special education services in Texas, a child must meet two criteria:

  • Have a Disability: The child must have one or more of the disabilities listed in the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law, such as autism, intellectual disability, learning disability, emotional disturbance, speech impairment, or other health impairment.
  • Need Special Education Services: The disability must adversely affect the child's educational performance to the extent that they require specially designed instruction to make progress in the general education curriculum.

If Eligible: If the ARD committee determines that your child is eligible for special education services, the committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written plan that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services and supports they will receive to meet those goals. You are an active member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in the development of the IEP.

If Not Eligible: If the ARD committee determines that your child is not eligible for special education services, they must provide you with a written explanation of their decision. You have the right to disagree with this decision and pursue further action, as outlined in the "What If They Refuse" section above. Consider requesting information about other support services that may be available to your child, such as Section 504 accommodations or Response to Intervention (RTI) services.

Ongoing Monitoring and Review: Once an IEP is in place, the ARD committee must meet at least annually to review the IEP and make any necessary revisions. You have the right to request an ARD committee meeting at any time if you have concerns about your child's progress or the effectiveness of the IEP.

Advocacy: Advocating for your child's needs can be challenging. Consider seeking support from parent advocacy organizations or legal aid services if you need assistance navigating the special education process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
