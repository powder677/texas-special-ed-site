What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

1. What Is an ARD Meeting?

If your child has been identified as having a disability and needs special education services in Texarkana ISD, you will be invited to attend an ARD meeting. ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. This meeting is essential to the process of providing your child with a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE), as mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, Chapter 29. Think of it as the core of your child's special education journey.

The purpose of the ARD meeting is to bring together a team of people who know your child and can contribute to developing an Individualized Education Program, or IEP. The IEP is a written document outlining your child's specific educational needs, goals, and the services the school will provide to help them succeed. The ARD meeting isn't just a formality; it's where crucial decisions about your child's education are made.

Who attends this important meeting? The ARD committee is composed of several key individuals. You, as the parent, are a vital member of the team. You know your child better than anyone, and your input is highly valued. Other required members include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education (often an administrator or special education director), your child's special education teacher, and a general education teacher (if your child is participating in general education). The school may also invite other professionals, such as diagnosticians, therapists (speech, occupational, physical), counselors, or any other individual who can provide expertise related to your child's needs. Finally, depending on your child's age and ability, your child may also be invited to participate in the ARD meeting, especially as they get older.

It's important to remember that these meetings are not optional. IDEA and Texas law require them to ensure that children with disabilities receive the specialized education and support they need to thrive. You have a right to be an active and informed participant in every stage of the ARD process. This guide is intended to empower you with the knowledge you need to effectively advocate for your child's best interests.

2. Your 5-Day Notice Right

Texarkana ISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is your right, and it's designed to give you ample time to prepare. The purpose of this notice is to ensure that you are aware of the meeting and have the opportunity to organize your thoughts, gather relevant information, and arrange for any support you might need.

The written notice must include specific information. It will tell you the purpose of the meeting (for example, to develop a new IEP, review an existing IEP, or discuss a change in placement). It will also state the date, time, and location of the meeting. Critically, the notice will list the names and titles of the individuals who are expected to attend the meeting. This allows you to anticipate who will be present and consider if you would like to invite anyone else.

Finally, the notice must inform you of your right to invite individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child. This could include a therapist, a doctor, a family member, or a friend who understands your child's needs and can offer valuable insights. You should inform the school district in advance if you plan to bring someone to ensure there is adequate space and that the team is aware of their presence. You have the right to choose who attends to support you.

There may be situations where you need to meet sooner than the five-day notice allows. In these cases, you can waive your right to the five-day notice in writing. This means you agree to hold the meeting even though you haven't had the full five days to prepare. Waiving the notice is entirely your choice, and you should only do so if you feel comfortable and prepared to proceed with the meeting. Never feel pressured to waive your rights if you need more time.

3. What to Bring to Your ARD

Coming prepared to your child's ARD meeting is crucial for ensuring their needs are adequately addressed. Here's a detailed checklist of what you should consider bringing:

First, gather any prior evaluations, reports, or assessments related to your child's disability. This includes evaluations from doctors, therapists, educational diagnosticians, or any other professionals who have worked with your child. These documents provide valuable data about your child's strengths and weaknesses, which can help inform the IEP goals and services. The more information you bring, the better the team can understand your child's individual needs.

Next, bring any relevant medical documentation, such as letters from your child's doctor or specialist. These letters can provide important medical information that supports your child's educational needs. For example, if your child has a medical condition that affects their ability to learn, a letter from their doctor can help explain the condition and how it impacts their education.

Assemble your child's school records, including report cards, progress reports, and any notes or communication from teachers. These records offer a snapshot of your child's academic performance and behavior in the classroom. They can also highlight areas where your child is excelling or struggling.

Prepare a written list of your specific concerns and goals for your child's education. Think about what you want your child to achieve in the coming year and what supports they need to reach those goals. Writing down your concerns beforehand will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all your questions are addressed.

Don't hesitate to bring a trusted advocate or support person with you. This could be a friend, family member, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, take notes, and help you articulate your concerns. Having someone by your side can be incredibly helpful, especially if you feel overwhelmed or intimidated by the ARD process. It's good to have another set of ears to ensure information is fully captured.

Finally, be aware that recording the ARD meeting is legal in Texas, but you must inform the ARD committee of your intention to record the meeting. This can be a useful tool for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions made. Be sure to bring a voice recorder and let the team know you plan to use it at the beginning of the meeting.

4. The 10-Day Recess Rule

The "10-Day Recess Rule" is a powerful tool available to every member of the ARD committee, including you, the parent. It allows for a temporary pause in the ARD process to gather more information, seek additional opinions, or simply take time to process the decisions being made. Any ARD team member, including the parent, can invoke it if they feel the need. This is your right!

If you feel that you need more time to consider the IEP being proposed, to consult with an advocate, or to obtain additional information from outside sources, you can request a recess of up to 10 school days. This recess allows you to step back from the meeting, gather your thoughts, and make a more informed decision about your child's education. During this time, no changes to your child's services can be implemented unless you give written consent.

To invoke the 10-Day Recess Rule, simply state your request during the ARD meeting. You don't need to provide a detailed explanation, although you can if you feel it's necessary. A simple statement like, "I would like to request a 10-day recess to further consider the IEP," is sufficient. The team is then obligated to grant your request. It is wise to make your request before decisions are finalized on the IEP paperwork.

The 10 school days begin the day after the ARD meeting where the recess was requested. During this time, you can use the opportunity to contact experts, review documents, or simply reflect on the decisions being made. After the recess period, the ARD team will reconvene to continue the discussion and finalize the IEP. Remember, this recess is designed to empower you to make the best possible decisions for your child.

5. Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the cornerstone of your child's special education. It is a legally binding document that outlines your child's unique educational needs, goals, and the specific services the school will provide to help them achieve those goals. The ARD meeting is where the content of the IEP is determined.

Several key components are decided at the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP. First, the team will discuss your child's present levels of academic achievement and functional performance. This section describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in all relevant areas, such as reading, writing, math, social skills, and behavior. The present levels provide a baseline for measuring progress and setting appropriate goals.

Next, the team will develop annual goals. These are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. The goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART goals). They should also be aligned with your child's present levels and address their identified needs. Goals will cover both academic and functional areas.

The IEP will also specify the special education and related services your child will receive. This may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, and other supports. The services section will describe the frequency, duration, and location of these services. Accommodations and modifications are another crucial aspect of the IEP. Accommodations are changes to how your child learns, such as extended time on tests or preferential seating. Modifications are changes to what your child learns, such as simplified assignments or reduced workload. These accommodations and modifications are designed to help your child access the curriculum and demonstrate their learning.

Finally, the IEP will determine your child's placement. This refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. Placement options range from the general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting, such as a resource room or a self-contained classroom. The goal is to find the least restrictive environment (LRE) where your child can make meaningful progress.

It is extremely important to know that you do not have to sign the IEP at the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and consider whether you agree with the decisions made. You can always reconvene the ARD team if you have questions or concerns.

6. When You Disagree With the Team

Disagreements during the ARD process are not uncommon. You, as the parent, are an equal member of the ARD committee, and your voice matters. If you disagree with the team's recommendations, it's important to understand your rights and options.

First, you have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you don't agree with the school's evaluation or the proposed services, you can decline to allow your child to receive special education at this time. If you refuse initial consent, the school district cannot force your child to receive special education services. However, they can pursue mediation or due process to try and override your refusal, but this is a more involved process.

Even if you agree with initial placement, you can still disagree with specific elements of the IEP. For example, you might disagree with the proposed goals, services, or placement. In these situations, you have the right to refuse to sign the IEP. Refusing to sign does not mean that the IEP cannot be implemented, but it does preserve your right to challenge the IEP later.

You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. The results of the IEE can provide valuable information to the ARD team and help resolve disagreements. The district may have specific requirements for who can conduct the IEE, but must consider your request.

Regardless of whether you sign the IEP or not, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the document. This statement clearly indicates that you do not agree with some or all of the IEP's contents. Writing "parent disagrees" can be a useful way to protect your rights and document your concerns.

7. Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve disagreements with the ARD team and are still unsatisfied, you have several formal escalation options available to you under IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89. It is important to be familiar with these. Here are your options, presented in the typical order you would pursue them:

First, you can file a formal State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Your complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or state special education laws. The TEA investigation may involve reviewing documents, interviewing witnesses, and conducting on-site visits.

Second, you can request mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a trained, impartial mediator to try and reach a mutually agreeable solution. Mediation is also free, and it can be a less adversarial way to resolve disagreements than a formal due process hearing. TEA will provide a mediator at no cost to you or the district.

Third, you can file a request for a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer. The hearing officer will then issue a decision that is binding on both you and the school district. Due process hearings can be complex and time-consuming, and it's often advisable to seek legal representation if you choose to pursue this option.

Finally, you also have the option of filing a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR). OCR investigates allegations of discrimination based on disability. If you believe that your child has been discriminated against, you can file a complaint with OCR. OCR investigations can lead to a variety of remedies, including changes to school district policies and practices.

It is important to remember that you have rights as a parent of a child with a disability. You have the right to participate in the ARD process, to disagree with the team's recommendations, and to pursue formal escalation options if necessary. Don't be afraid to advocate for your child's needs and ensure that they receive the Free Appropriate Public Education they deserve.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
