How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Texarkana ISD

If you believe your child may have a disability that requires special education services in Texarkana ISD, you have the right to request a full and individual initial evaluation (FIE). This guide outlines the process, timelines, and your rights under Texas law.

How to Submit a Written Request

The most important step is submitting a written request for an evaluation. This request should be clear and concise, stating your concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress. Address the letter to the school principal, the special education director, or the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) Committee chairperson at your child's school. Be sure to include your child's full name, date of birth, grade level, and school. Briefly describe the specific areas where you believe your child is struggling. You don't need to diagnose a specific disability, but highlight the difficulties you've observed. For example, you might say, "I am concerned about [child's name]'s reading comprehension and difficulty completing assignments. I am requesting a full and individual educational evaluation to determine if [he/she] is eligible for special education services." Keep a copy of the request for your records. Send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested or hand-deliver it and obtain a signed and dated receipt. This will serve as proof that you submitted your request.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Under Texas Education Code §29.004, once the school district receives your written request, they have a specific timeline to follow. First, they must provide you with written notice of their decision whether or not to evaluate your child. If they decide to evaluate, they must obtain your written consent to proceed. Once you provide consent, the district has 45 school days (not calendar days) to complete the evaluation and hold an ARD committee meeting to discuss the results. This 45-day timeline includes administering all necessary assessments, writing the evaluation report (FIE), and scheduling the ARD meeting. It's important to understand that school holidays, weekends, and summer breaks don't count toward the 45 school days. Communicate with the school to stay informed about the progress of the evaluation and the scheduled ARD meeting.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) must assess all areas related to the suspected disability. This includes, but is not limited to, academic achievement, intellectual functioning, adaptive behavior, communication skills, and social-emotional functioning. If there are concerns about physical or medical conditions, the school may request additional information from medical professionals, but the school district ultimately bears the responsibility for providing the appropriate educational evaluation. The FIE must be comprehensive and use a variety of assessment tools and strategies, not just one test. The evaluators must be qualified and trained to administer and interpret the tests they use. You have the right to review all of the evaluation data and reports before the ARD meeting.

Child Find Obligations

Texas school districts have a legal obligation under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and state law to identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities residing within their boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public schools. This is known as "Child Find." This responsibility extends to children who are suspected of having a disability but are not yet identified or receiving special education services. If you believe a child in your community may have a disability, you can contact Texarkana ISD's special education department to report your concerns. The district is then obligated to investigate and determine if an evaluation is warranted. This applies even to children who are homeschooled or attend private schools.

What If They Refuse

If the school district refuses to conduct an evaluation after you have made a written request, they must provide you with a written explanation of why they are refusing. You have the right to challenge this decision. You can request a due process hearing or pursue mediation to resolve the dispute. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) provides resources and information about these options. You may also consider filing a complaint with the TEA. It's crucial to document all communication with the school district and seek legal advice from an attorney specializing in special education law if you believe your child's rights are being violated. Contact Disability Rights Texas for possible free or low-cost legal assistance.

After the Evaluation

After the evaluation is completed, the ARD committee will meet to review the results. The ARD committee includes you (the parent), a school administrator, a special education teacher, and any other relevant professionals who participated in the evaluation. The committee will discuss whether your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services under one of the thirteen disability categories defined by IDEA. If your child is found eligible, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's specific needs. The IEP will outline your child's goals, accommodations, modifications, and the services they will receive. You have the right to participate fully in the ARD process and to disagree with the IEP if you believe it does not adequately address your child's needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
