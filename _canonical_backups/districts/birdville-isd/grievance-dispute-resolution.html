Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

As a parent in Birdville ISD, understanding your child's rights concerning discipline is crucial, especially if your child has an IEP or is suspected of having a disability. This guide aims to provide you with a rapid understanding of key aspects of discipline, focusing on suspensions, Manifestation Determination Reviews (MDRs), Change of Placement, grievances, and the steps you can take to protect your child. The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Birdville ISD, like all Texas public schools, adheres to the Texas Education Code regarding student discipline. A key concept is the 10-day suspension rule. This rule is particularly critical for students with IEPs or suspected disabilities.

What is it? A student can be suspended for up to 3 consecutive days, or 10 cumulative days in a school year, without triggering certain procedural safeguards, primarily a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). However, even short suspensions can have a long-term impact.

Why is it important? Once a student with an IEP has been removed for more than 10 school days in a school year (whether those removals are consecutive or cumulative), the school district is required to conduct an MDR before imposing any further disciplinary action that would result in a change of placement. This 10-day threshold also triggers other considerations under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

What to do:

  • Track Suspensions: Keep meticulous records of every suspension your child receives, even for a single day. Note the date, length, and reason for the suspension. Don't rely on the school to do this accurately.
  • Request Documentation: Demand official documentation from the school for each suspension. Do not accept verbal explanations only.
  • Monitor Cumulative Days: Be acutely aware of how many cumulative days of suspension your child has received throughout the school year. Be proactive - the school may not be.
  • If close to 10 days: If your child is approaching or has reached 10 cumulative days, immediately contact the school's special education department and request an MDR if one hasn't been offered. Emphasize that failure to conduct a timely MDR is a violation of IDEA.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a critical process designed to determine if a student's behavior leading to disciplinary action was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability. It also determines if the behavior was a direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

When is an MDR Required? An MDR *must* be conducted when a student with an IEP faces a disciplinary change of placement (see section below) due to a violation of the school's code of conduct and the student has accumulated more than 10 days of suspension in a school year. The MDR must occur no later than 10 school days after the decision to change the student's placement.

Who Participates? The MDR team includes the school district representatives, the parent(s), and relevant members of the IEP team (as determined by the parent and the school). It's crucial that you are actively involved and that you bring any relevant documentation you have (medical reports, independent evaluations, etc.).

What Happens in an MDR? The team reviews all relevant information in the student's file, including:

  • The student's IEP
  • Teacher observations
  • Any relevant information provided by the parents

The team then answers two critical questions:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the IEP?

What if the Behavior *Is* a Manifestation of the Disability? If the team determines that the behavior *is* a manifestation of the disability, the student *cannot* be disciplined in the same way as a student without a disability. The IEP team must conduct a functional behavioral assessment (FBA), unless one has already been conducted, and implement a behavior intervention plan (BIP). The school district must also return the student to the placement from which they were removed, unless the parent and the school district agree otherwise.

What if the Behavior *Is Not* a Manifestation of the Disability? If the team determines that the behavior is *not* a manifestation of the disability, the student may be subject to the same disciplinary actions that would apply to a student without a disability. However, the school district must still provide special education services during any period of suspension exceeding 10 days.

What to do:

  • Prepare Thoroughly: Before the MDR meeting, review your child's IEP, behavior plans, and any relevant medical or psychological evaluations. Gather any information that supports the connection between your child's behavior and their disability.
  • Advocate Strongly: Be prepared to advocate for your child. Do not be intimidated by school personnel. Remember, you are the expert on your child.
  • Record Everything: Take detailed notes during the MDR meeting. Record the names of all participants, the information presented, and the decisions made. If possible, bring a witness or record the meeting (with proper consent, if required by law).
  • Do Not Waive Rights: Do not sign anything at the meeting unless you fully understand it and agree with it. Seek legal counsel if you are unsure.
  • Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child's behavior or disability, you have the right to request an IEE at the school district's expense.

Change of Placement

A change of placement occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days, or when a series of removals constitutes a pattern. A pattern of removals exists when the removals total more than 10 school days in a school year, because the child's behavior is substantially similar to the child's behavior in previous incidents that resulted in the series of removals, and because of such additional factors as the length of each removal, the total amount of time the child is removed, and the proximity of the removals to one another.

A change of placement can include:

  • Suspension for more than 10 consecutive days.
  • Expulsion.
  • Placement in an alternative educational setting.

Why is it important? A change of placement triggers significant procedural safeguards under IDEA, including the requirement for an MDR (if the student has exceeded 10 cumulative days of suspension). The school district cannot simply move your child to a different setting without following these procedures.

What to do:

  • Object Immediately: If the school district proposes a change of placement, immediately object in writing. State that you believe the change of placement is inappropriate and that it violates your child's rights under IDEA.
  • Demand an MDR: If your child has exceeded 10 cumulative days of suspension, demand an MDR *before* the change of placement occurs.
  • Request a Stay-Put Order: If you disagree with the proposed change of placement, you have the right to request a stay-put order. A stay-put order means that your child remains in their current placement until the dispute is resolved through due process.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

Birdville ISD has a grievance policy that allows parents to formally address concerns regarding school policies and procedures. If you believe your child's rights have been violated, you can file a Level 1 grievance.

What is a Level 1 Grievance? A Level 1 grievance is the first step in the formal grievance process. It is typically filed with the school principal or the administrator responsible for the area of concern.

How to File a Level 1 Grievance:

  1. Obtain the Grievance Policy: Request a copy of Birdville ISD's grievance policy. This policy outlines the specific procedures for filing a grievance, including the required timelines and information. This is often found on the district website.
  2. Draft a Written Grievance: Prepare a written grievance that clearly and concisely describes the issue. Include:
    • Your child's name and grade level.
    • The date of the incident or violation.
    • A detailed description of the issue, including specific facts and circumstances.
    • The specific policy or law that you believe was violated.
    • The relief you are seeking (e.g., reinstatement of your child's placement, removal of a disciplinary action from their record).
  3. Submit the Grievance: Submit the written grievance to the appropriate administrator (usually the principal). Be sure to keep a copy of the grievance for your records.
  4. Follow Up: Follow up with the administrator to ensure that the grievance is being addressed. The grievance policy should outline the timeframe within which the administrator must respond.

What to do:

  • Document Everything: Keep detailed records of all communications related to the grievance, including dates, times, and the names of individuals you spoke with.
  • Meet Deadlines: Adhere strictly to the timelines outlined in the grievance policy. Failure to meet a deadline may result in the grievance being dismissed.
  • Escalate if Necessary: If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the Level 1 grievance, you may be able to escalate the grievance to the next level (e.g., the superintendent or school board).

TEA State Complaint

If you believe that Birdville ISD has violated federal or state special education laws, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a formal process that can trigger an investigation by TEA.

When to File a TEA Complaint: You can file a TEA complaint if you believe that the school district has failed to comply with IDEA or other applicable special education laws. This can include issues such as failure to provide a free appropriate public education (FAPE), failure to conduct a timely evaluation, or failure to implement the IEP.

How to File a TEA Complaint:

  1. Obtain the Complaint Form: Download the TEA complaint form from the TEA website.
  2. Complete the Complaint Form: Complete the complaint form accurately and thoroughly. Include:
    • Your child's name and school.
    • A detailed description of the alleged violation, including specific facts and dates.
    • Copies of relevant documents, such as the IEP, evaluations, and disciplinary records.
  3. Submit the Complaint: Submit the completed complaint form and supporting documentation to TEA.

What to do:

  • Act Quickly: There are strict timelines for filing a TEA complaint. Typically, you must file the complaint within one year of the alleged violation.
  • Be Specific: Provide specific details about the alleged violation. Vague or general complaints are less likely to be investigated.
  • Keep Copies: Keep copies of all documents submitted to TEA.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal process that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE).

When to Request Due Process: You can request due process if you disagree with the school district's decisions regarding your child's special education services. This can include disagreements about the IEP, placement, evaluations, or disciplinary actions.

How to Request Due Process:

  1. File a Due Process Complaint: File a written due process complaint with the school district and TEA. The complaint must include:
    • The child's name and address.
    • A description of the nature of the problem, including the facts relating to the problem.
    • A proposed resolution of the problem to the extent known and available to the parent at the time.

What Happens After a Due Process Complaint is Filed?

  • Resolution Session: The school district must offer a resolution session within 15 days of receiving the due process complaint. The purpose of the resolution session is to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  • Mediation: If the resolution session is unsuccessful, you may choose to participate in mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps the parties reach an agreement.
  • Due Process Hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful, you have the right to a due process hearing. The hearing is conducted by an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision based on the evidence presented.

What to do:

  • Consult with an Attorney: Due process is a complex legal process. It is highly recommended that you consult with an education attorney specializing in special education law before requesting due process.
  • Gather Evidence: Gather all relevant documentation to support your case, including the IEP, evaluations, disciplinary records, and any other relevant information.
  • Prepare for the Hearing: If you proceed to a due process hearing, prepare thoroughly. This includes gathering evidence, preparing witnesses, and developing a legal strategy.

Remember: Time is of the essence. Do not delay in seeking legal counsel if you believe your child's rights have been violated. Fight for your child's future!

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
