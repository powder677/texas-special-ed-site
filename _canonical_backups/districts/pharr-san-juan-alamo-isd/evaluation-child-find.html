Requesting an Evaluation in Pharr-San Juan-Alamo ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Pharr-San Juan-Alamo ISD

This guide provides information for parents and guardians seeking a special education evaluation for their child within the Pharr-San Juan-Alamo Independent School District (PSJA ISD) in Texas. This information is intended to provide guidance and is not a substitute for legal advice. It is always recommended to consult with an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law for personalized guidance.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating the special education evaluation process is to submit a formal, written request to the school district. This request should clearly state your concern that your child may have a disability that is impacting their educational progress and that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation (FIE) to determine eligibility for special education services.

Here's what your written request should include:

  • Date: The date you are writing the request.
  • Your Name and Contact Information: Include your full name, address, phone number, and email address.
  • Your Child's Name and Date of Birth: Clearly state your child's full name and date of birth.
  • Your Child's School and Grade Level: Specify the school your child attends and their current grade level.
  • Specific Concerns: Describe in detail your concerns about your child's academic, social, emotional, behavioral, or physical development. Provide specific examples of challenges your child is experiencing in the classroom or at home. This is crucial. Don't just say "My child is struggling." Say "My child struggles to read sight words and often guesses instead of sounding them out, which results in difficulty comprehending what they read. They are also easily distracted during lessons and have trouble focusing on tasks for extended periods."
  • Reasons for Requesting Evaluation: State that you believe your child's difficulties may be due to a disability requiring special education services. Reference the specific areas of concern and how they impact their learning.
  • Request for a Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE): Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation to determine if your child is eligible for special education and related services.
  • Request for All Records: It's a good idea to request copies of all of your child's school records at the same time. This allows you to review the records and have them available during the evaluation process.
  • Signature: Sign and date the letter.

Where to Submit the Request: The written request should be submitted to the school principal or the school's special education director. It is highly recommended that you send the request via certified mail with return receipt requested. This provides proof that the district received your request. Keep a copy of the request for your records.

Sample Language: "I am writing to request a full and individual initial evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], born on [Date of Birth], who attends [School Name] in [Grade Level]. I am concerned that [Child's Name] may have a disability that is impacting their educational progress. Specifically, [he/she] [describe specific concerns and examples]. I believe these difficulties may be due to a disability that requires special education and related services. I also request copies of all of [Child's Name]'s school records."

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas Education Code and federal law mandate specific timelines that the school district must adhere to once a written request for evaluation is received. It is important to be aware of these timelines to ensure your child's evaluation proceeds in a timely manner.

Within 15 School Days (Texas Administrative Code §89.1011): Within 15 school days of receiving your written request, the school district must either:

  • Obtain your written consent to conduct the FIE; or
  • Provide you with prior written notice that the district refuses to conduct the FIE.

If the district proposes to conduct the FIE, they must provide you with an "Assessment Plan." This plan will outline the specific areas they intend to evaluate and the assessments they will use. You must provide written consent to this plan before the evaluation can begin. Carefully review the assessment plan to ensure it adequately addresses your concerns. If you have concerns about the assessment plan, communicate those concerns to the school district in writing.

45 School Day Timeline (Texas Administrative Code §89.1011): Once the district receives your written consent to the assessment plan, they have a maximum of 45 school days to:

  • Complete the FIE; and
  • Convene an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services.

School Days Defined: It's crucial to understand that the 45-school-day timeline refers to school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks (e.g., Thanksgiving break, Christmas break, Spring Break) are not counted. This can significantly extend the overall time it takes to complete the evaluation process.

Timeline Extensions: There are limited circumstances under which the 45-school-day timeline can be extended. One such circumstance is if the parent repeatedly fails or refuses to make the child available for the evaluation. The school district must document these attempts and the reasons for the delays. It is in your child's best interest to cooperate with the evaluation process to ensure it is completed in a timely manner.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Initial Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if a child has a disability, the nature and extent of that disability, and the child's educational needs. The FIE must be conducted by qualified professionals. It's important to understand what areas the FIE should cover to ensure a thorough evaluation.

The FIE must address all areas related to the suspected disability. This typically includes:

  • Academic Achievement: Evaluates your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. This may involve standardized tests, classroom observations, and review of work samples.
  • Intellectual Ability: Assesses your child's cognitive abilities, such as reasoning, problem-solving, and memory. This is typically measured through standardized IQ tests.
  • Adaptive Behavior: Examines your child's ability to function independently in everyday life, including self-care, social skills, and practical living skills.
  • Communication Skills: Evaluates your child's expressive and receptive language skills, including articulation, fluency, and understanding of language.
  • Motor Skills: Assesses your child's gross motor skills (e.g., running, jumping) and fine motor skills (e.g., writing, buttoning).
  • Social and Emotional Development: Examines your child's social interactions, emotional regulation, and mental health. This may involve observations, interviews, and questionnaires.
  • Health: Reviews your child's medical history and may include a physical examination or referral to a medical specialist if necessary.
  • Vision and Hearing: Screening for vision and hearing impairments. If concerns are raised, a referral to an ophthalmologist or audiologist may be recommended.

Parent Input: Your input is a vital part of the FIE process. You should provide the evaluation team with information about your child's strengths, weaknesses, developmental history, medical history, and any other relevant information. Share any reports or evaluations from outside professionals (e.g., doctors, therapists) with the evaluation team.

Specific Assessments: The specific assessments used in the FIE will vary depending on the child's suspected disability and the areas of concern. However, the assessments must be reliable, valid, and administered in the child's native language or mode of communication. The school district should be able to explain the purpose and procedures of each assessment to you.

Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school district's FIE, you have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense. The district must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to show that its evaluation was appropriate. You are entitled to only one IEE at public expense each time the district conducts an evaluation with which you disagree. You are not required to use the district's list of approved evaluators for an IEE, however, there may be limits on the cost of the IEE the district is required to pay.

Child Find Obligations

The "Child Find" mandate is a legal requirement under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) that requires all school districts to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This includes children who are homeless, wards of the state, or attending private schools. PSJA ISD has a responsibility to fulfill its Child Find obligations.

District Responsibilities: PSJA ISD must have procedures in place to identify children who may have disabilities and who are not receiving special education services. This may involve:

  • Screening Activities: Conducting screenings of students to identify those who may be at risk for developmental delays or learning difficulties.
  • Referral Processes: Establishing clear referral processes for teachers, parents, and other professionals to refer children for evaluation.
  • Public Awareness Campaigns: Conducting public awareness campaigns to inform the community about special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation.
  • Collaboration with Community Agencies: Collaborating with community agencies and organizations to identify children with disabilities who may not be enrolled in school.

What to Do If You Suspect a Child Has a Disability: If you suspect that a child residing within PSJA ISD has a disability, even if they are not your own child, you can contact the district's special education department and express your concerns. The district is obligated to investigate your concerns and take appropriate action.

Private School Students: Child Find obligations extend to students attending private schools within PSJA ISD. However, the services provided to private school students with disabilities may differ from those provided to public school students. Contact the district's special education department for information about services available to private school students.

What If They Refuse

If PSJA ISD refuses to conduct an FIE after you have submitted a written request, they must provide you with prior written notice explaining the reasons for their refusal. This notice must comply with specific requirements outlined in IDEA and Texas special education law.

Reasons for Refusal: The district may refuse to conduct an FIE if they believe that your child does not have a suspected disability or that their difficulties are not impacting their educational performance. However, they must provide a valid and justifiable reason for their refusal. A simple statement that "your child is doing fine" is not sufficient.

Your Rights If They Refuse: If you disagree with the district's refusal to conduct an FIE, you have several options:

  • Request a Due Process Hearing: You have the right to request a due process hearing to challenge the district's decision. A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you can present evidence and arguments to support your request for an evaluation. You will need to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) to initiate a due process hearing.
  • File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a complaint with TEA alleging that PSJA ISD has violated IDEA or state special education law. TEA will investigate your complaint and determine if the district is in compliance.
  • Mediation: You can request mediation to try to resolve the dispute with the district. Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the district reach an agreement.

Seek Legal Assistance: It is highly recommended that you seek legal assistance from an attorney or advocate specializing in special education law if the district refuses to conduct an FIE. An attorney can advise you on your legal rights and options and represent you in a due process hearing or other legal proceedings.

After the Evaluation

Once the FIE is completed, the school district will convene an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services. You, as the parent, are a crucial member of the ARD committee.

ARD Committee Meeting: The ARD committee meeting is a collaborative process where the evaluation team, teachers, administrators, and you, as the parent, work together to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) for your child if they are found eligible for special education services.

Eligibility Determination: The ARD committee will review the evaluation data and determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education under one of the 13 disability categories outlined in IDEA. These categories include:

  • Autism
  • Deaf-Blindness
  • Deafness
  • Emotional Disturbance
  • Hearing Impairment
  • Intellectual Disability
  • Multiple Disabilities
  • Orthopedic Impairment
  • Other Health Impairment
  • Specific Learning Disability
  • Speech or Language Impairment
  • Traumatic Brain Injury
  • Visual Impairment

Individualized Education Program (IEP): If your child is found eligible for special education, the ARD committee will develop an IEP. The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's individualized educational needs, goals, and the specific special education and related services that will be provided to meet those needs. The IEP must be tailored to your child's unique needs and must be designed to enable them to make progress in the general education curriculum.

Components of the IEP: The IEP must include the following components:

  • Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance: A description of your child's current skills and abilities.
  • Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve in one year.
  • Special Education and Related Services: A description of the specific special education and related services that will be provided to your child, including the frequency, duration, and location of those services.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: A list of the accommodations and modifications that will be provided to your child in the classroom and on assessments to help them access the general education curriculum.
  • Placement: A description of the least restrictive environment (LRE) in which your child will receive their education. LRE means that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.
  • Transition Plan: For students age 16 and older, a transition plan outlining the goals and services needed to prepare them for life after high school.

Your Rights at the ARD Meeting: You have the right to actively participate in all ARD committee meetings. You have the right to express your opinions, ask questions, and provide input into the development of your child's IEP. You also have the right to bring an advocate or attorney to the ARD meeting to assist you.

Reviewing and Revising the IEP: The IEP must be reviewed and revised at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to request an ARD committee meeting at any time to review or revise your child's IEP.

Disagreement with the IEP: If you disagree with the IEP developed by the ARD committee, you have the right to request mediation or file a due process hearing to challenge the IEP.

Navigating the special education process can be complex and overwhelming. Remember to document all communication with the school district, keep copies of all relevant documents, and seek legal assistance if you have any questions or concerns. Your active involvement and advocacy are essential to ensuring that your child receives the appropriate special education services to meet their individual needs and reach their full potential within PSJA ISD.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
