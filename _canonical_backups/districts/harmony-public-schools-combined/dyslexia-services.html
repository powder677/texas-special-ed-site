Dyslexia Services in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)

Dyslexia Services in Harmony Public Schools (Combined)

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students attending Harmony Public Schools in Texas. It outlines key aspects of identification, intervention, and support, drawing upon the 2024 Texas Dyslexia Handbook and relevant state and federal guidelines.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (2024) (TDH) serves as the primary resource for understanding and addressing dyslexia in Texas public schools. It provides comprehensive guidance for educators and families on identifying, assessing, and providing evidence-based interventions for students with dyslexia. Harmony Public Schools adheres to the guidelines and recommendations outlined in the TDH. Key updates in the 2024 handbook include:

  • Emphasis on Early Identification and Intervention: The handbook stresses the importance of early screening and intervention to maximize student success.
  • Structured Literacy: The 2024 TDH reaffirms structured literacy as the recommended approach for dyslexia intervention, aligning with the science of reading.
  • Multi-Tiered Systems of Support (MTSS): The handbook promotes the use of MTSS to provide differentiated instruction and interventions based on student needs. Harmony Public Schools utilize Response to Intervention (RTI), a component of MTSS, for this purpose.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook emphasizes the critical role of parents in the dyslexia support process. Active communication and collaboration between schools and families are essential.
  • Updated Information on Assessment and Intervention: The TDH provides updated information on approved screening instruments and evidence-based intervention programs.

Parents and educators are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the full contents of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024, which can be accessed on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website.

Mandatory Screening

Harmony Public Schools follow Texas Education Code §38.003, which mandates dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. Screening is also conducted for students in grades 2-12 who exhibit characteristics of dyslexia or who are referred by a teacher or parent. These screenings are designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia and require further evaluation.

Screening Procedures:

  • Kindergarten and First Grade: All students are screened using TEA-approved instruments, typically focusing on phonological awareness, rapid naming, and letter-sound knowledge.
  • Grades 2-12: Students are screened based on teacher observation, parent referral, or data indicating potential reading difficulties. The screening tools used in these grades may include measures of reading fluency, decoding, and reading comprehension.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified of the screening results, regardless of whether the student is identified as potentially having dyslexia.

Screening Tools:

Harmony Public Schools utilizes TEA-approved screening instruments. Specific tools may vary by campus, but commonly used examples include:

  • Texas Primary Reading Inventory (TPRI) / Texas Kindergarten Entry Assessment (TXKEA): Often used in Kindergarten and early elementary grades.
  • Texas Middle School Fluency Assessment (TMSFA): Used for older students to assess reading fluency.
  • Other TEA-approved screeners: Schools may also use other validated screeners as determined by district policy and student needs.

It is important to remember that screening is just the first step in the identification process. A screening result indicating a potential risk for dyslexia does not automatically diagnose a student with dyslexia. Further evaluation is necessary to confirm a diagnosis.

Structured Literacy Programs

Harmony Public Schools utilize structured literacy approaches for dyslexia intervention, aligning with the recommendations of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. Structured literacy is an explicit, systematic, and multisensory approach to teaching reading, writing, and spelling. It focuses on foundational skills such as phonological awareness, phonics, morphology, syntax, and semantics.

Key Characteristics of Structured Literacy:

  • Explicit Instruction: Concepts are taught directly and clearly, with no assumptions made about prior knowledge.
  • Systematic and Sequential: Skills are taught in a logical order, building upon previously learned concepts.
  • Cumulative: New skills are continuously reviewed and practiced to ensure mastery.
  • Multisensory: Instruction involves the use of visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile modalities to enhance learning.
  • Diagnostic Teaching: Instruction is tailored to meet the individual needs of the student based on ongoing assessment.

Examples of Structured Literacy Programs Used (Examples - Varies by Campus):

While specific program implementation varies by campus based on student needs and staff training, some examples of programs and approaches used within Harmony Public Schools include:

  • Neuhaus Education Center Programs: Programs like Basic Language Skills and Camp Success are often used.
  • Orton-Gillingham based approaches: Harmony teachers are often trained in Orton-Gillingham principles and methodologies. Specific commercial programs based on Orton-Gillingham may also be utilized.
  • Heggerty Phonemic Awareness Curriculum: Used for developing phonological awareness skills, a critical foundation for reading.

Parents should contact their child's school to inquire about the specific structured literacy program being used and the qualifications of the teachers providing the intervention.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 or the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) through an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The determination of which plan is most appropriate depends on the student's individual needs and the level of support required.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Focus: Provides accommodations and modifications to ensure students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Eligibility: Students are eligible if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning.
  • Services: Provides accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments. Does *not* typically include specialized instruction, but can in some instances.
  • Legal Basis: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973.
  • Ideal for: Students who can access the general education curriculum with reasonable accommodations.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Focus: Provides specialized instruction and related services to meet the unique educational needs of students with disabilities.
  • Eligibility: Students are eligible if they have one of the 13 disabilities listed in IDEA (including Specific Learning Disability, which can encompass Dyslexia) and require specialized instruction to make progress in the general education curriculum. A full and individual evaluation is required.
  • Services: Includes specialized instruction (e.g., structured literacy intervention), accommodations, and related services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, or counseling.
  • Legal Basis: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).
  • Ideal for: Students who require significant modifications to the curriculum and specialized instruction to address their learning needs.

Key Differences Summarized:

Feature Section 504 Plan IEP (IDEA)
Focus Equal Access through Accommodations Specialized Instruction and Related Services
Eligibility Disability that substantially limits a major life activity One of 13 IDEA-defined disabilities requiring specialized instruction
Intervention Primarily accommodations Specialized instruction, accommodations, and related services

The decision to develop a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team that includes parents, teachers, and other relevant professionals. The team will consider the student's strengths, weaknesses, and educational needs to determine the most appropriate level of support.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are adjustments to the learning environment or instructional practices that allow students with dyslexia to access the curriculum and demonstrate their knowledge. The specific accommodations provided will vary depending on the student's individual needs and the recommendations of the 504 or IEP team.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Providing a seat in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Assistive Technology: Utilizing tools such as text-to-speech software, speech-to-text software, and audiobooks.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Use of Graphic Organizers: Providing visual aids to help students organize their thoughts and ideas.
  • Copy of Notes: Providing a copy of the teacher's notes.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the use of a calculator for math assignments.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing the use of a word processor with spell check for writing assignments.
  • Breaks: Allowing frequent breaks during instruction or testing.
  • Multisensory Instruction: Incorporating visual, auditory, kinesthetic, and tactile activities into instruction.
  • Reduced Workload: Reducing the amount of work required without changing the content.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Access to a Reader: Allowing a student to have text read to them.
  • Access to a Scribe: Allowing a student to dictate answers to someone who writes them down.
  • Highlighted Textbooks: Providing textbooks with key information highlighted.
  • Recording Lectures: Allowing students to record lectures for later review.

This is not an exhaustive list, and other accommodations may be appropriate depending on the student's individual needs. The 504 or IEP team will work collaboratively to determine the most effective accommodations for each student.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect their child may have dyslexia should contact their child's teacher or school principal to request an evaluation. The request should be made in writing and should include specific concerns about the student's reading, writing, or spelling skills.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Write a Letter: Prepare a written request for evaluation addressed to the school principal or special education coordinator. Clearly state your concerns about your child's reading, writing, or spelling skills. Mention any specific observations, such as difficulty with phonics, slow reading speed, or frequent spelling errors.
  2. Submit the Letter: Submit the letter to the school principal or special education coordinator. Keep a copy of the letter for your records.
  3. Review of Existing Data: The school will review existing data, including classroom performance, screening results, and any previous interventions.
  4. Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE): If the review of existing data indicates a potential need for special education services, the school will conduct a full and individual evaluation (FIE) to determine if the student meets the eligibility criteria for a specific learning disability, including dyslexia. Parent consent is required before the FIE can be conducted.
  5. Evaluation Components: The FIE will typically include assessments of reading fluency, decoding, reading comprehension, spelling, writing, phonological awareness, and other relevant areas.
  6. ARD Meeting: Following the evaluation, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting will be held to discuss the evaluation results, determine eligibility for special education services, and develop an IEP if appropriate. Parents are integral members of the ARD committee.

Important Considerations:

  • Timeline: Schools are required to conduct evaluations within a reasonable timeframe after receiving a written request. Specific timelines are outlined in the Texas Education Code.
  • Parent Rights: Parents have the right to participate in all stages of the evaluation and IEP development process. They also have the right to obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) if they disagree with the school's evaluation. This is done at public expense if certain conditions are met.
  • Collaboration: Open communication and collaboration between parents and the school are essential throughout the evaluation and intervention process.

Harmony Public Schools is committed to providing appropriate services and support to students with dyslexia. By working together, parents and educators can help students with dyslexia achieve their full potential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
