What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. What Is an ARD Meeting?

ARD stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal. An ARD meeting is a crucial meeting required by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29, for students with disabilities who are eligible for special education services. The primary purpose of the ARD meeting is to determine whether a student is eligible for special education, to develop, review, and revise the student's Individualized Education Program (IEP), and to determine the appropriate educational placement for that student. Think of it as a planning session, where everyone invested in your child's education comes together to create a roadmap for their success.

The ARD committee is a team of individuals, including you, the parent or legal guardian, who work together to make these important decisions. Other members of the ARD committee typically include a representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise the provision of special education, your child's general education teacher, your child's special education teacher, and other professionals such as a school psychologist, diagnostician, or therapist who have knowledge or expertise regarding your child's specific needs. The school district may also invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding the child, including related services personnel as appropriate. Importantly, you, as the parent, have the right to invite anyone you feel is important to participate in the meeting, such as a family member, friend, or advocate. Your child, if appropriate, should also be involved in the ARD process, particularly as they get older and can contribute to the discussion about their own needs and goals. The ARD committee works collaboratively to develop an IEP that is tailored to meet your child's unique needs and helps them make progress in the general education curriculum. The ARD meeting is not just a formality; it's a legally mandated process designed to ensure your child receives a free and appropriate public education (FAPE).

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This is not just a suggestion; it's a legal requirement designed to give you adequate time to prepare for the meeting. This advance notice, provided by Irving ISD, must include specific information, such as the purpose of the meeting (whether it's an initial ARD, an annual review, or a meeting to address specific concerns), the date, time, and location of the meeting, a list of the individuals who will be attending (to the extent that the school knows), and a clear explanation of your parental rights and procedural safeguards under IDEA and Texas Education Code. The notice should also specify if the district intends to discuss evaluation data, placement options, or any changes to your child's current IEP.

If you prefer to meet sooner than five days, you have the option to waive this right in writing. Waiving the five-day notice might be appropriate if you and the school agree on a quicker timeline, for example, if an urgent issue needs to be addressed promptly or if scheduling conflicts make it difficult to meet within the standard timeframe. However, it's important to consider carefully before waiving your right to the five-day notice. Make sure you feel fully prepared for the meeting and have had sufficient time to review any relevant documents or information. Don't feel pressured to waive your rights if you need more time to prepare. The five-day notice is there to protect your ability to actively participate in the ARD process and advocate for your child's needs.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Preparing for an ARD meeting is essential to ensure your child's needs are fully considered. Gathering and bringing relevant documents and information can significantly contribute to a productive and effective meeting. Start by compiling any prior evaluations or assessments that have been conducted on your child, whether they were completed by the school district, independent professionals, or medical specialists. This includes psychological evaluations, speech and language assessments, occupational therapy reports, and any other evaluations that provide insight into your child's strengths, weaknesses, and educational needs. Any letters from your child's doctors or other healthcare providers that explain your child's medical condition, diagnoses, or recommendations for accommodations or services are also helpful. School records, such as report cards, progress reports, attendance records, and disciplinary records, can provide valuable context for the ARD committee.

Before the meeting, take the time to create a written list of your specific concerns and priorities for your child's education. Be as detailed as possible, outlining any challenges your child is facing, areas where you believe they need additional support, and your hopes for their academic and personal growth. Having a trusted advocate or support person with you can be incredibly helpful. This could be a family member, friend, or professional advocate who can provide emotional support, take notes during the meeting, and help you articulate your concerns and perspectives. Texas is a one-party consent state for recording conversations, which means you are legally allowed to record the ARD meeting, as long as you inform the other attendees that you are doing so. This can be a useful tool for ensuring an accurate record of the discussion and decisions made during the meeting.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During an ARD meeting, you, or any other member of the ARD committee, have the right to request a recess of up to ten school days to gather more information, consult with experts, or simply have more time to consider the decisions being made. This is sometimes called the "10-day rule" or the "recess provision." This rule is in place to protect the interests of the child and ensure that all decisions are made with sufficient information and thoughtful consideration. If you feel overwhelmed, unprepared, or unsure about a particular aspect of your child's IEP, don't hesitate to invoke this rule. You can simply state that you would like to recess the meeting for up to ten school days to further consider the information presented or to seek outside consultation. There is no need to provide an elaborate explanation; your request must be honored.

The recess allows you to take the time you need to consult with an advocate, review relevant documents, gather additional data, or simply reflect on the discussion that has taken place. This time can be invaluable in ensuring that you are making informed decisions that are in the best interests of your child. Once the recess period has ended, the ARD committee will reconvene to continue the meeting and finalize the IEP. It is important to note that the 10-day recess applies to school days, not calendar days. Weekends and school holidays do not count towards the ten-day limit. Utilizing this provision ensures everyone is comfortable and confident with the decisions that will shape your child's educational path. If you feel rushed, pressured, or uncertain at any point during the ARD process, remember that you have the right to request a recess and take the time you need.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP, or Individualized Education Program, is the heart of the ARD process. It's a written document that outlines your child's unique educational needs and the specific services and supports that will be provided to help them succeed. The IEP is developed during the ARD meeting and is based on input from all members of the ARD committee, including you, the parent. Several key components comprise the IEP. "Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance" describe your child's current academic and functional skills. This section summarizes their strengths and weaknesses and is based on evaluations, observations, and input from teachers and parents. This sets the baseline for the plan.

The IEP also includes "Annual Goals," which are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be aligned with the state's academic standards and should address your child's individual needs. "Services" detail the specific special education and related services that will be provided to your child. This may include specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, transportation, or other supports. "Placement" refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could be in a general education classroom with support, a resource room, a self-contained classroom, or another setting. "Accommodations and Modifications" outline the changes to the curriculum, instruction, or assessment that will be made to help your child access the general education curriculum and demonstrate their learning. It's important to remember that you do not have to sign the IEP on the same day it is developed. You can request a copy of the document and take the time you need to review it thoroughly before providing your consent.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly acceptable, and sometimes necessary, to disagree with the ARD team. You are a vital member of the team, and your perspective on your child's needs is invaluable. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that if you do not agree with the ARD team's recommendation to place your child in special education, you can refuse to give your consent, and your child will not be placed in special education. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with any aspect of the document. If you choose not to sign the IEP, you should clearly communicate your concerns to the ARD team and document your objections in writing. Even if you sign the IEP, you can still indicate that you disagree with certain parts of it by writing "Parent Disagrees" next to the specific sections you object to. This protects your right to pursue further action if needed.

Another important right is the ability to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. If you request an IEE, the school district must either pay for the evaluation or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. You are entitled to receive a copy of the ARD document reflecting the decisions that were made. Do not feel pressured to agree to anything you are not comfortable with. Your role is to advocate for your child, and your opinions are important. Disagreement is a normal part of the process, and there are mechanisms in place to address disputes and ensure that your child's needs are met.

Escalation Options

If you've tried to resolve disagreements with the ARD team through discussion and negotiation, but you're still not satisfied, there are formal escalation options available to you. Understanding these options and how to utilize them is key to ensuring your child receives the appropriate education and support.

First, you can file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process, and TEA is required to investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. The complaint must allege a violation of IDEA or Texas Education Code. TEA will review the evidence and determine if the district has failed to meet its legal obligations. Second, mediation is another free option available through TEA. Mediation involves a neutral third party who helps you and the school district come to a mutually agreeable resolution. A mediator is trained to facilitate communication and help the parties explore options for resolving the dispute. This is a voluntary process, and both you and the school district must agree to participate.

Third, a due process hearing is a more formal legal proceeding. This involves presenting evidence and testimony to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about the dispute. You have the right to legal representation during a due process hearing, and the decision of the hearing officer is binding on both you and the school district, subject to appeal. Fourth, you have the right to file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. This applies if you believe your child's civil rights have been violated. Remember, your rights as a parent are protected by federal and state laws, including the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically 19 TAC Chapter 89. These laws provide a framework for ensuring that students with disabilities receive a free and appropriate public education. The Legal Framework for the Child-Centered Special Education Process outlines the procedures and guidelines for special education in Texas and can be a valuable resource for understanding your rights and responsibilities.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
