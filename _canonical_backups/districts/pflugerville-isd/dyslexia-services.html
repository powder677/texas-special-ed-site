Dyslexia Services in Pflugerville ISD

Dyslexia Services in Pflugerville ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services within Pflugerville Independent School District (Pflugerville ISD), adhering to Texas state guidelines and the updated Texas Dyslexia Handbook. This document is intended for parents, educators, and community members seeking to understand how Pflugerville ISD supports students with dyslexia.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook (updated January 2024 by the Texas Education Agency - TEA) serves as the primary resource for understanding dyslexia and related services in Texas public schools. Pflugerville ISD is committed to implementing the guidelines and recommendations outlined in this handbook. Key areas addressed in the handbook and relevant to Pflugerville ISD include:

  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook defines dyslexia as a specific learning disability that is neurological in origin. It is characterized by difficulties with accurate and/or fluent word recognition and by poor spelling and decoding abilities. These difficulties typically result from a deficit in the phonological component of language that is often unexpected in relation to other cognitive abilities and the provision of effective classroom instruction.
  • Identification Procedures: The handbook outlines the process for identifying students with dyslexia, including screening, evaluation, and data-driven decision-making.
  • Instructional Practices: It emphasizes the importance of providing evidence-based, multi-sensory, structured literacy instruction to students with dyslexia.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: The handbook discusses appropriate accommodations and modifications to support students with dyslexia in the classroom.
  • Parent Involvement: It highlights the crucial role of parents in supporting their child's literacy development and advocating for appropriate services.
  • Legal Requirements: The handbook summarizes relevant state and federal laws pertaining to students with dyslexia.

Pflugerville ISD adheres to the Texas Dyslexia Handbook's recommendations for screening, identification, instruction, and support for students with dyslexia. The district provides professional development to teachers and staff on the identification and instruction of students with dyslexia. Parents are encouraged to familiarize themselves with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook to understand their rights and responsibilities.

Mandatory Screening

Texas Education Code requires mandatory dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten and first grade. Pflugerville ISD adheres to this requirement through a systematic screening process designed to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia. Screening typically includes assessments of phonological awareness, letter knowledge, rapid naming, and decoding skills.

Pflugerville ISD's Screening Process:

  1. Kindergarten Screening: All kindergarten students are screened for dyslexia indicators during the school year, typically during the fall and winter.
  2. First Grade Screening: All first-grade students are screened, with follow-up screenings for students who were identified as potentially at-risk in kindergarten.
  3. Ongoing Progress Monitoring: Teachers continuously monitor students' progress in reading and writing throughout the year. If a student demonstrates persistent difficulties, further evaluation may be recommended, regardless of the initial screening results.
  4. Referral Process: If a student is identified as potentially at risk for dyslexia, parents are notified, and further evaluation may be recommended to determine if the student meets the criteria for dyslexia.

It's important to note that a screening is not a diagnosis. Screening identifies students who may benefit from further evaluation. If a screening indicates a potential risk for dyslexia, further assessment is needed to confirm a diagnosis and determine the appropriate level of support.

Structured Literacy Programs

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook emphasizes the importance of providing students with dyslexia with structured literacy instruction. Structured Literacy is a research-based approach that is explicit, systematic, cumulative, and multi-sensory. Pflugerville ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to address the specific needs of students with dyslexia.

Key Components of Structured Literacy Programs used in Pflugerville ISD:

  • Phonology: Focuses on the sounds of language (phonemes) and how they are combined to form words.
  • Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics): Teaches the relationship between letters (graphemes) and sounds (phonemes).
  • Syllable Instruction: Teaches students how to break words into syllables to improve decoding and spelling skills.
  • Morphology: Focuses on the meaning of word parts (prefixes, suffixes, roots).
  • Syntax: Teaches the rules governing word order and sentence structure.
  • Semantics: Addresses the meaning of words and sentences.

Pflugerville ISD uses a variety of structured literacy programs and interventions that may include (but are not limited to):

  • Take Flight: A Comprehensive Intervention for Students with Dyslexia: Designed for students in grades 1 and up, providing intensive, individualized instruction.
  • Reading by Design Specifically designed to support children with reading difficulties or dyslexia.
  • Other district-approved programs: The district may implement additional programs that align with the principles of structured literacy and meet the needs of individual students. Contact the Special Education Department for specific program information.

These programs are implemented by trained teachers and specialists who have expertise in dyslexia and structured literacy. Instruction is typically provided in small groups or individually, allowing for focused and targeted support.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may receive support through a 504 plan or an Individualized Education Program (IEP), depending on the severity of their needs and whether they meet the criteria for special education services. It is important to understand the differences between these two types of plans.

Section 504 Plan

A 504 plan is provided under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. This law prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in programs and activities that receive federal funding. A student with dyslexia may qualify for a 504 plan if their dyslexia substantially limits one or more major life activities, such as learning or reading.

Key characteristics of a 504 plan:

  • Focuses on providing accommodations to ensure equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Accommodations may include extended time on tests, preferential seating, and assistive technology.
  • Does not typically provide specialized instruction in reading or writing.
  • The 504 plan team usually consists of the student's teacher(s), parents, and a school administrator.

Individualized Education Program (IEP)

An IEP is provided under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). IDEA is a federal law that requires public schools to provide a free and appropriate public education (FAPE) to students with disabilities who meet specific eligibility criteria. A student with dyslexia may qualify for an IEP if their dyslexia significantly impacts their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.

Key characteristics of an IEP:

  • Provides specialized instruction tailored to the student's individual needs.
  • May include goals and objectives related to reading, writing, and spelling.
  • May include accommodations and modifications to the curriculum and instruction.
  • The IEP team includes the student's parents, teachers, a special education administrator, and other relevant professionals.

Dyslexia and Eligibility: A student diagnosed with dyslexia is not automatically eligible for special education services under IDEA. The IEP team must determine if the student's dyslexia significantly impacts their educational performance to the extent that they require specialized instruction. In Texas, the ARD committee (Admission, Review, and Dismissal Committee) makes this determination. A student with dyslexia may be found eligible under the specific learning disability category.

Which Plan is Right? The determination of whether a student needs a 504 plan or an IEP is made on a case-by-case basis, based on the student's individual needs and the severity of their dyslexia. If a student primarily needs accommodations to access the general education curriculum, a 504 plan may be appropriate. If a student requires specialized instruction to address their specific reading and writing difficulties, an IEP may be necessary.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns, without changing what they learn. These are designed to provide students with equal access to the curriculum and to demonstrate their knowledge and skills. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia in Pflugerville ISD may include:

  • Reading Accommodations:
    • Text-to-speech software
    • Audiobooks
    • Reading aloud
    • Preferential seating near the teacher
    • Reduced reading load
    • Highlighted text
    • Use of graphic organizers
  • Writing Accommodations:
    • Use of a keyboard or word processor
    • Speech-to-text software
    • Scribe
    • Extended time for writing assignments
    • Pre-writing graphic organizers
    • Use of spelling and grammar check tools
  • Testing Accommodations:
    • Extended time
    • Quiet testing environment
    • Reading test questions aloud
    • Use of assistive technology
    • Breaks during testing
  • Classroom Accommodations:
    • Copies of notes
    • Visual aids
    • Multi-sensory teaching strategies
    • Frequent checks for understanding
    • Opportunities for re-teaching
    • Use of mnemonic devices
  • Organizational Accommodations:
    • Color-coded folders
    • Assignment checklists
    • Help with prioritizing tasks

This is not an exhaustive list, and the specific accommodations provided will depend on the individual student's needs. Accommodations are determined by the 504 committee or the ARD committee, based on assessment data and input from parents, teachers, and the student (when appropriate). It's essential that accommodations are regularly reviewed and adjusted as the student's needs change.

How to Request an Evaluation

If you suspect that your child may have dyslexia, you have the right to request an evaluation from Pflugerville ISD. The process for requesting an evaluation is as follows:

  1. Contact the School: Begin by contacting your child's teacher or the school principal to express your concerns. Document the date and content of each communication.
  2. Written Request: Submit a written request for a dyslexia evaluation to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your contact information
    • A clear statement that you are requesting a dyslexia evaluation.
    • A summary of your concerns about your child's reading and writing skills.
    • Any relevant information, such as past academic performance or observations about your child's learning style.
  3. School Response: Pflugerville ISD is required to respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe (typically, within 15 school days). The school will either:
    • Provide you with a written explanation of why an evaluation is not warranted.
    • Obtain your written consent to proceed with the evaluation.
  4. Evaluation Process: If you provide consent for an evaluation, the school will conduct a comprehensive assessment to determine if your child meets the criteria for dyslexia. The evaluation may include assessments of phonological awareness, decoding skills, reading fluency, spelling, and written language.
  5. ARD or 504 Meeting: After the evaluation is complete, the school will schedule an ARD meeting (for potential special education services) or a 504 meeting to discuss the evaluation results and determine the appropriate course of action. You have the right to participate in this meeting and to advocate for your child's needs.

Parent Rights: It is crucial to understand your rights as a parent throughout this process. You have the right to:

  • Request an evaluation if you suspect your child has dyslexia.
  • Receive a copy of the evaluation report.
  • Participate in all meetings related to your child's education.
  • Disagree with the school's recommendations and request an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at public expense under certain circumstances.
  • File a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe the school is not complying with state or federal laws.

District Contact Information: For specific questions about dyslexia services in Pflugerville ISD, please contact the Special Education Department at the district administration office. Contact information can be found on the Pflugerville ISD website under the Special Education section. You can also ask the campus principal or school counselor for resources.

This guide is for informational purposes only and should not be considered legal advice. Always refer to the official Texas Dyslexia Handbook and consult with Pflugerville ISD personnel for specific guidance on your child's situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
