Dyslexia Services in Northside ISD

Dyslexia Services in Northside ISD

This guide provides an overview of dyslexia services available within Northside Independent School District (NISD) in Texas, aligned with the most recent guidance from the state. It covers important aspects such as the Texas Dyslexia Handbook, mandatory screening procedures, structured literacy programs, navigating 504 plans and IEPs, common accommodations, and the process for requesting an evaluation.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is the primary resource guiding dyslexia identification and intervention in Texas public schools. The 2024 update brings several key changes and clarifications. NISD adheres strictly to the guidelines outlined in the Handbook to ensure consistent and effective support for students with dyslexia.

Key Updates and Emphases in the 2024 Handbook:

  • Emphasis on Early Identification: The Handbook stresses the importance of early identification of dyslexia risk factors through universal screening and ongoing progress monitoring. NISD is committed to implementing these procedures effectively.
  • Structured Literacy: The Handbook reinforces structured literacy as the evidence-based approach for reading instruction for all students, but especially crucial for students with dyslexia. It provides detailed guidance on the components of structured literacy.
  • Progress Monitoring: Regular progress monitoring is crucial for determining the effectiveness of interventions and making adjustments as needed. The Handbook provides guidance on appropriate progress monitoring tools and frequency.
  • Parent Involvement: The Handbook emphasizes the importance of collaboration between parents and the school throughout the entire process, from screening to intervention. NISD values parent input and strives to maintain open communication.
  • Technology Integration: The Handbook recognizes the potential of technology to support students with dyslexia and encourages its appropriate use in instruction and accommodations.
  • Specific Learning Disability (SLD) Clarifications: The Handbook clarifies the relationship between dyslexia and SLD eligibility, ensuring accurate identification and appropriate services.

Parents are strongly encouraged to review the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. It is available for free download from the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Familiarity with the Handbook will empower parents to advocate effectively for their children.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten, 1st grade, and 2nd grade. NISD follows this mandate rigorously. The purpose of screening is to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia so that appropriate interventions can be provided early.

Screening Procedures in NISD:

  • Universal Screening: All students in kindergarten, 1st, and 2nd grade participate in dyslexia screening using TEA-approved screening instruments.
  • Screening Components: The screenings typically assess areas such as phonological awareness, letter knowledge, rapid automatized naming (RAN), and phonological memory. Specific instruments used may vary by campus but are aligned with state recommendations.
  • Screening Timeline: Screenings are typically conducted in the fall and/or winter of each school year. Parents will be notified by their child's school regarding the specific screening schedule.
  • Parent Notification: Parents are notified if their child demonstrates characteristics of dyslexia on the screening. This notification will include information about the screening results, recommended next steps, and available support services.
  • Additional Screening: Students in higher grades (3rd and above) may be screened if teachers or parents have concerns about their reading progress. This can be initiated through a request to the school's Response to Intervention (RTI) team.

It's important to remember that a screening is not a diagnosis. It simply identifies students who may benefit from further evaluation and intervention. A more comprehensive evaluation is needed to determine if a student has dyslexia.

Structured Literacy Programs

Structured Literacy is the evidence-based approach to reading instruction recommended by the Texas Dyslexia Handbook. It is a systematic, explicit, and cumulative approach that focuses on the foundational skills necessary for reading success. NISD implements structured literacy programs across its campuses to support students with dyslexia and other reading difficulties.

Key Components of Structured Literacy:

  • Phonology: Instruction focuses on the sound structure of language, including phonemic awareness (the ability to hear and manipulate individual sounds in words).
  • Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics): Students learn the systematic relationships between letters and sounds.
  • Syllable Instruction: Students learn about the different types of syllables and how to decode and encode multi-syllabic words.
  • Morphology: Students learn about morphemes (the smallest units of meaning in language, such as prefixes, suffixes, and roots) and how they contribute to word meaning.
  • Syntax: Students learn about the rules governing sentence structure.
  • Semantics: Students learn about word meanings and how to use context clues to understand unfamiliar words.

NISD's Approach to Structured Literacy:

  • Implementation: NISD utilizes various structured literacy programs that are aligned with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook and evidence-based practices. Specific programs may vary by campus and grade level.
  • Training: Teachers receive ongoing professional development in structured literacy to ensure they have the knowledge and skills necessary to effectively implement these programs.
  • Tiered Support: Students receive tiered support based on their individual needs. Students who are identified as being at risk for or having dyslexia receive intensive, individualized instruction in structured literacy.
  • Small Group Instruction: Structured literacy instruction is often delivered in small groups to allow for more individualized attention and targeted support.

Parents can inquire with their child's school about the specific structured literacy programs used at their campus and how their child will receive support.

504 vs IEP for Dyslexia

Understanding the difference between a 504 plan and an Individualized Education Program (IEP) is crucial for parents navigating dyslexia services. Both provide support, but they differ significantly in scope and eligibility.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Legal Basis: Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 is a civil rights law that prohibits discrimination based on disability.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., learning, reading, concentrating).
  • Focus: 504 plans provide accommodations to ensure students with disabilities have equal access to the general education curriculum.
  • Examples of Accommodations: Extended time on tests, preferential seating, audiobooks, assistive technology.
  • No Specialized Instruction: 504 plans generally do not provide specialized instruction or intensive intervention.
  • Evaluation: Evaluation is required to determine eligibility for a 504 plan. The evaluation typically includes a review of existing data, observations, and assessments.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Legal Basis: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is a federal law that governs special education services.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability that is specifically listed under IDEA (e.g., Specific Learning Disability, which can include dyslexia) AND the disability adversely affects their educational performance, requiring specially designed instruction.
  • Focus: IEPs provide specially designed instruction and related services to meet the unique needs of students with disabilities.
  • Components: IEPs include goals, objectives, accommodations, modifications, and a description of the specialized instruction and related services the student will receive.
  • Specialized Instruction: IEPs provide specialized instruction tailored to the student's individual needs, such as structured literacy intervention.
  • Evaluation: A comprehensive evaluation is required to determine eligibility for an IEP. This evaluation typically includes psychological testing, academic assessments, and observations.

Dyslexia and 504 vs. IEP:

  • A student with dyslexia may be eligible for either a 504 plan or an IEP, depending on the severity of their disability and their need for specialized instruction.
  • If a student with dyslexia requires only accommodations to access the general education curriculum, a 504 plan may be appropriate.
  • If a student with dyslexia requires specialized instruction, such as intensive structured literacy intervention, an IEP is typically necessary.
  • The determination of whether a 504 plan or an IEP is appropriate is made on a case-by-case basis by a team of professionals, including teachers, administrators, and specialists. Parents are integral members of this team.

Consult with your child's school to determine the most appropriate level of support for their specific needs.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to how a student learns the material. They are designed to provide students with equal access to the curriculum without changing the content of the material being taught. Common accommodations for students with dyslexia include:

  • Extended Time: Allowing extra time on tests and assignments.
  • Preferential Seating: Placing the student in a location that minimizes distractions.
  • Audiobooks: Providing access to textbooks and other materials in audio format.
  • Text-to-Speech Software: Using software that reads text aloud.
  • Speech-to-Text Software: Using software that converts speech to text.
  • Assistive Technology: Providing access to other assistive technology devices and software.
  • Note-Taking Assistance: Providing copies of notes or allowing the student to record lectures.
  • Modified Assignments: Adjusting the length or complexity of assignments.
  • Visual Aids: Using visual aids to support learning.
  • Graphic Organizers: Providing graphic organizers to help students organize their thoughts.
  • Frequent Breaks: Allowing the student to take frequent breaks as needed.
  • Quiet Testing Environment: Providing a quiet and distraction-free environment for testing.
  • Use of a Calculator: Allowing the use of a calculator for math assignments.
  • Word Processor with Spell Check: Allowing the use of a word processor with spell check and grammar check.
  • Oral Administration of Tests: Reading test questions aloud to the student.
  • Highlighting Key Information: Allowing the student to highlight key information in texts.
  • Reduced Workload: Reducing the amount of written work required.
  • Multi-Sensory Learning Materials: Utilizing materials that engage multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic).

This list is not exhaustive. The specific accommodations that are appropriate for a student will depend on their individual needs. The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, in the case of an IEP, or the 504 committee will determine the appropriate accommodations for each student. Parental input is a critical component of this decision-making process.

How to Request Evaluation

If you suspect your child may have dyslexia, it's important to communicate your concerns to the school. Here's how to request an evaluation in NISD:

  1. Communicate with the Teacher: Start by discussing your concerns with your child's teacher. Provide specific examples of the difficulties you are observing.
  2. Contact the School Counselor or Principal: If you are not satisfied with the initial response, or if you have significant concerns, contact the school counselor or principal.
  3. Submit a Written Request: To formally request an evaluation for dyslexia, submit a written request to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your contact information
    • A clear statement that you are requesting an evaluation for dyslexia
    • A description of your concerns and the specific difficulties your child is experiencing with reading, writing, and/or spelling
    • Any relevant documentation, such as previous testing results or teacher observations
  4. Response to Request: The school is required to respond to your request within a reasonable timeframe (typically 15 school days). They will either agree to conduct an evaluation or provide a written explanation of why they are declining the request.
  5. Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to conduct an evaluation, they will assemble an ARD committee (for special education eligibility) or a 504 committee. This committee will review existing data, conduct assessments, and determine if your child meets the criteria for dyslexia and requires specialized services.
  6. Parental Consent: The school must obtain your written consent before conducting any evaluations.
  7. Evaluation Report: After the evaluation is completed, the school will provide you with a written report outlining the findings and recommendations.
  8. ARD/504 Meeting: If your child is found eligible for special education or 504 services, an ARD or 504 meeting will be scheduled to develop an IEP or 504 plan. You have the right to participate fully in this meeting and provide input into the development of the plan.

Important Considerations:

  • Document all communication with the school, including dates, times, and summaries of conversations.
  • Familiarize yourself with the Texas Dyslexia Handbook.
  • Advocate for your child's needs. You are your child's best advocate.

NISD is committed to providing comprehensive dyslexia services to all students who need them. By working collaboratively with parents, teachers, and specialists, we can ensure that students with dyslexia receive the support they need to succeed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
