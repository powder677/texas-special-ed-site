Requesting an Evaluation in IDEA Public Schools

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in IDEA Public Schools

This guide provides information on requesting a special education evaluation for your child in IDEA Public Schools in Texas, focusing on your rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas state law. It outlines the process, timeline, required elements of the evaluation, and what to do if your request is denied.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first and most crucial step in obtaining a special education evaluation is submitting a written request to the school. This request formally puts the school on notice that you suspect your child may have a disability that requires special education services. While verbal requests may be considered, a written request provides clear documentation and establishes the start of the timeline outlined in IDEA and Texas law.

Key Elements of a Written Request: * Date: Include the date of the request. This is essential for tracking the 45-school-day timeline. * Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: Clearly identify your child. * School and Grade Level: Specify the school your child attends and their current grade. * Your Name and Contact Information: Provide your full name, address, phone number, and email address. Ensure the school has multiple ways to reach you. * Statement of Concern: Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual evaluation (FIE) for special education services under IDEA. Be specific and avoid vague language. For example, instead of saying "I'm concerned about my child's progress," say "I am requesting a full and individual evaluation to determine if my child is eligible for special education services due to concerns about their reading comprehension and difficulty completing math assignments." * Specific Concerns and Observations: Describe the specific academic, behavioral, or developmental concerns you have observed. Provide concrete examples. This could include difficulty with reading, writing, math, social skills, attention, or any other area where you believe your child is struggling. * Previous Interventions and Their Effectiveness: If your child has received any interventions or support services (e.g., Response to Intervention (RtI), tutoring, counseling), describe them and their impact (or lack thereof). This helps demonstrate that general education interventions may not be sufficient. * Supporting Documentation (Optional): Include any relevant documentation, such as report cards, teacher notes, medical records, or previous evaluations. While not required at this initial stage, providing this information can expedite the process. * Signature: Sign and date the request.

How to Submit the Request: * Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested: This is the recommended method. It provides proof that the school received your request. Keep the return receipt as documentation. * Hand Delivery with Acknowledgement: Deliver the request in person to the school principal or special education director. Obtain a written acknowledgement of receipt, including the date and the recipient's signature. * Email: While less reliable than certified mail, email can be used. Request a read receipt and/or a confirmation reply from the recipient. Print and save the email and any replies as evidence.

Who to Send the Request To: The request should be addressed to the school principal and the special education director or coordinator at your child's school or the district office. Addressing it to both ensures it reaches the appropriate personnel.

The 45 School Day Timeline

Texas law mandates a specific timeline for the special education evaluation process. Understanding this timeline is crucial to ensuring your child receives timely evaluation and services. The Texas Education Agency (TEA) sets forth that the evaluation timeline is 45 school days.

Starting the Clock: The 45-school-day timeline begins when the school receives your written request for an evaluation. This is why documenting the date of receipt is so important.

Key Milestones within the 45-Day Timeline: * Consent for Evaluation: Within a reasonable timeframe after receiving your request (ideally within a week or two), the school must provide you with a proposed evaluation plan and a consent form. This plan will outline the areas in which your child will be evaluated. * Parent Consent Required: The school cannot begin the evaluation until you provide written consent. Take time to review the proposed evaluation plan. If you disagree with any aspect of it, communicate your concerns to the school in writing. You have the right to request modifications to the plan. * Evaluation Completion: Once you provide consent, the school has the remainder of the 45 school days to complete the evaluation. This includes conducting assessments, gathering data, and preparing a comprehensive evaluation report (FIE). * ARD Meeting: Within 30 calendar days of completing the evaluation, an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting must be held to discuss the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services.

Important Considerations: * School Days vs. Calendar Days: Remember that the timeline is based on school days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks do not count towards the 45-day limit. * Summer Break: If you submit your request close to the end of the school year, the evaluation may not be completed until the following school year. However, the school is still obligated to adhere to the timeline. * Extensions: In very rare circumstances, an extension of the 45-day timeline may be granted. This usually requires mutual agreement between the school and the parent and must be documented in writing. * Monitoring the Timeline: It is your responsibility to track the timeline and ensure the school adheres to it. If you believe the school is not meeting its obligations, contact the school principal, special education director, or the TEA.

Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Reference: Refer to the Texas Administrative Code (TAC) §89.1011(c) for specific regulations regarding the evaluation timeline.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment conducted by qualified professionals to determine if a child has a disability and, if so, the nature and extent of their educational needs. The FIE must be tailored to your child's individual needs and concerns, considering all areas of suspected disability.

Key Areas to be Addressed in the FIE: * Areas of Suspected Disability: The evaluation must cover all areas where you have expressed concerns or where the school suspects a disability may exist. This could include academic skills (reading, writing, math), speech and language, motor skills, social-emotional development, adaptive behavior, and cognitive abilities. * Multiple Assessment Tools: The FIE should utilize a variety of assessment tools, including standardized tests, observations, interviews, and review of records. Relying solely on one assessment is generally insufficient. * Parent Input: The evaluation team must consider information provided by the parent, including observations, concerns, and relevant medical or developmental history. * Classroom Observations: Observations of the child in their classroom setting are essential to understand their learning behaviors and challenges in a natural environment. * Strengths and Weaknesses: The FIE should identify both the child's strengths and weaknesses to provide a comprehensive picture of their abilities and needs. * Educational Impact: The evaluation must determine how the suspected disability is impacting the child's educational performance. This includes their academic progress, participation in classroom activities, and social interactions. * Eligibility Determination: Based on the evaluation results, the FIE must clearly state whether the child meets the criteria for one of the disability categories under IDEA and Texas law. * Recommendations for Special Education Services: If the child is found eligible for special education, the FIE should include specific recommendations for appropriate services, supports, and accommodations to address their individual needs.

Specific Components of a Comprehensive FIE: * Intellectual Assessment: To assess cognitive abilities and potential learning difficulties. * Academic Achievement Testing: To evaluate skills in reading, writing, and math. * Speech and Language Evaluation: If speech or language difficulties are suspected. * Occupational Therapy Evaluation: If fine motor skills or sensory processing issues are suspected. * Psychological Evaluation: To assess social-emotional functioning and identify potential mental health concerns. * Adaptive Behavior Assessment: To evaluate self-help skills and independence. * Medical Information: Review of relevant medical records and reports.

Your Right to an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): If you disagree with the results of the school's FIE, you have the right to obtain an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense. The school must either pay for the IEE or initiate a due process hearing to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. You are only entitled to one IEE at public expense for each evaluation conducted by the school.

Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Reference: Refer to the Texas Administrative Code (TAC) §89.1011 for specific regulations regarding the FIE process.

Child Find Obligations

Under IDEA, all public schools, including IDEA Public Schools, have a "Child Find" obligation. This means they have a legal responsibility to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within their jurisdiction, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school.

Key Aspects of Child Find: * Proactive Identification: Schools cannot simply wait for parents to request evaluations. They must actively look for children who may have disabilities. This includes screening students, reviewing student records, and training staff to recognize potential signs of disability. * Referral Process: Schools must have a clear and accessible referral process for identifying students who may need special education services. This process should be available to parents, teachers, and other school personnel. * Public Awareness: Schools are responsible for informing the public about the availability of special education services and the process for requesting an evaluation. This information should be readily available on the school's website, in school handbooks, and through other communication channels. * Homeless and Ward of the State: Child Find includes specific requirements for identifying and evaluating children who are homeless or wards of the state, as these children may be at higher risk for disabilities. * Private School and Homeschool Students: Child Find obligations extend to students attending private schools and homeschools within the district's boundaries.

What This Means for You: If you suspect your child has a disability, you have the right to request an evaluation, regardless of whether the school has already identified your child as potentially needing special education services. The school cannot refuse to evaluate your child simply because they haven't been flagged through their Child Find activities.

If the School Fails to Fulfill its Child Find Obligation: If you believe the school is not fulfilling its Child Find obligation, you have several options: * Contact the School Principal and Special Education Director: Express your concerns and request that the school take steps to identify and evaluate students who may have disabilities. * File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA): You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe the school is violating IDEA or state special education laws. * Seek Legal Assistance: Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law.

Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Reference: Refer to the Texas Administrative Code (TAC) §89.1011(a) for specific regulations regarding Child Find obligations.

What If They Refuse

If the school refuses to conduct an evaluation after you have submitted a written request, you have legal rights and options to pursue. It's crucial to understand why the school is refusing and to document all communication and actions taken.

Common Reasons for Refusal and What to Do: * Insufficient Evidence of a Suspected Disability: The school may argue that there is not enough evidence to suggest your child has a disability. * What to Do: Gather additional documentation to support your concerns, such as medical records, teacher observations, or work samples. Clearly articulate the specific concerns and how they are impacting your child's education. You can also request an IEE to provide additional evidence. * RtI Interventions are Still in Progress: The school may claim that your child needs to complete the RtI process before an evaluation can be considered. * What to Do: While RtI is often a valuable intervention, it cannot be used to delay or deny a timely evaluation if there is reason to suspect a disability. If you believe the RtI interventions are not effective or are being used to avoid an evaluation, you can still insist on an evaluation. * Lack of Resources: The school may claim they lack the resources to conduct the evaluation. * What to Do: This is not a valid reason to deny an evaluation. Schools have a legal obligation to provide evaluations regardless of resource constraints. * Misunderstanding of the Law: The school may simply be unaware of their obligations under IDEA and Texas law. * What to Do: Educate the school about their legal responsibilities. Provide them with copies of IDEA regulations and Texas Administrative Code sections.

Your Recourse Options: * Request a Written Explanation: Demand a written explanation from the school outlining the specific reasons for refusing the evaluation. This explanation is crucial for documenting the school's actions. * Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process where a neutral third party helps you and the school reach an agreement. * File a State Complaint with the TEA: You can file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe the school is violating IDEA or state special education laws. The TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a decision. * Request a Due Process Hearing: A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where an impartial hearing officer hears evidence and makes a decision regarding the dispute. This is a more adversarial process than mediation. * Seek Legal Assistance: Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law. An attorney can advise you on your legal rights and options and represent you in negotiations or legal proceedings.

Important Considerations: * Documentation is Key: Keep meticulous records of all communication with the school, including dates, times, names of individuals, and summaries of conversations. * Deadlines: Be aware of deadlines for filing complaints and requesting due process hearings. * Parent Training and Information Centers: Contact your local Parent Training and Information Center (PTI) for assistance and support. They can provide you with information about your rights and help you navigate the special education process.

Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Reference: Review Texas Administrative Code §89.1151 regarding dispute resolution, including mediation and due process hearings.

After the Evaluation

Once the evaluation is complete, the school must schedule an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting. This meeting is crucial for determining your child's eligibility for special education services and, if eligible, developing an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

Key Aspects of the ARD Meeting: * ARD Committee Membership: The ARD committee must include: * Parents * At least one general education teacher * At least one special education teacher * A representative of the school district who is qualified to provide or supervise special education * An individual who can interpret the instructional implications of evaluation results * The child (when appropriate) * Review of Evaluation Results: The ARD committee will review the FIE results and discuss whether your child meets the criteria for a disability under IDEA and Texas law. * Eligibility Determination: The ARD committee will determine if your child is eligible for special education services. If eligible, the committee will identify the specific disability category under which your child qualifies. * IEP Development: If your child is found eligible, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's specific educational needs, goals, and services. * Placement Decisions: The ARD committee will determine the most appropriate educational placement for your child, considering the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) principle. * Parent Participation: You are an equal member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in all decisions. Your input and concerns are critical to developing an effective IEP for your child.

Key Elements of the IEP: * Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP): A description of your child's current academic and functional abilities. * Measurable Annual Goals: Specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals for your child's progress. * Special Education and Related Services: A description of the specific services and supports your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or assistive technology. * Accommodations and Modifications: Adjustments to the classroom environment, curriculum, or instruction that will help your child access the general education curriculum. * Assessment Modifications: Changes to how your child is assessed to accurately reflect their knowledge and skills. * Placement: The setting in which your child will receive their education (e.g., general education classroom, resource room, self-contained classroom). * Transition Plan: For students age 16 and older (or younger if deemed appropriate by the ARD committee), a plan to prepare for life after high school.

Your Rights After the ARD Meeting: * Review the IEP: Take time to carefully review the IEP. If you disagree with any aspect of it, you have the right to request changes. * Prior Written Notice (PWN): The school must provide you with Prior Written Notice (PWN) whenever they propose to initiate or change, or refuse to initiate or change, your child's identification, evaluation, or educational placement. * Dispute Resolution: If you disagree with the IEP or any decisions made by the ARD committee, you have several options for resolving the dispute, including mediation, state complaint, and due process hearing. * Annual Review: The IEP must be reviewed and updated at least annually, or more frequently if needed.

Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Reference: Review Texas Administrative Code (TAC) §89.1050 for specific regulations regarding ARD committee meetings and IEP development.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
