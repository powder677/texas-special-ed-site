Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

URGENT GUIDE: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Goose Creek CISD

If your child is facing disciplinary action in Goose Creek CISD, especially suspension or a potential change of placement, this guide is for you. Time is of the essence! Don't panic, but act quickly. This information is provided to help you understand your rights and navigate the often-confusing disciplinary process. This guide is intended for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Consult with an attorney or advocate experienced in special education and student discipline for personalized guidance.

DISCLAIMER: This guide is NOT a substitute for legal advice. Goose Creek CISD policies can change. Consult official district resources and seek legal counsel immediately. This information is based on general Texas education law and IDEA regulations, which may not apply precisely to your child's situation.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Texas law (and federal law through the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act - IDEA - if your child has an IEP or 504 plan) places limits on how long a student can be suspended, particularly when those suspensions accumulate. Keep careful track of the number of days your child is suspended, even if it's in-school suspension (ISS).

Key Points:

  • Cumulative Suspensions: If a student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, it can trigger specific protections under IDEA if the student has an IEP or 504 plan.
  • Change of Placement: Suspensions exceeding 10 cumulative days can be considered a "change of placement" if the suspensions create a pattern of removal from the student's educational program. A change of placement requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).
  • Document Everything: Keep records of every suspension, including the date, duration, reason, and any documentation provided by the school. Request copies of all disciplinary records from the school district.

What to Do:

  • Track Suspension Days: Immediately start a log of all suspensions, even partial-day suspensions.
  • Request Documentation: Demand a written explanation of the reason for each suspension.
  • Consult an Attorney/Advocate: If you believe your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 cumulative suspension days, seek legal advice immediately.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

An MDR is a critical process under IDEA for students with IEPs or 504 plans. It determines whether a student's misconduct is a manifestation of their disability.

When is an MDR Required? An MDR *must* be conducted if the district is contemplating a suspension that would result in a change of placement (typically, exceeding 10 cumulative days of suspension in a school year, or a series of shorter suspensions that constitute a pattern).

The MDR Team: The MDR team typically includes the parent(s), relevant members of the IEP team (including the student, if appropriate), and district representatives.

The Process: The team reviews all relevant information, including:

  • The student's IEP or 504 plan.
  • Teacher observations.
  • Disciplinary records.
  • Any relevant medical or psychological information.

The Question: The team must answer two crucial questions:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by or directly and substantially related to the student's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the district's failure to implement the student's IEP or 504 plan?

Possible Outcomes:

  • Manifestation: If the answer to either question is "yes," the conduct is considered a manifestation of the student's disability. The student cannot be suspended for more than 10 days, and the IEP team must conduct a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) and implement or revise a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) to address the behavior. The student generally returns to their previous placement.
  • Not a Manifestation: If the answer to both questions is "no," the conduct is not considered a manifestation of the disability. The student can be disciplined like any other student, but the district *must* continue to provide educational services to the student during the period of suspension to allow them to continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward meeting their IEP goals.

What to Do:

  • Demand an MDR: If your child with an IEP or 504 plan is facing a suspension that could trigger a change of placement, *insist* on an MDR. Put your request in writing.
  • Prepare Thoroughly: Gather all relevant documentation (IEP, 504 plan, medical records, behavioral data) and prepare to advocate for your child's needs.
  • Bring an Advocate or Attorney: Consider bringing a knowledgeable advocate or attorney to the MDR meeting. They can help ensure your child's rights are protected.
  • Document Everything: Keep detailed notes of everything discussed during the MDR meeting.

Change of Placement

A "change of placement" occurs when a student is removed from their current educational setting for more than 10 consecutive school days or when a series of shorter suspensions creates a pattern of removal. This is a critical trigger for IDEA protections.

What Constitutes a Change of Placement?

  • Long-Term Suspension: Suspension for more than 10 consecutive school days.
  • Pattern of Suspensions: A series of shorter suspensions that, when considered together, create a pattern of removal from the student's educational program. Factors to consider include:
    • The length of each suspension.
    • The proximity of the suspensions to one another.
    • The total amount of time the student is excluded from school.
    • The nature of the misconduct leading to the suspensions.

Rights Triggered by a Change of Placement:

  • Manifestation Determination Review (MDR): As discussed above, an MDR *must* be conducted.
  • Continued Educational Services: Even during suspension, the district must provide services to allow the student to continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward meeting their IEP goals.
  • Procedural Safeguards: You have the right to challenge the change of placement through due process.

What to Do:

  • Monitor Suspension Patterns: Be vigilant in tracking your child's suspensions and identifying any potential pattern.
  • Demand an MDR: If you suspect a change of placement is occurring, demand an MDR in writing.
  • Advocate for Appropriate Services: Ensure your child receives appropriate educational services during any period of suspension.
  • Consider Due Process: If you disagree with the outcome of the MDR or the proposed change of placement, consider filing for due process (see section below).

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

Goose Creek CISD has a grievance policy to address concerns about school actions. A Level 1 grievance is the first step in the formal complaint process.

Who Can File: Parents, students (depending on age and maturity), and employees can file grievances.

What Can Be Grieved: Grievances can be filed regarding any action, decision, or condition that violates district policy, state or federal law, or student rights.

The Process:

  1. Written Grievance: You must submit a written grievance to the appropriate school official (usually the principal or a designated administrator) within a specified timeframe (check the GC CISD policy - usually it's within 15 days of the event). The grievance must clearly state:
    • The specific action or decision being grieved.
    • The specific district policy, law, or right that was violated.
    • The remedy you are seeking.
  2. Meeting: The school official will typically schedule a meeting with you to discuss the grievance.
  3. Written Response: The school official must provide a written response to your grievance within a specified timeframe (again, check the GC CISD policy).

Important Considerations:

  • Timeliness: Strict deadlines apply to filing grievances. Missed deadlines can result in the grievance being dismissed. Act immediately.
  • Specificity: Be as specific as possible in your written grievance. Provide dates, names, and details to support your claim.
  • Documentation: Include any relevant documentation (e.g., suspension notices, emails, meeting notes) with your grievance.
  • Escalation: If you are not satisfied with the response to your Level 1 grievance, you can typically appeal to the next level (Level 2, usually the superintendent or designee). Check the GC CISD policy.

What to Do:

  • Obtain the GC CISD Grievance Policy: Locate the official grievance policy on the GC CISD website or request a copy from the school. Read it carefully.
  • Draft a Detailed Grievance: Prepare a clear and concise written grievance, including all required information.
  • Submit on Time: Submit your grievance within the specified timeframe. Keep a copy for your records.
  • Follow Up: If you do not receive a timely response, follow up with the school official.

TEA State Complaint

A TEA (Texas Education Agency) State Complaint is a formal complaint filed with the TEA alleging violations of state or federal special education laws. This is a more formal process than a district-level grievance.

When to File: You can file a TEA State Complaint if you believe Goose Creek CISD has violated a requirement of IDEA or state special education law.

Requirements: The complaint must:

  • Be in writing.
  • State the specific violation(s) of IDEA or state special education law.
  • Provide the facts on which the complaint is based.
  • Include a proposed resolution of the problem.
  • Be signed and dated.

TEA Investigation: The TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision within 60 days (subject to extension). The TEA may conduct interviews, review documents, and request information from the district.

What to Do:

  • Gather Evidence: Collect all relevant documentation to support your complaint (IEP, 504 plan, disciplinary records, emails, meeting notes).
  • Draft a Clear and Concise Complaint: Clearly state the specific violations and provide the factual basis for your claims.
  • Submit the Complaint: Submit the complaint to the TEA according to their instructions (typically by mail or email).
  • Cooperate with the Investigation: Provide any information requested by the TEA during the investigation.

Note: Filing a TEA State Complaint can be a complex process. Consider seeking legal advice or assistance from an advocate.

Due Process

Due process is a formal legal process under IDEA that allows parents to resolve disputes with the school district regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability, or the provision of a free appropriate public education (FAPE) to the child.

When to File: You can file for due process if you disagree with the district's decisions regarding your child's IEP, placement, or services, especially after exhausting other remedies like grievances. Common situations include disagreements about:

  • The appropriateness of the IEP.
  • The student's placement.
  • The district's refusal to provide specific services.
  • Disciplinary actions that violate IDEA.

The Process:

  1. Due Process Complaint: You must file a written due process complaint with the TEA and provide a copy to the school district. The complaint must meet specific requirements under IDEA.
  2. Resolution Session: The district is required to convene a resolution session with you within 15 days of receiving the complaint to attempt to resolve the dispute.
  3. Mediation (Optional): If the resolution session is unsuccessful, you and the district may agree to participate in mediation.
  4. Due Process Hearing: If the dispute is not resolved, a due process hearing will be conducted by an impartial hearing officer. You and the district will present evidence and witnesses.
  5. Hearing Officer Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision within a specified timeframe.

Important Considerations:

  • Statute of Limitations: There is a statute of limitations for filing a due process complaint (typically two years from the date of the action you are challenging).
  • Legal Representation: Due process is a legal process, and it is highly recommended that you seek legal representation from an attorney experienced in special education law.
  • Stay Put: During the due process proceedings, your child generally has the right to remain in their current educational placement (the "stay put" provision), unless you and the district agree otherwise.

What to Do:

  • Consult with an Attorney: If you are considering filing for due process, consult with an attorney immediately.
  • Gather Evidence: Collect all relevant documentation to support your case.
  • File a Timely Complaint: File the due process complaint within the statute of limitations.
  • Prepare for the Hearing: Work closely with your attorney to prepare for the due process hearing.

Remember, acting quickly and understanding your rights are crucial. Don't hesitate to seek professional legal advice to protect your child's educational rights in Goose Creek CISD.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
