Requesting an Evaluation in Lubbock ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Lubbock ISD

This guide outlines the process for requesting a special education evaluation for your child in Lubbock Independent School District (LISD) under Texas law. It details your rights and the school district's obligations throughout the evaluation process. Understanding these procedures is crucial to advocating effectively for your child's educational needs.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is submitting a formal, written request to LISD. While a verbal request might be acknowledged, a written request creates a clear record and triggers specific timelines under the law. The written request should be addressed to the appropriate school official. This is typically the school principal, special education director, or a designated administrator responsible for special education services at your child's school. It's advisable to copy the school's special education department on your request.

Your written request should be clear, concise, and include the following information:

  • Your Child's Full Name and Date of Birth: Accurate identification is essential.
  • Your Child's School and Grade Level: This helps the school identify the appropriate personnel.
  • Your Contact Information: Include your phone number, email address, and mailing address.
  • A Statement Expressing Concern: Clearly state that you are requesting a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services due to concerns about your child's academic, behavioral, or developmental progress.
  • Specific Areas of Concern: Be as specific as possible about the areas where your child is struggling. For example, you might mention difficulties with reading comprehension, math problem-solving, following directions, social interaction, or exhibiting disruptive behaviors. Providing concrete examples is helpful. Mention any interventions or supports that have already been tried and their effectiveness (or lack thereof).
  • Supporting Documentation (Optional but Recommended): Include any relevant documentation that supports your concerns. This could include report cards, teacher notes, standardized test scores, medical records, or independent evaluations. While not required at this initial stage, providing this information can expedite the process.
  • Your Signature and Date: A signed and dated request demonstrates your formal intent.

Keep a copy of your written request for your records. You can deliver the request in person (and request a signed receipt acknowledging its receipt), send it via certified mail with return receipt requested (to have proof of delivery), or email it (and request a read receipt). Documenting the date and method of delivery is crucial for tracking the 45-school-day timeline, discussed below.

Here's a sample template for your request:

[Your Name]
[Your Address]
[Your Phone Number]
[Your Email Address]

[Date]

[School Principal's Name]
[School Name]
[School Address]

Re: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Child's Full Name]

Dear [Principal's Name],

I am writing to formally request a full and individual initial evaluation for special education services for my child, [Child's Full Name], who is currently in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. [Child's Name]'s date of birth is [Child's Date of Birth].

I have concerns about [Child's Name]'s progress in [Specific Area of Concern, e.g., reading]. For example, [Child's Name] struggles with [Specific Example, e.g., decoding multi-syllable words] and [Another Specific Example, e.g., often avoids reading aloud]. We have tried [Interventions Already Tried, e.g., providing extra reading practice at home], but [Effectiveness, e.g., this has not significantly improved [his/her] reading skills]. [Optional: Other areas of concern could be: math (specifically...), behavior (specifically...), social skills (specifically...)].

I am attaching [Optional: List any attached documents, e.g., a copy of [Child's Name]'s most recent report card].

Thank you for your prompt attention to this matter. I look forward to hearing from you soon to discuss the next steps in the evaluation process.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Typed Name]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once LISD receives your written request for an evaluation, a strict timeline of 45 school days begins. This timeline is mandated by Texas Education Code (TEC) and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

The 45-school-day timeline encompasses several key steps:

  1. Review of Existing Data (Initial): The school must review existing data on your child, including academic records, previous evaluations, teacher observations, and information provided by you. This helps them determine if there is reason to suspect a disability.
  2. Determination of Need for Evaluation: Within a reasonable time after receiving the request, but well within the 45-day window, the school must decide whether or not to conduct an evaluation. If they suspect a disability, they *must* proceed with the evaluation. If they determine an evaluation is not warranted, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons. This explanation must include information about your right to appeal their decision.
  3. Obtaining Parental Consent: If the school decides to proceed with an evaluation, they must obtain your written consent before conducting any assessments. They must provide you with a clear explanation of the proposed evaluation plan, including the specific areas to be assessed and the types of assessments that will be used.
  4. Conducting the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE): The school must complete the FIE within the 45-school-day timeline. The evaluation must be comprehensive and address all areas of suspected disability.
  5. Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) Committee Meeting: After the evaluation is completed, the ARD committee, which includes you, school administrators, teachers, and evaluation specialists, must meet to review the evaluation results. The ARD committee will determine whether your child has a disability that qualifies them for special education services. If a disability is identified, the ARD committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to your child's specific needs.

It is crucial to understand that these are *school* days, not calendar days. Weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted. Keep accurate records of school days to ensure the timeline is followed correctly. If the school anticipates needing more time (for example, due to unforeseen circumstances), they must notify you in writing and request an extension. You have the right to refuse the extension.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment of your child's educational needs. It must be individualized to your child and address all areas of suspected disability. Under Texas law, the FIE must meet specific requirements to ensure its validity and reliability. The evaluation must be conducted by qualified professionals who are trained and experienced in administering and interpreting the specific assessments used.

The FIE must include, at a minimum, the following components:

  • A Review of Existing Data: This includes academic records, previous evaluations, teacher observations, and parent input.
  • An Assessment of Academic Achievement: This should include standardized tests to assess reading, writing, math, and other relevant academic skills.
  • An Assessment of Intellectual Ability (Cognitive Functioning): This typically involves an IQ test to measure your child's cognitive abilities. The specific test used will depend on the child's age and developmental level.
  • An Assessment of Adaptive Behavior: This assesses your child's ability to function independently in daily life, including skills such as self-care, communication, and social skills. Parents and teachers are often asked to complete adaptive behavior scales.
  • An Assessment of Communication Skills: This includes an evaluation of your child's receptive language (understanding language) and expressive language (using language).
  • An Assessment of Motor Skills (If Applicable): If there are concerns about your child's motor skills, the evaluation should include an assessment of gross motor skills (large muscle movements) and fine motor skills (small muscle movements).
  • An Assessment of Social and Emotional Functioning (If Applicable): If there are concerns about your child's social or emotional functioning, the evaluation should include an assessment of their behavior, emotional regulation, and social skills. This may involve observations, interviews, and standardized questionnaires.
  • A Medical Evaluation (If Necessary): If there are concerns about a medical condition that may be contributing to your child's difficulties, the school may request a medical evaluation from your child's doctor.
  • Other Assessments as Needed: Depending on the specific concerns, the FIE may also include other assessments, such as a vision screening, a hearing screening, or an occupational therapy evaluation.

The FIE report must provide a clear and comprehensive summary of the evaluation findings. It should include a detailed description of the assessments used, the scores obtained, and the evaluator's interpretation of the results. The report must also include recommendations for educational interventions and supports that may be helpful for your child. You are entitled to a copy of the complete FIE report.

Child Find Obligations

Lubbock ISD, like all school districts in Texas, has a legal obligation under IDEA to actively identify, locate, and evaluate all children with disabilities who reside within its boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school. This is known as "Child Find."

The Child Find mandate means that LISD cannot simply wait for parents to request evaluations. They have a proactive responsibility to identify children who may be eligible for special education services. This includes children who are:

  • Not yet enrolled in school (e.g., preschool-aged children).
  • Enrolled in private schools or homeschools.
  • Experiencing academic or behavioral difficulties in school.
  • Suspected of having a disability, even if they are performing at grade level.

If you suspect that your child has a disability, even if they are not enrolled in LISD or are homeschooled, you have the right to request an evaluation. LISD is obligated to respond to your request and conduct an evaluation if there is reason to suspect a disability. LISD conducts Child Find activities through screenings, observations, and referrals from parents, teachers, doctors, and other community professionals. If you believe LISD is failing to meet its Child Find obligations, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

What If They Refuse

LISD may refuse to conduct an evaluation if they determine that there is no reason to suspect that your child has a disability. However, they must provide you with a written explanation of their reasons for refusing the evaluation. This explanation must include information about your right to appeal their decision.

If you disagree with LISD's decision to refuse an evaluation, you have several options:

  • Request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE): You have the right to obtain an IEE at public expense if you disagree with the school's evaluation. You must first request the IEE from LISD. LISD has the option to either grant your request and pay for the IEE, or to file a due process complaint to demonstrate that their evaluation was appropriate. If LISD prevails in the due process hearing, you still have the right to obtain an IEE, but you will have to pay for it yourself.
  • Request Mediation: Mediation is a voluntary process in which you and LISD meet with a trained mediator to try to resolve your dispute. The mediator helps facilitate communication and explore possible solutions.
  • File a Due Process Complaint: Due process is a formal legal process that allows you to challenge LISD's decision. You must file a written complaint with the TEA. A due process hearing will be conducted by an impartial hearing officer who will hear evidence and make a decision.
  • File a Complaint with the TEA: You can file a complaint with the TEA if you believe that LISD has violated your child's rights under IDEA. The TEA will investigate your complaint and take appropriate action.

It is essential to document all communications with LISD and to keep copies of all relevant documents. Consider seeking legal advice from an attorney specializing in special education law if you are unsure about your rights or the best course of action.

After the Evaluation

Once the FIE is completed, the ARD committee will meet to review the results and determine whether your child is eligible for special education services. As a parent, you are a crucial member of the ARD committee and have the right to participate fully in the meeting.

If the ARD committee determines that your child has a disability that qualifies them for special education services, the committee will develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a written document that outlines your child's educational needs, goals, and the specific services and supports that will be provided to help them achieve those goals. The IEP must be tailored to your child's individual needs and must be based on the evaluation results. It must also be reviewed and updated at least annually.

If the ARD committee determines that your child does not have a disability that qualifies them for special education services, you have the right to disagree with the committee's decision and pursue the dispute resolution options described above (IEE, mediation, due process complaint, complaint with TEA).

Remember, advocating for your child's educational needs is an ongoing process. Stay informed about your rights and LISD's obligations. Communicate regularly with your child's teachers and school administrators. And don't hesitate to seek support from other parents, advocacy organizations, or attorneys specializing in special education law. The Texas Parent 2 Parent organization (txp2p.org) is a valuable resource for parents of children with disabilities in Texas.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
