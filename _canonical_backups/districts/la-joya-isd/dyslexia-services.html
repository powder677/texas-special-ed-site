Dyslexia Services in La Joya ISD

Dyslexia Services in La Joya ISD

This guide provides information about dyslexia services available to students in La Joya Independent School District (ISD) in accordance with Texas state law and guidelines. It is designed to help parents, educators, and students understand the processes involved in identification, evaluation, and support for dyslexia. Please note that specific procedures and programs may be subject to change, so it is always best to consult directly with school personnel for the most up-to-date information.

Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook, updated in 2024 by the Texas Education Agency (TEA), serves as the primary resource for districts and schools in Texas regarding dyslexia identification, instruction, and support. La Joya ISD adheres to the guidelines outlined in this handbook.

Key Updates and Information in the 2024 Handbook:

  • Legal Framework: The handbook clarifies the legal mandates related to dyslexia under Texas law, including the requirements for screening, evaluation, and intervention as outlined in the Texas Education Code and the Texas Administrative Code.
  • Definition of Dyslexia: The handbook provides a comprehensive definition of dyslexia, consistent with the International Dyslexia Association's definition. This definition emphasizes the neurobiological origin of dyslexia and its impact on accurate and/or fluent word recognition and spelling abilities.
  • Screening Procedures: The handbook details the required screening procedures for students in kindergarten through second grade, including the recommended screening instruments and the criteria for identifying students at risk for dyslexia.
  • Evaluation Procedures: The handbook outlines the process for conducting comprehensive evaluations for students suspected of having dyslexia. This includes information on the types of assessments that should be included in the evaluation, such as measures of phonological awareness, reading fluency, decoding skills, and spelling.
  • Instructional Components: The handbook emphasizes the importance of providing explicit, systematic, and multisensory instruction to students with dyslexia. It describes the key components of effective dyslexia instruction, including phonological awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension.
  • Structured Literacy: The handbook advocates for the use of structured literacy approaches in providing instruction to students with dyslexia. Structured literacy is a systematic and explicit approach to teaching reading that focuses on the essential components of language.
  • Accommodations and Modifications: The handbook provides guidance on the types of accommodations and modifications that can be used to support students with dyslexia in the classroom.
  • Parent Involvement: The handbook stresses the importance of parent involvement in the dyslexia identification and intervention process. It encourages schools to communicate regularly with parents and to provide them with information and resources to support their child's learning.
  • Technology: The handbook discusses the use of technology to support students with dyslexia, including assistive technology and educational software.

Where to Access the Texas Dyslexia Handbook:

The latest version of the Texas Dyslexia Handbook can be found on the Texas Education Agency (TEA) website. Search for "Texas Dyslexia Handbook 2024" on the TEA website to download the document.

Mandatory Screening

Texas law mandates dyslexia screening for all students in kindergarten, first grade, and second grade. La Joya ISD implements these screenings to identify students who may be at risk for dyslexia early on.

Screening Process in La Joya ISD:

  • Universal Screening: All students in kindergarten, first, and second grade will participate in universal screening for dyslexia.
  • Screening Instruments: La Joya ISD uses TEA-approved screening instruments that assess key indicators of dyslexia risk, such as:
    • Phonological awareness (e.g., rhyming, blending, segmenting)
    • Rapid automatized naming (RAN)
    • Letter-sound knowledge
    • Nonsense word decoding
    • Spelling
  • Timeline: Screening typically occurs at the beginning and middle of the school year. Parents will be notified of the screening schedule.
  • Notification of Results: Parents will be notified if their child's screening results indicate a potential risk for dyslexia. This notification will include information about the screening results and recommendations for further evaluation or intervention, if necessary.
  • Next Steps: If a student is identified as being at risk, the school will discuss appropriate interventions and support with the parents. This may include targeted intervention within the general education classroom, or referral for a more comprehensive evaluation.

Important Considerations:

  • Screening is not a diagnosis. It identifies students who are at risk and may need further evaluation.
  • Parents have the right to request a full individual evaluation (FIE) for dyslexia at any time, regardless of screening results.

Structured Literacy Programs

La Joya ISD utilizes structured literacy programs to provide explicit, systematic, and multisensory instruction to students with dyslexia. Structured literacy focuses on the essential components of reading and spelling and teaches these components in a sequential and cumulative manner.

Key Features of Structured Literacy Programs in La Joya ISD:

  • Explicit Instruction: Skills are taught directly and explicitly, with clear explanations and modeling.
  • Systematic and Cumulative: Skills are taught in a logical sequence, building upon previously learned concepts. New skills are integrated with previously learned skills to ensure mastery.
  • Multisensory Approach: Instruction engages multiple senses (visual, auditory, kinesthetic, tactile) to enhance learning and memory.
  • Focus on Phonological Awareness: Instruction includes activities that develop phonological awareness skills, such as rhyming, blending, segmenting, and manipulating sounds in words.
  • Phonics Instruction: Instruction teaches the relationships between letters and sounds (phonics) and how to decode words.
  • Fluency Practice: Instruction includes activities that improve reading fluency, such as repeated reading and choral reading.
  • Vocabulary Development: Instruction focuses on building vocabulary through direct instruction and contextual learning.
  • Comprehension Strategies: Instruction teaches students strategies for understanding and remembering what they read.

504 vs. IEP for Dyslexia

Students with dyslexia may be eligible for support under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act or through an Individualized Education Program (IEP) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). The determination of which plan is appropriate depends on the student's individual needs.

Section 504 Plan:

  • Purpose: To ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to the general education program.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for a 504 plan if they have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities (e.g., reading, learning).
  • Focus: Accommodations and modifications to the general education environment and curriculum.
  • Services: May include accommodations such as extended time on tests, preferential seating, assistive technology, and modified assignments. Does not typically involve specialized instruction (unless explicitly required for access).
  • Dyslexia and 504: A student with dyslexia who does not require specialized instruction may be served under a 504 plan. This plan would provide accommodations to help the student access the general education curriculum.

Individualized Education Program (IEP):

  • Purpose: To provide specially designed instruction and related services to students with disabilities who require more intensive support than can be provided through a 504 plan.
  • Eligibility: A student is eligible for an IEP if they have a disability (e.g., specific learning disability, which can include dyslexia) that adversely affects their educational performance and requires specialized instruction.
  • Focus: Specially designed instruction, accommodations, and related services to address the student's specific learning needs.
  • Services: May include specialized reading instruction (e.g., structured literacy programs), speech-language therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, and other related services, in addition to accommodations.
  • Dyslexia and IEP: A student with dyslexia who requires specialized reading instruction and/or related services to make meaningful progress in reading may be eligible for an IEP under the category of Specific Learning Disability.

Key Differences:

  • Level of Support: IEPs provide a higher level of support than 504 plans, including specialized instruction.
  • Eligibility Criteria: IEP eligibility requires a more significant impact on educational performance and the need for specialized instruction.
  • Legal Requirements: IEPs have more extensive legal requirements than 504 plans, including annual goal setting and progress monitoring.

Determining the Appropriate Plan:

The decision of whether a student should be served under a 504 plan or an IEP is made by a team that includes parents, teachers, and other relevant professionals. The team will consider the student's evaluation results, classroom performance, and individual needs to determine the most appropriate plan.

Accommodations List

Accommodations are changes to the way a student learns that do not change the content of what they are learning. They are designed to provide students with equal access to the curriculum and to demonstrate their knowledge and skills. The following is a list of common accommodations for students with dyslexia; the specific accommodations implemented for each student should be determined by their 504 or IEP team.

Common Accommodations for Students with Dyslexia:

  • Presentation Accommodations:
    • Audiobooks and text-to-speech software
    • Large print materials
    • Highlighted text
    • Notes provided by the teacher
    • Reduced workload
    • Copies of teacher's notes
    • Use of a slant board
  • Response Accommodations:
    • Extended time on tests and assignments
    • Use of assistive technology (e.g., speech-to-text software, word processors)
    • Oral responses to test questions
    • Shorter assignments
    • Breaks during testing
    • Use of a calculator
  • Setting Accommodations:
    • Preferential seating (e.g., near the teacher, away from distractions)
    • Quiet testing environment
  • Timing Accommodations:
    • Extended time on assignments and tests
    • Flexible deadlines
    • Breaks during instruction and testing
  • Other Accommodations:
    • Use of graphic organizers
    • Chunking of assignments
    • Direct instruction in study skills
    • Visual aids and manipulatives
    • Frequent checks for understanding
    • Positive reinforcement and encouragement

Important Note: Accommodations should be individualized to meet the student's specific needs and should be regularly reviewed and adjusted as necessary.

How to Request Evaluation

Parents who suspect that their child may have dyslexia have the right to request a full individual evaluation (FIE) from La Joya ISD. The evaluation is provided at no cost to the parent.

Steps to Request an Evaluation:

  1. Contact the School: Contact your child's teacher, school counselor, or principal to express your concerns about your child's reading and/or spelling difficulties.
  2. Submit a Written Request: Submit a written request for a full individual evaluation (FIE) to the school principal or special education coordinator. The request should include:
    • Your child's name and date of birth
    • Your contact information
    • A description of your concerns about your child's reading and/or spelling difficulties
    • Any relevant information or documentation (e.g., previous test scores, teacher observations)
  3. School Response: The school is required to respond to your request within a specified timeframe (usually 15 school days) under Texas Education Agency guidelines. The school will either agree to conduct an evaluation or provide a written explanation of why they are declining to do so.
  4. Evaluation Process: If the school agrees to conduct an evaluation, an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting will be scheduled to discuss the evaluation process and obtain your consent. The evaluation will be conducted by qualified professionals and will assess the student's reading, spelling, and related skills.
  5. ARD Committee Meeting: Once the evaluation is complete, another ARD committee meeting will be held to discuss the evaluation results and determine whether the student meets the criteria for a specific learning disability (including dyslexia) and requires special education services.

Parent Rights:

  • You have the right to participate in all ARD committee meetings.
  • You have the right to review all of your child's educational records.
  • You have the right to obtain an independent educational evaluation (IEE) at your own expense. If the IEE meets certain criteria, the school district may be required to consider it.
  • You have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA) if you believe that the school district has violated your child's rights under IDEA or Section 504.

La Joya ISD Contact Information:

Contact your child's school directly. You can also contact the La Joya ISD Special Education Department for more information about dyslexia services. Please consult the La Joya ISD website for current contact information for the Special Education Department.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

Is my child entitled to dyslexia testing in Texas? +

Yes. Texas law requires all districts to screen students for dyslexia and related disorders in Kindergarten and First Grade, and at any grade level if a parent or teacher requests it.

What accommodations can a dyslexic child get in Texas? +

Accommodations may include extended time on tests, text-to-speech software, oral administration of tests, reduced-answer-choice formats, and use of a calculator. These can be provided through a 504 Plan or an IEP.

What is the Texas Dyslexia Handbook? +

The Texas Dyslexia Handbook is a state guidance document updated in 2024 that outlines districts' responsibilities for identifying, evaluating, and serving students with dyslexia using structured literacy methods.
