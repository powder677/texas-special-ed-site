What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting?

The ARD meeting, which stands for Admission, Review, and Dismissal, is a crucial part of your child's special education journey in San Marcos CISD. It's a meeting specifically designed to discuss and make decisions about your child's Individualized Education Program, or IEP. This meeting is mandated by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code, specifically Chapter 29. Essentially, the ARD committee decides whether your child is eligible for special education services, what those services will look like, and where your child will receive them.

The ARD committee is comprised of several important members. You, as the parent, are a vital and equal member of the team. Other members usually include a school administrator, one or more of your child's teachers (both general education and special education, if applicable), an educational diagnostician or assessment specialist, and any related service providers who work with your child, such as a speech therapist, occupational therapist, or counselor. The school district may also invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise regarding your child, and you, as the parent, also have the right to invite anyone you feel would be beneficial to the process and your child's success. The student is also invited to attend when appropriate, particularly as they get older, to advocate for their needs and preferences. The entire purpose of the ARD meeting is to collaborate and develop a plan that best meets your child's unique learning needs, and your input is crucial to that process.

Your 5-Day Notice Right

San Marcos CISD is required to provide you with written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before the meeting date. This "5-day notice" is not just a formality; it's your right, and it's designed to give you adequate time to prepare for the meeting. This written notice must include several key pieces of information, including the purpose of the meeting (for example, annual review, initial eligibility determination, or amendment of the IEP), the date, time, and location of the meeting, and a list of individuals who will be attending. Importantly, the notice should also inform you of your right to participate in the ARD meeting and your right to invite other individuals who have knowledge or special expertise about your child.

Sometimes, circumstances might arise where you want to hold an ARD meeting sooner than five days. You have the option to waive the 5-day notice requirement in writing. This means you agree to hold the meeting even though you haven't received the full five days' notice. This is entirely your choice, and you should only waive the notice if you feel comfortable and prepared to participate fully in the meeting with less preparation time. Never feel pressured to waive your rights if you need the full five days to gather your thoughts, collect information, or consult with an advocate.

What to Bring to Your ARD

Being prepared for your child's ARD meeting can make a significant difference in the outcome. There are several documents and resources you should consider bringing to help you advocate effectively for your child. First, gather any prior evaluations or assessments of your child, both from the school district and from outside sources. This includes psychological evaluations, educational assessments, speech and language reports, occupational therapy reports, and any other relevant testing. These documents provide valuable information about your child's strengths and areas where they need support.

If your child has seen any doctors or specialists, bring letters or reports from them that outline your child's medical or developmental history and any recommendations they have for your child's education. School records, such as report cards, attendance records, and any documentation of disciplinary actions or academic struggles, can also provide important context. Perhaps most importantly, create a written list of your specific concerns and goals for your child's education. This will help you stay focused during the meeting and ensure that all your questions are addressed. Don't hesitate to bring a trusted advocate or support person with you to the ARD meeting. This could be a family member, friend, or a professional advocate who can help you understand the process and voice your concerns.

Finally, remember that in Texas, it is legal to audio record ARD meetings, provided that you notify the ARD committee in advance that you will be recording. A voice recorder can be helpful for reviewing the discussion later and ensuring that you have an accurate record of the decisions that were made. Make sure you inform the committee at the beginning of the meeting, for example, "I just wanted to let everyone know that I am recording this meeting for my personal records," before you start recording.

The 10-Day Recess Rule

During the ARD meeting, it's possible that new information is presented or a specific decision needs more careful consideration. The 10-day recess rule allows any member of the ARD committee, including you as the parent, to request a temporary pause, or "recess," of the meeting for up to 10 school days. This recess provides time to gather more information, consult with experts or advocates, or simply reflect on the issues being discussed before making a final decision. For example, if a new evaluation is mentioned during the meeting that you haven't had a chance to review, you can request a recess to study the report and formulate your response. Or perhaps you want to get a second opinion from an outside specialist before agreeing to a particular service or placement.

To invoke the 10-day recess rule, simply state that you would like to recess the meeting for a specific purpose. Be clear about what information you need to gather or who you want to consult with during the recess period. The other members of the ARD committee should respect your request and work with you to schedule a date to reconvene the meeting. It is important to understand that the 10 days are school days and do not include weekends or holidays. Don't hesitate to use this rule if you feel that you need more time to make an informed decision about your child's education. It is designed to protect your right to participate meaningfully in the ARD process.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP document is the written plan that outlines your child's special education program. The ARD meeting is where the details of this document are determined. Several key elements are addressed during the ARD meeting and documented in the IEP. "Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance" describe your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic and functional areas. "Annual goals" are measurable goals that your child is expected to achieve within one year. These goals should be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART). The IEP also specifies the services that your child will receive, such as specialized instruction, speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation. It will also note the frequency, duration, and location of these services.

The IEP also includes a section on "placement," which refers to the educational setting where your child will receive their special education services. This could range from a general education classroom with support to a more specialized setting. Accommodations are also listed, such as modifications to assignments, extended time on tests, or preferential seating. Remember that you do not have to sign the IEP document on the same day as the ARD meeting. You have the right to take the document home, review it carefully, and consult with others before making a decision. You should only sign the IEP if you fully understand and agree with the contents.

When You Disagree With the Team

It is perfectly acceptable to disagree with the ARD committee if you believe that their recommendations are not in your child's best interest. You have the right to refuse consent for your child's initial placement in special education. This means that your child cannot be placed in a special education program without your written consent. You also have the right to refuse to sign the IEP if you disagree with any aspect of it. By refusing to sign, you are not necessarily preventing your child from receiving services, but you are indicating that you do not agree with the plan as it is currently written.

You have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the school district's expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation of your child. An IEE is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional who is not employed by the school district. You can request one to gain a different perspective on your child's needs. Furthermore, you have the right to write "parent disagrees" on the IEP document before signing it (or not signing it). This clearly documents your disagreement with the ARD committee's decisions and preserves your right to pursue further action.

Escalation Options

If you are unable to resolve your disagreements with the ARD committee through informal discussions, there are several formal escalation options available to you. These rights are governed by IDEA 2004 and 19 TAC Chapter 89, which is available online through the Texas Education Agency's Legal Framework website.

The first option is to file a TEA State Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a free process where you file a formal written complaint detailing your concerns. TEA will investigate your complaint and issue a decision within 60 days. Secondly, you can pursue mediation through TEA. Mediation is a voluntary process where you and the school district meet with a trained, impartial mediator to try to reach a mutually agreeable resolution. This is also a free process offered by TEA. A third option is to request a Due Process Hearing. This is a more formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and arguments to an impartial hearing officer who will make a decision about your child's special education program. Due process hearings can be complex and may require the assistance of an attorney. Finally, you can file a complaint with the Federal Office for Civil Rights (OCR) if you believe that the school district has discriminated against your child based on their disability. OCR investigates complaints of discrimination and can order the school district to take corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How much notice must a Texas school give before an ARD meeting? +

Texas law requires the district to provide written notice at least 5 calendar days before an ARD meeting. Parents can waive this notice in writing if they choose to meet sooner.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting? +

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, parents have the right to bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding their child, including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney.

What is a 10-day recess in an ARD meeting? +

A parent or the district may request a 10-day recess (pause) during an ARD meeting to gather more information, seek independent advice, or consult an advocate before final decisions are made.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision? +

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request Mediation, or request a Special Education Due Process Hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
