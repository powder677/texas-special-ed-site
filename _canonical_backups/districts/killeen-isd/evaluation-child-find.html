Requesting an Evaluation in Killeen ISD

⚡ Quick Answer

How long does Texas have to complete an evaluation?
Once a written evaluation request is received, the district must complete the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) and hold an ARD meeting within 45 school days. This timeline is strictly enforced by the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Verbal requests do not start the clock — always submit in writing and keep a copy.

Requesting a Special Education Evaluation in Killeen ISD: A Comprehensive Guide

This guide provides parents and guardians of students attending schools within the Killeen Independent School District (KISD) with information about requesting a special education evaluation. Understanding the process, your rights, and the applicable laws is crucial to ensuring your child receives the support they need to succeed academically.

Texas law mandates that public schools identify, locate, and evaluate children with disabilities who need special education and related services. This responsibility falls under the "Child Find" mandate, detailed further below. If you suspect your child may have a disability impacting their educational performance, requesting a special education evaluation is your first step toward accessing appropriate support.

How to Submit a Written Request

The first step in initiating a special education evaluation is to submit a formal, written request to the school district. While a verbal request might prompt some initial discussions, a written request creates a formal record and triggers the legally mandated timeline. This ensures your request is documented and that the school district is obligated to respond within a specific timeframe.

Here's what your written request should include:

* **Date:** Clearly state the date of the request. * **Student's Full Name:** Provide your child's full legal name as it appears on school records. * **Student's Date of Birth:** Include your child's date of birth for identification purposes. * **Student's Grade Level:** State your child's current grade level. * **Student's School:** Specify the name of the KISD school your child attends. * **Your Name and Contact Information:** Include your full name, address, phone number, and email address. Make sure the contact information provided is accurate and easily accessible. * **Relationship to the Student:** Clearly state your relationship to the student (e.g., parent, legal guardian). * **Reason for Request:** This is the most crucial part of your request. Clearly and concisely explain why you believe your child needs a special education evaluation. Be specific and provide concrete examples of your child's difficulties in the classroom or at home that impact their learning. This might include:
  • Academic struggles (e.g., difficulty reading, writing, or understanding math concepts).
  • Behavioral concerns (e.g., difficulty focusing, hyperactivity, aggression).
  • Communication difficulties (e.g., speech delays, trouble understanding or expressing language).
  • Social-emotional challenges (e.g., anxiety, depression, difficulty interacting with peers).
  • Any other concerns that you believe are impacting your child's educational performance.
* **Supporting Documentation (Optional):** If you have any supporting documentation, such as previous evaluations, doctor's notes, or report cards, include copies with your request. This can provide valuable information to the school district. * **Request for Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE):** Clearly state that you are requesting a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) to determine if your child has a disability and is eligible for special education services. * **Signature:** Sign and date the request.

Once completed, send the written request to the appropriate school official. This is typically the school principal or the special education administrator at your child's school. Keep a copy of the request for your records. Certified mail with return receipt requested provides proof of delivery and is strongly recommended.

Example Request:

[Date]

Principal [Principal's Name]

[School Name]

[School Address]

Subject: Request for Special Education Evaluation for [Student's Full Name]

Dear Principal [Principal's Name],

I am writing to request a Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Student's Full Name], who is in [Grade Level] at [School Name]. [Student's Name]'s date of birth is [Date of Birth]. I am [Your Name], the [Relationship to Student] of [Student's Name].

I am concerned about [Student's Name]'s progress in [Specific Subject/Area]. For example, [Specific Example 1, e.g., "he struggles to read grade-level texts"]. Additionally, [Specific Example 2, e.g., "he has difficulty staying focused in class and often disrupts other students"]. [Specific Example 3, e.g., "He also has difficulty with written expression and struggles to organize his thoughts on paper"]. I believe these difficulties are impacting his educational performance and may indicate a disability.

I am requesting a Full and Individual Evaluation to determine if [Student's Name] is eligible for special education services. I have attached [Optional: List any attached documents, e.g., "a copy of his most recent report card"].

Thank you for your time and consideration. I look forward to hearing from you soon.

Sincerely,

[Your Name]

[Your Address]

[Your Phone Number]

[Your Email Address]

The 45 School Day Timeline

Once the school district receives your written request for a special education evaluation, they are required to adhere to a specific timeline dictated by Texas law. This timeline is crucial to ensuring that your child's needs are addressed in a timely manner.

The district has **45 school days** from the date they receive your written request to complete the following steps:

1. **Decision to Evaluate:** The ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee, including you as the parent, must decide whether or not to conduct an evaluation. This decision is based on whether or not there is reason to suspect the child has a disability and needs special education services. This is not a full-blown ARD meeting for IEP development; it is specifically to decide about the evaluation. 2. **Obtain Parental Consent:** If the ARD committee decides to evaluate, the district must obtain your written consent to proceed with the evaluation. They cannot proceed without your informed consent. You have the right to refuse consent. 3. **Conduct the Evaluation:** Once consent is obtained, the district must conduct the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE). This involves gathering information about your child's strengths and weaknesses in various areas, including academic achievement, cognitive abilities, communication skills, and social-emotional functioning. 4. **Complete the Evaluation Report:** The evaluation team must compile a comprehensive report summarizing their findings and making recommendations regarding eligibility for special education services. 5. **ARD Meeting to Determine Eligibility:** After the evaluation is complete, an ARD committee meeting must be held to review the evaluation results, determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for special education services, and, if eligible, develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

It's important to note that this is a *school day* timeline, meaning that weekends, holidays, and school breaks are not counted. The clock starts ticking the day the school receives your written request.

If the district fails to meet the 45-school-day timeline, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). Contacting an advocate or attorney can provide assistance with this process.

What the FIE Must Cover

The Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment designed to determine if your child has a disability that impacts their educational performance and requires special education services. The FIE must be tailored to your child's specific needs and concerns and should cover all areas of suspected disability.

The FIE typically includes assessments in the following areas:

* **Cognitive Abilities:** Assessing your child's intellectual functioning, problem-solving skills, and learning potential. This often involves standardized intelligence tests (IQ tests). * **Academic Achievement:** Evaluating your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas. This may involve standardized achievement tests and classroom-based assessments. * **Communication Skills:** Assessing your child's receptive and expressive language skills, articulation, fluency, and social communication abilities. This is particularly important if you suspect a speech or language impairment. * **Social-Emotional Functioning:** Evaluating your child's emotional well-being, social skills, and behavior. This may involve observations, interviews, and rating scales. * **Adaptive Behavior:** Assessing your child's ability to function independently in everyday life, including self-care, social skills, and community living skills. * **Motor Skills:** Evaluating your child's fine and gross motor skills, coordination, and physical abilities. This is particularly important if you suspect a physical disability. * **Sensory Processing:** Assessing how your child processes sensory information (e.g., sights, sounds, touch, movement). This is relevant if you suspect sensory sensitivities or processing difficulties.

In addition to these core areas, the FIE may also include other assessments based on your child's specific needs and concerns. For example, if your child has a history of medical issues, the evaluation may include a review of their medical records. If your child is suspected of having Autism, specific autism evaluation protocols should be used. The evaluation team should gather information from multiple sources, including you as the parent, teachers, and other relevant professionals. Observation of your child in the classroom and other settings is also essential.

It is critical that the FIE is conducted by qualified professionals who are trained in the appropriate assessment tools and procedures. The evaluation report should clearly explain the assessment results, provide a diagnosis (if applicable), and make recommendations for appropriate special education services and supports.

Child Find Obligations

Texas law places a significant responsibility on school districts to proactively identify, locate, and evaluate children with disabilities who may be in need of special education and related services. This is known as the "Child Find" mandate.

Child Find applies to all children residing within the district's boundaries, regardless of whether they are enrolled in public school, private school, or homeschooled. It also applies to children who are suspected of having a disability, even if they are not currently exhibiting significant academic difficulties. The duty exists whether you've asked the school to take action or not.

KISD is required to have procedures in place to identify and evaluate children with disabilities. This includes:

* **Screening and Observation:** Regularly screening and observing students to identify those who may be at risk for academic or behavioral difficulties. * **Referral Process:** Establishing a clear and accessible referral process for parents, teachers, and other professionals to request an evaluation. * **Public Awareness Activities:** Conducting public awareness activities to inform parents and the community about special education services and the Child Find mandate.

If you suspect that your child may have a disability, even if they are not currently enrolled in KISD or are being homeschooled, you have the right to request an evaluation. The district is obligated to respond to your request and determine if an evaluation is warranted. The Child Find mandate is a critical component of ensuring that all children with disabilities have access to the special education services they need to reach their full potential.

What If They Refuse

While KISD is legally obligated to respond to your request for a special education evaluation, there are instances where the school district may refuse to conduct an evaluation. If the district refuses to evaluate your child, they must provide you with written notice explaining their reasons for the refusal.

Common reasons for refusing an evaluation include:

* **Lack of Evidence:** The district may argue that there is insufficient evidence to suggest that your child has a disability that is impacting their educational performance. * **Intervention Strategies:** The district may argue that they need to implement intervention strategies (e.g., Response to Intervention or RTI) before conducting an evaluation. While RTI can be a valuable tool, it should not be used to delay or deny a special education evaluation if there is reason to suspect a disability. * **Academic Performance:** The district may argue that your child is performing at grade level and therefore does not need special education services. However, a child can be performing at grade level and still have a disability that requires special education support. * **Behavioral Issues:** The district might claim that behavior problems are a disciplinary issue and not disability-related. This should not be used as a blanket denial; the connection between potential disability and behavior must be explored.

If the district refuses to evaluate your child, you have several options:

1. **Request a Meeting:** Request a meeting with the school district to discuss their reasons for refusing the evaluation and to present any additional information you have to support your request. 2. **File a Complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA):** You have the right to file a formal complaint with the TEA if you believe that the district has violated your child's rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) or state special education laws. 3. **Request Mediation:** Mediation is a process where a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach an agreement. The TEA offers free mediation services to parents of children with disabilities. 4. **Due Process Hearing:** A due process hearing is a formal legal proceeding where you present evidence and argue your case before an impartial hearing officer. This is a more complex and time-consuming process, and it is generally recommended to seek legal representation. 5. **Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE):** You have the right to request an IEE at public expense if you disagree with the district's evaluation. The district must either pay for the IEE or file a due process hearing to prove their evaluation was appropriate.

It is important to remember that you have the right to advocate for your child's needs. Do not hesitate to seek assistance from advocacy organizations, legal aid services, or attorneys specializing in special education law.

After the Evaluation

Once the Full and Individual Evaluation (FIE) is complete, the next step is to convene an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) committee meeting to review the evaluation results and determine if your child is eligible for special education services. You, as the parent, are a critical member of the ARD committee.

At the ARD meeting, the committee will discuss the following:

* **Evaluation Results:** The evaluation team will present their findings and explain the results of the various assessments conducted. * **Eligibility Determination:** The committee will determine if your child meets the eligibility criteria for one or more of the specific disability categories recognized under IDEA (e.g., specific learning disability, autism, emotional disturbance). * **Present Levels of Academic Achievement and Functional Performance (PLAAFP):** The committee will develop a PLAAFP statement that describes your child's current strengths and weaknesses in academic and functional areas. * **Goals:** If your child is found eligible for special education services, the committee will develop measurable annual goals to address their specific needs. * **Services and Supports:** The committee will determine the appropriate special education services and supports that your child needs to achieve their goals. This may include specialized instruction, related services (e.g., speech therapy, occupational therapy), accommodations, and modifications. * **Placement:** The committee will determine the appropriate placement for your child, which is the least restrictive environment (LRE) where they can receive a free appropriate public education (FAPE). LRE means that your child should be educated with their non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

The outcome of the ARD meeting will be documented in an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The IEP is a legally binding document that outlines your child's goals, services, and supports. You have the right to disagree with any aspect of the IEP. If you disagree, you can request a recess of the ARD meeting to gather more information or seek outside opinions. If you are still not satisfied, you have the right to pursue dispute resolution options, such as mediation or a due process hearing.

Remember, the IEP is a living document that should be reviewed and updated at least annually, or more frequently if needed. You have the right to request an ARD meeting at any time to discuss your child's progress or to make changes to the IEP. Advocacy groups and attorneys specializing in special education can be invaluable resources to help you navigate the ARD process and ensure that your child receives the appropriate special education services and supports.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

How do I request a special education evaluation in Texas? +

Submit a written request directly to the school's principal or special education director. The written request starts the mandatory 45 school-day clock. Keep a dated copy for your records.

What is a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) in Texas? +

A Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) is a comprehensive assessment the school must complete to determine if your child has a disability and needs special education services. It must cover all areas of suspected disability and be completed within 45 school days of your written consent.

Can a Texas school refuse to evaluate my child? +

Yes, but only if the district provides written notice (Prior Written Notice) explaining why they are refusing and what data they relied on. You have the right to challenge that refusal through mediation or a due process hearing.
