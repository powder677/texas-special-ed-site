Is a 10-day suspension a Change of Placement?
Yes. Under IDEA, suspending a special education student for more than 10 cumulative school days in a year triggers a Change of Placement and requires a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) within 10 business days. The school must convene an ARD — you have the right to attend.

Urgent Guide: Discipline Rights & Grievances in Allen ISD

This guide is for parents who believe their child has been unfairly disciplined or whose rights under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) have been violated in Allen ISD. Act quickly. Time is of the essence. This is not legal advice, but a starting point to understand your rights and options. Consult with an attorney specializing in special education law immediately.

The 10-Day Suspension Rule

Allen ISD, like all school districts, has rules regarding suspensions. It's CRUCIAL to understand the 10-day suspension rule, especially if your child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) or a Section 504 plan. Here's the KEY POINT: **If your child is suspended for more than 10 school days in a school year (whether consecutive or cumulative), it can trigger significant procedural safeguards and require a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR).** This also applies to any series of shorter suspensions that create a pattern and constitute a change of placement.

Why is this important? Because a suspension exceeding 10 days (or a pattern of shorter suspensions) can be considered a "Change of Placement" under IDEA, meaning the school must determine if the behavior leading to the suspension was caused by or directly related to your child's disability. If it WAS, then the school cannot simply continue to suspend or expel your child without addressing the underlying issues related to their IEP or 504 plan.

What to do IMMEDIATELY:

  • Track Suspension Days: Keep a precise record of every suspension day your child has served this school year. Note the dates, reasons for the suspension, and length of the suspension.
  • Question Suspensions: If you believe a suspension is unfair or disproportionate, document your concerns in writing to the principal and special education director. Don't delay!
  • Demand an MDR if Applicable: If your child is approaching or has exceeded 10 cumulative suspension days, or if the suspensions appear to create a pattern of exclusion, IMMEDIATELY request a Manifestation Determination Review in writing. Use certified mail with return receipt requested to ensure proof of delivery.

What Is an MDR (Manifestation Determination Review)?

A Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) is a critical process under IDEA designed to determine whether a student's behavior that led to disciplinary action was a direct result of their disability or a failure by the school district to implement their IEP. This is VITAL for protecting students with disabilities from being punished for behaviors they can't control due to their disability.

Who participates in the MDR? Typically, the MDR team includes: you (the parent), relevant members of the IEP team (teachers, specialists, administrators), and any other individuals who have knowledge of the child and the behavior in question.

What happens during the MDR meeting? The team will review all relevant information, including:

  • Your child's IEP or 504 plan.
  • The child's disciplinary history.
  • Any relevant evaluations or assessments.
  • Observations of the child's behavior.
  • Information provided by the parent.

The team must answer TWO crucial questions:

  1. Was the conduct in question caused by, or did it have a direct and substantial relationship to, the child's disability?
  2. Was the conduct in question the direct result of the school district's failure to implement the child's IEP?

OUTCOMES OF AN MDR:

  • If the answer to EITHER question is YES: The behavior IS considered a manifestation of the child's disability. The school MUST take immediate steps to address the underlying causes of the behavior. This may involve revising the IEP, providing additional supports, or implementing a positive behavior intervention plan. The student must be returned to their current placement (unless the parents and school agree to a change of placement as part of the IEP revisions). The student can only be disciplined to the same extent as a non-disabled student if the district failed to implement the IEP.
  • If the answer to BOTH questions is NO: The behavior is NOT considered a manifestation of the child's disability. The school can discipline the child in the same manner as a non-disabled student. However, the school STILL MUST provide services during the period of suspension or expulsion to ensure the child can continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals.

IMPORTANT POINTS:

  • The MDR must be conducted within 10 school days of the decision to change the student's placement due to a disciplinary infraction. ACT FAST!
  • As a parent, you have the right to present information and participate fully in the MDR process. Prepare thoroughly. Bring documentation, witness statements (if applicable), and a clear understanding of your child's disability and how it may have contributed to the behavior.
  • If you disagree with the outcome of the MDR, you have the right to appeal. This is where legal counsel becomes essential.

Change of Placement

A "Change of Placement" is a significant term under IDEA. It goes beyond a simple suspension. It means any alteration in your child's educational setting that affects their ability to access the curriculum and receive the services outlined in their IEP. This can include:

  • Suspension for more than 10 consecutive school days.
  • A series of shorter suspensions that constitute a pattern of exclusion.
  • Expulsion.
  • Transfer to a different school or program.
  • In-school suspension that significantly limits access to the general education curriculum.

Key Considerations Regarding Change of Placement:

  • Parental Consent: Generally, a Change of Placement requires parental consent, especially if it involves a significant alteration of the IEP. The school cannot unilaterally change your child's placement without your agreement (unless through a formal due process hearing).
  • Manifestation Determination: As mentioned above, any Change of Placement triggered by disciplinary action MUST be preceded by a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) if the child has an IEP or 504 plan.
  • Stay Put Provision: If you disagree with a proposed Change of Placement, you have the right to invoke the "stay put" provision. This means your child remains in their current placement while you challenge the proposed change through due process proceedings. Invoke this RIGHT IN WRITING IMMEDIATELY!
  • Services During Removal: Even if a Change of Placement is deemed appropriate after an MDR (and the behavior is not a manifestation of the disability), the school district is STILL responsible for providing services to ensure your child can continue to participate in the general education curriculum and progress toward their IEP goals during the period of removal.

IF THE SCHOOL IS ATTEMPTING TO CHANGE YOUR CHILD'S PLACEMENT, CONSULT WITH AN ATTORNEY IMMEDIATELY. THIS IS A CRITICAL JUNCTURE.

Filing a Level 1 Grievance

Allen ISD likely has a grievance policy outlined in its student handbook or on its website. This is often the first formal step in addressing concerns about disciplinary actions or violations of student rights. A grievance is a formal complaint alleging that the school district has violated a policy, procedure, or law.

Level 1 typically involves:

  • Filing a written complaint with the principal or other designated administrator. The specific procedures will be outlined in the Allen ISD grievance policy.
  • Specifying the nature of the grievance: Clearly and concisely state what happened, when it happened, who was involved, and why you believe it violates your child's rights. Reference specific policies or laws if possible (e.g., IDEA, 504, Allen ISD student code of conduct).
  • Providing supporting documentation: Include copies of relevant documents, such as suspension notices, IEPs, emails, and witness statements.
  • Stating the desired outcome: What specific action do you want the school to take to resolve the grievance? (e.g., removal of the suspension, revision of the IEP, apology, training for staff).

Important Considerations:

  • Strict Deadlines: Grievance policies typically have strict deadlines for filing complaints. MISSING THE DEADLINE CAN MEAN LOSING YOUR RIGHT TO PURSUE THE GRIEVANCE. Find the Allen ISD policy and adhere to it precisely.
  • Keep Copies: Keep copies of EVERYTHING you submit.
  • Follow Up: If you don't receive a response within the timeframe specified in the policy, follow up in writing.
  • Level 2 Grievance: If you are not satisfied with the outcome of the Level 1 grievance, you will likely have the option to appeal to a higher level within the school district administration (e.g., the superintendent). The grievance policy will outline the process for appealing to Level 2.

While a Level 1 grievance might seem like a simple process, it's important to approach it strategically. Consider consulting with an advocate or attorney to help you prepare your complaint and ensure you are presenting your case effectively.

TEA State Complaint

If you believe Allen ISD has violated a requirement of IDEA or Section 504, you have the right to file a formal complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA). This is a more formal process than a school district grievance and can trigger a TEA investigation.

To file a TEA complaint, you must:

  • Submit the complaint in writing.
  • Sign the complaint.
  • Include a statement that Allen ISD has violated a requirement of IDEA or Section 504.
  • Provide the facts on which the complaint is based. Be specific and detailed. Include dates, names, and descriptions of the events.
  • Describe the specific violation you are alleging. Refer to the relevant sections of IDEA or Section 504.
  • Provide a proposed resolution to the problem. What specific action do you want TEA to take to remedy the violation?
  • File the complaint within one year of the date of the alleged violation.

TEA will investigate the complaint and issue a written decision. If TEA finds that Allen ISD has violated IDEA or Section 504, it will order the school district to take corrective action.

Filing a TEA complaint can be a complex process. Consider seeking assistance from a special education advocate or attorney. TEA provides information and complaint forms on its website.

Due Process

Due process is the MOST FORMAL and SERIOUS step you can take. It is a legal procedure available under IDEA to resolve disputes between parents and schools regarding the identification, evaluation, or educational placement of a child with a disability. This involves a formal hearing before an impartial hearing officer.

When to Consider Due Process:

  • When you disagree with the school district's decision regarding your child's IEP.
  • When you believe the school district has failed to provide your child with a free and appropriate public education (FAPE).
  • When you disagree with the results of a Manifestation Determination Review.
  • When you disagree with a proposed Change of Placement.
  • When you have exhausted other informal and formal resolution options (such as IEP meetings, grievances, and mediation) and are still unable to resolve the dispute.

Due Process Procedures:

  • Filing a Due Process Complaint: You must file a formal written complaint with the school district and the Texas Education Agency (TEA). The complaint must include specific information about the nature of the dispute.
  • Resolution Session: Within 15 days of receiving the due process complaint, the school district must convene a resolution session with you and relevant members of the IEP team. The purpose of the resolution session is to attempt to resolve the dispute informally.
  • Mediation: If the resolution session is unsuccessful, you and the school district may agree to participate in mediation. Mediation is a voluntary process in which a neutral third party helps you and the school district reach a mutually agreeable resolution.
  • Due Process Hearing: If mediation is unsuccessful or you choose not to participate in mediation, a due process hearing will be conducted before an impartial hearing officer. You have the right to present evidence, call witnesses, and cross-examine witnesses.
  • Hearing Officer Decision: The hearing officer will issue a written decision within 45 days of the hearing. The decision is binding on both you and the school district (unless appealed).

VERY IMPORTANT: Due process is a complex legal process. YOU NEED AN ATTORNEY SPECIALIZING IN SPECIAL EDUCATION LAW TO REPRESENT YOU. The stakes are high, and you need experienced legal counsel to protect your child's rights.

This guide is intended to provide general information only and should not be considered legal advice. Contact an attorney specializing in special education law immediately to discuss your specific situation and legal options. Time is NOT on your side. Act now to protect your child's rights.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every parent in Texas needs to know.

What is a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR) in Texas? +

An MDR is a meeting that must occur within 10 business days when a special education student is suspended for more than 10 cumulative days or faces expulsion. The ARD team determines whether the behavior was caused by the child's disability.

How do I file a Level 1 Grievance with a Texas school district? +

A Level 1 Grievance is filed directly with the campus principal within the timeframe stated in your district's grievance policy (often 15 days of the incident). Submit it in writing, describe the specific policy violated, and keep a copy.

What is a Change of Placement for a special education student? +

A Change of Placement occurs when a district removes a special education student for more than 10 consecutive or cumulative school days in a year. It triggers procedural protections including an MDR and requires an ARD meeting.
