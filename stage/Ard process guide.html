⚡ Quick Answer

What is an ARD meeting in Texas?
An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the IEP meeting required by Texas law where the school and parents together review a child's special education eligibility and services. Parents must receive written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting and may request a 10-day recess at any time to seek advice.

What Is an ARD Meeting in Abilene ISD?

An ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting is the Texas equivalent of an IEP meeting. Required by IDEA and Texas Education Code Chapter 29, it's where eligibility is determined, the IEP is developed, and progress is reviewed.

If your child attends school in Abilene ISD, understanding this process is the key to securing the services they deserve. The ARD committee — which includes you — makes binding decisions about your child's education for the entire year.

You're not a guest at this meeting. You're a legal member of the committee — with equal standing to the principal or diagnostician.
· · ·

Your 5-Day Notice Right

You have the right to receive written notice of an ARD meeting at least five calendar days before it takes place. The notice must state the meeting's purpose (Annual Review, Change of Placement, MDR, etc.), the location, and who from the district will attend.

You can waive the 5-day notice in writing in an emergency — but the school cannot pressure you to do so.

What to Bring to Your Abilene ARD

Coming prepared shifts the balance of power. Don't walk in empty-handed:

  1. Outside Medical Records — diagnoses and letters from private therapists, psychologists, or pediatricians.
  2. A Written List of Concerns — you will forget your talking points in the heat of the moment. Bring a printed list.
  3. Previous IEPs & Progress Reports — have the data ready to show whether last year's goals were actually met.
  4. A Support Person — you can legally bring an advocate, friend, or attorney. Never go alone if the meeting is contentious.
· · ·

The 10-Day Recess Rule

If at any point during the meeting you feel overwhelmed or pressured, you have a legally protected way to pause: request a 10-day recess.

Simply say: "I am requesting a 10-day recess to review this information." The Abilene ISD committee is required to grant it. The meeting pauses and must reconvene within 10 school days — before anything is finalized.

Understanding the IEP Document

The IEP is a legally binding contract. These are the four sections that matter most — and what to check in each one:

  1. Present Levels (PLAAFP) Describes your child's current strengths and needs. Push back if it's vague or doesn't match what you see at home — it should be grounded in real assessment data.
  2. Annual Goals Must be measurable. "Will improve reading" is not a legal goal. "Will read 90 words per minute by May" is. If you can't measure it, it doesn't count.
  3. Special Education Services Lists exactly what your child receives — Speech Therapy, Resource Room, OT, etc. Verify the exact minutes and frequency per week, not just the service name.
  4. Accommodations Changes that help your child access learning — extended time, preferential seating, graphic organizers. Make sure they're specific enough to actually be implemented.
· · ·

When You Disagree With the Team

Disagreeing is your right — not a confrontation. If you disagree with a proposed initial placement, you can refuse consent outright. Services cannot begin without your written agreement.

If you disagree with a revised IEP: do not sign the "Agree" box. Sign "Disagree" instead, request your 10-day recess, and ask the district to write your specific concerns into the official meeting minutes. You also have the right to request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at the district's expense if you believe their testing is flawed.

· · ·

If Informal Disagreement Doesn't Work: Your Escalation Options

If you can't resolve things at the table, these are your formal paths — all protected by IDEA and Texas law:

  • TEA State Complaint — free, must be filed within 1 year; TEA investigates and issues a decision within 60 days.
  • Mediation — free through TEA; a neutral third party helps both sides reach an agreement without going to a hearing.
  • Due Process Hearing — formal legal proceeding; a hearing officer makes a binding decision. File within 2 years. Legal representation recommended.
  • Office for Civil Rights (OCR) Complaint — if you believe your child was discriminated against based on disability.

You do not lose your right to services while pursuing any of these options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers every Abilene ISD parent needs to know.

How much notice must the school give before an ARD meeting?

Texas law requires written notice at least 5 calendar days before the meeting. You can waive this in writing if you choose to meet sooner — but the school cannot pressure you to do so.

Can I bring someone to my child's ARD meeting?

Yes. Under IDEA and Texas law, you can bring any individual with knowledge or special expertise regarding your child — including an advocate, educational consultant, or attorney. Notify the school in advance so they're prepared.

What is a 10-day recess and how do I request one?

A 10-day recess is a legally protected pause in the ARD meeting. Say clearly: "I am requesting a 10-day recess." The committee must grant it and reconvene within 10 school days before finalizing the IEP.

What happens if I disagree with the ARD team's decision?

You may refuse to sign the IEP, request a 10-day recess, file a State Complaint with the TEA, request mediation, or request a due process hearing. You do not lose your right to services during this process.
