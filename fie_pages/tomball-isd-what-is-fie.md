# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Tomball ISD Parents

If you're a parent in Tomball ISD and you've heard the term "FIE" mentioned at school or during a meeting, you're not alone in wondering what it means. Whether your child is struggling academically, socially, or behaviorally, understanding the Full Individual Evaluation process is an important first step toward getting them the support they need. This guide is designed specifically for Tomball ISD families to help you navigate this process with confidence and clarity.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A Full Individual Evaluation, commonly called a FIE, is a comprehensive assessment that Texas school districts conduct to determine whether a child qualifies for special education services. Think of it as a detailed look at your child's unique learning profile—their strengths, challenges, and individual needs.

The FIE is sometimes referred to as a "psychoeducational evaluation" and is required under both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and Texas state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29). No child in Texas can receive special education services without first having a FIE completed.

The evaluation is conducted by a team of qualified professionals and looks at many different areas of your child's development and learning. It's designed to give everyone—you, the school, and your child's teachers—a complete picture of how your child learns best.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Tomball ISD?

For Tomball ISD families, the FIE serves several crucial purposes:

**It opens doors to support.** If your child qualifies for special education services based on the FIE results, they become eligible for an Individualized Education Program (IEP) with specialized instruction and related services tailored to their needs.

**It provides a roadmap for learning.** Even beyond eligibility, the FIE gives valuable insights into how your child processes information, what motivates them, and where they may need additional help. This information benefits everyone who works with your child.

**It establishes a baseline.** The FIE creates documentation of your child's current abilities, which helps Tomball ISD track progress over time and adjust services as needed.

**It protects your child's rights.** The FIE process ensures that decisions about your child's education are based on objective, professional assessments rather than assumptions or incomplete information.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is comprehensive by design—it looks at your whole child, not just one area of concern. Depending on your child's suspected needs, the evaluation may include:

- **Cognitive ability** – How your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems
- **Academic achievement** – Current skill levels in reading, writing, math, and other subjects
- **Communication skills** – Speech, language, and how your child expresses themselves
- **Social and emotional development** – How your child interacts with others and manages emotions
- **Adaptive behavior** – Daily living skills and independence
- **Motor skills** – Fine motor (writing, cutting) and gross motor (running, jumping) abilities
- **Health and developmental history** – Medical factors that may affect learning
- **Behavioral concerns** – Attention, focus, and behavioral patterns in different settings
- **Sensory processing** – How your child responds to sensory input like sounds, textures, or lights

The evaluation team at Tomball ISD will determine which areas to assess based on your child's individual concerns. You'll have input into this process, and the team will use multiple methods including observations, standardized tests, interviews, and review of existing records.

## How to Request a FIE at Tomball ISD

Any parent or guardian can request a FIE for their child at any time. Here's how to do it in Tomball ISD:

### Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing

While you can make a verbal request, a written request creates documentation and starts the clock on legal timelines. You can send a letter or email to:

- Your child's principal
- The school counselor
- The Tomball ISD Special Education Department

Your letter should include:
- Your child's name, date of birth, and current school
- A brief description of your concerns
- Your request for a Full Individual Evaluation
- Your contact information
- The date

**Sample language:** "I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who attends [School Name]. I am concerned about [briefly describe concerns]. Please contact me to discuss this request and provide consent forms."

### Step 2: Wait for the District's Response

Once Tomball ISD receives your request, they must respond within 15 school days. They will either:
- Agree to evaluate and send you a consent form, OR
- Decline to evaluate and provide you with written notice explaining why

If the district declines, they must tell you about your right to challenge that decision.

### Step 3: Provide Written Consent

If the district agrees to evaluate, you'll receive a consent form. The FIE cannot begin until you sign and return this form. Take time to review what assessments are planned and ask questions if anything is unclear.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas law establishes clear timelines to ensure evaluations happen promptly:

| Milestone | Timeline |
|-----------|----------|
| District response to your request | Within **15 school days** |
| Completion of the FIE after consent | Within **45 school days** |
| ARD meeting after FIE completion | Within **30 calendar days** |

**Important notes:**
- The 45-school-day timeline does not include days when school is not in session for 5 or more consecutive days (like winter or spring break)
- If you provide consent fewer than 35 school days before the end of the school year, the district has until June 30 to complete the evaluation
- These are maximum timelines—Tomball ISD may complete the process sooner

If you feel timelines aren't being met, don't hesitate to follow up with the special education department in writing.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

The FIE report can be lengthy and full of technical language, but it contains important information about your child. Here's what you'll typically find:

**Background information** – A summary of your child's history, including medical, developmental, and educational information you provided.

**Assessment results** – Detailed findings from each evaluation conducted, often including standardized scores and what they mean.

**Observations** – Notes from professionals who observed your child in the classroom or during testing.

**Summary and recommendations** – The team's conclusions about your child's strengths, areas of need, and whether they meet eligibility criteria for special education.

**Tips for reviewing the FIE report:**
- Request a copy before the ARD meeting so you have time to read it carefully
- Highlight any terms you don't understand and ask for explanations
- Compare findings to what you see at home—does it match your experience of your child?
- Write down any questions to bring to the ARD meeting

You have every right to ask Tomball ISD staff to explain the report in plain language. This is your child, and you deserve to understand the results.

## What Happens After the FIE? (The ARD Meeting)

Once the FIE is complete, Tomball ISD must hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) committee meeting within 30 calendar days. This meeting is where the team—including you—reviews the evaluation results and makes decisions together.

At the ARD meeting, the committee will:

1. **Review the FIE results** – Each evaluator may present their findings
2. **Determine eligibility** – Does your child meet criteria for one of the 13 disability categories under IDEA?
3. **Develop an IEP (if eligible)** – If your child qualifies, the team will begin creating an Individualized Education Program

**You are a full member of the ARD committee.** Your input matters, and you have the right to:
- Ask questions about anything you don't understand
- Share your perspective on your child's needs
- Disagree with recommendations
- Request that the meeting be rescheduled if you need more time to review the report
- Bring someone with you for support

If your child does not qualify for special education, the team should discuss other options, such as a 504 plan or Response to Intervention (RTI) services.

## Your Rights During the FIE Process at Tomball ISD

As a parent in Texas, you have powerful rights throughout the evaluation process:

**Right to request an evaluation** – You can request a FIE at any time, regardless of whether the school has suggested it.

**Right to informed consent** – The district must explain what they plan to evaluate and get your written permission before testing begins.

**Right to participate** – You should be included in discussions about what areas to evaluate and which assessments to use.

**Right to access records** – You can review all evaluation materials and your child's educational records.
