# What Is a FIE? A Parent's Guide to Special Education Evaluations in Crowley ISD

If you're a parent in Crowley ISD and you've been hearing terms like "FIE" or "Full Individual Evaluation," you're not alone—and you've come to the right place. Whether your child is struggling in school, a teacher has expressed concerns, or you simply want to understand your options, this guide will walk you through everything you need to know about the FIE process in Crowley ISD. Serving approximately 16,920 students across the Fort Worth region, Crowley ISD has dedicated special education staff ready to support your family, and understanding the FIE is your first step toward getting your child the help they may need.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A Full Individual Evaluation—commonly called a FIE—is Texas's comprehensive assessment process used to determine whether a child qualifies for special education services. You might also hear it referred to as a "psychoeducational evaluation." Before any child in Texas can receive special education support, the school district must complete this evaluation.

The FIE isn't a single test. Instead, it's a collection of assessments, observations, and information-gathering designed to paint a complete picture of your child's strengths, challenges, and educational needs. It looks at how your child learns, processes information, communicates, and functions in a school setting.

This evaluation is required under both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and Texas state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29). These laws exist to protect your child's right to a free and appropriate public education.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Crowley ISD?

The FIE serves as the foundation for all special education decisions at Crowley ISD. Without a completed evaluation, the district cannot determine whether your child has a disability that affects their learning or what types of support they might need.

Here's why the FIE matters so much:

- **It identifies your child's unique needs.** Every child is different, and the FIE helps the school understand exactly how your child learns best.
- **It determines eligibility.** The evaluation results help decide if your child qualifies for special education services under one of the 13 disability categories recognized in Texas.
- **It guides services and supports.** The information gathered during the FIE directly shapes your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP), ensuring services are tailored specifically to them.
- **It establishes a baseline.** The FIE provides important data that can be used to measure your child's progress over time.

For Crowley ISD families, the FIE process is handled by trained professionals within the district, including licensed specialists in school psychology, educational diagnosticians, and other qualified evaluators.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is designed to be comprehensive, meaning it looks at all areas that might be affecting your child's ability to learn and succeed in school. Depending on your child's specific concerns, the evaluation may include some or all of the following:

**Cognitive Ability (Intelligence)**
This portion measures how your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems. It helps identify learning potential and any significant differences between ability and achievement.

**Academic Achievement**
Evaluators assess your child's skills in reading, writing, and math to see how they're performing compared to grade-level expectations.

**Speech and Language**
If there are concerns about communication, the FIE may include assessments of how your child understands language, expresses themselves, and uses speech sounds.

**Social and Emotional Functioning**
This looks at your child's behavior, emotional well-being, and social skills, helping identify any challenges that might be affecting their school experience.

**Adaptive Behavior**
Evaluators examine how your child handles everyday life skills appropriate for their age, such as self-care, following routines, and interacting with others.

**Motor Skills**
If there are concerns about coordination, handwriting, or physical development, occupational or physical therapy assessments may be included.

**Health and Developmental History**
Your input as a parent is crucial here. You'll share information about your child's medical background, developmental milestones, and family history.

The specific assessments used will depend on your child's individual needs. Texas law requires that evaluations be conducted in your child's native language and that no single test be used as the sole basis for determining eligibility.

## How to Request a FIE at Crowley ISD

As a parent, you have the right to request a FIE for your child at any time. You don't have to wait for the school to suggest it. Here's how to make that request in Crowley ISD:

**Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing**

While you can make a verbal request, putting it in writing creates a clear record and starts the legal timeline. Address your letter to your child's principal or the Crowley ISD Special Education Department.

Your letter should include:
- Your child's full name and date of birth
- Your child's school and grade
- A clear statement that you are requesting a Full Individual Evaluation
- A brief description of your concerns
- Your contact information
- The date

**Step 2: Submit Your Request**

Deliver your letter to the school office, email it to the special education coordinator, or mail it to the district. Keep a copy for your records, and note the date you submitted it.

**Step 3: Wait for the District's Response**

Crowley ISD must respond to your request within **15 school days**. They will either provide you with written consent forms to begin the evaluation or issue a prior written notice explaining why they're declining to evaluate. If the district refuses, they must explain their reasoning, and you have the right to dispute that decision.

**Step 4: Give Written Consent**

The evaluation cannot begin until you sign the consent form. Once you provide consent, the legal timeline for completing the FIE begins.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas has specific legal timelines to ensure evaluations happen promptly. Here's what you need to know:

**15 School Days to Respond**
After receiving your written request, Crowley ISD has 15 school days to either provide you with consent forms or issue a prior written notice declining the evaluation.

**45 School Days to Complete the Evaluation**
Once you sign the consent form, the district has **45 school days** to complete the FIE and hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to discuss the results. This timeline does not include days when school is not in session for more than five consecutive days (such as summer break or winter holidays).

**Exceptions to the Timeline**
There are limited circumstances where the timeline may be extended, such as if your child misses scheduled assessment appointments. The district must document any delays.

If Crowley ISD does not meet these timelines, you have the right to file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and filled with technical language, but don't let that overwhelm you—it's full of valuable information about your child.

**What's Included in the Report:**

- **Background information:** Details about your child's history, including developmental milestones and educational background
- **Assessment results:** Scores from each test administered, along with explanations of what they mean
- **Observations:** Notes from evaluators about your child's behavior during testing
- **Interpretation:** A summary explaining what the results suggest about your child's learning profile
- **Eligibility determination:** Whether your child meets the criteria for one or more disability categories
- **Recommendations:** Suggestions for services, accommodations, or supports

**Tips for Reviewing the Report:**

1. **Request a copy before the ARD meeting.** You have the right to receive the report in advance so you can review it carefully.
2. **Highlight questions.** As you read, note anything you don't understand or want to discuss.
3. **Ask for explanations.** Don't hesitate to ask the evaluators to explain test scores, terminology, or conclusions in plain language.
4. **Consider getting an outside opinion.** You may share the report with your child's pediatrician, a private psychologist, or an advocate for additional perspective.
