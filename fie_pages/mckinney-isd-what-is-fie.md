# What Is a FIE? A Guide for McKinney ISD Parents

If you're a parent in McKinney ISD and suspect your child might need extra support at school, you've likely heard the term "FIE" mentioned by teachers, counselors, or other parents. Whether your child attends a campus in the heart of McKinney or in one of the district's growing neighborhoods, understanding the FIE process is your first step toward getting your child the help they deserve. With approximately 23,320 students across the district, McKinney ISD serves a diverse community of learners—and some of those students need specialized support to thrive. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about the FIE process in clear, straightforward language.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A **Full Individual Evaluation (FIE)** is a comprehensive assessment that determines whether your child has a disability and qualifies for special education services in Texas. Think of it as a detailed portrait of your child's unique learning profile—their strengths, challenges, and specific needs.

Unlike a simple screening or classroom test, a FIE is conducted by a team of qualified professionals, including licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs), educational diagnosticians, and sometimes speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, or other specialists depending on your child's suspected needs.

The FIE is sometimes called a "psychoeducational evaluation" and is required by both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and Texas state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29) before any child can receive special education services. This evaluation is provided **at no cost to you**—the school district is responsible for conducting and funding the entire process.

## Why Is a FIE Important at McKinney ISD?

At McKinney ISD, the FIE serves as the foundation for all special education decisions. Without a completed FIE, your child cannot be determined eligible for special education services, and the district cannot create an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored to their needs.

Here's why the FIE matters for your family:

- **It identifies your child's specific learning needs.** The evaluation goes beyond surface-level observations to understand how your child processes information, communicates, and interacts with their environment.

- **It provides a roadmap for support.** The FIE results guide teachers and specialists in designing interventions that actually work for your child.

- **It protects your child's rights.** Once your child is found eligible for special education through the FIE process, they're entitled to a free appropriate public education (FAPE) under federal and state law.

- **It ensures appropriate placement.** The evaluation helps determine not just *if* your child qualifies, but *what kind* of support they need—whether that's specialized instruction, therapy services, accommodations, or a combination of supports.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is truly comprehensive—it looks at multiple areas of your child's development and functioning. Depending on your child's suspected disability, the evaluation may include assessments in the following areas:

### Cognitive Ability
How your child thinks, reasons, solves problems, and processes information.

### Academic Achievement
Your child's current skill levels in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas compared to grade-level expectations.

### Communication Skills
How your child understands and uses language, including both spoken and written communication.

### Social-Emotional Functioning
Your child's emotional well-being, behavior patterns, social skills, and ability to regulate their emotions.

### Adaptive Behavior
How your child handles everyday life skills like self-care, following routines, and functioning independently.

### Motor Skills
Fine motor skills (like writing and cutting) and gross motor skills (like running and balance), if concerns exist in these areas.

### Health and Vision/Hearing
A review of medical history and sensory functioning that might impact learning.

The evaluation team will also gather information from:
- Parent interviews and questionnaires (your input is essential!)
- Teacher observations and rating scales
- Review of your child's educational history and records
- Classroom observations
- Direct assessment sessions with your child

## How to Request a FIE at McKinney ISD

Any parent or guardian can request a FIE at any time if they suspect their child has a disability that impacts their education. Here's how to do it in McKinney ISD:

### Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing
While you can make a verbal request, **we strongly recommend submitting your request in writing**. This creates documentation with a clear date, which is important for timeline purposes.

Your written request can be simple:

> *Dear [Principal's Name or Special Education Department],*
>
> *I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is currently in [grade] at [school name]. I have concerns about [briefly describe your concerns—for example, "reading difficulties," "attention and focus," "social interactions," etc.]. I believe my child may have a disability that is affecting their ability to learn.*
>
> *Please contact me to discuss next steps. I can be reached at [phone number] and [email address].*
>
> *Thank you,*
> *[Your Name]*

### Step 2: Submit Your Request
You can submit your request to:
- Your child's principal
- Your child's teacher (who should forward it to the appropriate staff)
- The campus special education coordinator
- McKinney ISD Special Education Department

Keep a copy of everything you submit, and note the date you submitted it.

### Step 3: Wait for the District's Response
Once McKinney ISD receives your written request, they must respond within **15 school days**. The district will either:
- Provide you with a **Notice of Proposed Evaluation** and consent forms to sign, OR
- Provide you with a **Prior Written Notice** explaining why they are declining to evaluate, along with information about your rights

If you disagree with a decision not to evaluate, you have the right to dispute that decision through the procedures outlined in your procedural safeguards.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas law establishes clear timelines for the FIE process to ensure evaluations happen promptly. Here's what you need to know:

| Milestone | Timeline |
|-----------|----------|
| District response to your written request | Within **15 school days** |
| Completion of the FIE (after you sign consent) | Within **45 school days** |
| ARD/IEP meeting to review results | Within **30 calendar days** of FIE completion |

### Important Notes About the Timeline:

- The **45 school days** doesn't include days when school is closed for holidays or breaks lasting more than 5 consecutive school days.
- If your child is absent for 3 or more school days during the evaluation period, the timeline may be extended by the same number of days.
- The clock starts ticking **the day after** you provide written consent—so don't delay signing those forms once you've decided to move forward!

**Pro tip:** Keep a simple calendar or log noting important dates—when you submitted your request, when you received and signed consent, and when the 45-day deadline falls. This helps you track whether the district is meeting its legal obligations.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and technical, but understanding it is essential for advocating for your child. Here's a breakdown of what you'll typically find:

### Reason for Referral
Why the evaluation was requested and what concerns prompted it.

### Background Information
Your child's developmental, medical, and educational history, including information you provided.

### Assessment Results
Detailed findings from each area assessed, including:
- Test names and what they measure
- Your child's scores (often reported as standard scores, percentiles, or age/grade equivalents)
- How your child performed compared to same-age peers

### Observations
What evaluators noticed during testing and classroom observations about your child's attention, behavior, and approach to tasks.

### Summary and Conclusions
A synthesis of all the findings, including:
- Whether your child meets criteria for a disability under IDEA
- Your child's specific strengths and challenges
- How the disability (if identified) impacts your child's educational performance

### Recommendations
Suggestions for instructional strategies, supports, services, and accommodations that may help your child succeed.
