# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Texas City ISD Parents

If you're a parent in Texas City ISD and you've been hearing terms like "FIE" or "Full Individual Evaluation," you're not alone in wondering what it all means. Whether your child is struggling in the classroom, showing signs of a learning difference, or has already been referred for special education services, understanding the FIE process is an important first step. This guide is designed specifically for families in the Texas City ISD community—serving approximately 7,870 students in the Houston region—to help you navigate this process with confidence and clarity.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A FIE, or Full Individual Evaluation, is a comprehensive assessment that Texas school districts use to determine whether a child qualifies for special education services. You might also hear it called a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

Think of the FIE as a detailed portrait of your child's unique learning profile. It looks at how your child thinks, learns, communicates, and behaves—painting a complete picture that helps educators understand both strengths and areas where your child may need additional support.

Under federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and the Texas Education Code Chapter 29, school districts like Texas City ISD are required to conduct a FIE before a child can receive special education services. This isn't just paperwork—it's the foundation for creating an educational plan tailored to your child's specific needs.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Texas City ISD?

The FIE serves as the gateway to special education services in Texas City ISD. Without this evaluation, your child cannot be formally identified as having a disability under IDEA or receive an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

Here's why the FIE matters for your family:

- **It provides answers.** If you've been wondering why your child struggles with reading, has difficulty paying attention, or faces social challenges, the FIE can help identify underlying causes.
- **It guides educational planning.** The results inform what types of support, accommodations, and specialized instruction your child needs.
- **It's legally required.** Texas City ISD must complete a FIE that meets state and federal standards before providing special education services.
- **It establishes a baseline.** The FIE creates a starting point for measuring your child's progress over time.

For Texas City ISD families, this evaluation ensures your child's needs are properly understood and addressed within the district's special education programs.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

The FIE is called "full" for a reason—it examines multiple areas of your child's development and functioning. While the specific assessments depend on your child's suspected areas of need, a comprehensive FIE typically includes:

**Cognitive Ability (Intelligence)**
Assessments that measure how your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems.

**Academic Achievement**
Testing in reading, writing, and math to see how your child performs compared to grade-level expectations.

**Communication Skills**
Evaluation of speech, language comprehension, and expressive language abilities.

**Social and Emotional Functioning**
Assessment of emotional well-being, behavior patterns, and social skills.

**Adaptive Behavior**
How your child handles everyday life skills appropriate for their age.

**Motor Skills**
Fine motor (writing, cutting) and gross motor (running, jumping) abilities when relevant.

**Health and Developmental History**
Review of medical records, developmental milestones, and any previous evaluations.

**Classroom Observations**
Direct observation of your child in their natural school environment.

The evaluation team at Texas City ISD will select assessments based on your child's specific concerns—they won't conduct unnecessary testing, but they will ensure all suspected areas of disability are thoroughly examined.

## How to Request a FIE at Texas City ISD

As a parent, you have the right to request a FIE at any time if you suspect your child has a disability that affects their learning. Here's how to do it:

**Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing**

While you can make a verbal request, submitting a written request creates a paper trail and starts the clock on legal timelines. Write a simple letter or email that includes:

- Your child's name and date of birth
- Your child's school and grade
- A brief description of your concerns
- A clear statement that you are requesting a Full Individual Evaluation
- Your contact information
- The date

**Step 2: Submit Your Request**

Send your written request to any of these people at Texas City ISD:

- Your child's teacher
- The school principal
- The campus special education coordinator
- The district's Special Education Department

Keep a copy for your records and note the date you submitted it.

**Step 3: Wait for the District's Response**

Texas City ISD must respond to your request within 15 school days. They will either:

- Agree to evaluate and send you a consent form, or
- Decline to evaluate and provide written notice explaining why

If the district agrees, you'll receive paperwork asking for your written consent. The evaluation cannot begin until you sign and return this consent form.

**Sample Request Letter:**

*Dear [Principal/Special Education Coordinator],*

*I am writing to request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is in [grade] at [school name]. I am concerned about [briefly describe concerns, such as "difficulty reading at grade level" or "challenges with attention and completing work"].*

*Please send me the consent forms so the evaluation process can begin. I look forward to hearing from you within 15 school days as required by Texas law.*

*Thank you,*
*[Your Name]*
*[Phone Number]*
*[Email Address]*
*[Date]*

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas has specific legal timelines for the FIE process, and it's helpful to understand them so you can advocate for your child effectively.

**15 School Days:** After receiving your written request, Texas City ISD must decide whether to evaluate and either send you consent forms or provide written notice of refusal.

**45 School Days:** Once you sign and return the consent form, the district has 45 school days to complete the evaluation AND hold the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to review the results.

**Important Notes:**

- "School days" means days when school is in session, so summer breaks and holidays don't count
- If your child is absent for an extended period, the timeline may pause
- The 45-day clock starts the day after the district receives your signed consent

These timelines exist to protect your child from unnecessary delays. If Texas City ISD isn't meeting these deadlines, you have the right to ask why and request immediate action.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and filled with technical terms, but don't let that intimidate you—you have every right to understand what it says about your child.

**Key Sections to Look For:**

- **Background Information:** Summary of your child's history, including information you provided
- **Assessment Results:** Scores and descriptions from each test administered
- **Behavioral Observations:** Notes on how your child performed during testing
- **Summary and Conclusions:** The evaluator's interpretation of all the data
- **Eligibility Determination:** Whether your child qualifies for special education under one of the 13 disability categories recognized by IDEA

**Tips for Reading the Report:**

- Request a copy before the ARD meeting so you have time to review it
- Look up unfamiliar terms or ask the school to explain them
- Pay attention to standard scores—a score of 100 is average, with most children scoring between 85 and 115
- Notice patterns across different assessments
- Don't focus only on weaknesses—note your child's strengths too

You can always ask Texas City ISD staff to walk you through the report. Understanding this document is essential for participating meaningfully in the next step: the ARD meeting.

## What Happens After the FIE? (The ARD Meeting)

Once the FIE is complete, Texas City ISD will schedule an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting. This is where you and the school team come together to review the evaluation results and make important decisions about your child's education.

**At the ARD Meeting, the Team Will:**

1. **Review the FIE results** and discuss what they mean for your child
2. **Determine eligibility** for special education services
3. **If eligible, develop an IEP** (Individualized Education Program) with specific goals and services

**Who Attends the ARD Meeting?**

- You (and anyone you invite for support)
- A school administrator
- Your child's teacher(s)
- A special education teacher
- The person who conducted or can interpret the evaluation
- Other specialists as needed

**Your Role as a Parent:**

You are a full and equal member of the ARD committee
