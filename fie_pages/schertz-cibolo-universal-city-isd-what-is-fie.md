# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD Parents

If you're a parent in the Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City Independent School District and suspect your child may need additional support at school, you're in the right place. Whether your family lives in Schertz, Cibolo, Universal City, or the surrounding areas, understanding the Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) process is an essential first step toward getting your child the help they deserve. With approximately 15,590 students across the district, SCUC ISD serves a diverse community, and this guide will walk you through exactly what a FIE means for your child's educational journey.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A Full Individual Evaluation—commonly called a FIE—is Texas's comprehensive assessment process used to determine whether a child qualifies for special education services. You might also hear it referred to as a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

Think of a FIE as a complete picture of your child's strengths, challenges, and unique learning needs. It's not just one test—it's a collection of assessments, observations, and information gathering designed to understand how your child learns, processes information, and functions in a school setting.

Under both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Texas Education Code Chapter 29, school districts must conduct a FIE before a child can receive special education services. This evaluation is provided at no cost to families and is conducted by trained professionals within the school district.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD?

A FIE is the gateway to special education services at SCUC ISD. Without this evaluation, your child cannot be formally identified as having a disability that requires specialized instruction or receive an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

Here's why the FIE matters for your family:

- **It identifies your child's specific needs.** Rather than guessing what might help, the FIE provides concrete data about how your child learns best.
- **It establishes eligibility.** Texas recognizes 13 disability categories for special education, and the FIE determines if your child meets the criteria for one or more of these.
- **It guides educational planning.** The results directly inform the goals, accommodations, and services your child will receive.
- **It protects your child's rights.** The FIE ensures decisions about your child's education are based on comprehensive, professional assessment—not assumptions.

For families in SCUC ISD, the FIE process connects you with district specialists who understand your local schools and can help create a plan that works within your child's specific campus environment.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE looks at many different areas of your child's development and functioning. The specific assessments included will depend on your child's suspected disability and individual concerns, but typically include:

**Cognitive Ability (Intelligence)**
This measures how your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems. It helps identify intellectual strengths and areas where your child may need more support.

**Academic Achievement**
These tests look at your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas compared to grade-level expectations.

**Communication Skills**
If there are concerns about speech or language, a speech-language pathologist will assess how your child understands and uses language.

**Social and Emotional Functioning**
This examines your child's behavior, emotional regulation, social skills, and mental health needs.

**Adaptive Behavior**
These assessments look at everyday life skills—how your child functions independently in self-care, communication, and social situations.

**Motor Skills**
If needed, occupational or physical therapists may evaluate fine motor skills (like writing and cutting) or gross motor skills (like walking and coordination).

**Health and Developmental History**
The evaluation team will gather information about your child's medical history, developmental milestones, and any relevant family history.

**Observation and Teacher Input**
Evaluators will observe your child in the classroom and gather input from teachers who work with your child daily.

**Parent Input**
Your perspective as a parent is invaluable. You'll be asked to complete questionnaires and share your observations about your child at home.

## How to Request a FIE at Schertz-Cibolo-Universal City ISD

You have the right to request a FIE at any time if you suspect your child has a disability that affects their learning. Here's how to make that request in SCUC ISD:

**Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing**

While you can verbally ask for an evaluation, a written request creates documentation and starts the official timeline. Address your letter to your child's principal or the campus special education contact.

Your letter should include:
- Your child's full name and date of birth
- Your child's school and current grade
- A clear statement that you are requesting a Full Individual Evaluation for special education
- A brief description of your concerns about your child's learning or development
- Your contact information
- The date

**Step 2: Submit Your Request**

Deliver your letter to the school office and keep a copy for your records. You can hand-deliver it (ask for a date-stamped copy), send it via email, or mail it with delivery confirmation.

**Step 3: Wait for the District's Response**

Once SCUC ISD receives your written request, they must respond within 15 school days by either:
- Providing you with a Notice of Proposed Evaluation and consent forms, OR
- Providing you with a Prior Written Notice explaining why they are refusing to evaluate

**Step 4: Give Written Consent**

If the district agrees to evaluate, you'll receive consent forms. The evaluation cannot begin until you sign and return these forms. Review them carefully—they should explain what assessments will be conducted and who will conduct them.

**Contact Information for SCUC ISD Special Education:**

For specific contact information, visit the SCUC ISD website or contact your child's campus directly. The district's central administration office can direct you to the appropriate special education personnel.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas law establishes clear timelines to ensure evaluations happen promptly. Here's what you need to know:

**15 School Days**
After receiving your written request for evaluation, the school district must respond within 15 school days. They'll either provide consent forms to move forward or give you written notice explaining why they're declining to evaluate.

**45 School Days**
Once you sign and return the consent forms, the district has 45 school days to complete the FIE and hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to discuss the results.

**Important Notes About the Timeline:**

- "School days" means days when school is in session—holidays, weekends, and breaks don't count
- If your child is absent for an extended period, missed days may not count toward the 45 days
- Summer break typically pauses the timeline unless the district offers summer evaluations
- The timeline can be extended if both you and the district agree in writing

**What if the District Misses the Deadline?**

If SCUC ISD doesn't complete the evaluation within the required timeline, you have options. Contact the district's special education director to discuss the delay. If needed, you can file a complaint with the Texas Education Agency (TEA).

## Understanding Your FIE Report

The FIE report can be lengthy and filled with technical language. Here's how to make sense of it:

**What's Included in the Report:**

- Background information about your child
- A summary of all assessments administered
- Test scores and what they mean
- Observations from evaluators
- Input from teachers and parents
- Conclusions about your child's strengths and needs
- Eligibility recommendations

**Understanding the Scores:**

Most tests use standard scores, with 100 being average. Scores are often described in ranges:
- **130+**: Very Superior
- **120-129**: Superior
- **110-119**: High Average
- **90-109**: Average
- **80-89**: Low Average
- **70-79**: Below Average (Borderline)
- **Below 70**: Significantly Below Average

**Tips for Reviewing the Report:**

1. **Request a copy before the ARD meeting.** You have the right to receive the report in advance so you can review it thoroughly.
2. **Highlight questions.** Write down anything you don't understand to ask about at the meeting.
3. **Look at the big picture.** Individual scores matter less than the overall pattern of strengths and challenges.
4. **Focus on recommendations.** The suggestions at the end of each section guide what services your child might need.

**Don't Understand Something?**

You can request a meeting with the evaluator to review the report before the ARD meeting. Never feel embarrassed to ask for explanations—these professionals are there to help you understand your child.
