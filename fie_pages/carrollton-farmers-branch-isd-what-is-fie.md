# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD Parents

If you're a parent in Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD and you've heard the term "FIE" mentioned by a teacher, counselor, or another parent, you're in the right place. Whether your child is struggling in school, has a suspected disability, or you simply want to understand what resources might be available, this guide will walk you through everything you need to know about the Full Individual Evaluation process. With approximately 25,120 students across the district, Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD serves a diverse community in the DFW metroplex, and understanding how special education evaluations work here can help you become your child's best advocate.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A Full Individual Evaluation, commonly called a FIE (pronounced "fie," rhyming with "pie"), is a comprehensive assessment that Texas school districts conduct to determine whether a child has a disability and needs special education services. You might also hear it referred to as a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

Think of a FIE as a detailed snapshot of your child's unique learning profile. It looks at how your child thinks, learns, communicates, and functions in a school setting. The evaluation gathers information from multiple sources—including formal testing, observations, teacher input, and your insights as a parent—to create a complete picture of your child's strengths and challenges.

Under federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and the Texas Education Code Chapter 29, school districts must complete this evaluation before a child can receive special education services. The FIE is free to families and conducted by qualified professionals employed by or contracted through the school district.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD?

For families in Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD, a FIE serves as the gateway to special education support. Without this evaluation, your child cannot be officially identified as having a disability under IDEA or receive an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

Here's why the FIE matters for your family:

- **It identifies your child's specific needs.** Rather than guessing what might help, the FIE provides data-driven insights into how your child learns best.
- **It determines eligibility.** Texas recognizes 13 disability categories for special education eligibility, and the FIE helps determine if your child qualifies under one or more of these categories.
- **It guides educational planning.** The information gathered shapes the goals, accommodations, and services your child will receive.
- **It protects your child's rights.** A properly conducted FIE ensures your child receives the appropriate level of support required by law.

Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD has a dedicated special education department that coordinates these evaluations, and understanding the process helps you partner effectively with district staff to support your child.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is designed to look at your whole child, not just one area of concern. The specific assessments used will depend on your child's suspected disability and areas of need, but a comprehensive FIE typically includes evaluation of:

### Cognitive Ability (Intelligence)
This measures how your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems. It helps identify strengths in areas like verbal reasoning, visual-spatial skills, working memory, and processing speed.

### Academic Achievement
These tests look at your child's skills in reading, writing, and math compared to same-age peers. This helps identify specific learning gaps or areas where your child excels.

### Communication Skills
Speech-language assessments evaluate how your child understands language (receptive) and expresses themselves (expressive), including articulation, vocabulary, and social communication.

### Social-Emotional and Behavioral Functioning
This includes questionnaires and observations to understand your child's emotional well-being, behavior patterns, social skills, and any mental health concerns that affect learning.

### Adaptive Behavior
These assessments look at your child's everyday life skills, such as self-care, communication, and social interaction in real-world settings.

### Motor Skills
If there are concerns about coordination, handwriting, or physical development, occupational or physical therapy assessments may be included.

### Health and Developmental History
Your input about your child's medical history, developmental milestones, and family background provides important context for the evaluation team.

The evaluation team at Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD will select appropriate assessments based on your child's referral concerns. You have the right to provide input on areas you'd like evaluated.

## How to Request a FIE at Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD

As a parent, you have the right to request a FIE at any time if you suspect your child may have a disability affecting their education. Here's how to make that request in Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD:

### Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing
While you can verbally ask for an evaluation, a written request creates documentation and starts the official timeline. Write a simple letter or email to your child's principal or the campus special education coordinator.

**Sample language:**
> "I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is in [grade] at [school name]. I am concerned about [briefly describe your concerns—for example, 'difficulty reading at grade level' or 'challenges with attention and focus in the classroom']. Please send me the necessary consent forms to begin the evaluation process. Thank you for your attention to this request."

### Step 2: Submit to the Right People
You can send your request to:
- Your child's principal
- The campus special education coordinator or diagnostician
- The Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD Special Education Department

Keep a copy of your request and note the date you submitted it.

### Step 3: Respond to the District's Notice
Within 15 school days of receiving your request, the district must either:
- Provide you with a "Prior Written Notice" agreeing to evaluate and consent forms to sign, OR
- Provide you with a "Prior Written Notice" explaining why they are refusing to evaluate

If the district agrees to evaluate, you'll receive consent forms that explain what assessments will be conducted. Review these carefully and sign if you agree.

### Step 4: Complete Parent Input Forms
The district will ask you to fill out questionnaires about your child's development, behavior, and history. Take your time with these—your insights are valuable and help evaluators understand your child outside of school.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas law establishes clear timelines to ensure evaluations happen promptly. Here's what you need to know:

| Milestone | Timeline |
|-----------|----------|
| District response to your request | Within **15 school days** of receiving your written request |
| Completion of the FIE | Within **45 school days** of receiving signed consent |
| ARD/IEP meeting to review results | Within **30 calendar days** of completing the FIE |

**Important notes about the 45-school-day timeline:**
- School days do not include days when school is closed for holidays, summer break, or other official closures
- If your child is absent for an extended period, this may affect the timeline
- If your child enrolls in another Texas district during the evaluation, the new district must complete it within 45 school days if the parent and district agree to a specific timeline

If you signed consent close to summer break, understand that the 45-day clock pauses when school is not in session. You can request that evaluations continue over the summer, but the district is not required to do so.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

Once the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can feel overwhelming at first glance—it's often lengthy and filled with numbers, scores, and professional terminology. Here's how to approach it:

### Before the ARD Meeting
Request a copy of the FIE report at least a few days before your ARD meeting. Texas law requires the district to provide it to you in enough time to review before the meeting. Don't hesitate to ask for it if you haven't received it.

### Key Sections to Focus On

**Background Information:** This summarizes your child's history and the concerns that led to the evaluation.

**Assessment Results:** This section includes scores from each test administered. You'll often see:
- Standard scores (average is typically 100)
- Percentile ranks (shows how your child compares to peers)
- Age or grade equivalents

**Observations:** Evaluators describe what they noticed during testing and sometimes include classroom observations.

**Summary and Recommendations:** This is often the most helpful section, as it pulls everything together and offers conclusions about your child's needs.
