# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Belton ISD Parents

If you're a parent in Belton ISD and you've been hearing terms like "FIE" or "Full Individual Evaluation," you're not alone—and you've come to the right place. Whether your child attends Lake Belton High School, Belton Middle School, or one of the district's many elementary campuses, understanding the FIE process is an essential first step toward getting your child the support they need to thrive. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about FIEs in Belton ISD, from what they are to how to request one.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A FIE, or Full Individual Evaluation, is a comprehensive assessment that determines whether your child has a disability and qualifies for special education services in Texas public schools. You might also hear it called a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

Think of a FIE as a detailed picture of your child as a learner. It looks at how your child thinks, learns, processes information, and functions in a school setting. The evaluation is conducted by a team of trained professionals—including licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs), educational diagnosticians, and other specialists—who work together to understand your child's unique strengths and challenges.

Under both the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code Chapter 29, a school district cannot provide special education services to a child without first completing a FIE. This evaluation is the gateway to an Individualized Education Program (IEP) and the specialized support your child may need.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Belton ISD?

For families in Belton ISD, a FIE serves several crucial purposes:

**It opens doors to support.** Without a FIE, your child cannot receive special education services, no matter how much they may be struggling. The evaluation is the required first step.

**It provides answers.** Many parents sense that something is different about how their child learns but can't quite pinpoint what's happening. A FIE can identify specific learning disabilities, attention challenges, autism spectrum disorder, speech and language delays, or other conditions affecting your child's education.

**It guides instruction.** The information gathered in a FIE doesn't just determine eligibility—it also helps teachers and specialists understand exactly how your child learns best. This leads to more effective, personalized instruction.

**It creates a baseline.** For Belton ISD students who do qualify for services, the FIE establishes a starting point that the district can use to measure progress over time.

In a district serving approximately 14,140 students, individualized attention matters. A FIE ensures that your child is seen as an individual, not just a number.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is not a single test—it's a collection of assessments designed to look at your child from multiple angles. The specific areas evaluated depend on your child's suspected disability, but a FIE typically includes:

**Cognitive ability (intelligence):** This measures how your child reasons, solves problems, and processes information.

**Academic achievement:** This looks at your child's current skill levels in reading, writing, and math compared to grade-level expectations.

**Communication skills:** For some children, the FIE includes speech and language assessments to evaluate how well they understand and use language.

**Social-emotional functioning:** This examines your child's behavior, emotional regulation, and social skills.

**Adaptive behavior:** This measures your child's ability to handle everyday tasks appropriate for their age, such as self-care, following routines, and interacting with others.

**Motor skills:** If there are concerns about fine motor skills (like handwriting) or gross motor skills (like coordination), occupational or physical therapy assessments may be included.

**Health and developmental history:** The evaluation team will also review medical records, developmental milestones, and any relevant family history.

Texas law requires that the FIE assess your child in *all* areas related to the suspected disability. This means the evaluation should be thorough enough to capture the full picture of your child's needs.

## How to Request a FIE at Belton ISD

Any parent can request a FIE for their child at any time. You don't need the school's permission, and you don't need to wait for teachers to bring it up first. Here's how to make a request in Belton ISD:

**Step 1: Put your request in writing.** While you can make a verbal request, a written request creates a clear record and starts the legal timeline. You can email or hand-deliver a letter to your child's school.

**Step 2: Be specific.** State clearly that you are requesting a "Full Individual Evaluation" or "FIE" to determine whether your child has a disability and qualifies for special education services. You can briefly describe your concerns, but you don't need to diagnose your child.

**Step 3: Keep a copy.** Always keep a copy of your written request and note the date you submitted it. If you hand-deliver your letter, ask for a date-stamped copy.

**Step 4: Send it to the right people.** You can submit your request to your child's principal, the campus special education coordinator, or the district's special education department. When in doubt, send it to more than one person.

Here's a sample letter you can adapt:

---

*Dear [Principal's Name or Special Education Coordinator],*

*I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is currently in [grade] at [school name]. I have concerns about [briefly describe concerns, such as "my child's difficulty with reading" or "my child's struggles with focus and attention"].*

*I understand that this evaluation will help determine whether my child has a disability and is eligible for special education services. Please contact me to discuss next steps.*

*Thank you for your attention to this matter.*

*Sincerely,*
*[Your Name]*
*[Your Phone Number]*
*[Your Email Address]*

---

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Once you submit a written request for a FIE, the clock starts ticking. Texas law establishes clear deadlines to protect families:

**Within 15 school days:** Belton ISD must provide you with written notice of whether they agree to evaluate your child, along with a consent form. If they agree to evaluate, you'll receive a "Notice and Consent for Full Individual Evaluation" form to sign.

**The timeline officially begins:** Once you sign and return the consent form, Belton ISD has **45 school days** to complete the FIE and hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to review the results with you.

**Important notes:**
- The 45-day timeline counts *school days*, not calendar days. Summer break, winter break, and other holidays don't count.
- If your child is absent for an extended period or if you agree to an extension in writing, the timeline can be adjusted—but only under specific circumstances.

If Belton ISD decides *not* to evaluate your child, they must provide you with "Prior Written Notice" explaining their decision and your options. You have the right to challenge this decision.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and full of technical language, but don't let that intimidate you—understanding it is within your reach.

A typical FIE report includes:

**Background information:** A summary of your child's developmental, educational, and medical history, often based on information you provided.

**Assessment results:** Scores and descriptions from each test administered. You'll see terms like "standard scores," "percentiles," and "age equivalents." Don't hesitate to ask the evaluation team to explain what these numbers mean for your child.

**Observations:** Notes from the evaluators about how your child behaved during testing, including their attention, motivation, and approach to tasks.

**Interpretation:** A discussion of what the results mean—how your child's scores compare to typical development and what patterns or concerns emerge.

**Eligibility determination:** The report will state whether your child meets the criteria for one or more of the 13 disability categories recognized under IDEA in Texas.

**Recommendations:** Suggestions for how to support your child, which may inform the development of an IEP.

**Tips for reviewing the report:**
- Request a copy of the FIE report *before* the ARD meeting so you have time to read and process it.
- Highlight any terms or scores you don't understand and bring questions to the meeting.
- Remember that you know your child best—if something in the report doesn't match what you observe at home, speak up.
