# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Del Valle ISD Parents

If you're a parent in Del Valle ISD and you've heard the term "FIE" mentioned by your child's teacher, counselor, or another parent, you're not alone in wondering what it means. Whether your child is struggling in school, has been flagged for possible learning differences, or you simply want to understand the special education process better, this guide is here to help. Del Valle ISD serves a diverse community of approximately 11,240 students across the Austin area, and understanding how the district evaluates children for special education services is an important first step in getting your child the support they need.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A FIE, which stands for **Full Individual Evaluation**, is a comprehensive assessment conducted by your school district to determine whether your child has a disability and needs special education services. You might also hear it called a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

Think of the FIE as a detailed snapshot of your child—how they learn, where they excel, and where they may need additional support. It's not a single test but rather a collection of assessments, observations, and information-gathering designed to give a complete picture of your child's unique needs.

In Texas, the FIE is required by both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29) before a child can be found eligible for special education services. This ensures that decisions about your child's education are based on thorough, individualized information rather than assumptions or a single observation.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Del Valle ISD?

The FIE serves as the foundation for everything that follows in the special education process at Del Valle ISD. Without it, the district cannot determine whether your child qualifies for special education services or what type of support would be most helpful.

Here's why the FIE matters so much:

- **It identifies your child's specific needs.** Every child learns differently, and the FIE helps pinpoint exactly where your child needs support.
- **It determines eligibility.** Texas recognizes 13 disability categories for special education, and the FIE helps determine if your child meets the criteria for one or more of these.
- **It guides the creation of your child's IEP.** If your child qualifies for special education, the information from the FIE will be used to develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) tailored specifically to them.
- **It ensures your child receives appropriate services.** The right evaluation leads to the right services—whether that's specialized instruction, speech therapy, counseling, or other supports.

At Del Valle ISD, the FIE process is designed to consider your child as a whole person, including their cultural background, home language, and unique circumstances.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE looks at many different areas of your child's development and learning. The specific assessments included will depend on your child's suspected disability and individual needs, but a typical FIE may evaluate:

- **Cognitive ability** – How your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems
- **Academic achievement** – Skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas
- **Communication skills** – Speech, language, and how your child expresses and understands information
- **Social and emotional development** – How your child interacts with others, manages emotions, and handles social situations
- **Adaptive behavior** – Daily living skills and how your child functions in everyday situations
- **Motor skills** – Fine motor abilities (like writing or cutting) and gross motor skills (like running or balance)
- **Health and developmental history** – Information about your child's medical background and developmental milestones
- **Behavior** – Patterns of behavior at home and school that may affect learning

The evaluation team at Del Valle ISD will select assessment tools that are appropriate for your child, including considerations for their primary language and cultural background. Texas law requires that evaluations be conducted in a nondiscriminatory manner, ensuring that language barriers or cultural differences don't unfairly influence the results.

## How to Request a FIE at Del Valle ISD

As a parent, you have the right to request a FIE for your child at any time. You don't need to wait for the school to suggest it. Here's how to make your request in Del Valle ISD:

**Step 1: Put your request in writing.**
While you can make a verbal request, putting it in writing creates a clear record and starts the official timeline. You can write a simple letter or email that includes:
- Your child's name, grade, and school
- Your relationship to the child
- A clear statement that you are requesting a Full Individual Evaluation
- A brief description of your concerns about your child's learning or development
- The date
- Your signature

**Step 2: Submit your request to the appropriate person.**
You can submit your written request to:
- Your child's principal
- The school counselor
- The special education department at Del Valle ISD
- Your child's teacher (who should forward it to the appropriate administrator)

**Step 3: Keep a copy for your records.**
Always keep a copy of your request and note the date you submitted it. This is important for tracking the timeline.

**Step 4: Wait for the district's response.**
Del Valle ISD must respond to your request within **15 school days**. They will either provide you with consent forms to begin the evaluation or explain in writing why they are declining to evaluate.

**Sample Request Letter:**

> Dear [Principal's Name],
>
> I am the parent of [Child's Name], a [grade] student at [School Name]. I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and Texas Education Code Chapter 29.
>
> I am concerned about my child's [brief description of concerns, such as "difficulty with reading," "struggles with attention," or "challenges with social interactions"]. I believe a comprehensive evaluation would help us understand their needs and determine if special education services would be beneficial.
>
> Please provide me with the necessary consent forms to begin this process. I understand the district has 15 school days to respond to this request.
>
> Thank you for your attention to this matter.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]
> [Your Phone Number]
> [Your Email Address]
> [Date]

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas has specific timelines that Del Valle ISD must follow when conducting a FIE. Understanding these deadlines helps you know what to expect and ensures the process moves forward appropriately.

| Step | Timeline |
|------|----------|
| Parent submits written request for evaluation | Day 1 |
| District responds with consent forms or written refusal | Within **15 school days** |
| Parent signs and returns consent form | When you're ready (the clock pauses until you return this) |
| District completes the FIE | Within **45 school days** of receiving signed consent |
| ARD/IEP meeting to review results | Within **30 calendar days** of completing the FIE |

**Important notes about the timeline:**

- The **45 school days** do not include days when school is not in session for more than 5 consecutive days (like winter or spring break).
- If your child is absent for an extended period, the timeline may be adjusted.
- The clock doesn't start until you sign and return the consent form, so returning it promptly helps move the process along.
- If Del Valle ISD misses these deadlines, don't panic—but do follow up in writing to request an update and remind them of the required timelines.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

Once the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and full of technical language, but it contains crucial information about your child. Here's how to approach it:

**Before the meeting:**
- Request a copy of the FIE report at least a few days before your ARD meeting so you have time to read it carefully.
- Highlight any sections you don't understand or want to discuss.
- Write down questions as you read.

**Key sections to look for:**

- **Background information** – This should accurately reflect your child's history and your concerns.
- **Assessment results** – Scores from various tests, often presented as standard scores, percentiles, or grade equivalents.
- **Observations** – What evaluators noticed about your child during testing.
- **Summary and conclusions** – The evaluator's interpretation of what the results mean.
- **Eligibility recommendations** – Whether the evaluator believes your child qualifies for special education services.

**Understanding the numbers:**
- Standard scores typically have an average of 100. Scores between 85-115 are generally considered within the average range.
- Percentile ranks tell you how your child performed compared to other children the same age. A percentile of 25 means your child scored as well as or better than 25%
