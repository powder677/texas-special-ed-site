# What Is a FIE? A Guide for College Station ISD Parents

If you're a parent in College Station ISD and suspect your child may need extra support at school, you've likely heard the term "FIE" mentioned by teachers or administrators. Whether your child attends A&M Consolidated High School, College Hills Elementary, or any of the district's campuses, understanding the FIE process is an important first step in getting your child the help they deserve. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about the Full Individual Evaluation in clear, straightforward language.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A FIE, or Full Individual Evaluation, is Texas's comprehensive assessment used to determine whether a child qualifies for special education services. Think of it as a detailed snapshot of your child's unique strengths, challenges, and learning needs.

Unlike a simple classroom test or screening, a FIE looks at the whole child. It examines cognitive abilities, academic achievement, social-emotional functioning, and other areas relevant to your child's suspected disability. In Texas, this evaluation is required by both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29) before a child can receive special education services.

You might also hear people call this a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation." Whatever the name, the purpose remains the same: to gather the information needed to understand how your child learns and what support they need to succeed.

## Why Is a FIE Important at College Station ISD?

For families in College Station ISD, the FIE serves as the foundation for your child's entire special education journey. This evaluation matters because it:

- **Identifies specific learning needs**: College Station ISD serves approximately 14,080 students, each with their own unique profile. The FIE helps pinpoint exactly where your child needs support.

- **Opens doors to services**: Without a completed FIE, your child cannot be found eligible for special education services, no matter how apparent their struggles may seem.

- **Creates a roadmap for success**: The evaluation results guide the development of your child's Individualized Education Program (IEP), ensuring services are tailored specifically to their needs.

- **Provides legal protection**: Once your child qualifies through the FIE process, they gain important legal protections under IDEA and Texas state law.

College Station ISD's special education department works with families throughout this process, but understanding your role and rights empowers you to be an effective advocate for your child.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is comprehensive by design. Depending on your child's suspected disability and areas of concern, the evaluation may include:

**Cognitive Ability**
This measures how your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems. It's often what people think of as an "IQ test," though it reveals much more about how your child processes information.

**Academic Achievement**
Evaluators assess your child's skills in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas compared to grade-level expectations.

**Communication Skills**
If language is a concern, a speech-language pathologist may evaluate how well your child understands and uses language.

**Social-Emotional Functioning**
This examines your child's emotional well-being, behavior patterns, and social skills through observations, interviews, and standardized assessments.

**Adaptive Behavior**
Evaluators look at how independently your child handles daily living skills appropriate for their age.

**Motor Skills**
If concerns exist about coordination or physical development, occupational or physical therapists may assess fine and gross motor abilities.

**Health and Vision/Hearing**
Medical factors that might affect learning are also considered as part of the complete picture.

The specific components of your child's FIE will depend on their individual needs. Texas law requires that evaluations be conducted in all areas related to the suspected disability.

## How to Request a FIE at College Station ISD

Any parent or guardian can request a FIE for their child at any time. Here's how to do it in College Station ISD:

**Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing**

While you can make a verbal request, we strongly recommend submitting a written request. This creates documentation and starts the legal timeline. Your letter should include:

- Your child's full name and date of birth
- Your child's school and current grade
- A brief description of your concerns
- A clear statement that you are requesting a Full Individual Evaluation
- Your signature and the date

**Step 2: Submit Your Request**

You can deliver your written request to:
- Your child's principal
- Your child's teacher
- The campus special education coordinator
- College Station ISD's Special Education Department

Keep a copy of everything you submit, and consider sending your request via email or certified mail so you have proof of delivery.

**Step 3: Wait for the District's Response**

Once College Station ISD receives your written request, they must respond within 15 school days. The district will either:
- Agree to evaluate and send you a consent form to sign, OR
- Decline to evaluate and provide you with a written explanation of why

If the district agrees to evaluate, you'll receive a "Notice and Consent for Full Individual Evaluation" form. The evaluation cannot begin until you sign and return this consent.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas has specific timelines that protect your family during the evaluation process:

**15 School Days**: After receiving your written request, College Station ISD must provide you with a notice of their decision (to evaluate or not) within 15 school days.

**45 School Days**: Once you sign and return the consent form, the district has 45 school days to complete the evaluation AND hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to review the results.

Important notes about timing:
- "School days" means days when school is in session, so summer break and holidays don't count
- If your child is absent for an extended period, or if you both agree to an extension, the timeline may be adjusted
- These timelines are set by Texas Education Code §29.004 and are legally binding

Mark your calendar when you submit consent. If College Station ISD is approaching the deadline without scheduling your ARD meeting, don't hesitate to follow up with the special education department.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and filled with technical terms, but understanding it is crucial. Here's what to look for:

**Background Information**: A summary of your child's history, including your concerns, developmental milestones, and educational background.

**Assessment Results**: Detailed findings from each evaluation area, often including standard scores, percentile ranks, and grade equivalents.

**Observations**: Notes from evaluators who watched your child during testing or in the classroom.

**Conclusions**: A summary of what the data reveals about your child's learning profile.

**Eligibility Determination**: A statement about whether your child meets criteria for one of Texas's 13 disability categories.

**Recommendations**: Suggestions for educational support and services.

**Tips for reviewing your FIE report:**
- Request a copy before your ARD meeting so you have time to read it thoroughly
- Write down questions about anything you don't understand
- Look for consistency between what you observe at home and what the report describes
- Don't hesitate to ask evaluators to explain their findings in plain language

## What Happens After the FIE? (The ARD Meeting)

Once the FIE is complete, College Station ISD will schedule an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting. This is a team meeting where you and school personnel review the evaluation results together.

**Who attends the ARD meeting?**
- You (and anyone you invite for support)
- A school administrator or representative
- Your child's teacher(s)
- A special education teacher
- The professionals who conducted the evaluation
- Related service providers (speech therapists, counselors, etc.) as appropriate
- Your child, when appropriate

**What happens at the ARD meeting?**

1. **Review of the FIE**: The team discusses the evaluation findings and what they mean for your child.

2. **Eligibility Decision**: Based on the FIE, the team determines whether your child qualifies for special education under one of Texas's disability categories.

3. **IEP Development**: If your child is eligible, the team works together to create an Individualized Education Program outlining goals, services, and accommodations.

4. **Placement Decision**: The team decides where your child will receive services, with a preference for the least restrictive environment.

Remember: You are an equal member of this team. Your input about your child is invaluable, and you should never feel pressured to sign documents you don't understand or agree with.
