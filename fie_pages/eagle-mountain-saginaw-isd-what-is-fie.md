# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD Parents

If you're a parent in the Eagle Mountain-Saginaw Independent School District and you've been hearing terms like "FIE" or "Full Individual Evaluation" at school meetings, you're not alone. Many families in our growing district—serving approximately 23,870 students across northwest Tarrant County—find themselves navigating the special education process for the first time. This guide is designed specifically for Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD families to help you understand what a FIE is, why it matters for your child, and how to work effectively with your school team throughout the evaluation process.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A FIE, which stands for Full Individual Evaluation, is a comprehensive assessment that Texas school districts must conduct before a child can qualify for special education services. You might also hear it called a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

Think of the FIE as a detailed portrait of your child—their strengths, challenges, learning style, and unique needs. Unlike a simple classroom test or report card, a FIE digs deep into how your child learns, processes information, communicates, and functions in the school environment.

The FIE isn't just a formality. It's required by both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and Texas state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29). These laws exist to protect your child's right to a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE) and to ensure that any educational decisions are based on solid, comprehensive information.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD?

At Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD, the FIE serves as the foundation for all special education decisions. Without a completed FIE, your child cannot be found eligible for special education services—no matter how obvious their struggles might seem.

Here's why the FIE matters so much:

- **It identifies whether your child has a disability** that affects their educational performance under one of the 13 eligibility categories recognized in Texas
- **It determines what your child needs** to succeed in school, whether that's specialized instruction, related services, accommodations, or a combination
- **It provides baseline information** that helps teachers and specialists track your child's progress over time
- **It ensures decisions are data-driven**, not based on assumptions or incomplete information

Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD, like all Texas school districts, must follow specific procedures when conducting FIEs. The district employs licensed specialists—including educational diagnosticians, licensed specialists in school psychology (LSSPs), speech-language pathologists, and other professionals—who are trained to evaluate students fairly and thoroughly.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE is called "full" and "individual" for good reasons. It looks at your whole child as a unique learner, not just one area of concern. Depending on your child's suspected needs, the evaluation may assess:

**Cognitive Ability (Intelligence)**
How your child thinks, reasons, solves problems, and processes information.

**Academic Achievement**
Your child's current performance in reading, writing, math, and other academic areas compared to grade-level expectations.

**Communication Skills**
How your child understands and uses language, including listening, speaking, and social communication.

**Social and Emotional Functioning**
Your child's behavior, emotional regulation, social skills, and how they relate to peers and adults.

**Adaptive Behavior**
Your child's ability to handle everyday life skills appropriate for their age, like self-care, following routines, and personal responsibility.

**Motor Skills**
Fine motor abilities (like writing and cutting) and gross motor skills (like running and balance), if concerns exist.

**Health and Developmental History**
Information about your child's medical background, developmental milestones, and any relevant family history.

**Observation and Input from Multiple Sources**
The evaluation team will gather information from teachers, review classroom work, observe your child in different settings, and—importantly—listen to your insights as a parent.

Texas law requires that evaluations be conducted in all areas related to the suspected disability. This means if your child has multiple areas of concern, the FIE should address each one.

## How to Request a FIE at Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD

As a parent, you have the legal right to request a FIE at any time if you suspect your child may have a disability affecting their learning. Here's how to do it in Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD:

**Step 1: Put Your Request in Writing**

While you can make a verbal request, we strongly recommend submitting a written request. This creates a paper trail and starts the official timeline. You can write a simple letter or email to your child's principal or the campus special education coordinator.

Your letter doesn't need to be fancy. Here's a sample:

*"Dear [Principal's Name], I am requesting that my child, [Child's Name], be evaluated for special education services. I am concerned about [briefly describe your concerns, such as reading difficulties, attention problems, or speech delays]. Please consider this my formal written request for a Full Individual Evaluation. Thank you, [Your Name and Date]"*

**Step 2: Submit to the Right Person**

At Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD, you can submit your request to:
- Your child's principal
- The campus special education department chair
- The district's special education office

Keep a copy of everything you send, and consider sending it via email so you have a timestamp, or hand-deliver it and ask for a receipt.

**Step 3: Wait for the District's Response**

Within 15 school days of receiving your request, Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD must provide you with:
- A "Prior Written Notice" explaining whether they agree to evaluate (and why or why not)
- If they agree, a "Notice and Consent for Evaluation" form for you to sign
- A copy of the Procedural Safeguards (your rights as a parent)

**Step 4: Give Your Written Consent**

The evaluation cannot begin until you sign the consent form. Review it carefully, ask questions if needed, and sign when you're ready. The official evaluation timeline begins on the date you provide written consent.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas has strict timelines to ensure evaluations don't drag on indefinitely. Here's what you need to know:

**15 School Days**
The district has 15 school days from receiving your written request to provide you with the notice and consent paperwork.

**45 School Days**
Once you sign consent, the district has 45 school days to complete the evaluation and hold the ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting to review results. Note that this is 45 *school days*, not calendar days—summer breaks, holidays, and school closures don't count.

**Exceptions to the Timeline**
The 45-day timeline may be extended if:
- Your child misses school and can't be assessed
- You and the district mutually agree in writing to an extension
- Your child enrolls in a new district after the process has started (specific rules apply)

If Eagle Mountain-Saginaw ISD fails to meet these timelines without a valid exception, you have the right to take action. Document everything and don't hesitate to follow up if deadlines approach.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

When the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and filled with specialized terminology, but don't be intimidated—you have every right to understand what it says about your child.

**What's Typically Included:**

- **Background Information**: Your child's history, including developmental milestones, medical information, and educational background
- **Assessment Procedures**: A list of all tests administered and methods used to gather information
- **Test Results**: Scores from formal assessments, often presented as standard scores, percentiles, or grade/age equivalents
- **Observations**: Notes from watching your child in different settings
- **Parent and Teacher Input**: Information gathered from questionnaires or interviews
- **Summary and Interpretation**: What all the data means when put together
- **Eligibility Determination**: Whether your child meets criteria for one or more disability categories under Texas law
- **Recommendations**: Suggestions for services, supports, and accommodations

**Tips for Understanding the Report:**

- **Ask for a preview**: You can request a copy of the FIE report before the ARD meeting so you have time to read and prepare questions
- **Look up unfamiliar terms**: Don't hesitate to ask the evaluator to explain jargon or scores you don't understand
- **Focus on the recommendations**: This section often contains the most actionable information
- **Remember that you know your child best**: The report should match what you observe at home
