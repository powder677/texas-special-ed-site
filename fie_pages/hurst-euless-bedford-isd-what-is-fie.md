# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD Parents

If you're a parent in the Hurst-Euless-Bedford Independent School District and you've recently heard the term "FIE" from your child's teacher, counselor, or another parent, you're not alone in wondering what it means. With approximately 21,890 students across the HEB ISD community, many families each year navigate the special education evaluation process for the first time. This guide is designed specifically for HEB ISD parents like you—to help you understand what a Full Individual Evaluation is, why it matters, and how to advocate effectively for your child throughout the process.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A Full Individual Evaluation, commonly called a FIE, is a comprehensive assessment conducted by your child's school district to determine whether they have a disability that affects their ability to learn and whether they qualify for special education services. In Texas, this evaluation is sometimes referred to as a "psychoeducational evaluation."

Think of the FIE as a detailed portrait of your child's unique strengths, challenges, and educational needs. It's not just one test—it's a collection of assessments, observations, and information gathered from multiple sources to get a complete picture of how your child learns.

The FIE is required by both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and state law (Texas Education Code Chapter 29) before any child can begin receiving special education services. This ensures that every child is carefully evaluated and that services are tailored to their individual needs.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD?

At Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD, the FIE serves as the foundation for all special education decisions about your child. Without a completed FIE, your child cannot be found eligible for special education services or receive an Individualized Education Program (IEP).

Here's why the FIE matters so much for HEB ISD families:

- **It identifies your child's specific needs.** The evaluation pinpoints exactly where your child needs support, whether that's in reading, math, communication, behavior, or other areas.
- **It determines eligibility.** Texas recognizes 13 disability categories under which a child may qualify for special education. The FIE helps determine if your child meets the criteria for one or more of these categories.
- **It guides service planning.** The information gathered during the FIE directly shapes the services, accommodations, and supports your child will receive.
- **It establishes a baseline.** The FIE provides a snapshot of your child's current abilities, which helps the school track progress over time.
- **It protects your child's rights.** A thorough, properly conducted FIE ensures that decisions about your child are based on accurate, comprehensive information—not assumptions or incomplete data.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

A FIE looks at multiple areas of your child's development and functioning. The specific assessments included will depend on your child's suspected disability and areas of concern, but a comprehensive FIE typically examines:

**Cognitive Ability (Intelligence)**
This measures how your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems. It helps identify learning strengths and areas where your child may need additional support.

**Academic Achievement**
These assessments evaluate your child's skills in reading, writing, and math compared to other students their age or grade level.

**Communication Skills**
If there are concerns about speech or language, a speech-language pathologist will assess your child's ability to understand and express language.

**Social and Emotional Functioning**
This area looks at your child's behavior, emotional well-being, and social skills. It may include behavior rating scales completed by parents, teachers, and sometimes the child.

**Adaptive Behavior**
This assesses your child's practical, everyday life skills—things like self-care, communication, and social interaction outside of academic settings.

**Motor Skills**
If there are concerns about fine motor skills (like writing) or gross motor skills (like running or balance), an occupational therapist or physical therapist may conduct additional assessments.

**Health and Developmental History**
The evaluation team will review your child's medical history, developmental milestones, and any previous evaluations or diagnoses.

**Observation**
Evaluators will observe your child in their natural school environment to see how they function in the classroom.

**Parent and Teacher Input**
Your perspective as a parent is invaluable. You'll be asked to complete questionnaires and share information about your child's history, strengths, and challenges.

## How to Request a FIE at Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD

Any parent can request a FIE for their child at any time. You don't need to wait for the school to suggest it. If you're concerned about your child's learning, development, or behavior, you have the right to ask HEB ISD to evaluate your child.

**Here's how to make the request:**

1. **Put your request in writing.** While you can make a verbal request, a written request creates documentation and starts the official timeline. Address your letter or email to your child's principal or the HEB ISD Special Education Department.

2. **Be specific about your concerns.** Describe what you're observing at home and why you believe an evaluation is needed. You don't need to know what disability your child might have—just share your concerns.

3. **Keep a copy for your records.** Always save a copy of your request letter and note the date you submitted it.

4. **Follow up.** If you don't hear back within a few days, contact the school to confirm they received your request.

**Sample language for your request:**

*"Dear [Principal's Name], I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) for my child, [Child's Name], who is in [grade] at [school name]. I am concerned about [briefly describe your concerns—for example, 'difficulty reading at grade level,' 'challenges with focus and attention,' or 'trouble making friends and managing emotions']. I understand that under IDEA and Texas Education Code Chapter 29, the district must respond to my request within 15 school days. Please contact me to discuss next steps. Thank you, [Your Name]"*

Once the district receives your written request, they have **15 school days** to respond. They will either agree to evaluate your child and provide you with consent forms, or they will provide a written explanation of why they are declining to evaluate (called a "prior written notice"). If they decline, you have the right to challenge that decision.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas law sets clear deadlines to ensure that the evaluation process moves forward promptly. Here's what you can expect:

| Step | Timeline |
|------|----------|
| **District response to your request** | Within 15 school days of receiving your written request, HEB ISD must provide you with a notice of their decision and, if agreeing to evaluate, a consent form. |
| **Completion of the FIE** | The evaluation must be completed within **45 school days** from the date the district receives your signed consent. |
| **ARD/IEP meeting** | Within **30 calendar days** of completing the FIE, the school must hold an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting to review results and determine eligibility. |

**Important notes about the timeline:**

- The 45-school-day timeline does not include days when school is not in session for more than 5 consecutive days (like summer break or winter break).
- If your child was absent for an extended period, there are limited exceptions to the timeline.
- If the district misses these deadlines, don't hesitate to follow up in writing and reference the legal requirements.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and may feel overwhelming at first, but understanding its contents is important because it will guide all future decisions about your child's education.

**Here's what to look for in the FIE report:**

- **Background information:** A summary of your child's history, including medical, developmental, and educational information.
- **Assessment results:** Scores and descriptions from each evaluation area, often including standard scores, percentile ranks, and age or grade equivalents.
- **Observations:** Notes from evaluators who observed your child in the classroom or during testing.
- **Summary and conclusions:** An interpretation of what all the data means when considered together.
- **Eligibility determination:** The team's preliminary conclusion about whether your child meets the criteria for a disability category under Texas law.
- **Recommendations:** Suggestions for services, supports, or accommodations based on the evaluation findings.

**Tips for reviewing the FIE report:**

- Request a copy of the report **before** the ARD meeting so you have time to review it thoroughly.
