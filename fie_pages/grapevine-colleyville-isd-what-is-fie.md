# What Is a FIE? A Guide for Grapevine-Colleyville ISD Parents

If you're a parent in Grapevine-Colleyville ISD and you've heard the term "FIE" mentioned by a teacher, counselor, or another parent, you're in the right place. Whether your child is struggling in the classroom, showing signs of a learning difference, or you simply want to understand what resources might be available, learning about the Full Individual Evaluation is an important first step. This guide is designed specifically for families in the Grapevine-Colleyville community to help you navigate this process with confidence and clarity.

## What Is a FIE (Full Individual Evaluation)?

A Full Individual Evaluation, commonly called a FIE, is a comprehensive assessment used in Texas to determine whether a child has a disability and needs special education services. You might also hear it referred to as a "psychoeducational evaluation" or simply a "special education evaluation."

The FIE isn't just one test—it's a collection of assessments, observations, and reviews conducted by qualified professionals. The goal is to create a complete picture of your child's strengths, challenges, and unique learning needs. This evaluation looks at many areas of development and learning, and it's required by both federal law (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, or IDEA) and the Texas Education Code Chapter 29 before a child can receive special education services.

Think of the FIE as a deep dive into understanding how your child learns best. It's not about labeling your child—it's about unlocking the right support so they can thrive at school.

## Why Is a FIE Important at Grapevine-Colleyville ISD?

Grapevine-Colleyville ISD serves approximately 13,120 students across its campuses, and the district is committed to meeting the needs of all learners. For families whose children may need additional support, the FIE serves as the gateway to special education services.

Without a completed FIE, your child cannot be found eligible for an Individualized Education Program (IEP), which provides legally protected, customized support in the classroom. The evaluation helps ensure that any services your child receives are truly tailored to their individual needs—not a one-size-fits-all approach.

In a district the size of Grapevine-Colleyville ISD, having a thorough FIE also helps teachers and specialists work together effectively. The evaluation results become a roadmap that the entire team can use to support your child's success.

## What Does a FIE Evaluate?

The FIE is designed to be comprehensive, meaning it looks at multiple areas of your child's development. While every evaluation is individualized based on the concerns raised, a FIE typically assesses:

- **Cognitive ability**: How your child thinks, reasons, and solves problems
- **Academic achievement**: Skills in reading, writing, math, and other school subjects
- **Communication skills**: How your child understands and uses language
- **Social and emotional development**: How your child interacts with others and manages emotions
- **Adaptive behavior**: Daily living skills and how your child functions independently
- **Motor skills**: Fine motor (like handwriting) and gross motor (like coordination) abilities
- **Health and developmental history**: Medical factors that may affect learning
- **Behavioral observations**: How your child acts in different settings

The evaluation team will use a variety of tools including standardized tests, interviews with you and your child's teachers, classroom observations, and a review of school records. Your input as a parent is a valuable part of this process—no one knows your child better than you do.

## How to Request a FIE at Grapevine-Colleyville ISD

Any parent or guardian can request a FIE for their child at any time. You don't need to wait for the school to suggest it. Here's how to get started in Grapevine-Colleyville ISD:

**Step 1: Put your request in writing.** While you can certainly talk to your child's teacher or counselor, it's best to also submit a written request. This creates a clear record and starts the legal timeline.

**Step 2: Address your letter to the right person.** You can send your request to your child's principal, the campus special education coordinator, or the district's Special Education Department.

**Step 3: Be specific about your concerns.** In your letter, briefly describe why you believe your child may need an evaluation. For example, you might mention struggles with reading, difficulty staying focused, or challenges with social skills.

**Step 4: Keep a copy for your records.** Always keep copies of any letters you send and note the date you submitted them.

Here's a simple template you can use:

*Dear [Principal or Special Education Coordinator],*

*I am the parent of [Child's Name], who attends [School Name] in [Grade]. I am writing to formally request a Full Individual Evaluation (FIE) to determine whether my child may be eligible for special education services. I have concerns about [briefly describe concerns, such as reading difficulties, attention challenges, etc.].*

*Please contact me at your earliest convenience to discuss next steps. Thank you for your time and attention to this matter.*

*Sincerely,*
*[Your Name]*
*[Your Phone Number]*
*[Your Email Address]*
*[Date]*

Once the district receives your written request, they must respond within 15 school days to either provide you with consent forms to begin the evaluation or explain in writing why they are declining to evaluate.

## The FIE Timeline: What Texas Law Requires

Texas law is very clear about timelines to protect your child's right to a timely evaluation. Here's what you need to know:

- **Within 15 school days** of receiving your written request, the district must provide you with a Notice of Proposed Evaluation and consent forms, or a written explanation if they are refusing to evaluate.
- **Within 45 school days** of receiving your signed consent, the district must complete the evaluation and present the FIE report at an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting.

These 45 days refer to school days, so summer break, winter break, and other holidays don't count toward this timeline. However, if your child is absent for an extended period or if there are other extenuating circumstances, the timeline may be adjusted—but the district must document this.

It's important to sign and return your consent forms promptly. The clock doesn't start until the district receives your written consent.

## Understanding Your FIE Report

After the evaluation is complete, you'll receive a written FIE report. This document can be lengthy and full of technical terms, but don't be intimidated—you have every right to understand it fully.

The FIE report typically includes:

- **Background information**: Your child's history, including health, developmental milestones, and educational background
- **Assessment results**: Scores from the various tests administered, often presented as standard scores, percentiles, or grade equivalents
- **Observations**: Notes from professionals who observed your child in different settings
- **Summary and conclusions**: An explanation of what the results mean and whether your child meets the criteria for a disability under IDEA
- **Recommendations**: Suggested next steps, which may include eligibility for special education services

If you receive the report and feel overwhelmed, that's completely normal. Here are some tips:

- **Request the report before the ARD meeting.** You have the right to receive the FIE report at least 5 school days before the ARD meeting, giving you time to review it.
- **Make a list of questions.** Write down anything you don't understand so you can ask the evaluation team to explain.
- **Ask for a plain-language summary.** You can request that the school psychologist or diagnostician walk you through the report in everyday terms.

## What Happens After the FIE? (The ARD Meeting)

Once the FIE is complete, you'll be invited to an Admission, Review, and Dismissal (ARD) meeting. This is where the team—including you—will review the evaluation results and make decisions about your child's eligibility and services.

At the ARD meeting, the team will:

1. **Review the FIE findings**: Each evaluator may share their results and answer questions.
2. **Determine eligibility**: Based on the FIE, the team will decide if your child qualifies for special education under one of the 13 disability categories in IDEA.
3. **Develop an IEP (if eligible)**: If your child is found eligible, the team will begin creating an Individualized Education Program—a written plan that outlines the specific services, goals, and accommodations your child will receive.

As a parent, you are a full and equal member of the ARD committee. Your insights, concerns, and preferences matter. If you disagree with any decisions, you have the right to voice your concerns and request additional meetings or mediation.
